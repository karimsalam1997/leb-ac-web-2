# Lebanon Still Votes in 1932

By Karim Salam

In Lebanon, "where are you from?" is not just a question about identity. It is a political fact.

You can be born in Beirut, work in Beirut, pay rent in Beirut, raise children in Beirut, and still not be "from" Beirut in the eyes of the state. Your official belonging is tied to your `nufus`, the civil registry that links your family to a place that may have very little to do with your actual life.

This is why the 1932 census matters. It was the only official census Lebanon ever conducted, and it happened under the French Mandate, before independence, before the Civil War, before mass urbanization, before the suburbs became the real country, and before the waves of emigration that emptied entire towns.

The census did not simply count people. It helped turn people into political units. Families were fixed to places. Sects were counted. Representation was attached to demographic balance. Over time, that old count became one of the foundations of the Lebanese system.

This is where Lebanese identity becomes stranger than we like to admit. We talk as if being "from" a place is blood, origin, purity, or some deep ancestral truth. We talk about old Beiruti families, real villagers, outsiders, newcomers, and people who "only recently" arrived. But go back far enough and almost everyone came from somewhere else. People moved before 1932, after 1932, during the war, after the war, after the economy collapsed, and after every crisis that made life somewhere else more possible than life at home.

The difference is that the state froze one moment and made it feel eternal. If your ancestor was registered in Beirut at the right time, you can inherit the status of being Beiruti. If your family arrived later, you can remain a newcomer for generations. The difference may look like identity, but it is also timing, paperwork, and political design.

In Lebanon, you do not vote where you live. You vote where you are registered. That one fact explains much of the country. Your municipality can fail you, your street can flood, your garbage can pile up outside your building, and the people responsible for governing that place may not need your vote at all. They may be accountable to another population: the registered voters of the area, many of whom may not live there anymore.

Beirut is the obvious example. In the 2025 municipal elections, official figures put Beirut at around 514,800 registered voters, with about 108,600 actual voters. That is roughly 21 percent turnout among registered voters. The people who use the city every day are not the same as the people who politically count there.

Jounieh makes the same point in a smaller frame. Public figures vary, but older reporting described a city with roughly 17,000 registered voters and a population many times larger than that. The exact number matters less than the structure. A small registered electorate can be managed. A large resident electorate cannot.

If only 17,000 people can vote, and maybe half of them actually show up, politics becomes a game of small margins. Families can be counted. Mukhtars can be useful. Funeral attendance matters. A few hundred votes can decide the balance. This is the world the Lebanese political machine understands best: small pools, inherited loyalties, old names, and voters whose social location is already known.

Now imagine that everyone voted where they actually lived.

This would not simply change a few municipal results. It would change the logic of politics itself. Urban voters would become harder to control. Renters, young families, and new residents would matter. Politics would be pushed toward transport, housing, schools, electricity, public space, waste, policing, rents, and the everyday problems people actually live with.

That is exactly why the system resists it.

The usual explanation is that Lebanon avoids a new census because it would reveal the real sectarian balance: how many Christians, how many Sunnis, how many Shia, how many Druze, and so on. That is true, but incomplete. The deeper threat is that every political class would lose control of its own fiefdom.

Christian parties would be threatened because old balances help preserve old claims. Muslim parties would be threatened because their power also depends on fixed districts, controlled blocs, and inherited registration. Zaims would be threatened because they know how to manage villages better than cities. Everyone benefits from a system where voters are administratively predictable.

That is why changing the `nufus` question is so dangerous. It sounds like bureaucracy. But if people could shift their political belonging to where they actually live, the map would start to move. Empty places would lose some of their weight. Overcrowded places would gain it. Cities and suburbs would become harder to govern through sectarian inheritance alone.

This is why Lebanon does not count itself. Not because the system is confused, and not only because sects fear the numbers. Lebanon does not count itself because counting would make the real country visible, and the real country is much harder to control than the registered one.

The result is a strange kind of identity. Not fake because people's attachments are fake. Villages matter. Families matter. Memory matters. What is fake is the claim that these identities are fixed, pure, and politically natural.

They are not. They are historical arrangements that became files, then social status, then electoral machinery.

Lebanon is not simply refusing to update an old census. It is preserving a system where people live in one country and vote in another. That is the structure: a moving society governed through a frozen registry.

You may think you are from a place because your family has always been from there. Maybe there is truth in that. But in the Lebanon that votes, registers, marries, allocates power, and decides who matters, being "from" somewhere is also something colder: where the state froze your family, and where the system finds you useful.
