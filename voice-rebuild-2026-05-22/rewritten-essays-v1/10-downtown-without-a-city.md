# Downtown Without a City

The Beirut Souks are clean enough to make memory suspicious. You can walk there and feel that something has been restored, then notice that the thing restored is the image of a souk, not the argument of one.

Rafic Hariri is difficult because he built. That must be said before anything else. In the early 1990s, Downtown Beirut was shattered, legally tangled, physically dangerous, and politically heavy with all the things Lebanon did not want to discuss after the war.

But building can also erase. The old center was not precious because it was pretty. It mattered because it forced use. Merchants, clerks, students, priests, shoppers, bankers, drivers, port workers, and families crossed each other there because the city required it.

The civil war destroyed that shared machinery. The reconstruction could have treated the loss of ordinary encounter as the central wound. Instead, it treated destruction as an opening for comprehensive control.

The defenders of the new Downtown will say the old souks were not democratic. They are right. The old center had class hierarchy, sectarian lines, patriarchal habits, and property violence of its own. Nostalgia can lie as smoothly as development.

Still, the answer to a flawed city is not a stage set for capital. Solidere gave Beirut a center that looked international and felt supervised. The result was beautiful in places, sterile in others, and often unsure whether it wanted citizens or consumers.

Memory was allowed in as style. Old names returned. Stone returned. Facades returned. What did not return was the social disorder that makes a city belong to more than the people who can afford to linger in it.

After 2019, the weakness became impossible to hide. A district built around confidence did not know what to do with collapse. Banks closed their moral doors before their physical ones. Shops emptied. Streets that had once performed prosperity began to look like props after the actors left.

Downtown still can be repaired, but only if Beirut stops treating it as a trophy. It needs schools, cheap food, offices beyond the elite circuit, public transport, cultural rooms, libraries, small trades, benches, mixed housing, and reasons for ordinary people to pass through without buying anything.

The old center cannot come back. Nobody should pretend otherwise. The question is whether the next center can learn how to be needed.

For now, Downtown Beirut often feels like a city remembering itself through glass.
