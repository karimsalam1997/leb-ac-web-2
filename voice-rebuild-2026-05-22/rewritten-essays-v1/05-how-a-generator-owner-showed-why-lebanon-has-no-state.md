# How a Generator Owner Showed Why Lebanon Has No State

Abu Ali Itani's office is not a ministry, but it teaches the citizen faster than a civics textbook. You go there with a complaint about electricity and leave with a lesson about sovereignty in Lebanon.

The fair thing to say first is that Abu Ali did not invent the collapse. He did not design the postwar electricity policy, empty the treasury, block power plants, protect fuel interests, or turn Electricite du Liban into a national wound. He entered a gap others made.

But men like Abu Ali become powerful because gaps harden into systems. A service fails, a substitute appears, the substitute becomes normal, then the substitute develops clients, prices, rules, favors, punishments, and political protection. After enough years, the emergency is someone's property.

That is the Lebanese lesson. The country does not lack clever people. It has too many clever people trained to survive against institutions instead of through them. The citizen learns to ask who can help, who knows whom, who can call, who can bend the rule, who can make the office remember the file.

The word mehtail is ugly because it contains admiration and disgust at the same time. The mehtail is more than a scammer. He is fluent in the unwritten Lebanon, the Lebanon of timing, tone, names, doors, hints, pressure, gifts, and the exact sentence that makes a refusal soften.

The strongest argument for this culture is survival. If the ministry does not work, if the hospital has no medicine, if the judge is blocked, if the school is private because the public one was abandoned, then a family that refuses wasta can look noble and lose.

But survival habits become political habits. A person trained to solve everything privately begins to expect nothing publicly. He becomes brilliant at escape and weak at solidarity. He can fix his building, his cousin's file, his son's job, his mother's hospital bed. The commons remains a rumor.

That is how the Lebanese individual can look astonishing abroad and exhausted at home. In Sao Paulo, Abidjan, Sydney, Montreal, or Dubai, the same agility finds opportunity. In Lebanon, it turns inward, because each person is fighting the same broken gate from a different angle.

Abu Ali is funny until he isn't. He is the generator man, the informal mayor, the collector, the negotiator, the man you resent and still need. He proves that when the state retreats, power does not vanish. It moves closer to the staircase.

The reformist fantasy says new faces can fix this by speaking better. Lebanon has had plenty of better CVs since 2019. The cake got smaller. The knife stayed in the same hand.

The question is whether the citizen can be trained again. That sounds abstract until the elevator stops, the water tank empties, the school asks for dollars, and the office tells you to come back Monday. Lebanon's state problem is not hidden in theory. It waits at the counter.
