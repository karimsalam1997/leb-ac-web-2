# Sovereignty Theatre

In Beirut, everyone praises sovereignty until sovereignty asks for something. Then the word becomes decoration, useful on television, dangerous in a ministry, unbearable at the port.

The strongest case for the sovereignty talk is obvious. Lebanon is violated constantly. Israel bombs when it chooses. Iran arms through Lebanese territory. Syria has never fully accepted the border in its political imagination. France, America, Saudi Arabia, Qatar, and others speak to Beirut as if the country is a conference table.

So yes, sovereignty matters. A country that cannot decide war, control borders, protect judges, secure its port, pay its soldiers, or keep foreign patrons from choosing local winners is not fully sovereign in any serious sense.

But Lebanese sovereignty discourse often stops exactly where it becomes expensive. One camp says sovereignty means Hezbollah disarms. Another says sovereignty means resistance against Israel. A third says sovereignty means no foreign interference, except from the foreign friend currently funding its side.

Actual sovereignty would disappoint everyone. It would say no to Iranian weapons routes, no to Israeli raids, no to Saudi money as political muscle, no to American red lines, no to French tutelage, no to Syrian habits, and no to the Lebanese politician who wants the state strong only against his enemy.

That is how Resolution 1701 becomes theatre. Diplomats invoke it, parties interpret it, Israel violates it, Hezbollah maneuvers around it, and the Lebanese state is asked to perform authority in southern towns where authority is already contested by force.

The hard truth is that sovereignty begins with boring powers. Tax collection. Courts. Army salaries. Port manifests. Electricity bills. Customs. Municipal law. Banking losses. The right of a judge to ask a dangerous question without being destroyed for it.

Here every faction grows nervous. A real state would absorb Hezbollah's military role, then audit the banks, expose the generator economy, regulate quarries, inspect customs, discipline ministries, and turn services from private favors into public obligations.

The Lebanese love sovereignty as an accusation. We use it to expose the other side's patron. We rarely use it as a mirror. That is why the word can sound grand on a podium and dead in a public office.

Sovereignty is not a flag above Baabda Palace. It is the moment a citizen does not need a party, a cousin, a priest, a sheikh, a militia, a banker, or an embassy to make the law move.
