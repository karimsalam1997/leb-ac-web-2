# The Seventeen Countries

Lebanon does not always feel like one country. Some days it feels like seventeen small sovereignties sharing traffic, weather, debt, and a passport line at General Security.

The generous reading says this pluralism is Lebanon's genius. Beirut, Tripoli, Zahle, Saida, Nabatieh, Jounieh, Aley, Baalbek, and the villages between them really do carry different habits of speech, food, worship, memory, and fear. A flat republic would be a lie.

But pluralism becomes a trap when every service turns into a border. A road contract belongs to one faction. A school belongs to one network. A hospital bed moves through one office. A garbage route becomes sectarian because someone must live near the stink.

The Lebanese citizen learns the map twice. There is the official map in school, with governorates and districts. Then there is the map that matters when something breaks: who controls this municipality, which party answers here, which zaim can call that clerk, which checkpoint is friendly, which neighborhood requires silence.

This is not cultural richness. It is administrative fragmentation. The country keeps producing citizens who are intimate with their small sphere and suspicious of the common one.

The defense is always security. Communities remember massacres, displacement, betrayal, siege, occupation, and abandonment. No one gives up local protection because a writer in Beirut says the republic would be nicer without it.

That fear has evidence. The civil war is not ancient history. Many people who lived it still decide elections, own buildings, fund parties, teach children, command loyalty, and carry the private inventory of who did what in which street.

But a country cannot remain forever organized around the memory of what might happen if the other side gains too much. That turns every institution into a hostage exchange. The army, the court, the school, the ministry, the municipality, the port, and the budget all become questions of communal exposure.

The tragedy is that Lebanon is too interconnected to survive as fragments and too frightened to become a republic. The electricity grid does not care about sect. Neither does the banking collapse. Neither did ammonium nitrate on August 4, 2020.

The map we need is not a fantasy of sameness. It is a map where difference stops being the excuse for private rule. A Maronite village, a Shia town, a Sunni city, a Druze mountain, an Armenian quarter, and a mixed street in Beirut can stay themselves without each becoming a miniature state.

Until then, the citizen will keep carrying two passports in his head: one for Lebanon, and one for the Lebanon that actually answers the phone.
