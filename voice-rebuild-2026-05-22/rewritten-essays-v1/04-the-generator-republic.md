# The Generator Republic

The generator bill in Beirut is more honest than the constitution. It tells the citizen who has power, who can punish, who must be paid, and who will answer faster than the ministry when the lights go out.

The easy story says Lebanon has bad services because the state is incompetent. There is truth there. Electricite du Liban has been a national disgrace for decades, and no serious person can look at the grid after 1997, after 2006, after 2019, and pretend this is normal failure.

But incompetence alone cannot last this long with such precision. A broken system that survives for decades is usually doing work for someone. In Lebanon, darkness has a market. Shortage has owners. The hour of electricity withheld from the public grid appears again as profit on a private bill.

The generator owner is not just a villain. That is what makes him useful to study. In Hamra or Sakiet el-Janzeer, he can be rude, generous, predatory, protective, and weirdly necessary in the same week. He is a private sovereign with diesel in his veins.

The customer hates him and pays him. The customer curses him and calls him when the line burns. The customer knows the state should provide electricity, then asks the generator man for mercy because the fridge is full, the elevator is stuck, and the child has homework.

This is how Lebanon trains its citizen. The person learns that law is too slow, institutions are theatrical, and survival depends on knowing the informal path. The mehtail is born there, in the gap between the printed rule and the rule that actually moves the file.

The strongest defense of the mehtail is that he is intelligent. He reads the room. He knows which clerk is strict, which officer is vain, which document is fake-important, which cousin can reach the right person. In another country, some of that talent might become logistics, law, management, or entrepreneurship.

Here, too much of it becomes evasion. The system rewards the person who knows how to get around the thing more than the person who tries to build the thing. A Lebanese man can solve an impossible problem by noon and still live in a country where nothing common works by sunset.

That is why fixing electricity would do more than turn on the lights. It would turn off an economy. It would weaken a whole class of private substitutes: generator networks, fuel importers, local brokers, municipal understandings, party protections, and the quiet power of the man who can decide whether your apartment breathes tonight.

No reform plan is serious if it treats the generator as a technical side effect. He is political evidence. He shows how sovereignty is broken into neighborhoods, priced by amperes, collected in cash, and defended as necessity.

In Beirut, the state does not disappear. It shrinks into a bill pushed under the door.
