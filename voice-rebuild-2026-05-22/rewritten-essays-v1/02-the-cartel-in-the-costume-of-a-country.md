# The Cartel in the Costume of a Country

In Beirut, the word "state" can sound like a joke before breakfast. The citizen pays Electricite du Liban in theory, the generator man in fact, the private school because the public one has been starved, the hospital because illness cannot wait for reform, and the party because somebody must answer the phone.

The usual defense of Lebanon's parties is that they protect communities abandoned by the state. That defense is not imaginary. In Nabatieh, Tripoli, Zahle, Dahieh, Chouf, and Akkar, people have learned from experience that the formal state often arrives late, arrives weak, or arrives as a clerk asking for a document nobody told them to bring.

But the parties are not outside the weakness. They live from it. Hezbollah, the Lebanese Forces, Amal, the Free Patriotic Movement, the PSP, and the rest all describe the state as a missing thing while feeding from the exact arrangement that keeps it missing.

Hezbollah's weapons make the imbalance obvious. A party that can make war decisions has a different weight from a party that can only block a file, fill a ministry, or trade a judge. That fact must be said plainly. Villages in the south pay in bodies for decisions no ordinary Lebanese family was asked to approve.

Still, the weapons argument becomes a shelter for everyone else. The anti-Hezbollah camp can speak of sovereignty while leaving untouched the port, the banks, the generators, the quarries, the customs networks, the contracting rackets, and the municipal machines that make sovereignty unprofitable for them too.

A real state in Lebanon would not simply ask Hezbollah for its rockets. It would ask the banker where the deposits went after 2019. It would ask the minister why electricity was made impossible. It would ask the port authority what entered Hangar 12. It would ask every party why citizens meet them first and the republic second.

That is the cartel. It is not one secret room. It is a public arrangement in which each faction points to the other faction's crime as proof of its own necessity. The Shia party needs the Christian fear. The Christian party needs the Shia weapon. The banker needs the politician. The politician needs the judge to hesitate.

People call this sectarianism because sect is the visible costume. Under it sits brokerage. Each citizen is trained to ask who can solve the problem, not which law applies. Each leader becomes valuable by controlling the passage between helplessness and service.

The hardest part is that many of these services are real. A hospital bed found by a party office in 2021 was real. A school fee covered by a zaim was real. A passport file moved by a phone call was real. That is why the system survives. It gives relief with one hand and preserves the wound with the other.

Lebanon is not missing a state because Lebanese people are uniquely chaotic. That insult is too easy, and it lets the powerful hide behind national character. Lebanon has a state-shaped cartel because too many people with power prefer a weak republic that can be milked to a strong one that can count, tax, audit, and punish.

The costume still matters. Flags matter. Martyrs matter. Sermons matter. But under the fabric there is a simpler body: ministries as prizes, courts as threats, services as pressure, citizens as clients, and sovereignty as a word everyone praises until it knocks on their own door.
