# AI Writing Research Dossier

Built on 2026-05-22 for the Lebanese Academic voice rebuild.

## Bottom Line

The problem with AI writing is not one punctuation mark, one word, or one detector score. The problem is that the prose often behaves as if it has no body. It explains without risk. It balances without deciding. It moves from claim to claim with a neatness that feels learned from school essays, strategy decks, and platform copy rather than from a person who has sat in traffic on Corniche el Mazraa, argued with a ministry clerk, or watched Beirut lose power at the same hour three nights in a row.

For Lebanese Academic, the cure is not fake informality. The cure is pressure: lived place, named institutions, uneven rhythm, argumentative nerve, and sentences that can survive contact with the reader's own memory of Beirut.

## What the Research Says

### 1. AI prose has measurable style habits

AlAfnan and MohdZuki's 2023 study of ChatGPT-4 writing found that generated academic responses often came in steady paragraph units, with sentences clustering around similar lengths and a declarative explanatory mode. That matters because the current essays sometimes do the same thing: a claim, a clarification, a concession, a larger abstraction, then a landing sentence.

Source: [Journal of Artificial Intelligence and Technology](https://ojs.istp-press.com/jait/article/view/267)

### 2. AI prose can be formally competent and still rhetorically thin

The 2025 Frontiers corpus study comparing student essays and generated essays points to a familiar problem: AI texts can overuse repeated phrases and formulaic patterns, which weakens rhetorical depth. The danger for us is not bad grammar. The danger is clean prose that keeps giving the reader the same type of paragraph.

Source: [Frontiers in Education](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/education/articles/10.3389/feduc.2025.1616935/full)

### 3. LLM style is entering academic writing itself

Geng and Trotta's arXiv study looked at roughly one million arXiv papers and found a measurable rise in ChatGPT-style abstract language after 2022, especially in computer science. The useful lesson is simple: AI style is not just detectable by "bad writing." It can appear inside polished academic prose, especially through word-frequency drift and revision habits.

Source: [arXiv: Is ChatGPT Transforming Academics' Writing Style?](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.08627)

### 4. The common pattern is formal, impersonal, and repetitive

Tercon and Robnik-Sikonja's survey describes reported AI-text traits such as formal and impersonal style, lower lexical diversity, smaller vocabulary range, and repetition. For Lebanese Academic, that means every paragraph needs a human pressure point: a place, date, object, person, argument, memory, accusation, or concession that could not be swapped into another country.

Source: [arXiv: Linguistic Characteristics of AI-Generated Text](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.05136)

### 5. Personal-experience prose is where AI feels most false

Markowitz, Hancock, and Bailenson compared ChatGPT hotel reviews with human reviews and found that generated text differed across style and structure. Their larger point is useful for essays: when a text claims proximity to experience, but the language has no actual particularity, the reader feels the fraud before they can name it.

Source: [Stanford Social Media Lab](https://sml.stanford.edu/publications/2023/linguistic-markers-inherently-false-ai-communication-and-intentionally-false)

### 6. Readers now hear "AI" as interchangeable identity

TIME's 2026 piece on the insult "did AI write that?" is not an academic source, but it captures the social fact: people read AI voice as generic identity. The accusation stings because it says the writer could be anyone. Lebanese Academic cannot sound like anyone.

Source: [TIME](https://time.com/7371832/looks-like-ai-writing-online-insult/)

## What This Means For Karim's Essays

### The AI-shaped habits to kill

- The explanatory treadmill: "This is why," "This matters," "That does not mean," "The deeper point is."
- Symmetrical concession: a paragraph that grants the obvious objection in a perfectly balanced way, then returns to the planned thesis.
- Abstraction pile-ups: memory, identity, sovereignty, heritage, infrastructure, belonging, authenticity, all inside one breath.
- Soft symbolic verbs: "serves as," "represents," "stands as," "marks," when "is," "does," "kills," "protects," "hides," or "sells" would be stronger.
- The tidy ending: a paragraph that lands on a moral sentence rather than an image, a person, a place, or a cost.
- Overcomplete explanation: telling the reader every implication instead of trusting them to follow a wound once it has been shown.

### The new voice discipline

- Start from scene before thesis.
- Let a paragraph turn once, not three times.
- Keep one named thing in every paragraph: Abu Ali Itani, Solidere, Nahr el Kalb, Hamra, Ras Beirut, the 1932 census, Rafic Hariri, Albert Naccache, Harissa, Horsh Beirut.
- Use short sentences as verdicts.
- Use long sentences only when the thought needs accumulation.
- Keep a live opponent in the room. State their strongest case fairly, then cut through it.
- Prefer ugly clarity to beautiful vagueness.
- When theory appears, make it pay rent by explaining an object the reader can see.

## The Practical Test

Before any rewritten essay is accepted, ask:

1. Could this paragraph be moved to Cairo, Athens, or Mexico City with only the names changed?
2. Does the paragraph contain a sentence that sounds like a grant proposal?
3. Is the ending earned by an image, or is it a school-essay conclusion?
4. Did the paragraph explain more than it witnessed?
5. Does the prose sound like a person with a stake in the argument, or like a machine arranging ideas by topic?

If the answer is wrong, rewrite from the place outward.
