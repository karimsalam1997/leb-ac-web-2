# Flagship Essay

## The City That Could Not Repair Itself

**Final title:** The City That Could Not Repair Itself
**Dek:** Downtown Beirut was not simply rebuilt badly after the war. It was rebuilt in a way that made later crises harder to absorb: vacancy became rational, repair became optional, ownership became distant, and the symbolic center of the capital lost the ordinary citizens who make cities heal.
**Byline:** Karim Salam
**Publication date:** May 8, 2026
**Estimated reading time:** 30 min read

**Excerpt:** A political-economy and memory essay on Downtown Beirut: how Solidere's reconstruction turned the symbolic center of the capital into a polished but fragile district, why archaeology and war memory were thinned into curated heritage, and why vacancy made later repair so weak.

**Full body:**

Walk through Downtown Beirut now and the old critique no longer quite fits. It is not simply glossy. That would be easier to condemn. Gloss has a clean surface. Downtown today is stranger: restored stone beside blast-damaged facades, expensive apartments above dark windows, reopened restaurants near empty streets, international brands returning to souks that still feel more like a commercial experiment than a city center, sidewalks that can look manicured in one stretch and broken in the next.

This is not only the failure of a 1990s reconstruction project. It is the failure of a district to recover from everything that came after: the 2006 sit-in, the long security closure around Parliament, the 2019 uprising, the financial collapse, Covid, the port explosion, the years of stalled repair, the 2024 war, and the fragile attempts at revival in 2025. Downtown was built to signal that Lebanon had returned. But when Lebanon kept breaking, the center did not know how to repair itself.

That is the deeper riddle. Downtown did not fail because nothing was built. It failed because the wrong thing was built with astonishing confidence, and because that wrong thing had no ordinary social metabolism. Beirut's old center was not only a piece of land. It was the city's shared nervous system: souks, cinemas, offices, homes, bus routes, banks, churches, mosques, hotels, bookstores, coffee shops, shortcuts, arguments, rituals of shopping and passing and recognizing. It was not innocent. No city center is. It carried class hierarchy, sectarian tension, uneven ownership, bad infrastructure, and all the ordinary cruelties of commerce. But it performed the work a center must perform. It forced difference into use.

After the war, Downtown was treated less as a wounded civic organ than as an underperforming asset. The reconstruction promised to return Beirut to itself. Instead it asked Beirut to recognize itself in an image.

The common story is familiar: the war destroyed Downtown, Rafic Hariri rebuilt it, Solidere privatized it, and the result became beautiful but empty. That story is not wrong. It is only too thin. It misses the deeper violence, which was not only architectural or economic. Downtown Beirut was the place where Lebanon might have confronted three layers of memory at once: the Ottoman and Mandate city of everyday coexistence, the civil war city of rupture and accountability, and the ancient city buried beneath the asphalt, nearly five millennia of continuous habitation under the streets of the modern capital.

Instead, the reconstruction selected the memories it could monetize, displayed the ruins it could domesticate, erased the claims it could not absorb, and called the whole operation progress.

This is why the word memorycide matters. Albert Naccache used it to describe the destruction of Beirut's archaeological record in the 1990s. It is a severe word, and it should be. It names something more precise than neglect. It names the killing of the conditions through which a people can remember itself.

Downtown Beirut was not only rebuilt. It was taught to forget.

But forgetting was only the first failure. The second was more practical, and in some ways more damaging: Downtown was rebuilt in a way that made repair weak.

### The Second Destruction

The first destruction of Downtown came through war. The second came through reconstruction. The third has been quieter: the slow destruction of non-use.

Non-use does not look dramatic at first. A shuttered shop is not a massacre. A dark apartment is not a bulldozer. A sidewalk left broken after a blast does not appear in history books the way a battle does. But non-use changes the city at the cellular level. It means fewer residents pressing for repairs, fewer shopkeepers sweeping the same pavement every morning, fewer children making the street answer to daily life, fewer neighbors noticing damage before it becomes abandonment.

A city occupied by residents complains. A city held as an asset can wait.

That difference became brutally visible after the Beirut port explosion. In lived neighborhoods such as Gemmayzeh, Mar Mikhael, Rmeil, and parts of Achrafieh, residents and volunteers moved immediately because the place was inhabited. People knew which staircases were unsafe, which old woman lived alone, which shopkeeper needed plywood, which architect could assess a wall, which NGO was working on which block. The response was exhausted, uneven, underfunded, and unfair, but it had social intelligence.

Downtown had something else: distance. Too much of it had been built for owners, investors, visitors, office tenants, high-end consumers, and political-security arrangements that do not behave like a resident community. When such a district is damaged, there is less neighborhood pressure to repair quickly. A storefront can wait for the right tenant. A luxury apartment can remain dark until the right buyer appears. A damaged facade can sit beside a restored one because the social cost of vacancy is not paid by the same people who hold the asset.

This does not mean nothing is happening. In 2024 and 2025, the souks began to reopen in pieces, restaurants and brands returned, Saifi's food-and-beverage scene revived, and Solidere announced the removal of concrete barriers, flower boxes, and other obstacles from major streets to improve circulation. This matters. A dead center should not be romanticized as proof of purity. Life returning is good.

But revival is not the same as recovery. A district can reopen without becoming public. It can attract brands without creating residents. It can fill restaurants without solving ownership. It can raise occupancy claims without restoring civic life. The question is not whether a few streets are busier than they were. The question is whether Downtown has regained the social machinery that lets a city heal itself after damage.

So far, the answer is not convincing.

### The Old Center Was Not a Symbol

Lebanese nostalgia often lies by making the past too lovely. The old Beirut souks can easily become a soft-focus postcard: tiled arcades, elegant shopkeepers, mixed crowds, a vanished cosmopolitanism that asks nothing difficult of the present. That version is comforting because it turns the old center into mood.

But the real importance of the old Downtown was not that it was charming. It was that it was useful.

Al-balad worked because it gathered necessities. People came because they had to come: for cloth, shoes, gold, documents, banking, transport, repairs, books, restaurants, offices, cinema, government, gossip, work. Christians, Muslims, Armenians, Palestinians, Syrians, rural migrants, merchants, port workers, students, bankers, tourists, taxi drivers, clerks, servants, rich families, poor families, the respectable and the marginal, all moved through a center that belonged to no single community because it was too necessary to be possessed by one.

That kind of coexistence is not sentimental. It is infrastructural. A city does not become shared because everyone loves each other. It becomes shared when people must use the same streets.

The civil war shattered that use. The center became a front line, a scar, a battlefield, a buffer, a place avoided by the living and occupied by militias, snipers, weeds, and memory. When the fighting ended, the question was not simply how to rebuild buildings. The question was whether the center could again become a mechanism of shared life.

That question required patience. It required property settlement, public transport, archaeology, housing, low-rent commerce, memorialization, democratic planning, and some honest account of what had happened there. It required the state to behave like a custodian of collective memory.

Lebanon did not have that state. What it had was a postwar elite settlement, a ruined capital, a prime minister with immense ambition and financial reach, a planning imagination trained by Gulf modernity, and a political class eager to turn reconstruction into the architecture of the new order.

In a broken country, doing something can become an argument against asking what should be done.

Saree Makdisi saw this almost immediately. In his 1994 "Letter from Beirut," he described a city covered in the imagery of an imaginary future, posters and construction sites presenting reconstruction as destiny. The public was offered a false choice: accept the present ruins or accept the future being sold to you. The logic was brutally simple. Something must be done. This is something. Therefore this must be done.

That sentence could be the motto of postwar Lebanon.

### The Machine That Replaced the City

Solidere was not only a developer. It was a legal invention.

The Beirut Central District had an enormous property problem. Lots were fragmented. Ownership claims were layered across generations. Tenants, shopkeepers, landlords, heirs, waqf properties, damaged buildings, missing records, contested rights, and ruined infrastructure made ordinary reconstruction nearly impossible. Makdisi describes the scale as extraordinary, with hundreds of thousands of property-related claims tied to the old center. Tamam Mango's thesis traces the way this crisis became the justification for a private real estate holding company with powers no ordinary developer should possess.

Law 117 of 1991 created the framework. Solidere was established in 1994. Former property rights were converted into Class A shares; fresh capital entered through Class B shares. The company was given the authority to acquire, assemble, develop, sell, lease, and exploit the central district. In Solidere's own annual-report language, its mission included acquiring real estate, financing infrastructure, preparing and reconstructing the Beirut Central District, restoring and erecting buildings, developing the landfill, and selling or leasing lots. That sounds administrative. In practice, it meant that the symbolic center of the republic was transferred into a corporate structure whose obligations were not the same as the obligations of a city.

The distinction matters. A city center must answer to memory, access, citizenship, use, and time. A real estate company must answer to capital, land value, shareholders, debt, market timing, and saleable product. Sometimes those interests overlap. In Downtown Beirut, the overlap was never strong enough to carry the moral weight of the place.

Mango's work gives the abstract machinery a human scale. In one case, a property owner whose home fell inside the new district found that title had shifted into the company's framework and that retaining ownership required a payment he could not simply produce. The language around such cases was always clean: compensation, shares, redevelopment, public interest. But the lived experience was closer to dispossession translated into paperwork.

The old owners did not simply lose buildings. They lost position inside a social map. A merchant in the old souk did not own only square meters. He owned a route, a customer network, a reputation, a place in the memory of others. A tenant did not simply rent space. She participated in the dense web through which the city knew where things were and who belonged near them. When those rights were transformed into shares, the legal system could pretend value had been preserved. It had not. A balance sheet can price land. It cannot price being known.

This is the first central mistake of the Downtown project: it confused settlement with repair.

Settlement is what happens when claims are processed. Repair is what happens when a social world is given conditions to live again. Solidere settled many claims by absorbing them into a new corporate order. But the city that had produced those claims, the actual tissue of shops, residents, trades, paths, habits, and mixed use, was not restored. It was replaced by a cleaner and more expensive idea of itself.

Hariri's defenders have always had one serious argument: without a powerful vehicle, Downtown might have remained rubble for decades. That argument should not be dismissed too quickly. Lebanon's public institutions were weak, broke, captured, and exhausted. The postwar state could barely discipline warlords it had just reabsorbed into government. The bureaucratic route might have produced paralysis, theft, delay, and decay.

But this defense proves only that Lebanon needed capacity. It does not prove that capacity had to be organized as a private land bank with extraordinary public powers. It does not prove that the center had to be rebuilt primarily for high-end consumption, foreign capital, and land-value appreciation. It does not prove that archaeology had to be sacrificed, ordinary commerce priced out, and public memory thinned into ornament.

The tragedy is not that Hariri built. It is that he built the postwar order in stone.

### The Future As Blackmail

Downtown was sold to the Lebanese through the language of return: the heart will beat again, the souks will come back, memory will be preserved, the ancient city will meet the modern future. The promotional vocabulary was almost tender. Beirut would be ancient and futuristic, wounded and healed, familiar and new.

This language mattered because it disguised a change in ownership as an act of restoration.

Makdisi was especially sharp on this point. Solidere's reconstruction did not abandon memory. It used memory constantly. It promised old names, old facades, old routes, old souks, old charm. But the memory was increasingly visual and theatrical. A new shopping district could be called the Beirut Souks because it gestured toward the old souks in location and design, even if the social order that made a souk a souk had disappeared. The sign survived the referent.

A souk is not just a retail form. It is a memory machine. It remembers through repetition and informality: the tailor who knows a family across generations, the vendor who extends credit, the clerk who knows which alley avoids the heat, the argument over quality, the old religious institution at the edge of the commercial lane, the cheap meal, the bus nearby, the mixed crowds produced by need rather than ideology.

Replace that with a high-end shopping complex and you may preserve the name, but you have changed the verb. The old souk gathered. The new souk filters.

One early text about rebuilding the souks promised that the old memory would be carried into the future. It described a vast new structure on the original location, shops, offices, housing, leisure, cafes, esplanades, a large underground parking lot, archaeological integration, and green public spaces. The promise was not crude erasure. It was something subtler: curated continuity.

Curated continuity can be more dangerous than destruction because it lets the destroyer speak in the voice of the custodian.

The souks became the perfect object lesson. Downtown's reconstruction did not simply replace old with new. It replaced lived complexity with controlled reference. Beirut received heritage as atmosphere, not inheritance. It received memory as branding, not obligation.

The result was visible years later. Michael Karam's 2014 account of walking through Downtown described a district that once seemed to carry postwar pride but had become a ghost town, fenced, securitized, and economically stalled. He had once believed in the project, or at least in the feeling it produced. By 2014, the patient had gone into arrest.

That ghostliness was not a mysterious failure of Lebanese vibes. It followed from the business model. Downtown had been made to depend on forms of demand that were never stable enough to animate a national center: Gulf tourists, high-end retail, luxury real estate, foreign investors, wealthy expatriates, regional calm, and the idea that land values would keep climbing if the surfaces stayed polished. When assassinations, war, political paralysis, regional crisis, and economic collapse came, the model had little ordinary life underneath it. A center built as a premium destination could not fall back on being a city.

This is the second central mistake of the Downtown project: it treated public life as an aesthetic effect of development, not as the development's foundation.

### The Political Economy of Emptiness

Downtown Beirut was never only an urban project. It was a political economy.

The postwar Hariri vision imagined Lebanon as a finance, service, tourism, and real estate hub, a Mediterranean-Gulf connector able to attract regional capital and restore Beirut's prewar role as a commercial capital. In that worldview, Downtown was not simply the old center. It was a signal. If investors could see cranes, polished facades, restored streets, and luxury addresses in the heart of the capital, they could believe Lebanon had returned.

The problem is that a signal can become detached from the system it claims to represent.

Lebanon's reconstruction relied heavily on borrowing, capital inflows, real estate appreciation, and the ability of the state and banking system to keep confidence alive. A large body of work on postwar Beirut has shown how real estate became a storage device for external money: diaspora savings, Gulf capital, speculative investment, and elite wealth seeking safety in land. Bruno Marot calls this "pegged urbanization," a useful phrase because it captures the way Beirut's property market was tied to the larger Lebanese financial model. The city did not merely grow. It absorbed money into concrete.

Downtown was the flagship of that order. It turned land into shares, memory into real estate, and the symbolic center into a balance-sheet instrument. It was supposed to prove that Lebanon could make the future bankable.

But ordinary Beirutis did not have bankable lives at the scale Downtown required. The luxury apartments were too large, too expensive, and too disconnected from local purchasing power. Research on the postwar housing market shows a persistent mismatch: high-end units built for foreigners, expatriates, and the upper classes, while actual demand in Beirut was smaller, poorer, and more local. The foreign buyer was always more fantasy than foundation. Even before the full financial collapse, Beirut was full of apartments designed for a market that appeared in presentations more reliably than in transactions.

This is why Downtown's emptiness has a political meaning. It is not just that restaurants closed or tourists stopped coming. It is that the center was built around people who did not need it enough to save it.

A real city center survives crisis because ordinary life has no choice but to keep using it. People still need the bus, the clinic, the cheap shop, the school, the ministry counter, the repairman, the cafe where one coffee can last an hour, the public bench, the crossing point, the square where politics can happen. Downtown Beirut, as reconstructed, reduced too many of these necessities. It became important symbolically and weak functionally.

There is a cruel lesson here. The old Downtown was valuable because people who disagreed about everything still needed it. The new Downtown became fragile because too many of its intended users could leave.

Luxury demand is a weak basis for national memory.

### Vacancy Is A System

This is where the draft has to become harder, because "empty" is not just a mood. It is a structure.

Public numbers on Downtown vacancy are difficult to trust because occupancy can mean different things: leased, owned, used, inhabited, open to the public, or simply not available for sale. A company can report high occupancy and a passerby can still experience emptiness. Both may be describing different realities. A luxury apartment can be owned and uninhabited. A shop can be leased and rarely active. A street can technically be open and socially avoided.

The larger Beirut data makes the pattern harder to dismiss. World Bank project documents after the port explosion describe a speculative housing market with a severe mismatch between local needs and high-end supply. They note that vacancy in higher-end multi-storied apartment buildings in Beirut Municipality exceeded 50 percent, while the vacancy rate for plots of land was about one third. They also describe a housing system that incentivized homeownership and speculation over secure rental housing, with land prices rising far faster than incomes in the decade before the collapse.

Downtown is the most concentrated expression of that system. It is what happens when the city center becomes the most prestigious place to store value, but not necessarily the most necessary place to live.

Vacancy in such a system is not an accident. It can be rational. If owners expect future appreciation, they wait. If selling at the real market price would force everyone to admit values have fallen, they hold. If rents must drop enough to attract ordinary users, owners resist because a lower rent becomes a public signal of lower value. If the buyer pool is made of expatriates, Gulf capital, wealthy locals, and investors with fresh dollars, then the building is priced for people who may not be there. The asset survives by not becoming too honest.

This is why Downtown can feel frozen. Price discovery would be politically and financially dangerous. If Downtown is the ceiling of Beirut real estate, then admitting that Downtown prices are inflated would put pressure on the rest of the market. The empty apartment is not only an unused home. It is a defense against repricing.

The same logic applies to shops and offices. A living city lowers the threshold for use because use itself creates value: foot traffic, habit, safety, familiarity, reputation, neighborhood. A financialized district often does the reverse. It protects notional value even when that protection kills use. The rent stays too high, the shop stays empty, the street loses foot traffic, the remaining shops suffer, and the owner can still say the space is worth what it was worth in the model.

That is not a market. It is a pause button.

The consultancy version of the Downtown problem begins here: the central question is not "how do we beautify Downtown?" It is "how do we force inactive value to become active use?" Without that shift, more lighting, more events, more luxury tenants, and more marketing will remain surface treatments. The district does not need only activation. It needs an anti-vacancy regime: incentives for occupancy, penalties for long-term emptiness, public reporting of use, rent structures that reward local businesses, repair obligations tied to ownership, and a development model that treats inhabited space as more valuable than speculative silence.

### The City Beneath The City

The political economy of Downtown would be damaging enough if the only losses were social and financial. But Beirut's center carried another archive, older than the war, older than the Ottomans, older than Islam and Christianity, older than the Roman city whose columns became the acceptable face of heritage.

Under Downtown lay ancient Beirut.

Naccache's writing on this is devastating because it removes the comfort of abstraction. He argued that the Beirut archaeological site was among the oldest continuously inhabited urban sites of any world capital, with nearly five millennia of history concentrated beneath the old center. The postwar reconstruction was therefore not only an urban planning challenge. It was a once-in-a-civilization archaeological opportunity.

Lebanon could have dug its capital and found a common story under the sectarian one.

The timing should have made this obvious. The civil war had ended with no real national truth process, no common history book, no honest reckoning with responsibility, no shared account strong enough to stand above sectarian memory. Every community carried its own wounds and its own heroic lies. The ancient city under Downtown could not have solved that. Archaeology cannot redeem politics by itself. But it could have offered Lebanon a rare thing: material evidence of continuity before the categories that now divide the country.

Not purity. Not Phoenician fantasy. Not sectarian archaeology. Continuity.

Layers of Bronze Age, Hellenistic, Roman, Byzantine, Islamic, Crusader, Mamluk, Ottoman, Mandate, and modern Beirut under the same ground. A city repeatedly destroyed, reused, rebuilt, renamed, and inhabited. A place where each layer complicated the slogans of the present. A country that cannot agree on whether it is Phoenician, Arab, Mediterranean, Islamic, Christian, Eastern, Western, or all of these at once could have stood over its own center and seen that the answer was not a slogan but a stratigraphy.

Instead, much of that archive was bulldozed.

Naccache reported that, in the areas where infrastructure and construction work had moved ahead, the overwhelming majority of archaeological deposits were destroyed without adequate study. In "The Price of Progress," he described ancient material hauled away in dump trucks and emptied into the Mediterranean. He estimated more than seven million cubic feet of ancient Beirut lost. In "Beirut's Memorycide," he insisted that the destruction was needless, avoidable, and carried out despite the existence of knowledge, time, and money.

The numbers matter because they puncture the usual excuse. This was not a poor country forced to choose between feeding children and preserving potsherds. The Downtown reconstruction was a multibillion-dollar project planned over many years. Naccache argued that a small fraction of the total budget, spread over time, could have allowed serious archaeological work. He noted that the company had the unusual advantage of controlling the whole site and could have integrated excavation into long-term development. The problem was not impossibility. The problem was priority.

Here the moral scale becomes almost unbearable. A private company was given extraordinary control over a national archaeological site precisely when that control could have made preservation easier. Fragmented ownership was one of the stated reasons for creating Solidere. But once the land was unified, the same unification did not produce a unified archaeology policy worthy of the site. The corporate power that could assemble the city for development did not assemble the city for memory.

This is the third central mistake of the Downtown project: it treated archaeology as an obstacle or amenity, not as the ground of national repair.

### Why Archaeology Was Dangerous

Ancient ruins are usually safer than recent memory. Everyone can admire a Roman column. A Roman column will not name the militia that shelled a street, the party that stole a property, the minister who signed a decree, the developer who benefited, the banker who financed, or the family that was evicted. Antiquity can become a neutral zone where Lebanon performs unity without accountability.

So why was the archaeological record under Downtown treated so poorly?

Because serious archaeology is not the same as decorative antiquity.

Decorative antiquity gives a development prestige. Serious archaeology interrupts the schedule. Decorative antiquity produces a plaza, a glass floor, a brochure, a heritage trail. Serious archaeology asks who owns the past, who gets to interpret it, how long excavation should take, what should remain visible, what must be changed in the master plan, and whether profit should bend before the claims of the dead.

Serious archaeology also makes identity harder to simplify. It does not give the Phoenicianist, the Arab nationalist, the sectarian communalist, or the cosmopolitan developer exactly what he wants. It shows mixture, rupture, conquest, adaptation, reuse, continuity, and loss. It shows that Beirut did not belong cleanly to any one story. That is precisely why Lebanon needed it.

Naccache understood the political stakes. He connected the loss of the site to Lebanon's failure to produce a consensual national history. He wrote from within a country where identity documents, sectarian labels, and historical narratives had life-and-death consequences. The old argument between Phoenician and Arab identity, between sectarian origin myths and shared civic belonging, was not academic vanity. It shaped how Lebanese imagined one another.

A serious excavation of Downtown could have helped teach a different civic instinct: the land is older than the sects, and every community is late.

That lesson would not erase politics. It would discipline it. It would make it harder for any faction to claim exclusive metaphysical ownership over Lebanon. It would make the center of Beirut a school of complexity. Children could have walked through the city and learned that identity is not a weapon dug from one layer, but a responsibility toward all the layers.

Instead, the ancient city was often rescued only in fragments, while the development moved on.

### The Museum That Never Became The City

To be fair, not everything was destroyed. This story should not become a lazy accusation that every archaeologist failed or every planner was indifferent. There were excavations, rescue efforts, publications, preserved elements, and later attempts to make archaeology visible through heritage trails and site integrations. Beirut's buried past did not disappear entirely.

That partial survival is part of the tragedy.

Fragments can indict as powerfully as absences. A column in a plaza, a wall behind glass, a planned garden around ruins, a buried mosaic removed into storage, a route on a heritage trail: each says the city knew there was something there. The question is why the knowledge did not govern the project.

The Garden of Forgiveness is the perfect symbol. Conceived as a place where Beirut's past, present, and future could meet, it was designed to integrate archaeological remains with a landscape of reflection near churches and mosques. Its very name carried the moral ambition postwar Lebanon avoided everywhere else. It should have been impossible to miss the significance: in the center of a city that had not forgiven because it had not confessed, a garden would ask citizens to stand among the layers and think about loss.

And yet the garden sat delayed, suspended, "on hold," like so much of Lebanon's better self.

The same can be said of other archaeological episodes in and around Downtown: the Roman hippodrome debates, the site identified as a Phoenician harbor, the modernist Egg, the traces of the war, the cemeteries and ruins that resisted smooth incorporation. Giorgio Tarraf's thesis, with its title "Beirut: The Story of a City Destroyed by Peace," captures the perversity. War destroyed the city violently. Peace continued the destruction legally, administratively, and aesthetically.

Tarraf's work is useful because it refuses to treat heritage as only old houses. Heritage is the Egg, the port, modernist buildings, social fabric, archaeological layers, war memory, green space, and the remaining physical evidence that Beirut did not begin with the latest investor deck. Save Beirut Heritage's broader definition matters for this reason. A city is not preserved by saving a few pretty facades while erasing the systems of meaning around them.

Downtown's reconstruction often behaved as though memory could be edited into a development after the financial logic had already been fixed. That is backwards. In a city like Beirut, memory is not an accessory. It is infrastructure.

### The War Layer

There is another absence in Downtown, and it may be the most politically revealing one: the civil war itself.

Postwar Lebanon did not want to remember the war publicly because public memory would have endangered the men who survived it politically. The warlords entered the state. Militias became parties. Commanders became ministers. Amnesties became foundations. Families grieved privately while the public order insisted on moving forward. The phrase "no victor, no vanquished" became less a principle of reconciliation than a method of organized forgetting.

Downtown was the ideal place to challenge that forgetting. It had been the center, the front line, the wound, the gap between East and West Beirut, the place where the city split open. A serious reconstruction could have made the war legible without turning it into sectarian revenge. It could have marked destroyed streets, named civilian loss, preserved selected ruins, built a museum of the war, recorded testimonies, created public rituals of mourning, and taught young Lebanese that the center was lost once because politics failed.

Instead, the war became strangely absent from the polished center. Ancient layers could appear because they were politically safer. The recent dead were harder. A Roman wall does not accuse a living party. A war memorial might.

This created a grotesque hierarchy of memory. Beirut could display antiquity, imitate the souks, invoke heritage, and promote a future, while avoiding the historical event that had made reconstruction necessary.

The city was asked to remember everything except what had just happened.

That omission helps explain why Downtown feels haunted. It is not haunted because Lebanese are nostalgic. It is haunted because the place contains unprocessed claims. The old owners who were transformed into shareholders. The shopkeepers whose social world vanished. The archaeological layers hauled away. The war dead unnamed in the very district where the war's geography was most visible. The citizens who are told the center belongs to them but experience it as conditional access.

Ghosts are not always supernatural. Sometimes they are unresolved rights.

### The Blast Exposed The Repair Problem

The port explosion exposed something the Solidere debate had already suggested: Beirut has buildings, owners, tenants, developers, NGOs, ministries, municipalities, religious institutions, foreign donors, and security agencies, but it often lacks a public framework strong enough to make repair happen justly.

This matters for Downtown because repair is not only technical. It is legal, social, and financial. Who owns the building? Who lives there? Who has the right to authorize work? Who pays? Who benefits if the building is repaired? Who benefits if it remains damaged and the tenant leaves? Who can afford construction material in fresh dollars? Who is waiting for a chance to sell?

The post-blast evidence is stark. L'Orient Today's 2021 reporting found that, a year after the explosion, no one could give a clear assessment of how much blast-damaged Beirut had been rebuilt. The state had largely left reconstruction to NGOs and private initiatives. The Beirut Urban Lab's work on self-recovery argued that tenure status shaped households' ability to control the repair process, and that the recovery framework worsened pre-existing housing vulnerabilities. World Bank project documents describe affected historic residential units that still needed complex interventions to become habitable. In a UN-Habitat sample, more than 41 percent of affected buildings were already vacant before the explosion, and more than 32 percent had some kind of legal issue.

This is the part that makes today's Downtown different from the old critique. The old critique said the center was too polished and exclusive. The current critique must say something more difficult: the center is polished in fragments and damaged in fragments because it is not governed as a living district. It can have repaired stone and unrepaired trauma on the same street. It can have a luxury lobby beside a broken pavement. It can have a reopened cafe across from a dark apartment stack. It can have a blast scar waiting years because the people with the legal power to repair are not the people living with the daily consequences of non-repair.

That is what absentee ownership does to urban time. Residents experience damage as urgency. Distant owners can experience damage as accounting.

Lebanon's post-blast heritage crisis made the danger explicit. World Bank documents warn that speculation threatens the cultural identity of central Beirut, including owners' plans to repurpose or upgrade property, refusal to rehabilitate damaged property, and pressure on owners without repair funds to sell to developers or investors. This is the same historical fear in a new form: not a single Solidere-scale takeover, but a scattered aftershock of ownership decisions that can remove residents, weaken heritage, and turn repair into a route toward displacement.

Downtown should be the place where Lebanon proves it has learned from that history. Instead, it remains the place where the old lesson keeps repeating: when the city is treated as property first, people become temporary.

### From Custodian To Liquidator

One distinction clarifies the whole project: the difference between a custodian and a liquidator.

A custodian holds an asset across generations. It asks how land can keep producing use, memory, identity, rent, and civic value without exhausting itself. A custodian can still be financially disciplined. It can lease, restore, develop, and profit. But it understands that some places become more valuable when they are not sold too quickly, not homogenized, not priced beyond ordinary life, not emptied of the people who make them meaningful.

A liquidator converts assets into saleable value. It asks how to unlock land, assemble parcels, raise capital, clear obstacles, and produce returns. Liquidation can look dynamic. It creates movement. It satisfies the hunger for visible action. It can even rebuild beautifully. But it struggles with the slow obligations of a city center because those obligations do not always maximize short-term financial value.

Solidere often behaved less like the custodian of Lebanon's symbolic center than like the liquidator of a wounded city.

This does not mean every decision was malicious. That is too easy and probably false. The deeper problem is structural. Once Downtown was placed inside a corporate model organized around land value, share value, sale, lease, development rights, and premium positioning, many later choices became predictable. Archaeology became delay. Old tenants became complications. Low-margin commerce became inefficient. Public disorder became brand risk. Political assembly became security threat. Ordinary Beirutis became visitors. The center's success was measured by criteria that could rise while civic life fell.

This is why the critique cannot stop at Rafic Hariri as a person. Hariri matters, of course. His ambition, charisma, money, Saudi connections, planning imagination, and postwar dominance shaped the project. But Downtown is larger than one man. It is the spatial form of a whole Lebanese settlement: public weakness, private substitution, elite capture, financialization, amnesty, selective memory, and a future sold as proof that the past no longer needs to be argued over.

Downtown is Lebanon's postwar order with sidewalks.

### The Center As Connector

What would it mean to think of Downtown differently?

Start with a simple premise: a capital's center is not primarily a trophy. It is a connector.

It should connect East and West Beirut, coast and interior, citizen and institution, old memory and new use, archaeology and education, commerce and public life, mourning and assembly, transport and walking, everyday needs and symbolic space. It should be the place where the Lebanese encounter one another without needing a sectarian broker, a private membership, a luxury budget, or a political invitation.

By that standard, Downtown's reconstruction was too weak at connection and too strong at display.

A different model would have made the center harder to monetize quickly and easier to inhabit deeply. Long leases instead of aggressive land sales. Public-interest covenants tied to affordability, cultural use, and local business. A real archaeology authority with budget and power. A war memory institution in the center, not hidden at the edge of polite society. Public transport through the district. Universities, libraries, archives, artist studios, repair trades, small shops, clinics, municipal services, mixed-income housing, and public spaces where sitting is not treated as loitering. Incentives tied not only to square meters developed, but to occupancy, diversity of use, local employment, and public access.

Some of this sounds obvious because it is. But Lebanon has a genius for making obvious public goods seem naive once a private substitute has already occupied the space.

The point is not to resurrect the old souks exactly as they were. That would be another kind of nostalgia. Cities cannot be embalmed. The old Downtown had problems that should not be romanticized. The point is to recover the function the old center performed: shared use under conditions of difference.

The center does not need to look old. It needs to behave publicly.

That means measuring different things. Not only land sales, rental income, and square meters delivered, but active ground-floor frontage, actual residential nights, affordable commercial leases, cultural programming that does not require luxury consumption, public seating, pedestrian continuity, repaired blast damage, local business survival, and the number of routes by which an ordinary person can cross the district without feeling managed.

The most important word is use.

Use is what turns architecture into city. Use is what makes a sidewalk politically meaningful. Use is what forces maintenance. Use is what makes security less paranoid because presence itself creates safety. Use is what makes memory repeatable rather than ceremonial. Use is what makes a shopkeeper care about the pavement outside his door and a resident care about the lamp below her window.

Downtown Beirut does not need another claim that it is coming back. It needs a public audit of what "back" means.

### The Question Now

The legal and corporate horizon of Solidere has always been part of the political problem. The company was created in 1994 under the postwar legal framework, with an original duration described in its annual reports as twenty-five years and later amendments and extensions that complicated the timeline. The details matter, but the larger question is simpler: how long can the center of Beirut remain governed by a model whose original promise has already been tested by history?

The regional-tourism dream did not produce a stable civic center. The luxury real estate model did not create ordinary life. The shareholder logic did not protect memory. The security logic did not produce belonging. The curated heritage did not replace the archive that was destroyed. The polished streets did not heal the war. The old owners did not return as a social world. The souks did not become souks in the old urban sense. The Garden of Forgiveness did not become the country's ritual of repair. The archaeological site did not become the national classroom it could have been.

At some point, the defense that "at least something was done" expires.

Downtown Beirut now asks a different generation a different question. Not whether the ruins should have remained. Not whether Hariri was visionary or villain. Not whether Solidere built nice streets. The real question is whether Lebanon can take its center back conceptually before it takes it back administratively. Can the Lebanese imagine Downtown not as a luxury district, not as a nostalgia object, not as a battlefield between old ideological camps, but as a memory site that still has future work to do?

That future work is not abstract. It means archives, museums, schools, public transport, rent structures, small commerce, archaeology, housing, programming, public benches, accessible waterfronts, war memory, preserved modernism, and planning authority that answers to citizens rather than only to capital. It means treating the center as a public trust even where private ownership remains. It means making memory useful, not decorative.

Above all, it means refusing the clean slate.

The clean slate is the most dangerous fantasy in Lebanon. Every faction wants one. Every developer wants one. Every foreign patron wants one. Every postwar settlement promises one. Forget the war. Forget the theft. Forget the old owners. Forget the sectarian bargain. Forget the bones under the street. Forget the money trail. Forget who signed. Forget who benefited. Forget who was priced out. Forget who cannot enter. Forget the archive at the bottom of the excavation pit. Look at the rendering. Look at the future. Look at the lights.

But countries do not heal by forgetting. They heal by learning what to do with memory that cannot be made convenient.

Downtown Beirut is still the right place to begin because it contains all of Lebanon's problems in one square geography: sectarian coexistence and rupture, public weakness and private power, real estate and debt, archaeology and identity, war amnesia and curated heritage, Gulf ambition and local exclusion, beauty and emptiness, the dream of return and the mechanics of dispossession.

The center failed as a national room because it was rebuilt as a premium address. It can only matter again if it becomes a place where the country is allowed to meet itself without a cover charge.

### The Country Under The Parking Lot

There is something almost unbearable about the parking lots beneath Downtown. Not because parking is unimportant. Cities need infrastructure. But in Beirut, the image is too perfect: layers of ancient city under asphalt and concrete, old souks replaced by controlled retail, a national memory site reorganized around cars, capital, and access.

The country was not literally buried under one parking lot. But something like the country was buried under the logic that made the parking lot feel more urgent than the archive.

Naccache's warning remains the deepest one. The loss was not only scholarly. It was civic. Lebanon lost evidence that might have helped it narrate itself beyond sectarian fragments. It lost the chance to make the capital's ground into a shared text. It lost the opportunity to teach its children that their history is not a competition between purified origins, but a crowded inheritance under their feet.

That is what memorycide means in Beirut. It is not only the destruction of ruins. It is the destruction of a people's ability to recognize itself across time.

And yet Downtown is not finished. Empty places can still be reinterpreted. Failed models can still become evidence. The ruins that survived, the documents that remain, the photographs of demolished souks, the testimonies of property owners, the archaeological reports, the ghost streets, the unfinished gardens, the Egg, the old street names, the reels people still make while walking through the polished absence, all of it can still be gathered into a sharper public memory.

Maybe that is the work now: not to mourn Downtown as though it died once, but to understand how it was killed in stages, by shelling, by law, by bulldozer, by capital, by silence, by beauty without use, by memory without obligation.

The old city is not coming back. That is not the task.

The task is to build a center that no longer needs to lie about what happened there.

Beirut does not need another rendering of a future that asks for amnesia as the price of entry. It needs a downtown capable of holding the past without freezing inside it, developing without erasing, remembering without becoming a museum, and welcoming the ordinary citizen without making him feel like he has entered someone else's investment.

The city was once bulldozed by war. Then it was bulldozed by peace.

If it is ever rebuilt again, it should be rebuilt against forgetting.

Every donor conference begins with the same courtesy: Lebanon is treated as a state that has misplaced its capacity. Ministers arrive, flags stand, envoys speak of reform, and everyone pretends the machinery exists somewhere behind the curtains, waiting for enough pressure or money to restart.

The more accurate picture is less flattering and more useful. Lebanon's public weakness has been administered for so long that the weakness itself has become an economy. Ministries, embassies, motorcades, communiques, uniforms, and donor paperwork remain visible, while the powers that should belong to a state - taxation, judgment, defense, monopoly over force, the punishment of protected thieves - are distributed through factions that profit from keeping the center incomplete.

Lebanon's founding wound lay in turning division into an operating system. The 1943 National Pact was an unwritten elite bargain over offices, identity, and external alignment. The presidency, premiership, speakership, command posts, directorates, and eventually almost every serious public appointment became sectarian property before they became national responsibility. Taif, in 1989, reorganized the bargain after the war. The warlords were not defeated by the state. They entered it.

### The Perfect Argument

The argument over "resistance" and "sovereignty" has become the perfect Lebanese argument: morally charged enough to feel ultimate, structurally narrow enough to keep the real question offstage. Are you with the resistance or against it? Are you with the state or against the militia? Are you with Iran or the West? Each question sounds decisive. Each one keeps the citizen inside the room the cartel built.

The missing question is simpler: did anyone ever intend to build a state?

The resistance camp has a real historical wound behind it. Southern Lebanon was abandoned for decades. Israel invaded, occupied, bombed, and returned. Villages learned that the Lebanese state arrived late, if it arrived at all. Hezbollah did not emerge from fantasy. It emerged from a vacuum, and vacuums are never innocent. A community repeatedly left undefended will not be persuaded by lectures from people who never paid the same price.

But Hezbollah has also been inside the state for decades. It has had ministers, MPs, veto power, alliances, budgets, and bargaining weight. It demanded a strong state as the condition for disarmament while helping preserve the political order that made such a state impossible. It did not spend its capital building an army able to replace its own role. It did not make tax capacity, judicial independence, or a national defense doctrine the center of its politics. It protected a parallel authority because that authority is the basis of its power.

The anti-resistance camp has its own real wound. It is not madness to object to a non-state army deciding war and peace. It is not colonialism to want one authority, one defense policy, one law. No functioning republic can tolerate a military actor that can commit the whole country to consequences the whole country did not choose.

But this camp spent years asking Hezbollah to hand its weapons to a state it never built. It demanded disarmament without constructing the institutional capacity that would make disarmament anything other than demilitarization. It made "sovereignty" its television language while relying on embassies, donors, Gulf money, American guarantees, and foreign pressure. It wanted the word state, not the cost of one.

### The Cost of a Real State

The core fraud is shared fear. Both camps need the state as rhetoric and fear it as reality. A real state would force Hezbollah to submit its military decision to public authority. It would also force Amal, the Lebanese Forces, the PSP, the Kataeb, the FPM, Sunni successor formations, banking families, port operators, quarry owners, generator networks, private school empires, and religious charities to submit their finances, patronage networks, judicial protections, municipal fiefdoms, and foreign relationships to scrutiny. The same authority able to absorb Hezbollah would also be able to audit everyone.

More deeply, a real state would force Lebanese parties to have politics. Not identity. Not grievance. Politics. Tax policy, labor law, electricity reform, school curricula, banking accountability, housing, transport, agriculture, military procurement. These are the questions real parties answer. Lebanese sectarian parties cannot answer them because each one is a vertical slice through its sect. Rich and poor, employer and worker, landlord and tenant, depositor and banker, all are kept in one confessional column. Any serious economic position would split the column. The resistance question keeps it together.

The binary keeps the state from appearing.

The generator explains the country better than most constitutions. The public grid fails. A private operator fills the failure. The operator becomes indispensable. The politician protects him. The citizen pays twice, once for the absent state and once for the private substitute. Reform becomes dangerous because it would remove the substitute through which loyalty and money flow. Scale this outward and you have Lebanon: private schools replacing education, private hospitals replacing health, party charities replacing welfare, militias replacing defense, banks replacing economic policy, sects replacing citizenship.

The cartel never failed to fix the weak state. It learned to trade on it.

### The Foreign Floor Plan

The market extends beyond Lebanon. Every Lebanese faction is also an embassy in local costume. Iran, Syria, Saudi Arabia, France, the United States, Israel, Turkey, Qatar, the UAE: each has rented Lebanese weakness in one form or another. A sovereign Lebanese state would have to say no. A cartel Lebanon never has to say no because no one voice speaks for it. Every patron can find a faction. Every faction can find a patron. The country becomes a floor plan for other people's strategies.

October 2019 was the test. For a brief moment, Lebanese anger escaped the binary. "All of them means all of them" was dangerous because it named the cartel as a cartel. It did not say one sect had stolen the state from another. It said the state had been stolen through all of them. The response came quickly. Every faction worked to return the uprising to familiar lines. Hezbollah called it foreign. Others tried to narrow it to Hezbollah. Amal attacked camps. Old parties tried to harvest new anger. The structure recognized the threat before many protesters did.

The port explosion was the second test. More than two hundred lives lost, a capital wounded, a paper trail of warnings, negligence, and impunity. If any event could have forced a state to appear, it was that. Instead the investigation itself became evidence of the thing it was investigating. Judges were threatened, sued, obstructed, removed, paralyzed. The dead were mourned publicly and abandoned procedurally. The system did not deny grief. It absorbed grief, televised grief, commemorated grief, and refused accountability.

Current wars and negotiations therefore cannot be understood through the language being offered. A Lebanese government may sit at a table. That does not mean Lebanon, as a sovereign actor, is sitting there. A cabinet may issue a position. That does not mean the position binds the ground. A foreign envoy may speak of disarmament, stabilization, withdrawal, reconstruction. These words assume the existence of an authority capable of delivering them. Lebanon has a performance of authority, distributed among people whose private survival depends on keeping authority incomplete.

### The Harder Position

None of this means neutrality. Israel is an external threat. Its history in Lebanon is not imaginary; it is written in occupations, invasions, bombardments, and renewed buffer-zone fantasies. Southern communities have paid in land, bodies, displacement, and memory. Any Lebanese politics that treats their exposure as acceptable collateral is not sovereign politics. It is sectarian resentment with a passport.

Nor does the reality of Israeli aggression absolve Hezbollah. The party turned a legitimate defense need into a permanent parallel authority. It fused community protection with regional strategy. It made the south defensible in one register and politically captive in another. It is possible, and necessary, to say both things without letting either side recruit the sentence.

The position worth holding sits outside the available menu. Israel is an external aggressor. The cartel is the enduring internal arrangement. Hezbollah is both a response to abandonment and a participant in the system that profits from abandonment. The anti-resistance camp is right that no republic can survive a private army, and fraudulent when it offers no republic capable of replacing it. The state is the name of the missing thing, not the property of either camp.

The conclusion asks for discipline. Refuse the binary's frame. Distinguish communities from the parties that claim them. Defend Lebanese territory without sanctifying every armed actor that fights on it. Demand army capacity without turning the army into a factional instrument. Defend judges, journalists, auditors, archivists, teachers, and civic institutions because these are the tissues from which a state is eventually made. Direct diaspora money away from sectarian channels where possible. Build a language in which young Lebanese can imagine themselves as citizens before clients.

The cartel's greatest victory has been making the Lebanese forget that statehood has to outlive the men who speak in its name. Lebanon has had heroes, martyrs, billionaires, generals, warlords, clerics, bankers, and mourners. It still lacks a state strong enough to make these men less important.

The task begins where the costume ends.

## How a Generator Owner Showed Why Lebanon Has No State

**Full body:**

A few months ago, a generator dispute in West Beirut briefly became the most honest political event in the country.

The state tried to move against Abu Ali Itani, a generator operator in Sakiet el-Janzeer. Within hours, the roads around Koreitem, Cola, Qasqas, Verdun, and Corniche al-Mazraa were blocked. Tires and garbage burned. Men on motorcycles appeared. Sheikh Ahmad al-Hariri went on television to defend him. The story was officially about electricity fraud, meters, tariffs, paperwork. But nobody in the neighborhood experienced it as a technical file.

That is what made it interesting.

I live around there. I remember thinking: what is this really about? Is the government finally trying to crack down on the generator mafia? Is this a statement? Why him and not others? Could there be something legitimate in the file that everyone is now sweeping under the rug because the street moved faster than the paperwork?

That is the part that made the story feel so Lebanese. It was not an abstract case study about "clientelism" or "weak institutions." It was the sound of the Lebanese state discovering that even a generator bill has a sectarian border around it. One man was called in over electricity, and suddenly the whole question became: who has the right to enter this neighborhood, touch this operator, and call it law?

This is the kind of Lebanese story that looks absurd until you realize it is the country explaining itself.

Abu Ali was not a minister. He was not an MP. He did not command an official militia. But he controlled something more immediate than most ministries: electricity. In a country where the public grid is unreliable, the generator owner is not simply a businessman. He is the man who keeps the elevator moving, the fridge cold, the shop open, the street half-lit. He is hated, needed, resented, negotiated with, cursed at, defended. That mixture is Lebanon.

The state looked at him and saw an illegal operator. His supporters looked at him and saw a local provider. His political defenders looked at him and saw a Sunni neighborhood figure being targeted. His lawyer, Majed Damashqiyeh, spoke of extensions, documents, and "settling violations," as if the law were not a line but a bargaining table. Everyone was speaking honestly, in their own way. That was the problem.

Because the question was never only whether Abu Ali broke the law. He probably did, in the ordinary Lebanese sense in which half the country survives by breaking laws that are enforced only when someone decides enforcement is useful. The real question was why him, why now, and why only him.

Lebanon has tens of thousands of private generators. The sector is not a few men with engines in alleyways. It is a national shadow utility. It runs on diesel, meters, neighborhood understandings, political protection, municipal tolerance, and the permanent failure of Electricite du Liban. For years, governments have promised regulation. Meters, tariffs, licensing, crackdowns. The file returns every few years like a bad joke.

But serious enforcement would not be a reform. It would be a war.

If the state really went after the generator economy, it would have to go everywhere at once: Dahieh, Ashrafieh, Sakiet el-Janzeer, Aley, Tripoli, Metn, the villages, the suburbs, the buildings with two generators and three subscription rates. It would have to confront fuel importers, local bosses, municipalities, party men, security services, and the ordinary households that hate the generator owner but still need him tonight.

That is the trap. A selective crackdown looks like justice on paper and sectarian targeting on the street. The state cannot act like a state in one neighborhood while negotiating with everyone else. In Lebanon, that is not law. That is a message.

And people know how to read messages.

This is where the usual language fails. Calling Lebanon "corrupt" is too lazy. It makes the country sound like a normal system infected by bad behavior. But what if the behavior is the system? What if the brokenness is not an interruption, but the way money, power, favors, survival, and humiliation are distributed?

That is the question I keep returning to. Not "why do Lebanese people break rules?" That is too easy. The better question is: what kind of country makes the rule-abiding person look like the idiot?

You see it in electricity first because electricity is physical. Darkness arrives in your house. The state disappears from the wall socket. Then someone else arrives with a wire, a meter, and a monthly price. At first he is the substitute. Then he becomes the infrastructure. Then he becomes politically protected because too many people earn, bargain, and survive through the substitute.

Fixing electricity would not only turn the lights on. It would turn off an economy.

That is why Lebanon is so hard to reform. Every failure has employees. Every delay has a broker. Every missing public service has a private replacement. Every private replacement eventually gets a political guardian. The state fails, someone profits from the failure, and then that person or network gains an interest in making sure the state never fully returns.

This is not a conspiracy theory. It is just the incentive structure, visible in public.

The same logic appears in smaller, uglier ways all over daily life. Streetlights do not work, so buildings light the pavement directly in front of them. A new tower repaves its own sidewalk while the next stretch remains cracked. Public beaches are privatized into beach clubs. Public parks barely exist, so leisure becomes an entry fee. Even public space begins to feel like something you access through a bill, a reservation, a membership, or a connection.

Lebanon is often described as a failed state. In some ways it is closer to a privatized state. Not privatized in the clean ideological sense of free markets and competition, but in the dirtier sense: every public absence becomes someone's little kingdom.

You do not get a country. You get subscriptions.

Electricity subscription. Water delivery. Private school. Private hospital. Generator amperes. A beach club instead of a beach. A political contact instead of an office that works. A foreign passport if you are lucky. A relative abroad sending dollars if you are luckier. The citizen is not served by the state; he assembles a private state around himself, one monthly payment at a time.

And then we call him individualistic.

But Lebanese individualism is not simple. It is not the Western fantasy of the sovereign person standing alone. No Lebanese person is really alone. He is wrapped in family, sect, neighborhood, school networks, village origin, religious court, cemetery, diaspora, WhatsApp groups, old favors, old shame, old obligation. The Lebanese person is intensely communal at the intimate level and almost completely unprotected at the civic level.

That is the contradiction.

He may do anything for his family, but he does not trust the public. He may mobilize instantly for a funeral, a wedding, a hospital bill, a cousin's tuition, but he will not treat the traffic lane, the sidewalk, the tax system, or the ministry counter as a shared moral space. The circle of obligation is real. It just stops before it reaches the citizen.

This is why the word mehtail matters. It is not just "scammer." That translation is too crude. The mehtail is the person who knows the gap between the written rule and the actual rule. He knows when the office closes, who can still answer, which document matters, which document is theater, which person needs respect, which person needs pressure, which sentence turns a refusal into a possibility. He is not outside the system. He is fluent in the system.

And Lebanon rewards fluency in the unofficial.

That is the uncomfortable part. The mehtail is not only a moral failure. He is evidence of an environment. In a functioning country, some of that same intelligence might become entrepreneurship, law, logistics, management, politics, institution-building. In Lebanon, too much of it becomes evasion. People learn how to get around the thing because going through the thing often makes them fools.

The banking collapse made this national. The system did not collapse because Lebanese people lacked intelligence. It collapsed partly because intelligence was everywhere. Financial engineering, political cover, banking sophistication, legal ambiguity, dollar games, circular schemes, elite exits before the door closed. It was not stupid. It was brilliant in the clinical sense, and morally empty.

That is what hurts. Lebanon does not waste talent by failing to produce it. It wastes talent by routing it into escape.

The same people who can build businesses in Lagos, Sao Paulo, Dubai, Montreal, Abidjan, and Sydney spend their lives in Lebanon solving problems that should not exist. How do I get electricity? How do I protect my savings? How do I get paperwork done? How do I keep my money outside the country? How do I make sure my children can leave if they need to? How do I survive the next devaluation, the next war, the next capital control, the next official lie?

At that point, leaving does not feel like betrayal. It feels like competent planning.

This is the deepest damage. The country trains its best people to treat the future as something to evacuate from. Money leaves before it can be trapped. Children are educated toward departure. Families measure success by the quality of the exit route. The diaspora then sends money back, and the system survives on the very people it pushed out.

Again, this is not simply failure. It is a working arrangement.

Remittances keep households alive. Tourism becomes a national prayer. The airport road gets cleaned because that is where the visitor enters. Posters come down, asphalt appears, the facade is polished. The tourist, the Gulf visitor, the expat with dollars: everyone waits for them with the desperate dignity of a country that has forgotten how to produce and learned how to receive.

Meanwhile, the deeper system remains untouched. New faces can enter government. Better CVs can appear. Technocrats can speak well. But each ministry is still a prize, each appointment still passes through sectarian arithmetic, each service still carries leverage. The cake is smaller after 2019, but the knife is the same.

This is why I do not believe the story of Lebanon as mere incompetence. Incompetence cannot be this durable. A bad system that survives this long is doing something for someone.

Abu Ali Itani is useful because he makes the abstraction visible. A generator owner becomes a neighborhood figure because the state left a space open. The state then tries to step back into that space and discovers it is no longer empty. It has been occupied by money, service, loyalty, fear, sect, and habit. The people may resent the man. They may know the bill is unfair. They may know the whole arrangement is rotten. But when the state arrives late, selectively, and with the smell of someone else's agenda, resentment turns into defense.

That is Lebanon's political tragedy in miniature: people defending the very substitutes that prove how badly they have been abandoned.

So the reform question cannot be asked innocently. You cannot just say "fix electricity" without asking who loses the darkness. You cannot say "enforce the law" without asking why the law sleeps in one district and wakes up in another. You cannot say "end corruption" as if corruption were a stain on the surface rather than a method of distribution.

The real reform would be much more boring and much more radical: make the official route work.

Not inspirational speeches. Not one heroic minister. Not another donor conference. Offices that process paperwork without humiliation. Electricity that does not require a neighborhood baron. Public schools that are not treated as surrender. Hospitals where access does not depend on sectarian proximity. Courts where the file is not a negotiation. Streets lit because they are public, not because a building nearby paid for light.

This sounds basic because it is. But in Lebanon, the basic is revolutionary.

The opposite of the mehtail republic is not a country of saints. It is a country where ordinary honesty is not punished, where waiting your turn does not make you a sucker, where the public thing works often enough that the private workaround loses its glamour.

Until then, Lebanon will keep producing people who are excellent at surviving the country and exhausted by the effort. People who can solve everything except the arrangement that made their cleverness necessary.

And somewhere in Sakiet el-Janzeer, or any other neighborhood with its own small sovereign, the meters will keep spinning.

## The Census That Cannot Be Taken

**Final title:** The Census That Cannot Be Taken
**Dek:** The country's last official count is older than its living politics, and every serious reform crashes into the same forbidden question: who gets counted?
**Byline:** Karim Salam
**Publication date:** May 5, 2026
**Estimated reading time:** 8 min read
**Topic tags:** Demographics, Constitutional Reform, Sectarianism, History
**Excerpt:** A census would force Lebanon to decide whether the diaspora, refugees, sects, and citizens belong to one political reality or several convenient fictions.

2. The Taif Agreement, English text: https://www.un.int/lebanon/sites/www.un.int/files/Lebanon/the_taif_agreement_english_version_.pdf

**Full body:**

Lebanon has not counted its population since 1932. The fact sounds like trivia until it becomes the locked room at the center of the republic.

A modern state counts because counting is the first act of governing. The census tells a government who exists, where they live, what they need, how they work, how old they are, which schools and roads and hospitals must be built, how taxation should be organized, where poverty is concentrated, where representation is distorted. Counting carries politics. Refusing to count carries more.

Lebanon has become a state forbidden to know itself. It has identity cards, civil registries, sectarian courts, voter rolls, school records, hospital records, passport offices, municipal ledgers, tax files, party lists, security files, church archives, waqf archives, and embassy estimates. It is drowning in partial knowledge. What it refuses is public synthesis. The state knows enough to control the citizen but not enough to reconstitute the citizenry.

### The Frozen Photograph

The 1932 census was conducted under the French Mandate, in a country whose borders had been drawn only twelve years earlier and whose political destiny was still being argued over by communities with different fears. It did not merely measure Lebanon. It helped manufacture the Lebanon that would later claim the measurement as destiny. The confessional distribution it recorded became the basis for parliamentary ratios, office allocation, and the Christian-majority premise of the new state.

The National Pact of 1943 then converted demographic arithmetic into political metaphysics. The presidency would be Maronite, the premiership Sunni, the speakership Shia. Parliamentary seats were distributed by confessional ratio. The country was not asked, as a citizen body, what political order it wanted. Its communities were counted, ranked, represented, and frozen.

Taif adjusted the freeze without melting it. The old 6:5 Christian-Muslim parliamentary ratio became 1:1. This is often described as reform. It was, at best, a renegotiation of the cartel's internal pricing mechanism after the civil war changed the balance of force. The deeper premise remained untouched: Lebanese political identity would be mediated through sect; public offices would remain community property; the citizen would arrive at the state already sorted.

Article 95 says the abolition of political confessionalism is a fundamental national objective. The sentence has survived as constitutional decor. The state repeats it the way a family repeats the name of a dead uncle at lunch: present in language, absent from life.

### The Number Nobody Wants

Everyone knows the 1932 photograph is obsolete. Nobody agrees on the replacement photograph because the replacement would immediately become a demand. If one community is larger than its share of offices, it will ask for more. If another is smaller than its share, it will insist on guarantees. If the diaspora counts, one balance appears. If only residents count, another. If refugees are counted as residents but not citizens, a third. If naturalization is discussed, the room explodes.

The census cannot be taken because the question "who counts?" cannot be separated from "who rules?" Palestinian refugees, Syrian refugees, expatriate Lebanese, dual citizens, naturalized citizens, emigrants whose families remain registered in villages they left generations ago: each category changes the political meaning of the count. Lebanon avoids the constitutional consequences of data.

The diaspora question alone could break the table. Lebanon is a country whose external population is not peripheral to national life but central to it. Remittances sustain families. Foreign passports sustain mobility. Expatriate votes are courted with patriotic sentiment while expatriate numbers remain politically explosive. Count the diaspora as part of the national body and Christian representation looks different from resident reality. Count only residents and the old balance is harder to defend. The state therefore keeps the diaspora as romance, not arithmetic.

The refugee question is even more combustible. Palestinians were excluded from naturalization because naturalization would alter the confessional balance and because the right of return remained central to Palestinian politics. Syrian refugees are discussed through humanitarian, security, labor, and demographic languages at once. In each case, the human being becomes a demographic instrument before becoming a person. Lebanon's refusal to count its citizens properly makes it even less able to discuss non-citizens honestly.

Demographic discussion in Lebanon therefore happens through rumor, private spreadsheets, embassy estimates, electoral extrapolations, and sectarian confidence games. Every number arrives carrying a political weapon. Christian leaders warn of demographic disappearance. Shia leaders point to underrepresentation. Sunni leaders fear being squeezed between Hezbollah's military weight and Christian-Muslim parity formulas. Druze leaders treat demographic fragility as an argument for permanent brokerage. In this system, the count arrives as ammunition before it can become evidence.

The result is a republic governed by a demographic ghost. The ministries distribute offices according to balances nobody can officially verify. Parliamentary seats are allocated according to a formula nobody can honestly defend as democratic. Senior posts are assigned by custom, not law. A young Lebanese citizen born in 2004 is governed by the political consequences of a census conducted before his grandparents were born.

The ghost enters more than high politics. It enters hiring, school histories, village anxieties, church sermons, party speeches, apartment purchases, and migration decisions. Families discuss whether "we" are becoming fewer. Parties discuss whether "they" are becoming more. The absence of official knowledge does not calm the country. It privatizes demographic fear and lets every faction publish its own nightmare.

### The Representation Deficit

The census taboo also helps explain why arms, patronage, and veto power become substitutes for representation. When a political system cannot peacefully adjust representation to demographic and social change, communities seek compensation elsewhere. They build parties, militias, welfare networks, diaspora channels, religious institutions, foreign patron relationships, and informal vetoes. The official formula remains frozen. Politics moves underground.

This helps explain, without excusing, how parallel arms become narrated as protection. A community that believes its demographic weight is not reflected in the state can be taught to see extra-state power as the missing arithmetic. A community that fears demographic decline can be taught to see constitutional rigidity as survival. Each side calls its distortion justice because the shared instrument for establishing justice - public counting - has been disabled.

The tragedy is that a census alone would not save Lebanon. A badly designed census could make things worse. Counting without a prior constitutional agreement would turn data into sectarian panic. The country needs something more difficult: a national enumeration tied to a transition away from sectarian allocation, with protections for cultural communities and representation for citizens as citizens. In other words, the census can only be taken by a state already committed to becoming post-confessional. But the state cannot become post-confessional without the evidence a census would provide. The closed loop.

The way out would require sequencing, which is the one art Lebanon's ruling class uses for delay rather than transition. First, agree that no community loses civil protections because of the count. Second, separate service planning from office allocation so schools, hospitals, and roads can be planned on evidence before sectarian seats are renegotiated. Third, create a constitutional timetable for moving political competition from sectarian quota to civic representation. Fourth, make the raw data auditable. Without that sequence, the census is a grenade. With it, the census becomes the first honest document of the republic.

### Counting as Revolution

The Lebanese state is the only entity in the country forbidden to know the country. Parties know their voters. Churches know their faithful. Security agencies know their files. Banks know their depositors. Schools know their students. Hospitals know their patients. Embassies know their visa applicants. The only forbidden knowledge is the knowledge that would let the public imagine itself as a public.

The census would be a revolutionary act disguised as administration. It would force Lebanon out of the shrine of the 1932 balance and into the living society its institutions have spent decades avoiding.

The cartel cannot permit that. Its genius is to make ignorance look like prudence. Do not count, they say, because counting will cause conflict. But the conflict already exists. It has merely been moved into every ministry, every electoral district, every cabinet formation, every school curriculum, every public appointment. Lebanon did not avoid conflict by avoiding the census. It constitutionalized conflict and then forbade the evidence.

One day the country will have to count. Not because numbers are magic, and not because demography should rule politics, but because a republic that cannot look at its own people cannot honestly call them citizens. Until then, Lebanon will remain a country governed by an old photograph everyone knows is false and everyone is paid to keep framed.

## Sovereignty Theatre

**Final title:** Sovereignty Theatre
**Dek:** Every patron praises Lebanese sovereignty at the exact volume that keeps Lebanon useful.
**Byline:** Karim Salam
**Publication date:** May 5, 2026
**Estimated reading time:** 8 min read
**Topic tags:** Sovereignty, Foreign Patrons, Diplomacy, State Failure
**Excerpt:** From Resolution 1701 to IMF reform language, Lebanon supplies flags, signatures, and communiques for agreements that rarely travel downward into authority.
**Pull quote:** "Diplomacy requires an address. Lebanon supplies one."

2. UNIFIL mandate and 1701 implementation background: https://unifil.unmissions.org/unifil-mandate
3. Announcement of a Cessation of Hostilities and Related Commitments on Enhanced Security Arrangements and toward the Implementation of UNSCR 1701, November 26, 2024: https://www.peaceagreements.org/media/documents/IL_LB_241126_Announcement_of_a_Cessation_of_Hostilities_and_Related_Commitments.pdf
4. World Bank, "Lebanon's Crisis: Great Denial in the Deliberate Depression": https://www.worldbank.org/en/news/press-release/2022/01/24/lebanon-s-crisis-great-denial-in-the-deliberate-depression
5. IMF staff statements and Article IV materials on Lebanon's delayed reforms and financing preconditions: https://www.imf.org/en/Countries/LBN

**Full body:**

The Lebanese state is very good at the choreography of sovereignty. Flags stand in the right place. Ministers receive envoys in the right rooms. The communiques contain all the expected nouns: stability, reform, borders, institutions, Resolution 1701. The staging works because command would endanger too many arrangements at once.

The scene is familiar. A foreign envoy arrives. The prime minister receives him. The flags stand behind them with theatrical symmetry. The communique mentions stability, reform, Resolution 1701, reconstruction, institutional capacity, border control, and the exclusive authority of the Lebanese state. Everyone nods gravely. The envoy leaves. The state remains unable to collect electricity bills evenly, control its ports fully, disarm non-state actors, prosecute financial crimes, appoint officials without sectarian bargaining, or tell a foreign patron no.

The performance has value. It produces loans, postponements, diplomatic cover, donor conferences, ceasefire frameworks, aid packages, and the illusion that the international system is negotiating with a sovereign actor. The stage is real. Authority remains somewhere offstage.

### The Patrons of Sovereignty

Every external actor claims to support Lebanese sovereignty. Iran supports it against Israel and the United States while sustaining Hezbollah's regional military role. The United States supports it against Iran while shaping the Lebanese army, banking rules, and diplomatic ceiling. France supports it with sentimental imperial melancholy and an astonishing faith that Paris still possesses a Lebanese vocation. Saudi Arabia supports it when Lebanon is not acting as an Iranian platform. Israel supports it whenever Lebanese sovereignty means restraining Hezbollah and ignores it whenever Israeli aircraft, shells, or buffer-zone designs cross the border. Syria historically supported it by occupying the country in the name of stabilizing it.

The joke at the center of Lebanese diplomacy is that a fully sovereign Lebanon would be unbearable to almost everyone currently praising Lebanese sovereignty. It would say no to Iranian weapons routes, no to Israeli raids, no to Saudi money as political leverage, no to French tutelage, no to American red lines, no to Syrian interference, no to the habit of treating Beirut as a conference room for other people's anxieties. Nobody wants that Lebanon enough to pay its cost.

External patrons prefer managed sovereignty. The state should be strong enough to sign agreements, absorb blame, secure borders when convenient, receive funds, and supply ministers who can be pressured. It should not be strong enough to become strategically independent. A client with a flag is easier to manage than a state with a will.

Lebanese factions understand this perfectly. Each performs sovereignty in the dialect of its patron. One faction says sovereignty means resistance. Another says sovereignty means neutrality. Another says sovereignty means Arab legitimacy. Another says sovereignty means international legality. In practice, each definition opens a different foreign door. Lebanese foreign policy becomes a lobby map.

### The Resolution as Prop

Resolution 1701 is the great prop of sovereignty theatre. It says what every reasonable person says they want: no armed authority in southern Lebanon other than the Lebanese state and UNIFIL, support for Lebanon's territorial integrity, a cessation of hostilities, and the extension of state authority. The text is clear enough. The political conditions for implementing it have never existed.

For Hezbollah, full implementation threatens the military architecture that sustains the party's regional role and domestic veto. For Israel, full implementation would also require an end to violations and a commitment to Lebanese territorial integrity it has not consistently honored. For the Lebanese state, full implementation would require army capacity, political consensus, and institutional authority it does not possess. For foreign sponsors, 1701 is useful precisely because it allows them to demand impossible things in the language of international law.

So 1701 becomes a diplomatic ritual. Everyone invokes it. Nobody pays for the state that would make it real. The Lebanese army is praised as the national institution while being asked to perform missions far beyond its budget and political protection. UNIFIL is asked to monitor a reality no peacekeeping force can fix without a sovereign partner. Envoys speak of deployment as if men in uniform can substitute for a political settlement. The border becomes a grammar lesson in absent authority.

International law thus becomes Lebanese theatre. The text is placed on the table; the table has no legs.

The army suffers most from this ritual because it is the one institution almost everyone wants to use as an alibi. Hezbollah invokes the army's weakness to justify retaining arms. Hezbollah's rivals invoke the army's legitimacy to demand disarmament. Foreign governments invoke the army as the acceptable recipient of aid. Citizens invoke the army because it is one of the last national symbols that still feels less contaminated than the parties. Each invocation is partly true. None gives the army the political mandate required to become the state.

The army cannot be both sacred symbol and actual sovereign instrument if every faction reserves exceptions for itself. A sovereign army must sometimes disappoint the community that loves it. Lebanon praises the army most when it is not forced to decide.

### Reform as Performance

The same pattern governs economic reform. The IMF and World Bank say, correctly, that Lebanon needs banking restructuring, capital controls, fiscal reform, electricity reform, transparent public finance, and accountability. Lebanese governments say, correctly, that they are committed to reform. Parliament debates laws. Committees form. Drafts circulate. Deadlines pass. Depositors wait. The political class survives.

The reason is not mysterious. Real reform would assign losses. Assigning losses would name winners and thieves. Naming winners and thieves would attack the coalition that controls parliament, banks, ministries, courts, and media. So reform is performed at the level of vocabulary. The same political class that presided over collapse adopts the language of recovery. Governance, transparency, accountability, resilience, stabilization: the donor lexicon becomes a costume rack.

The international community is not innocent in this theatre. It often prefers an inadequate Lebanese commitment to the admission that there is no credible counterpart. It needs someone to receive the communique, someone to initial the framework, someone to appear at the conference. Lebanon's ruling order understands this hunger for process and feeds it. The result is an economy of staged seriousness: everybody knows the reform is insufficient, but the performance allows everyone to postpone the harder conclusion.

Here is the counterparty problem. Diplomacy requires an address. Aid requires a recipient. Agreements require a signature. Lebanon supplies all three while withholding the fourth requirement: an authority capable of making the signature travel downward into reality. The international system then mistakes contact for capacity. It has met a minister, therefore it has met the state. In Lebanon those are not the same encounter.

### The Sovereignty Nobody Wants

Actual sovereignty would be ugly before it became noble. It would require a state capable of disappointing every patron, investigating every party, closing profitable illegalities, deploying law where custom now rules, and forcing citizens to discover that a state is not only protection but obligation. Taxes would have to be collected. Subsidies would have to be rationalized. Port authorities would have to become answerable. Judges would need security. The army would need doctrine, money, and a political mandate. Parties would have to become parties rather than substitute states.

Sovereignty stays safer as a slogan. The slogan lets every faction accuse its rival of betrayal while preserving its own exception. Hezbollah can demand sovereignty against Israel while limiting sovereignty at home. Its rivals can demand sovereignty against Hezbollah while inviting foreign pressure to do what they cannot build internally. External actors can demand sovereignty while sponsoring the very clients that fragment it.

The citizen is left watching the play. The state announces authority. The faction negotiates the real terms. The patron finances the scenery. The envoy praises progress. Then the lights go out, and the generator turns on.

The generator is no accidental metaphor. It is the sovereignty theatre of daily life. The state promises electricity as a public right. The private network delivers electricity as a negotiated privilege. The citizen pays both and thanks neither. In the evening, the city glows from thousands of little sovereignties humming under balconies.

Lebanon needs the boring, frightening, expensive infrastructure of sovereignty: courts that survive pressure, budgets that reveal priorities, soldiers paid enough to serve, schools that teach one history, borders controlled by one authority, and parties forced to compete over policy rather than custody. Until that exists, every speech about sovereignty should be treated as an audition.

Declarations are plentiful. The missing thing is a set of institutions capable of making declarations matter.

## The Rubble Zone

**Final title:** The Rubble Zone
**Dek:** Israel's old security-zone logic has returned to the south, promising calm through distance while producing the grievance Hezbollah knows how to use.

Southern Lebanon is where Israel repeatedly mistakes a map for a solution. Draw a line. Empty a strip. Occupy the heights. Patrol the roads. Call the result a buffer. Then wait for the same lesson to return with new dead.

The logic is seductive because it looks geometric. If rockets come from here, push fighters over there. If villages shelter militants, empty the villages. If the border feels exposed, create depth. Military maps encourage this kind of thinking because maps cannot show humiliation, revenge, inheritance, recruitment, or the private memory of a child watching a house become a crater. The map shows distance. It does not show what distance costs.

The new language of a "yellow line" in southern Lebanon belongs to an old Israeli habit. It promises security through controlled emptiness: no civilians, no fighters, no ambiguity, only a managed zone of denial. But Lebanon has already hosted this experiment. It was called the security zone. It lasted from 1985 to 2000. It did not produce security. It produced Hezbollah as a mature military-political force.

The phrase buffer zone sanitizes what the zone is made of. It is made of orchards people cannot tend, schools children cannot enter, roads that become military corridors, houses converted into coordinates, and families waiting in borrowed rooms for a return date nobody can promise. Military language turns inhabited places into depth. People do not experience their villages as depth. They experience them as inheritance.

### The Experiment Already Ran

The first security zone was not a footnote. It was a fifteen-year laboratory in the failure of occupation as counterinsurgency. Israel and the South Lebanon Army held territory in the south after the 1982 invasion and the later pullback. The premise was that a local proxy plus Israeli depth would protect northern Israel. The effect was to give Hezbollah terrain, grievance, legitimacy, and a continuous military problem around which to build an organization.

Every occupation tells itself that it is temporary. Every local community learns that temporary can become a generation. Checkpoints become biographies. Collaborators become family histories. Detention centers become political universities. Funerals become recruiting offices. The occupier measures the cost in soldiers and budgets. The occupied measure it in stories. Stories are lighter to carry and harder to defeat.

Israel withdrew in 2000 not because Hezbollah conquered Tel Aviv, but because the zone became a strategic liability. The casualties, the proxy dependence, the absence of a convincing end-state, and the growth of Hezbollah's legitimacy made the occupation more expensive than the depth it bought. That is the paradox of southern Lebanon: the longer Israel stays to reduce the threat, the more material Hezbollah has for the narrative that sustains the threat.

### The Buffer That Buffers Nothing

A buffer zone can change the location of danger while leaving the political production of danger intact. Hezbollah operates as an organization, a memory system, a welfare network, a regional instrument, and a claim about who protected the south when the Lebanese state did not. Flattening or emptying border villages may disrupt infrastructure. It also writes the next paragraph of the claim.

The security zone gives Hezbollah a place to narrate the problem it claims to solve.

Hezbollah still carries responsibility for the trap. The party turned the south into a front for regional strategy, embedded military decision-making in communities that then pay the price, and used resistance legitimacy to protect domestic veto power. The party's choices made southern civilians vulnerable to wars they did not democratically authorize. The south has been defended and captured by the same structure.

Lebanese politics keeps trying to forbid this double truth. Israel's violence is real. Hezbollah's instrumentalization of the south is real. The Lebanese state's abandonment is real. Southern loyalty to the force that fought occupation is real. Southern exhaustion with paying for regional strategy is also real. Remove any one of these realities and analysis turns into recruitment.

But Israeli strategy repeatedly behaves as though destroying the built environment can solve an organizational and political problem. That error is cruel and analytically lazy. Guerrilla organizations do not require normal civic life to operate. They thrive in torn civic life when they can claim to be the only disciplined force still standing. A demolished village can be harder to govern than a living one. Rubble carries politics.

Rubble changes the evidence available to every side. Israel can point to cleared terrain and call it security. Hezbollah can point to cleared terrain and call it proof. The Lebanese state can point to cleared terrain and call for aid. Donors can point to cleared terrain and call for stabilization. The village itself becomes less a place than a document everyone cites for a different argument.

### The Lebanese Absence

The missing actor in every version of this story is the Lebanese state. Resolution 425 assumed Lebanese authority could eventually return to the south. Resolution 1701 assumes the Lebanese army and UNIFIL can secure the area south of the Litani. The 2024 ceasefire arrangements again rely on the Lebanese state as the entity that should prevent armed groups from operating in the south. On paper this is correct. In practice it names the absence around which the entire conflict is organized.

The Lebanese army is asked to become sovereign in the one region where sovereignty has been most violently contested by Israel, Hezbollah, the Syrian legacy, Iran, the United States, UNIFIL, local communities, and memories of abandonment. It is underfunded, politically constrained, and expected to perform a task no army can perform without a national political settlement behind it. Deploying soldiers is not the same as transferring authority.

Authority requires more than the right to stand at a checkpoint. It needs the right to tell a party no, the capacity to protect civilians after saying no, the money to keep soldiers fed, the courts to punish violations, the diplomacy to bind Israel, and the trust of villagers who believe the state will still be there after the cameras leave. Lebanon has uniforms in the south. It has not yet built that chain.

The south becomes a test Lebanon keeps failing before the exam begins. The state cannot defend the territory alone, so Hezbollah claims necessity. Hezbollah claims necessity, so Israel claims the state is absent. Israel acts on that absence, deepening the grievance that sustains Hezbollah's necessity. The loop is perfect. Everyone has evidence. Nobody has a solution.

### What Security Would Require

Real security for northern Israel and southern Lebanon would require the one arrangement almost nobody invests in seriously: a Lebanese state with enough legitimacy, military capacity, economic support, and political cover to monopolize force without appearing to be the contractor for an Israeli demand. Bombed villages cannot produce that state. Neither can Hezbollah's private war power, nor envoys recycling the same resolution without the state that can implement it.

Security would require reconstruction that does not pass through sectarian ownership, army deployment linked to local trust, border arrangements that bind Israel as well as Lebanon, and a political process that separates the defense of southern communities from Hezbollah's regional autonomy. Every word in that sentence is difficult. That is why the easier policy returns: lines, zones, patrols, empty land.

The south is tired of being converted into other people's proof. Israel uses it to prove deterrence. Hezbollah uses it to prove resistance. Lebanese factions use it to prove their rivals' treason. Foreign diplomats use it to prove the need for one more framework. The people who live there prove, by surviving, that every abstraction has a landlord, a field, a mother, a shop, a grave.

The next occupation will not solve the old one. The next buffer will not buffer the memory it creates. Southern Lebanon has taught this lesson for half a century. The tragedy is that everyone keeps arriving with a ruler and calling it strategy.

## The Seventeen Countries

The laziest myth about Lebanon begins with the map. A strip of coast and mountain, smaller than many world cities, should be easy to administer. The opposite is closer to the truth. Lebanon has been made small in the wrong way: administratively chopped, legally sorted, confessionally fenced, municipally multiplied, and politically miniaturized until no public can appear at the scale where power actually operates.

The country is smaller than many world cities. Yet it contains more than a thousand municipalities, districts and governorates layered over cadastral boundaries, sectarian personal-status jurisdictions, party territories, informal security zones, religious endowments, family holdings, refugee geographies, and foreign patronage corridors. It is less a state than a stack of maps that refuse to align.

"The Seventeen Countries" names a diagnosis rather than a census of provinces. Lebanon is one country in international law and many countries in daily administration. The border says republic. The lived map says negotiated archipelago.

### Fragmentation as Policy

Administrative fragmentation is often defended as local democracy. In a normal state, decentralization can bring government closer to citizens. In Lebanon, decentralization often brings citizens closer to their za'im. The municipality works as a patronage gate, a property interface, a waste contract, a policing relationship, a symbolic flag, a boundary around demographic anxiety.

A road becomes political when its maintenance contract belongs to a municipal faction. A garbage route becomes a sectarian problem when the landfill question determines which community bears the stink of another's consumption. A building permit becomes leverage when the property registry can reward insiders and punish outsiders. A local police force becomes a border patrol when it guards the social edge of a sectarian enclave.

The number of municipalities matters because more than a thousand local authorities in such a small territory multiplies small sovereignties. Each unit can be captured, bargained over, defended, inherited, and used as proof that a community must manage its own affairs because nobody else can be trusted.

The garbage crisis made this visible in smell. Waste management should be one of the least metaphysical functions of government: collect, sort, transport, treat, landfill, recycle. In Lebanon it became a national confession of administrative fragmentation. Every proposed site was someone else's insult. Every route crossed someone's political territory. The trash did not pile up because Lebanese people had forgotten how trucks work. It piled up because the country had no legitimate scale at which a dirty public good could be allocated.

The same is true of water, sewage, quarries, transport, and electricity. Lebanon's crises often look technical at the surface and cartographic underneath. The question is never only how to provide the service. It is which community absorbs the cost, which leader controls the contract, which municipality gets blamed, and which boundary turns a shared problem into someone else's burden.

### The Border Inside the Citizen

In Lebanon, gerrymandering does not stop at the voting district. It reaches the cemetery. The citizen's life passes through confessional and administrative boundaries from birth to death. Birth is registered into a sectarian civil record. Marriage is governed by religious courts because civil marriage does not exist inside Lebanon. Divorce, inheritance, custody, and burial follow communal law. Schooling often follows sectarian or class geography. Health care often follows party or religious brokerage. Voting follows districts engineered through confessional arithmetic. Even grief has a jurisdiction.

Pluralism requires a shared public arena in which differences meet. Lebanon has segmentation: differences managed by keeping them institutionally separate and then calling the separation coexistence.

Village registration deepens the distortion. Millions of Lebanese live politically in one place and vote administratively in another. The city may shape their work, friendships, schools, rent, and daily life, but the ancestral village remains the electoral origin. This preserves family and sectarian geography long after actual social life has moved. The citizen becomes urban in practice and village-bound in representation.

This segmentation produces the citizen's most exhausting psychological habit: instant cartography. Every Lebanese learns to read names, accents, neighborhoods, license plates, surnames, school affiliations, village origins, party posters, flags, feast days, and funeral routes as political information. The citizen becomes a border officer inside his own head. Before a public appears, it has already been sorted.

The map then reproduces itself. People shop where they feel socially legible, seek services where they are known, vote where their family is registered rather than where they live, and return to village politics even when their economic life belongs to the city. The state calls this representation. It is actually a system for preventing residence, work, and citizenship from forming a common political identity.

### The Efficiency Nobody Wants

The reform case is obvious and old: consolidate municipalities into larger service-delivery units; rationalize waste, water, transport, land-use, and emergency planning; professionalize municipal finance; create institutions large enough to deliver public goods and transparent enough to be audited. None of this is revolutionary in administrative theory. It is basic statecraft.

It does not happen because inefficiency is useful. A tiny municipality may not be able to manage waste effectively, but it can employ loyalists. It may not plan roads rationally, but it can approve permits selectively. It may not coordinate with its neighbors, but it can preserve a local leader's symbolic kingdom. The service failure that results then becomes evidence that each community must rely on its own networks. Fragmentation produces failure; failure justifies fragmentation.

The generator structure reappears at municipal scale. The public utility fails. The private substitute becomes necessary. Reform threatens the substitute. The substitute funds the politics that prevents reform. At the municipal level, the substitute is the local fiefdom: a miniature state that proves the larger state cannot be trusted while helping ensure that it cannot be trusted.

### The Map as Prescription

The Lebanese map tells people where they belong, which office they approach, which leader they need, which court governs their family, which patron can accelerate the paperwork, which neighbor is a demographic threat, which public good must be privatized before someone else captures it. Geography becomes a prescription for keeping citizens from becoming a public.

Political change in Lebanon cannot be only ideological. It must be spatial. The country needs institutions that force practical cooperation: shared schools, shared transport, shared hospitals, shared waste systems, shared registries, shared public space, shared archives. Citizens do not become citizens by being told to transcend sect. They become citizens by depending on institutions that make cross-sectarian life materially ordinary.

Buses can be more radical than speeches. A public transport network does not sound like constitutional reform, but it creates a daily practice of shared dependence. A public school with children from multiple communities does more deconfessionalizing work than a hundred televised debates. A functioning waste authority teaches citizenship in the least romantic way possible: by making everyone responsible for the same unpleasant reality.

The cartel understands this, which is why it prefers symbolic coexistence to administrative integration. Coexistence festivals are harmless. Integrated service delivery is dangerous. A festival lets communities stand next to each other for an afternoon. A shared public school lets their children learn the same history for twelve years.

Lebanon can contain many communities. It cannot keep giving each community a separate counter at the ruins of the state. The cure does not erase difference; it builds enough shared machinery that difference stops needing a private government to survive.

Until then, Lebanon will remain a republic in passport form and a federation of anxieties in daily life: seventeen countries, give or take, all wearing the same cedar on their documents.

## The Land That Mourns in One Language

It began with a voice in a video.

The clip was from the war. A fighter was behind a weapon, half hidden by the machinery around him, and just before he fired he shouted something that stayed with me: "Ya Fatima al-Zahra."

It was not a speech. It was not the kind of phrase people say for a camera. It came out like a reflex, the way people call for their mother when they are afraid, or whisper the name of a saint when the plane shakes. In that moment, under fire, he did not seem to be making an argument about theology or politics. He was reaching for someone.

That is what interested me.

Fatima al-Zahra, in the simplest terms, is the daughter of the Prophet Muhammad and Khadija, the wife of Ali, and the mother of Hassan and Hussein. In Shia Islam, she is much more than a respected woman from sacred history. The whole line of the Imams passes through her. She is the daughter of the Prophet, but also the mother of the sacred household that follows him. One of her titles, "Umm Abiha," means "mother of her father." It is a strange phrase at first, almost impossible if you read it literally. But as a devotional idea, it makes emotional sense. Fatima is imagined not only as Muhammad's child, but as a source of tenderness, protection, and spiritual shelter.

This is where the thought opened for me.

Why did that cry, "Ya Fatima," sound so familiar? Why did it feel close to the Christian "Ya Adra," the call to the Virgin Mary? Why, in Lebanon, do so many people who insist they belong to completely different religious worlds still turn, at moments of fear, grief, or need, toward a holy woman who stands near God?

The easy answer would be to say that all religions are the same. That is not true, and it is not interesting. Mary is not Fatima. Fatima is not Astarte. Astarte is not a Christian saint hiding under another name. The doctrines are different, and serious believers are right to care about those differences.

But doctrine is not the only thing religion carries. Religion also carries habits. It carries gestures, fears, places, songs, family memories, ways of mourning, ways of asking for help. A people can change its official religion faster than it changes the deep emotional habits through which it approaches the sacred.

Lebanon is a good place to see this because our religious life is not abstract. It is attached to hills, villages, springs, churches, shrines, roads, feast days, funerals, and family stories. Faith here has geography. It has a view of the sea. It has a mountain behind it.

Long before Lebanon was Christian or Muslim, the coast had its own sacred world. Among its most important figures was Astarte, or Ashtart, a Phoenician-Canaanite goddess associated with love, fertility, war, sexuality, the morning star, and the sea. She was not simply a soft mother figure. She was powerful, protective, local, and dangerous in the way old gods often were. In the ancient Levant, divine figures were often tied to particular places. A goddess was not only a concept. She was the lady of a city, a harbor, a hill, a people.

Something of that local instinct survives in Lebanese Christianity. Mary is universal in Christian doctrine, but in Lebanon she is also intensely local. Our Lady of Lebanon. Our Lady of the Sea. Our Lady of Mantara. Our Lady of the Mountain. Every place seems to have its Mary, its own angle of protection, its own story of where she stood, rested, watched, appeared, or listened.

This does not mean Lebanese Christians consciously took Astarte and renamed her Mary. History almost never works that cleanly. It is subtler than that. When people convert, they do not become new human beings overnight. They bring their landscapes with them. They bring their need for protection, their fear of the sea, their habit of climbing to high places, their trust in a maternal figure who can soften the distance between heaven and earth.

Mary gave those needs a Christian language.

That is why Harissa feels like more than a statue. The monument is modern, but the feeling around it is older than the monument. People climb toward a woman above the bay and ask her to watch over the land below. You do not need to force the comparison too far to feel the continuity. The name has changed. The posture has not entirely changed.

This is also why the Lebanese Marian cult feels so powerful. I do not know whether one can prove that Lebanon has the strongest devotion to Mary in the world, and the claim is probably too large to make casually. But anyone who grew up here knows that Mary is not marginal. She is everywhere. She appears in churches, on dashboards, in school names, on hilltops, in village processions, in songs, in candles, in the quiet language of mothers. For many Lebanese Christians, she is not only a figure in theology. She is part of the emotional furniture of the country.

And then, in another religious language, there is Fatima.

For Shia Muslims, Fatima is tied to grief from the beginning. In Shia memory, she is wronged after the death of the Prophet. She is associated with the loss of inheritance, with the injury done to Ali's claim, with the first wound in the story that will later culminate in Karbala. Her son Hussein is killed there, and Shia Islam becomes, in part, a religion that refuses to let his death become ordinary history. It returns to the wound every year. It mourns as a form of memory, and memory as a form of loyalty.

Fatima stands behind that world of mourning. She is the mother of the martyred line, the wounded daughter, the radiant woman whose grief gives moral shape to the community. When someone calls on her, he is not praying to her as God. In formal Shia theology, that distinction matters. He is asking for intercession, for nearness, for someone beloved by God to carry his plea.

But lived religion is not only formal theology. At the level of emotion, "Ya Fatima" and "Ya Adra" do something similar. They give fear a name to call. They give the believer someone tender to approach before the vastness of God.

This is one of the things strict monotheism has always struggled with. A single, absolute God is beautiful as an idea, but difficult as an address. God can feel too high, too unknowable, too immense. So people look for nearness. Catholics and Orthodox Christians have Mary, saints, icons, relics, and shrines. Shia Muslims have the Prophet's household, the Imams, Hussein, Fatima, and the sacred places attached to them. Sunni and Protestant reformers have often tried to strip these mediators away, insisting on a more direct relationship with God. There is power in that directness. There is also loneliness.

Most people, most of the time, do not want an empty heaven. They want a populated one. They want someone to hear them in the language of the family.

This is where Lebanon becomes fascinating. Christians and Muslims here are often taught to see each other as radically separate, almost as different civilizations sharing a small piece of land by accident. Each community has its own wounds, authorities, suspicions, feast days, heroes, and dead. And of course these differences are real. But underneath them, one finds repeated forms: the shrine, the candle, the procession, the vow, the saint, the martyr, the mother, the village feast, the hilltop, the holy grave, the name cried out in danger.

The religions are different. The human problem is often the same.

That is why sectarian contempt can feel so historically thin. The Christian who thinks Shia devotion is alien may be looking at a cousin of his own Marian world. The Muslim who thinks devotion to Mary is excess may recognize, if he is honest, the same need in the love of Fatima, Hussein, the Imams, or the Prophet's household. Each side is suspicious of the other's intermediaries while protecting its own.

This does not erase doctrine. It does not mean everyone secretly believes the same thing. It means that human beings, living on the same land, under the same fears, often build similar bridges toward God.

Maybe that is the real story. Not that Astarte became Mary, and Mary became Fatima, like a straight line in a textbook. That is too neat. The better story is that the function survived. The protective woman. The grieving mother. The lady of the place. The intercessor. The one who stands close enough to human suffering to make the divine feel reachable.

Astarte held part of that function in the old coastal world. Mary carried it into Lebanese Christianity. Fatima carries it in Shia devotion. They are not the same figure. But they answer questions that resemble each other.

What do you do when God feels far away?

What do you do when the world has injured you?

Who receives the grief that men turn into war, pride, slogans, and revenge?

Again and again, in this land, the answer takes a feminine form.

That is what I heard in the video. Not proof of some hidden pagan survival, not a conspiracy of borrowed gods, not a cheap claim that all religions are one. I heard a man at war calling for a woman of mercy. I heard a Shia phrase that, in Lebanon, echoes against Christian hills. I heard behind both of them the older habit of a coast that has long imagined protection, grief, and intercession through the figure of a sacred woman.

The name changes.

The need remains.

## Downtown Without a City

Rafic Hariri is difficult to judge because he built something. That fact makes the argument about downtown Beirut harder than the usual anti-corruption sermon. He did not only steal, privatize, centralize, or distort. He also looked at a ruined city center and imagined cranes where others saw paralysis. He understood that postwar Lebanon needed a stage on which to tell itself the war had ended.

The problem is the stage he built. Downtown Beirut was reconstructed as an address for capital and a substitute for civic memory. It was cleaned, paved, regulated, polished, securitized, branded, and priced beyond the social life that once made it a center. The old souks had been messy, mixed, commercial, intimate, noisy, and porous. The new center became elegant and vacant in the specific way privatized public space often is: visually impressive, socially thin.

Solidere converted the city's wound into a real-estate instrument and called the result recovery.

### The Souk as Memory Machine

A souk remembers in ways a mall cannot. It remembers through repetition: the shopkeeper who knows the family, the route a worker takes after payday, the shortcut between quarters, the smell of spices and damp cloth, the argument over price, the balcony above the storefront, the religious and class mixture produced not by ideology but by need. People who would never attend a coexistence conference meet at the same counter because the city makes them.

The old Beirut souks were not a utopia. They were unequal, crowded, patriarchal, contested, and embedded in the social hierarchies of their time. But they were urban in the deep sense: they produced encounter. Their destruction during the civil war was not only architectural. It severed habits of shared use. The reconstruction could have treated that loss as the central problem. Instead, it treated war damage as an opportunity for comprehensive redesign.

The new downtown preserved fragments of heritage while replacing the social ecology that gave heritage meaning. Facades remained. Routes changed. Ownership changed. The scale changed. The user changed. The city center became a place Lebanese could visit but no longer politically own.

### The Private Republic

Solidere's genius was juridical as much as architectural. A private real-estate company was granted extraordinary authority over the reconstruction of the Beirut Central District. Property rights were transformed into shares. Former owners and tenants were compensated through instruments many could not afford to hold. The public interest was reimagined through the balance sheet of a company tasked with rebuilding the symbolic center of the republic.

The human cost of that juridical transformation is easy to flatten into policy language. Expropriation, compensation, shareholding, redevelopment: the words are clean. The experience was not. A shopkeeper who had inherited a place in the souk did not lose only square meters. He lost customers, routines, neighboring trades, informal credit, a route through the day, and the right to recognize himself in the center of the city. Compensation can price property. It struggles to price belonging.

The Lebanese model appears in miniature. The state fails, or is made to fail. A private substitute appears. The substitute becomes indispensable. The substitute then acquires public authority without public accountability. Citizens are told to be grateful because something is finally being done.

That gratitude is politically disciplining. Once the public has accepted incapacity as the baseline, any visible private capacity becomes almost immune from critique. The question shifts from "who has the right to remake the city?" to "would you rather keep the rubble?" This is the blackmail of reconstruction in a failed public order: accept dispossession because the alternative is paralysis.

Hariri's defenders argue that without this model, downtown would have remained rubble for decades. They may be partly right. That is what makes the critique serious. Lebanon's state was weak, broke, and paralyzed. The private vehicle had capacity. It produced roads, infrastructure, restored buildings, and an internationally legible city center. Solidere did something; the harder question is what kind of city its doing produced, and who was removed from the answer.

The rebuilt center solved the problem of visible ruin by creating a subtler absence: the absence of ordinary citizens from the place that was supposed to symbolize national recovery.

### Memory Without the War

Postwar downtown Beirut also demonstrates Lebanon's larger refusal to remember the civil war publicly. Reconstruction made archaeology visible while making recent violence strangely absent. Ancient columns could be displayed because they did not accuse anyone currently in parliament. The Roman layer was safe. The war layer was dangerous.

Heritage can become a method of forgetting. A city may lovingly preserve a column while refusing to mark a massacre, curating memory away from accountability. The deeper past becomes a neutral alibi for evading the recent past. Everyone can agree on Rome. Not everyone can agree on who shelled the street.

The port explosion tore through this arrangement with obscene clarity. Here was Beirut again wounded by the union of negligence, corruption, institutional hollowing, and impunity. The World Bank estimated billions in damages and losses after the blast. Neighborhoods outside the polished center - Gemmayzeh, Mar Mikhael, Karantina - absorbed the violence with the fragile intimacy of lived city fabric. Volunteers arrived before the state. Residents swept glass from apartments before officials could agree on responsibility. Once again, Beirut's social body performed the state the state could not perform.

The blast also revealed which parts of Beirut still had a city under the architecture. People knew which elderly neighbor lived alone, which staircase had collapsed, which cafe could become a supply point, which architect could assess damage, which diaspora cousin could wire money, which volunteer group had lists. The response was chaotic, uneven, and exhausted, but it was urban in a way the polished center often is not. It moved through relationship.

The cruel comparison is plain: downtown had infrastructure without intimacy; the blast neighborhoods had intimacy without protection. A real city needs both.

### The City Citizens Need

The argument against Solidere asks for something more serious than nostalgia for brokenness. Ruins are not better than reconstruction, capital can have a role in rebuilding, and old souks should not be embalmed for romantic consumption. But a city center carries more than development value. It is the spatial form of citizenship.

A real downtown should let the poor pass without feeling they are trespassing. It should allow political assembly without becoming a security exception. It should contain memory of the war without turning memory into sectarian accusation. It should preserve archaeology without using antiquity to erase living claims. It should host commerce without making consumption the price of belonging. It should be governed by public authority answerable to residents, workers, shopkeepers, visitors, and future generations.

Beirut received a downtown without a city: a center whose surfaces were restored faster than its social contract, a capital district where the absence of the public was disguised by the presence of stone.

This matters beyond Beirut because Lebanon's whole postwar order was a Solidere logic. Privatize the wound. Financialize the repair. Replace public authority with elite capacity. Call the result reconstruction. Then wonder why the rebuilt thing feels haunted.

Nostalgia will not repair the city. Restitution might: legal, spatial, archival, social. Beirut needs public squares that are truly public, rent structures that permit ordinary life, memorials that name the war, museums that explain the layers without laundering the present, and planning that begins from citizens rather than investors.

Beirut was never only beautiful. It was usable. That is what must be recovered.

There is a strange thing that happens when you spend enough time looking at Beirut from above. The city starts to appear less like a capital than a record of interruptions. The coastline is interrupted. The old souks were interrupted. Downtown's memory was interrupted. Even walking through public space can feel interrupted by gates, guards, cars, private claims, and the unspoken feeling that you may pass through the city while belonging to it remains conditional.

That was the feeling I carried into the Beirut Park project.

At first, the lazy brief was obvious. Beirut has too little green space. The site is large. A park can always be sold with trees, paths, shade, a water feature, and a cafe. That is how these projects die before the first stone is set. They are treated as amenities, when in reality they are arguments. A park tells a city what kind of people it thinks live there. It tells them whether they are visitors or citizens, consumers or dwellers, a crowd to be moved through space or a public worthy of being held by it.

Beirut does not need another place to move through. Beirut needs a place to stay.

The numbers make the urgency obvious. Beirut has around 0.8 square meters of green space per person. The commonly cited minimum standard is 9. The waterfront site is roughly 70,000 square meters, one of the last large unbuilt sites in the central district. In another city, this might be a major public amenity. In Beirut, it is something rarer. It is one of the last chances to decide what the public face of the reconstructed city should actually mean.

That is why the question was never simply: what should the park look like?

The real question was: what does Beirut need to remember in order to become livable again?

The official language of contemporary park design tends to be frictionless. It likes continuous movement, soft curves, abstract forms, imported materials, open lawns, and water features that look beautiful in renderings. This aesthetic is internationally legible. It can signal polish, wealth, and professional competence. There is nothing inherently evil about it. In the right place, with the right maintenance culture, it can even work.

But Beirut is not a blank site in a stable state. It is a city after civil war, after financial collapse, after the port explosion, after three decades of reconstruction that often felt like erasure. A generic park on this site would not be neutral. In Beirut, neutrality often means the removal of anything specific enough to be contested. It means no Phoenician layer, no Roman layer, no Ottoman layer, no French Mandate layer, no working-class practices, no rituals, no local materials, no difficult memory. It means a polished surface that carefully avoids the question of who the city belongs to.

That avoidance was exactly what we wanted to challenge.

The version we developed is built around a different principle: if the city has been fragmented by history, the park must make history legible again. It must gather what the city has been told to keep separate. It must let people walk through continuity. Not a fake continuity, not a pure nationalist myth, and not a museum display. A living continuity. A place where old forms are given new work.

Eric Hobsbawm helps here.

Hobsbawm argued that many traditions people experience as ancient are actually modern inventions. They are constructed during periods of rupture, when societies need symbols that make continuity feel possible again. The point is not that invented traditions are fake and therefore worthless. The point is that societies are always making themselves. Nations do not survive on facts alone. They survive through rituals, ceremonies, monuments, songs, public spaces, and shared habits that teach strangers to imagine themselves as part of the same story.

Lebanon is full of invented traditions. Phoenicianism was one. Arab nationalism was another. The French Mandate architectural language of downtown Beirut was another, even if people do not usually describe it that way. Those buildings fused Beaux-Arts planning, Lebanese stone, Ottoman arches, Islamic motifs, mountain-house proportions, and local craftsmanship. They were not ancient. They were constructed. Yet over time, many Lebanese came to experience them as deeply Lebanese because they gave physical form to the country's real condition: not purity, but synthesis.

That is the hinge. Lebanese identity has never been pure. It is layered. Canaanite, Phoenician, Greek, Roman, Byzantine, Arab, Islamic, Crusader, Mamluk, Ottoman, French, modern, diasporic. The mistake is trying to choose one layer and declare it the whole country. The opportunity is to design a space where the layers can coexist without needing to defeat each other.

So yes, the park invents tradition. It does so openly.

We did not pretend the Pigeon Tower had always existed on this site. We designed it. We did not pretend the Adonis Grove was an uninterrupted public ritual from antiquity to today. We composed it. We did not pretend that a Roman column naturally wanted to become a sundial. We gave it that job. The point was never to hide the scaffolding. The point was to ask whether an invented tradition can be more honest when it admits that it has been invented.

I think it can.

Historical exactness was never the test. The test was whether the invention grew from the real grammar of the place. A fake tradition uses the past as costume. A living tradition takes an older logic and gives it new social work to do.

That is how we approached every intervention.

The Roman Sundial takes the memory of ancient Berytus and turns it into orientation. A column fragment, instead of sitting as a dead artifact, becomes a gnomon, the shadow-casting arm of a public clock. It tells time without electricity. It connects the visitor to the sun, to the seasons, to a scale of time larger than political crisis. In a country where time is often measured by collapse, waiting, delay, and emergency, the sundial offers cosmic time. It says: stand here. Watch the earth move. You are in history, not outside it.

The Ottoman Gazebo sits at the heart of the axis. It draws from the kiosk, the sabil, the triple arch, and the civic culture of water as hospitality. A sabil is more than a fountain. It is an ethic: water offered freely in public. In a city where so many basic services have become private burdens, the act of drinking cold water from a beautiful stone fountain becomes quietly political. It turns utility into dignity.

The Pigeon Tower is perhaps the most misunderstood intervention, which is why it matters so much. Pigeon keeping in Beirut is often dismissed as a rough rooftop subculture, associated with boys and men on the margins, with neighborhoods that respectable society prefers to ignore. But that is exactly why it needed a monument. The point was to take something stigmatized and reveal the discipline, tenderness, rivalry, and longing inside it. Pigeon keeping is about control and release, ownership and sky, poverty and majesty. A pigeon tower in the center of the city says that working-class culture is not dirt to be cleaned away from national identity. It is part of the nation.

It also reaches deeper. The bird connects to older Near Eastern dovecotes and to the sacred associations of Astarte. The tower houses birds, collects guano for the gardens, creates an ecological loop, and produces a daily spectacle at sunset. It is symbol, infrastructure, and social repair at once.

The Adonis Grove carries the emotional center of the project. Tammuz, later known as Adonis, belongs to the deep mythological memory of this land. The story is simple and devastating: a beautiful young god dies, his blood enters the soil, and red anemones bloom. The river runs red. The land mourns and returns. Death becomes seasonal. Grief becomes botanical.

This mattered because Lebanon has no shortage of grief, but it has very few shared containers for it. Every sect has its martyrs. Every community has its wound. Even mourning is divided. The Adonis Grove looks for a symbol older than the divisions, older than the confessional claims, older than the modern state. It does not ask a Christian, Muslim, Druze, secular Lebanese person, refugee, or visitor to surrender their identity. It gives them a pre-sectarian ground on which grief can be felt without being owned.

In the grove, red anemones bloom every spring. The memorial is not a plaque. It is an event. You do not debate a flower. You return to it. You bring someone to see it. You remember that the land itself has carried stories of death and return for thousands of years.

The idea is practical too. In a country where enforcement is weak, sacredness becomes a kind of infrastructure. People behave differently around places they experience as extensions of identity. They do not need a sign to tell them not to litter in a mosque, carve into a shrine, or treat a cemetery like an empty lot. The restraint comes from inside the person. The question was whether a public park could borrow that same mechanism without belonging to one sect. The Adonis Grove tries to create that kind of shared sacredness, civic care without sectarian ownership.

Narrative infrastructure begins there. Branding names a place from outside. A slogan tells people what to feel. A serious public space makes memory behave through use, so that the place teaches without needing to lecture.

The material strategy follows the same logic. We chose Ramli sandstone, basalt, gravel, iron, and local craft because materials are moral in a city exhausted by surfaces. If something looks like stone, it should be stone. If a path suggests earth, it should breathe like earth. Concrete often looks best on the day it is poured and worse every year after. Ramli stone does the opposite. It darkens, absorbs, cools, records, and ages. It is made of the coast. It is not simply a local material in the procurement sense. It is Beirut's geological memory.

Gravel matters for the same reason. A gravel path slows you down. It makes sound. It asks the body to become aware of walking. It is cheap, repairable, permeable, and honest. If a root moves beneath it, the gravel can shift. If a path is damaged, a worker can repair it with simple tools. In a fragile country, beauty that requires constant imported systems is irresponsible. The park had to be built from things that can survive electricity cuts, budget cuts, institutional neglect, and time.

That is why we replaced the fantasy of the artificial river with the dry wadi. A pumped water feature in Beirut is a promise the state cannot keep. It looks luxurious in a rendering, then becomes a cracked basin, stagnant trench, or maintenance headache. A dry wadi is honest about the Mediterranean climate. In winter, it carries stormwater. In summer, it rests. It uses gravity rather than electricity. It teaches that dryness can still be alive.

Maintenance is governance. A public space that depends on invisible systems will eventually reveal whether the institution behind it is serious. When the pump fails, when the imported tile cracks, when the lawn burns, the public reads that failure instantly. It becomes another lesson in abandonment. We wanted the opposite: a park whose beauty depends on things Lebanon can realistically maintain, repair, and understand.

The planting strategy is political too. We did not want a decorative green ceiling of identical trees. We wanted a sovereign arboretum, a living collection of Lebanon's ecological regions. Cedar, oak, fir, olive, pomegranate, oleander, rosemary, anemone. The point was to make the country physically present in the capital. Walking the park should feel, quietly, like moving through Lebanon's ecological memory.

There are also smaller, more social interventions, because a park is finally judged by where people sit.

Backgammon tables carved into stone dignify tawleh as a civic ritual, answering those who dismiss it as wasted time. A cafe serving coffee and nargileh gives lingering a legitimate home. Benches are placed for conversation, rather than rest alone. The goal is to turn loitering into inhabiting. In a society where public space is often policed by class signals, the right to sit for hours with one coffee is not trivial. It is the beginning of civic life.

The comparison with the other version becomes clearest here. The difference is larger than style. It is a different theory of the human being.

A park optimized for flow sees people as bodies in motion. A park optimized for dwell time sees people as citizens who need reasons to stay. The first produces corridors. The second produces rooms. The first is usually easier to render. The second is harder to design because it requires asking awkwardly human questions. Where does an old man sit without feeling exposed? Where does a teenager go without being treated as a threat? Where does a couple argue quietly? Where does a child discover something? Where does a grieving person stand when the country has given them nowhere to put their grief?

Those questions are not soft. They are the hard questions of public life.

Ray Oldenburg called cafes, parks, libraries, and plazas third places: spaces outside home and work where society becomes itself. Beirut has lost too many of these. Some were destroyed. Some were privatized. Some became too expensive. Some became symbolically unavailable to large parts of the population. When third places disappear, people lose more than leisure. They lose the ordinary ground on which trust forms.

A public park in Beirut cannot be decoration. It is social infrastructure. It is political infrastructure. It is psychological infrastructure.

It is also economic infrastructure, though not in the shallow sense of saying "parks raise property values." Of course they can. But the deeper economic argument is that identity is increasingly the scarce asset in a world of copyable forms. A generic park competes with every other generic park in Dubai, Doha, Riyadh, Singapore, and Miami. Beirut cannot out-generic the places with more money, better maintenance, and stronger institutions. Its advantage is that it can only be Beirut.

That advantage has been underused. Sometimes it has been buried. Sometimes literally.

The reconstructed downtown solved certain problems of physical stabilization, but it created another problem: a district many Lebanese experience as beautiful, expensive, and not fully theirs. It is a moral issue. It is also a business issue. Places that people do not feel attached to become fragile. They require heavier security, heavier programming, heavier marketing, heavier spending to remain alive. Places that people claim as their own generate care. They create what developers call value and what citizens call belonging.

The Beirut Park proposal tries to align those interests instead of pretending they are opposed. A park made of local stone, local stories, local rituals, local ecology, and local craft can be more beloved and more economically valuable. It can reduce maintenance costs because its systems are simpler. It can increase dwell time because there are reasons to stay. It can improve the reputation of the district because it gives the public something back. It can become a future heritage asset because it was designed to age.

The phrase matters to me: future heritage.

We often speak about heritage as if it is only something we inherit. But every inherited thing was once new. Someone laid the first stone of Beiteddine. Someone designed the first triple arch that later felt inevitable. Someone decided that a public ceremony, a costume, a ritual, a monument, or a garden would stand for a people. Tradition is always being made. The real question is whether we make it cheaply or seriously, cynically or lovingly, as costume or as structure.

The Beirut Park proposal argues for serious invention.

It says Lebanon can build toward the future without erasing memory. It can stop imitating the global city as proof of progress. It can make a new public place that admits the layers beneath it.

Nostalgia wants the old city returned intact, as if Beirut could walk backward through blast dust and Solidere hoardings. Continuity accepts the break, then builds across it.

That is why the park had to be layered rather than pure. Roman, Ottoman, Canaanite, Islamic, Mediterranean, French Mandate, working-class Beirut, ecological Lebanon, post-blast Beirut, future Beirut. If that sounds messy, good. Lebanon is messy. The task was never to purify it. The task was to give the mess order without killing it.

While preparing to speak about the project to a Theory of Knowledge class, I realized the park was asking a harder question than style. How do we know what belongs in a place? Who gets to decide what is authentic? Can a space produce knowledge that an essay can only describe?

The park answers in concrete ways.

You know a place through the body as much as through the mind. Shade. Stone under your hand. Gravel underfoot. Rosemary after rain. Water offered in public. Red anemones returning each spring. An old man teaching a child tawleh. Birds rising over Beirut at sunset. A Roman column telling time because someone finally gave it work again.

That is knowledge. It does not sit in a textbook. It slowly teaches a population how to feel at home.

Beirut has been trained to accept too little from public space. It accepts beauty without life, security without welcome, and modernity without place. It accepts the idea that a park is a patch of green rather than a civic instrument. It accepts the idea that memory belongs in museums while the city itself becomes generic.

I do not accept that.

Beirut deserves public spaces as intelligent as its history and as durable as the stone beneath it. It deserves a park that does more than soften the waterfront. It deserves a park that helps the city recognize itself.

That is what we tried to design: a place where an old man can play tawleh beside a child watching birds rise over the water, without either of them buying a ticket.

If it is built seriously enough, one day it may feel older than the men who approved it.
