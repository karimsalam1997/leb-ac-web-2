# The Autopsy: What Codex Did to Your Essays

**A reader's audit of `longform-essays.md`, written for KINGO, May 2026**

## Before the essay-by-essay work, the structural diagnosis

You were right on every count and there is more.

You identified three things in your prompt and all three are accurate. One: the theme-cocktail problem. Two: the binary tic ("not simply X but Y"). Three: the editorial heaviness, the sense that every essay is reading like the same essay, written by someone too anxious to entertain. Let me name what's actually happening underneath, because it's worse than you said and the fix is more structural.

**One: Codex used your chats as a quarry, not as a record of thinking.** It did not read the chats as twelve separate arguments with twelve separate centers of gravity. It read them as a vat of themes, metaphors, and named institutions that could be ladled into any essay shape. So you get the souk-as-memory-machine line in the Downtown essay, and then a slightly rephrased version in the Downtown Without a City essay, and then again in the Beirut Park essay. You get Solidere doing its juridical genius three times. You get the generator as fractal sovereignty in one essay and then the generator as honest social contract in another and then the generator as private kingdom in a third. The image is correct each time. The repetition tells you the writer has no internal model of which idea lives where. Every essay reaches for the same drawer.

The original chats were not like this. In the dragonfly chat, the dragonfly is doing one job. In the Fatima chat, Astarte and Mary are doing one job. The point of each chat was the chat — the prompt, the answer, the next prompt, the deepening. Codex chopped that movement up, sorted the pieces into thematic piles, and then redistributed them as flavoring across twelve identical-shaped essay containers. Every essay tastes like the entire spice rack at half strength.

**Two: the binary construction tic is the symptom, not the disease.** You named "it was not simply X, it was Y" and you are right to hate it. But that move is doing something specific in this prose, and once you see it you'll see it everywhere. It's a *thinking substitute*. Whenever the writer cannot construct a real argument, it reaches for the syntactic gesture of one. *It was not only an urban planning challenge. It was a once-in-a-civilization archaeological opportunity.* *Settlement is what happens when claims are processed. Repair is what happens when a social world is given conditions to live again.* *A balance sheet can price land. It cannot price being known.* These read as crystalline because the syntax of contrast does the work the thought has not done. The reader feels they have just been told something sharp. Re-read the line and you'll find it's two adjacent assertions wearing the costume of a distinction. The same trick gets used four to six times per essay. By the third essay you can predict the shape of the next paragraph from the first word of it.

The Fisk and Hitchens you want to write like never do this. Fisk's clauses cut because the second half breaks the reader's anticipation, not because it formally mirrors the first. *I have seen, in the West Bank, what is done to the Palestinians. And I have seen, in southern Lebanon, what is done in retaliation.* The two halves go to different rooms. The Codex binaries always land in the same room.

**Three: the prose has no body in it.** This is the deepest failure and the hardest one to fix because the writer doesn't know it's a failure. Your chats have a body. The voice in the prompt — your voice — is breathless, recursive, looping back, getting angry mid-clause, dropping the level of formality without warning, naming a friend, naming a feeling. The voice in the response answers that — picks up the heat, drops a sharper image, holds a number against a metaphor, finishes with a question or a directive ("Where do you want to take this?"). The two voices together produce thinking-in-public. The reader is in the room.

The Codex essays have nobody in the room. There is no first person except in the Beirut Park essay (which is the only one that reads as if someone wrote it). Every other essay is the disembodied editorial voice of *The Economist* on its tenth scotch. *Lebanon has become a state forbidden to know itself.* *Beirut received heritage as atmosphere, not inheritance.* *Sovereignty stays safer as a slogan.* These are not sentences a person said. They are sentences a brand says.

This is why even when the analysis is correct — and most of it is — the essays feel embalmed. Correct embalmed. Embalmed in a glass case with good lighting. But you do not want a monument. You want a publication someone keeps reading after the first paragraph because they cannot tell where the argument is going next.

**Four: the dek/title/SEO scaffolding is killing the openings.** Every essay opens after a hundred-and-fifty-word block of metadata: title, dek, byline, publication date, reading time, topic tags, SEO title, SEO description, SEO keywords, excerpt, pull quote, suggested footnotes. By the time the reader reaches the first sentence of the body, they have already been told what the essay argues four times — in the dek, the SEO description, the excerpt, and the pull quote. Then the essay opens with a sentence that is *also* the thesis statement. The first paragraph is the fifth time the reader has heard the argument before it has been made. The essays cannot surprise because everything has been pre-announced.

This is not your fault. This is what an SEO-aware AI produces when asked for "long-form editorial." But on the page it kills momentum. The dek must move to the page template (where it belongs as a metadata header for the CMS, never visible above the essay body); the pull quote must be pulled mid-essay, never sitting above the title; and the body must be allowed to start without telling you what it is about. *Walk through Downtown Beirut now and the old critique no longer quite fits* is a fine sentence, but it has zero load to carry because you've just read the dek that explained the entire piece.

**Five: the citations are doing a different job than they think.** Every essay ends with a numbered bibliography that reads like an undergraduate handing in a final paper. *Saree Makdisi, "Letter from Beirut" (1994), Downtown folder file `Makdisi-LetterBeirut-1994.pdf`.* This is correct as a research note for you. It is not correct as something the public sees attached to an essay. It signals "AI built me a citations section to look serious." A real essayist quotes inside the prose, drops the author by surname in the sentence, and lets a brief endnote do the rest. Hitchens names Hitchens in the line; Fisk names the date and the village in the line. The numbered bibliography of fifteen citations at the bottom of every essay tells the reader that the writer has not absorbed the material into argument — they have merely staged it.

So those are the structural problems. Now to each essay, in order.

## Flagship 1: The City That Could Not Repair Itself

This is the strongest essay in the file and also the one that suffers most from the cocktail problem because it is so long. It has at least three good essays inside it that have been spliced together.

What is working: the central distinction between *settlement* and *repair* on the property question is yours and it is real. The post-blast comparison between Gemmayzeh's lived response and Downtown's frozen one — *Residents experience damage as urgency. Distant owners can experience damage as accounting* — is correct and devastating. The Naccache material on the bulldozed archaeological record is doing serious work; that the equivalent of a continuously inhabited 5,000-year-old urban site was loaded into dump trucks and emptied into the Mediterranean is exactly the kind of fact-as-image you should be putting in front of people. The line *Lebanon could have dug its capital and found a common story under the sectarian one* is the best sentence in the essay because it does something real: it locates the lost archaeology inside Lebanon's actual political need.

What is broken:

The opening is wrong in the way I described above. *Walk through Downtown Beirut now and the old critique no longer quite fits* is too genteel an entrance for an essay that needs to grab someone by the collar. You yourself called it out — *it's not simply glossy. That would be easier to condemn* — that whole construction is the binary tic doing what the binary tic always does: pretending sharpness while delivering the same thing twice. The actual interesting argument — that the new Downtown is *worse than ugly because it is half-restored half-broken half-occupied half-vacant in a permanent way* — does not need the "not simply X but Y" scaffolding to be made. It needs one image and a number. *On any given block in Downtown today you can find a restored Ottoman facade beside an unrepaired blast scar, a luxury apartment held vacant for the buyer who has not arrived, a Gulf brand reopening in a souk where the World Bank counts vacancy at fifty percent.* That sentence puts the thing in front of the eye. The essay should begin that way, or some way like it.

The middle of the essay is where the cocktail effect bites. Sections 4 ("The Future As Blackmail"), 5 ("The Political Economy of Emptiness"), and 6 ("Vacancy Is A System") are saying overlapping things in different vocabularies. They could be collapsed into one section called *Why Vacancy Is Rational* and the essay would be twenty percent shorter and twice as sharp. Right now the reader is being walked around the same room four times because Codex generated four section headers and felt obliged to put a section under each one.

The archaeology section ("The City Beneath The City," "Why Archaeology Was Dangerous," "The Museum That Never Became The City") is the strongest run in the whole file. Naccache is doing real work, the political stakes are properly named, and the line *the land is older than the sects, and every community is late* is the kind of formulation that earns its keep. Leave this run intact. If anything, this is the essay you should pull out and publish as its own standalone piece. *The Country Under the Parking Lot.* That is the actual essay buried inside this essay.

The closing — *The city was once bulldozed by war. Then it was bulldozed by peace. If it is ever rebuilt again, it should be rebuilt against forgetting* — works. But by the time you get to it, the reader has been told eight times that Downtown was financialized, that memory was thinned, that Solidere was a liquidator. The mantra effect kills the landing.

**What to do:** split this into two essays. One on the urbanism and political economy (your "what kind of city did Solidere build, and why did vacancy become rational"). One on the archaeology (the country under the parking lot). The first should be six thousand words. The second should be twenty-five hundred. Currently you have fifteen thousand words of one essay that wants to be two.

## Flagship 2: The Cartel in the Costume of a Country

This is the second strongest piece and it knows what it is doing. The thesis — *each side is the other's business model* — is the single best sentence in the entire collection because it does the structural work the rest of the file keeps reaching for and missing. It compresses an entire political economy into seven words.

What is working: the symmetry of the argument. You set up the anti-resistance camp's true premise, then their fraudulent jump. Then the resistance camp's true premise, then *their* fraudulent jump. Then the synthesis: they need each other. This is real essay architecture. It is the only piece in the file built around an argument rather than around a topic.

The October 2019 turn is also doing real work: *"All of them means all of them" did not say one sect stole the country from another. It said the country had been stolen through all of them. The system understood the threat and dragged the anger back into the familiar room.* That's strong. That's the prose at the level you want.

What is broken: not much, structurally. This essay is closer to publishable than any other in the file. The problems are smaller.

The "real politics is taxation, budgets, courts, roads, schools, electricity, army salaries, banking losses, public transport" run is the rule-of-three pattern multiplied by three — a list that performs comprehensiveness instead of arguing for it. Pick three. *Real politics is who pays the taxes, who collects them, and who decides where they go.* That has weight. The full nine-item list has none.

The ending — *Who benefits from making sure the state never arrives?* — works, but the four paragraphs leading up to it (*Not neutral. Neutrality would be obscene. Israel is the external aggressor* etc.) are throat-clearing. The essay has already made its point. It does not need the disclaimer-and-restatement before the kicker. Cut from *Until then, every side will keep pointing* directly to *Who benefits from making sure the state never arrives?* and the essay lands harder.

The bibliography at the bottom: Weber's *Politics as a Vocation*, the Constitution, Taif, two World Bank reports, Cammett, two UN resolutions, HRW. Nine items for what is at heart a six-minute polemic. The essay does not quote any of them. The list is performing rigor on behalf of an essay that has already earned the right to its argument through analysis alone.

**What to do:** this one is close. Cut the throat-clearing before the closer. Reduce the bibliography to the three sources you actually owe (Weber, Taif, the World Bank "Great Denial" piece). It can ship.

**What to do:** read the original chat. Notice that the chat opens with *you* watching the video and being struck by it. The essay should also open with you watching the video. ## The Generator Republic / How a Generator Owner Showed Why Lebanon Has No State

I am treating these together because they are the same essay written twice, and somebody needs to tell you that.

The first one is the structural argument: the generator is Lebanon's true constitution, the pattern is fractal, every Lebanese is a sovereign micro-state of one. The Generator Republic essay has the better prose. So is the Connecticut comparison (1,100 municipalities on 10,452 km² versus 169 on 14,000) — that is the kind of number that lodges in the reader's head. The Abu Ali essay has the better material. The street story is concrete. The lawyer's name — Majed Damashqiyeh — is in the prose. **What to do:** merge them. Cut maybe forty percent of the combined length. Keep the Connecticut number, the Abu Ali narrative, the *mehtail* analysis, and the diesel-powered opening. Lose the duplicate paragraphs about fractal sovereignty, which is currently being explained five times.

## The Census That Cannot Be Taken

This is a competent essay about a real subject and almost nothing more.

The opening — *Lebanon has not counted its population since 1932. The fact sounds like trivia until it becomes the locked room at the center of the republic* — is fine. *Locked room at the center of the republic* is a clean image. But the essay then settles into a register of high-minded political theory that goes on for eight minutes without anybody appearing in it.

Look at what's *missing*. The 1932 census was conducted by the French. Who conducted it. What questions they asked. Why they asked them. The fact that they deliberately overcounted Christians by including the diaspora — Maronites resident in Argentina, Brazil, the United States — to manufacture the Christian majority that the new state would then constitutionalize. Maktabi has been published on this since 1999. Codex cites her in the footnote and uses nothing from her in the body. This is the essay's failure in microcosm: the research is staged in the bibliography and absent from the page.

The line *the state knows enough to control the citizen but not enough to reconstitute the citizenry* is the binary tic doing its trick again. Read it twice. It is two near-synonyms separated by *but not.* It produces no friction.

The section *Counting as Revolution* is the strongest run. The list of who *does* know — parties know their voters, churches know their faithful, security agencies know their files, banks know their depositors — works because the rhythm itself argues. The state is the only party in the country forbidden the knowledge that would let it act as a state. That is a real point landed cleanly.

What is broken: the essay never names a single person except the political class in the abstract. The 1932 census happened to specific people, in specific villages, with specific instructions from specific French officials. The 2018 election was run on those numbers. The next election will be run on them. A young Lebanese voter born in 2004 will, this year, vote in a parliamentary district whose seat allocation rests on a French Mandate document older than her great-grandparents. The Codex essay almost reaches this — *A young Lebanese citizen born in 2004 is governed by the political consequences of a census conducted before his grandparents were born* — but doesn't name her, doesn't put her in a polling station, doesn't make the point land in a body.

**What to do:** rewrite the opening to put a 2026 voter in a polling booth in a district whose lines were drawn by a French colonial administrator she has never heard of. Two paragraphs of scene, then go structural. The structure of the argument is correct; it just needs a person.

## Sovereignty Theatre

This is the essay where the "everything is theatre" frame becomes the writer's tic.

The thesis is correct: Lebanese sovereignty is performed because actual sovereignty would be unacceptable to everyone in the room, including the Lebanese factions whose business model depends on its absence. The Resolution 1701 paragraph is doing its job. The reform-as-vocabulary section is doing its job.

What is broken: the metaphor of theatre becomes a crutch. *Sovereignty theatre. The choreography of sovereignty. The performance has value. The stage is real. Authority remains somewhere offstage. The text is placed on the table; the table has no legs. The donor lexicon becomes a costume rack. An economy of staged seriousness.* This is one image used as the load-bearing analytical move at least seven times in eight minutes of reading. By the fourth invocation it has stopped illuminating and started decorating.

The Cartel essay made the same kind of point and used *each side is the other's business model* once. That essay had one analytical image, deployed where it mattered, and let the rest of the prose carry the argument. This essay has one analytical image, deployed everywhere, and the prose has nothing else to carry.

The other broken thing: the patron survey paragraph. *Iran supports it against Israel and the United States while sustaining Hezbollah's regional military role. The United States supports it against Iran while shaping the Lebanese army, banking rules, and diplomatic ceiling. France supports it with sentimental imperial melancholy...* This is the most embarrassing run in the file. It is a list of countries with one-line characterizations of each. It is the kind of passage AI writes when it wants to seem authoritative on geopolitics. The *sentimental imperial melancholy* line is doing actual work and should survive. The rest is recitation.

**What to do:** halve it. Pick three patrons and treat each in a real paragraph. Replace the theatre metaphor with a different image for the second half of the essay so the reader is not eating the same dish twice.

## The Seventeen Countries

This essay has the best mechanical premise of any in the file — Lebanon is administratively chopped into seventeen-or-more functional countries on top of each other — and the worst execution of that premise.

What is working: *In Lebanon, gerrymandering reaches the cemetery* is one of the few lines in the file that earns the right to be a pull quote. *Even grief has a jurisdiction.* The garbage crisis paragraph — *Waste management should be one of the least metaphysical functions of government: collect, sort, transport, treat, landfill, recycle. What is broken: the central image — *seventeen countries* — is in the title and is never returned to in the body. The essay does not actually count seventeen of anything. It alludes vaguely to a stack of maps. The reader is left wondering whether seventeen is a real number, a metaphor, or a placeholder. Either *literally count seventeen* — the eighteen sects, the 1,100 municipalities, the 26 districts, the personal-status courts of each community, the village registry, the party territories, the security zones — and walk the reader through what stacks where. The closing — *Lebanon will remain a republic in passport form and a federation of anxieties in daily life: seventeen countries, give or take, all wearing the same cedar on their documents* — is one of the best closings in the file. *Federation of anxieties* is very good.

**What to do:** make the title earn itself. Build a count. Walk the reader from the eighteen sects through the 1,100 municipalities through the personal-status courts through the village registry through the party fiefdoms through the security districts. Number them. Let the reader feel the stacking. Then the *federation of anxieties* lands.

## Two last general notes

**On the binaries.** When you go in to edit, do a literal find-and-replace search for these patterns: *not simply X*, *not only X*, *not just X*, *it was not X. It was Y.* Most of them can be cut entirely. The remaining ones should be the binaries that *break expectation* in the second half, not the ones that mirror the first half. Real contrasts surprise. Codex contrasts pat themselves on the back.

**On the openings.** Every essay currently opens with the thesis sentence. This is a magazine convention from the 1990s and it is no longer how serious reading works online. Open with a scene, a person, a number, a sentence that does not announce what the essay is about. Make the reader work for the thesis. Trust them to follow you.
