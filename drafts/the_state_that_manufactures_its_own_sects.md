# The State That Manufactures Its Own Sects

**Working title:** The State That Manufactures Its Own Sects
**Dek:** Lebanon suffers from sectarian communities, then turns those communities into political machines and calls their competition national life.
**Byline:** Karim Salam
**Draft date:** June 26, 2026
**Status:** Working follow-up to `The Cartel in the Costume of a Country` and `The Census That Cannot Be Taken`
**Source spine:** Ussama Makdisi, Mehdi Amel, Bassel Salloukh, Joanne Nucho, Maya Mikdashi, Fawwaz Traboulsi

## Full Draft

The generator office on Sakiet el-Janzeer knows more about the Lebanese state than most ministries in Beirut.

It knows who pays, who delays, who gets forgiven, who gets threatened, who has a cousin in the municipality, who can call a party office, who can turn a billing dispute into a neighborhood question by sunset. Nothing about this looks constitutional. Nobody is quoting Article 95 while standing in the stairwell with a receipt in his hand. But the whole republic is there, reduced to diesel, favors, fear, and the small humiliation of needing a private man to sell you what a public state could not provide.

This is where the books change the argument.

The older way of talking about Lebanon says the state is weak because the sects are strong. Christians, Sunnis, Shia, Druze, Armenians, Alawites, and the rest are treated as ancient blocks pressing against a fragile republic, each with its own memory and wound, each pulling the country away from citizenship. There is truth in that, enough truth to make the sentence believable. Communities are real. Fear is real. A family in Zahle does not remember the war the same way a family in Nabatieh remembers it, and no serious person should ask either of them to surrender memory as the price of political analysis.

But that explanation stops too early. It lets the state look like the victim of sectarian society, when the state is also one of the factories that produces sectarian politics.

Lebanon contains sects, then organizes power through them. It registers people through them. It marries them through them. It buries them through them. It hires, appoints, promotes, delays, counts, avoids counting, distributes, and withholds through them. A Lebanese citizen does not meet the republic as a plain citizen who then happens to carry a communal history. He meets the republic already sorted, already priced, already translated into a share.

That is the deeper scandal. The sect is a religious community, yes. In Lebanon, it is also an administrative machine.

Ussama Makdisi helps here because he refuses the lazy comfort of saying this is how the mountain always was. The violence of 1860 in Mount Lebanon was not the eruption of eternal hatred from the soil. It was part of a modern political transformation, shaped by Ottoman reform, European pressure, local class struggle, clerical power, and new ways of imagining community. Sectarianism had history. It had makers. It had institutions. Once you understand that, the whole Lebanese alibi begins to rot.

If sectarianism was made, then it can no longer be treated as weather.

Mehdi Amel cuts even deeper. His point, translated out of heavy theory and into Beirut language, is brutal: the sect becomes politically powerful because the state needs it to become politically powerful. The system does not simply discover Maronites, Sunnis, Shia, Druze, and others waiting outside the door as finished political units. It teaches them to arrive as units. It rewards them for arriving as units. It punishes them when they arrive as workers, tenants, students, depositors, farmers, patients, or citizens.

That is why the sectarian leader always wants you vertical.

The zaim needs the banker and the bankrupt depositor under one roof. He needs the hospital owner and the uninsured patient under one roof. He needs the contractor and the worker under one roof. He needs the landlord and the tenant under one roof. The roof is the sect. Under that roof, people who should be fighting each other are taught to fear another roof. This is not stupidity. It is design.

October 2019 terrified the system because, for a few weeks, people in Beirut, Tripoli, Nabatieh, Jal el Dib, Tyre, and Baalbek began leaving those roofs. The slogan was simple enough to fit on a wall and dangerous enough to threaten the whole order: all of them means all of them. It did not say one sect had stolen the country from another. It said the theft had passed through all of them. That was the unforgivable part.

The machine responded with the only language it trusts.

Shia anger was told it was being used against the resistance. Christian anger was told it was being swallowed by Hezbollah. Sunni anger was told it was losing its place. Druze anxiety was folded back into survival. Every door that had opened toward citizenship was patiently pushed back toward sect. The uprising did not fail because Lebanese people suddenly stopped wanting dignity. It failed because the system still controlled the practical routes through which fear travels.

This is why the census matters.

The last official count in Lebanon, in 1932, is usually described as a demographic problem. It is more than that. It is the founding photograph of the political machine. The French Mandate counted people and helped turn the count into a distribution of power. Then the National Pact made the distribution feel like destiny. Then Taif adjusted the shares after the civil war without breaking the logic of shares. The country changed. The formula survived.

The refusal to count again is not ignorance. It is governance.

Every faction knows enough to run its own machine. Parties know their voters. Churches know their faithful. Waqf institutions know their properties. Banks know their depositors. Schools know their students. Security agencies know their files. Municipalities know who needs a permit. The only forbidden knowledge is the public knowledge that could let the country see itself as one society rather than a negotiation among communal estates.

The strongest argument against a census should be conceded. A reckless count in Lebanon could be dangerous. Numbers can become weapons here because every number is immediately heard as a claim on office, territory, money, fear, or revenge. A count without constitutional protection could produce panic before it produces reform.

But this is also the perfect excuse for permanent darkness.

The cartel says counting would create conflict. In truth, the conflict already exists. It has been moved into cabinet formation, public hiring, electoral districts, school curricula, army appointments, land anxiety, refugee policy, and every conversation where someone says, quietly, "how many are they now?" Lebanon did not avoid sectarian conflict by avoiding the census. It privatized the conflict and let every faction sell its own numbers to its own frightened public.

The same machine appears in the Hezbollah argument.

The strongest version of the pro-resistance case is not foolish. Israel invaded Lebanon, occupied the south, bombed villages, displaced families, and killed civilians. The Lebanese state did not protect the south when protection mattered most. A man from Bint Jbeil or Khiam does not need a seminar in sovereignty from a politician whose state arrived as a rumor and left as a condolence note. Hezbollah grew in a real wound.

But the wound became an institution. The institution became a party. The party entered the state. The party then used the weakness of the state as proof that the party must remain outside it. This is the circle nobody wants to name because naming it makes everyone uncomfortable. Hezbollah did not invent Lebanese state failure, but Hezbollah has learned to live from it.

The strongest version of the anti-Hezbollah case is also not foolish. No republic can survive when one party decides war and peace alone. No citizen in Achrafieh, Tripoli, Saida, Zahle, or Beirut should be told that his home, savings, son, passport, and future are collateral in a decision taken by a party command linked to a regional axis. The anger at Hezbollah's weapons is real.

But the anti-resistance camp also uses the state as a costume. It asks Hezbollah to hand its weapons to institutions it has spent decades underfunding, bargaining over, weakening, or offering to foreign patrons. It speaks of sovereignty in English at donor tables, then returns to Beirut and treats every ministry as a sectarian estate. It wants the symbol of the state without the burden of building one.

So the two camps keep circling the same dead center.

One side says the state is too weak, so the weapons must remain. The other says the weapons remain, so the state is too weak. Both sentences contain truth. Both sentences protect someone. Both sentences avoid the harder one: every major faction in Lebanon has found a way to profit from the state arriving late, arriving partially, arriving as paperwork, arriving as a threat, arriving as a sectarian message, or not arriving at all.

This is where Salloukh and Nucho make the daily machinery visible. Sectarianism is a speech on television, but it is also a clinic bill, a school seat, a municipal favor, a party scholarship, a condolence visit, a security call, a job application, a road repair, a generator subscription. It is the ordinary route through which a person survives. That is why people return to the sect even when they curse it. The sect answers the phone.

A normal state would make that phone call less sacred.

That is what every faction fears. A public school that works weakens the party school. A hospital system that works weakens the charity network. A court that works weakens the phone call. An army that works weakens the militia. A census that works weakens demographic myth. A bus network that works weakens the checkpoint in the mind. A public archive that works weakens the private war story. A state that works does not abolish identity. It abolishes dependency.

Maya Mikdashi is useful here because she reminds us that the state is intimate. It is parliament, cabinet, and army command, but it also enters marriage, inheritance, custody, documents, citizenship, and the family. The Lebanese state may look absent when electricity fails, but it is very present when it sorts the citizen through personal status, sectarian record, gendered law, and paperwork. The state is absent where care is needed and present where classification is useful.

That is not weakness. That is a choice.

The old reform language is tired because it keeps asking the sectarian machine to supervise its own disappearance. Article 95 says political confessionalism should be abolished. Taif says the direction is away from the old order. Politicians repeat these words the way a family repeats a promise nobody intends to keep. The sentence survives because it is cheaper than the act.

Abolishing political sectarianism would not mean asking people to stop being Maronite, Sunni, Shia, Druze, Armenian, Alawite, Catholic, Orthodox, secular, frightened, faithful, historical, wounded, or proud. That is the lie used to scare them. The question is not whether communities should exist. Of course they will exist. The question is whether the state should keep turning communities into political containers for jobs, offices, fear, and veto power.

There is a difference between protecting communal life and governing through communal captivity.

Lebanon has spent a century pretending those are the same thing.

The next serious Lebanese politics has to begin there. Not with another beautiful slogan about unity. Not with another savior cabinet. Not with another donor conference where foreigners ask for reform and Lebanese officials nod as if they have not mastered the theater of nodding. It begins with a simpler, harder question: where does the citizen meet the state without passing through a sectarian broker?

If the answer is almost nowhere, then the sectarian system is not an opinion people hold. It is the road they must use.

This is why better language matters. To say Lebanon is divided is too easy. To say Lebanon is a country of sects is too old. To say the state is weak is too polite. The sharper sentence is this: Lebanon built a state that manufactures sects as political machines, then blamed the citizens for living inside the machines it built.

The generator office on Sakiet el-Janzeer understands this without footnotes.

At night, when the public current cuts and the private motor starts coughing behind the building, the republic announces itself in the only way it still knows how. A bill under the door. A wire across the alley. A man with a notebook. A citizen waiting for light from a kingdom small enough to fit in a basement.
