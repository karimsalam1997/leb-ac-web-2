# The Generator Pasha

**By Karim Salam**
**Lebanese Academic, May 2026**

---

One night in April, the Lebanese state tried to question a generator owner. By midnight, the state had given up and gone home.

The neighborhood is mine. Sakiet el-Janzeer, Sunni West Beirut, just below Koreitem. I live above it. The garbage being thrown that night was, in some literal sense, being thrown outside my window. The motorcycles I could hear from the kitchen. The man at the center of it was Abu Ali Itani. Generator operator. Café owner. Mosque-goer. The kind of figure every Beirut neighborhood has but no one quite knows how to classify.

State Security, acting on a financial prosecutor's order, had moved to detain him over electricity fraud. Manipulated meters. Inflated rates. The standard generator-cartel business. Within ninety minutes, the roads around Koreitem, Cola, Qasqas, Verdun, and Corniche al-Mazraa were blocked. Tires burned. Men on motorcycles arrived from neighborhoods I do not know how anyone could have summoned that fast. Sheikh Ahmad al-Hariri, the Future Movement's Beirut coordinator, went on live television and announced that an investigation into a man who steals electricity from his neighbors was, in fact, a sectarian aggression against the Sunni community of Beirut. By the time I went to sleep, the lawyer, a man named Majed Damashqiyeh, had publicly explained that his client had already been granted a one-month extension by the financial prosecutor Maher Shaiteh — to "settle the violations." The summons had become an ambush. The raid had become a kidnapping. The state had become, on Abu Ali's television, a rogue faction operating outside the law.

By morning the roads were open again. Abu Ali was at home. The meters were spinning.

This is the country, in one night, in two square kilometers, on the street under my balcony. Everything I want to say about how Lebanon actually works is somewhere in that ninety minutes between the summons and the burning tires. If you stand the incident on its head and look at it from underneath, you can see the entire system.

## The choreography

What I watched that night was not improvisation. It was a script everyone had rehearsed without rehearsing, the way Lebanese politics rehearses everything — by living inside the same logic for so long that the moves feel like reflexes.

Act one was the laundering gambit. Abu Ali, on camera, the patriarch of the neighborhood with his hands open, explaining that none of the meters were in his name. His cousins owned them. The paperwork was somewhere being prepared. He himself was merely a humble man who provided light to the mosque and the school out of generosity. This is the universal grammar of every Lebanese cartel from the port to the pharmaceuticals — *I am not the owner; I am the servant. I do not profit; I provide. The papers exist; they are being prepared.* It is the rhetoric of a feudal lord pretending to be a humble functionary. Everyone in Beirut knows who runs the meters in Sakiet el-Janzeer. The point of the speech was not to convince anyone he was innocent. The point was to put the legal exposure on relatives, on absent paperwork, on the air itself — anywhere except on him.

Act two was the inversion. The summons had not been a summons. It had been a *trap*. He had been lured out of the mosque, away from his café, intercepted by men he could not identify — they could have been his brothers, they could have been anyone. He moved himself, in three sentences, from the category of *suspect* into the category of *victim*. This is the only category in which a Lebanese street figure can survive scrutiny. As long as you are the one being prosecuted, every move you make is suspect. The moment you are the one being persecuted, every move you make is courageous.

Act three was the one that mattered. Sheikh Ahmad al-Hariri, on Al Jadeed, flanked by Abu Ali, says the sentence I want you to read twice: *an attack on Abu Ali is an attack on all of us.* The file had just been upgraded. What had begun as a meter-reading dispute in a Sunni neighborhood of Beirut was now, on live television, a sectarian assault on the Sunni community of Lebanon. Hariri then went one step further and dropped a phrase whose ambiguity was the entire point of it: this raid, he suggested, had been timed to *disturb the Arab winds blowing toward Lebanon.* Anyone watching knew what he meant. The Saudis. The Gulf money. The rumored aid packages. Touch our street operators and you will sabotage the rapprochement this country needs to survive. The Sunni political establishment had just publicly offered up its own corruption as a hostage against state reform.

The lawyer, meanwhile, was doing his own quieter work. He explained that the financial prosecutor had already granted his client an extension to *settle the violations.* Notice the verb. Not face. Not be prosecuted for. *Settle.* He was saying this on camera, as a defense, in front of the country. He thought he was defending. He was confessing. The casual disclosure that violations are settled administratively, between gentlemen, on extensions, with the prosecutor's blessing — that was the architecture of the Lebanese state laid bare in one sentence, by an officer of the court, in defense of his own client, on the public record.

By the end of the segment, the State Security operation had been characterized as the unauthorized excess of a rogue unit operating outside the law. The state had been split. Some of it was legitimate, namely the parts willing to negotiate. Some of it was rogue, namely the parts trying to enforce. The lesson Abu Ali was teaching everyone watching, including the prosecutor watching, was that the institutions of the Lebanese state could be triaged into useful and dangerous halves, and that the dangerous halves could be neutralized by being publicly accused of operating outside the law they were trying to enforce.

This is what coronation looks like when it is interrupted halfway through.

## Why him

The thing nobody on Lebanese television was willing to say that night, because it would have given the game away, is that Abu Ali Itani is not one of the truly untouchable barons.

The country has somewhere between forty and fifty thousand private generators. The cartel turns over an estimated two billion dollars a year — at one point, more than the entire budget of Electricité du Liban. The truly powerful operators run in the dahyeh under Hezbollah's protective umbrella, in the Christian heartland under FPM-aligned families, in the Druze mountain under Jumblatt's network. Those men have armed protection, party backing, and structural integration into the security architecture itself. The state cannot touch them, and everyone in the state knows it.

So the question that night was never *why is the state finally moving against the generator mafia.* The question was *why this man.*

Three things converged. The first is what I will call sovereignty theatre. The Salam government, in spring 2026, is a government desperate to look like a government for foreign cameras. The Gulf is back at the table. The Americans want deliverables. Reconstruction money, when it comes, will come on the condition that the Lebanese state has performed something legible as governance. The state cannot do the hard thing, which is disarm Hezbollah. The state cannot do the medium-hard thing, which is investigate the port. So it does what it can do: it stages a crackdown on the generator mafia, the kind of crackdown that photographs well for a Saudi delegation. Abu Ali was supposed to be an exportable arrest.

The second is the targeting calculation. The State Security analysts looked at the map and asked which baron they could touch with the least blowback. The Hezbollah operators would have triggered a war. The FPM operators would have triggered a cabinet crisis. The Druze operators would have triggered Jumblatt. The Sunni operator, in a community that has been politically orphaned since Saad Hariri's withdrawal in 2022, looked like the soft target. The Sunni street had no party leader, no militia, no Nasrallah-equivalent on the phone. They calculated wrongly.

The third is the part that I think Lebanese commentary will keep missing for the next year. The Sunni community in Beirut, since the Hariri withdrawal, since the wipeout of Sunni representation in parliament, since the war began with Hezbollah dragging the country into ruin and the Sunni community watching with the particular humiliation of people who have lost the ability even to object — that community had been building, for four years, an enormous reservoir of unspent grievance. Abu Ali did not summon the motorcycles by himself. The motorcycles were already there, idling, waiting for a cause. He was simply the vessel that arrived first. The protest was never really about him. It was about a community announcing to the state, and to the other sects, and to itself, that it had not yet been written out of the country. *Touch one of ours.* The motorcycles came.

This is the part of the story that is structurally interesting because it is not a story about generators at all. It is a story about how Lebanese politics produces neighborhood emergencies as substitutes for political leadership. The Sunni street had no zaeem that night. By midnight it had one. He happened to be a generator owner. He could have been anyone with enough territory under his hand to put a hundred motorcycles on the road in twenty minutes.

## What kind of creature Abu Ali actually is

The thing I kept asking myself the next morning, the question that started this whole essay in my head, was this: what *is* this man, exactly? Politically, what category is he?

He is not a politician. He has never run for anything. But a Future Movement coordinator dropped everything and went on live television to defend him.

He is not a militia commander. He has no men under arms. But he can put a hundred motorcycles on the street in twenty minutes.

He is not a religious figure. He preaches nothing. But he emerges from the mosque as the protected son of his community.

He is not a businessman in any sense that would make sense in another country. There is no LLC, no audited accounts, no business plan. But he controls the most essential utility in his neighborhood — more essential than water, in a Mediterranean city.

He is not a sectarian leader. He holds no formal title. But the moment he is touched, every Sunni street in Beirut interprets it as a sectarian assault.

Standard political-science vocabulary breaks against him. He is none of the categories and all of them. The fourteenth-century historian Ibn Khaldun, who watched a different kind of broker rise out of a different kind of state collapse in North Africa, would have recognized him immediately. He would have called what holds Abu Ali up *'asabiyyah* — group solidarity organized around a man who delivers what the formal state cannot deliver. Light. Employment. Protection. Dignity. Ibn Khaldun was writing about Bedouin tribes consolidating around a chief in the absence of a functioning Caliphate. The structure is the same. The chieftain is the man through whom the community now experiences the things the state has stopped providing.

This kind of figure exists elsewhere. In Naples, the Camorra clan boss who runs the trash collection and gets weeping defense from his neighborhood when the carabinieri move in. In Sicily, *l'amico degli amici* who arranges the loans, the marriages, the doctor's appointments, the funerals. In Karachi, the MQM ward boss who decides who gets a water connection and who does not. In Rio's favelas, the trafficker-providers who run the schools and the medical clinics while the formal state has not entered the neighborhood in twenty years.

But Lebanon has perfected something the others only approximate. In Naples, the Camorra is still recognizably distinct from the church, from the political party, from the neighborhood patriarchy. In Karachi, the MQM is a political party with a militia. In Rio, the trafficker is a trafficker. They are different things that happen to overlap. In Lebanon, the *quasi*-everything has fused. Mafia, party, sect, mosque, neighborhood patriarch — these are no longer four overlapping circles. They are one figure. Abu Ali is not the quasi-anything. He is the synthesis.

He is what the Lebanese state has decomposed into.

## The brutal joke

When the state finally tries to reassert itself, even partially, even justly, it discovers something that the political-science textbooks tend not to prepare you for. The citizens have transferred their loyalty to the broker.

This is the part of the night that I keep returning to, because it is the part most people watching from outside cannot believe. The state asks the neighborhood, *why are you defending a man who inflates your electricity bills, who manipulates the meters in your own apartment, who taxes you more per kilowatt-hour than any utility on earth?* And the neighborhood answers — and this answer is correct, it is the only correct answer the neighborhood can give — *because he gave us light when you gave us darkness.*

That is the joke and it is not funny. The state cannot reform the broker, because the broker is the only reason the neighborhood has light. The broker cannot be reformed without first replacing him, and the state cannot replace him, because if the state could replace him, the broker would never have appeared in the first place. The state's incapacity is what produced the broker. The broker's existence is what perpetuates the state's incapacity. Each is the other's alibi.

The way the Sunni street defended Abu Ali that night is the way the Shia south defends Hezbollah, the way Achrafieh defends the FPM-aligned families that own the contracts there, the way the Chouf defends Jumblatt. In every neighborhood in the country, the same exchange has already happened. The state failed. Someone arrived. The community now belongs to whoever arrived. Asking the community to abandon him is asking them to surrender, voluntarily, the only protection they have ever known. Nobody does this. Nowhere has anybody ever done this. The state would have to *first* provide an alternative — a real grid, a real police, a real school — and then ask the community to switch over. But the state cannot provide the alternative, because the people who run the state are the same people who run the brokers, or they are paid by the brokers, or they are protected by the brokers, or they are afraid of the brokers.

The circle closes. The generator hums. The meter spins.

## Why this scales

The generator is the perfect Lebanese metaphor because the pattern it encodes does not stop at electricity. It replicates at every scale of Lebanese life, and once you see the shape, you cannot stop seeing it.

The state failed to provide power. A private operator stepped in. The private operator became politically protected. Any reform that fixed the grid would destroy him. So he prevents the reform. The state stays broken. The generator stays running. The meter keeps spinning.

Now replace electricity with anything else. The state failed to provide schooling. Private schools appeared. The private schools became politically protected, because each sect runs its own. Any serious reform of the public education system would threaten the entire sectarian schooling economy. So the reform never comes. The state's schools stay gutted. The private schools stay full. The teacher keeps teaching.

The state failed to provide hospitals. Private hospitals appeared, mostly sectarian. The private hospitals became politically protected. Reform never comes. The waiting rooms stay full. The bribe to the nurse stays standard.

The state failed to provide water. Water tanks appeared on roofs, water deliveries appeared on streets. Beirut's municipal water network loses, at the last serious measurement, forty-eight percent of what it carries to leakage that no one is paid to fix. The reform never comes. The truck keeps coming.

The state failed to provide policing. Sectarian parties polic e their neighborhoods. Building syndicates police their buildings. Families police their families. The reform never comes.

The state failed to provide justice. Eighteen sectarian courts handle marriage, divorce, inheritance, custody, burial. The reform never comes. There is no civil marriage in this country. There is no civil cemetery.

The state failed to provide elections worth holding. Every Lebanese is registered to vote not where they live but in their ancestral village, which means a banker in Beirut votes in a district his grandfather left in 1956 for reasons of family origin that the banker himself cannot fully reconstruct. The reform never comes. The village stays politically dominant. The city stays politically homeless.

Every public service that, in a country that functioned, would produce the feeling that *we are all in this together* has instead produced a private substitute, a private patron, a private kingdom, a private monthly bill. You do not get a country. You get subscriptions. Electricity subscription. Water delivery. Private school. Private hospital. Generator amperes. A beach club instead of a beach. A foreign passport if you are lucky. A relative abroad sending dollars if you are luckier. The Lebanese citizen does not live in a country. He assembles a private state around himself, one monthly payment at a time.

## The fractal

The shape that emerges is what I want to call fractal sovereignty, because the same logic reproduces at every level of organization simultaneously, each level reinforcing the others.

At the national level, the country fractures into eighteen sects, each running its own hospitals, schools, courts, and political machine.

At the sectarian level, each sect fractures into competing zaeem networks, each controlling a district, a port entrance, a ministry.

At the district level, each area becomes a micro-sovereignty. Achrafieh has its informal governance. Dahieh has its own. Tariq el-Jdideh has its own. During the 2024 war, the mayor of Rmeil, a Christian neighborhood, explained on the record that his neighborhood had no shelter space available for the displaced. Everyone in the country understood what he meant. The displaced were Shia. Rmeil is not. During the 2026 war, when displaced families tried to rent apartments in Christian Achrafieh, their names were forwarded to the government for confirmation they were not "involved in any activity related to Hezbollah." Israel, for its part, informed Christian and Druze community leaders in southern Lebanon that they were free to remain in evacuation zones, but pressed them to force out the Shia families taking refuge in their villages. The Israeli army and the Achrafieh syndicate and the Rmeil mayor were, structurally, doing the same thing.

At the neighborhood level, the building takes over. Each apartment building has its own generator, its own water tank, its own syndicate, its own internal arrangement for who pays for what and who is allowed in.

And at the bottom of the descent, the Lebanese person himself becomes a sovereign micro-state of one. He runs his own wasta network. He maintains his own informal law. He drives by his own rules. He parks where he likes. He plays every outside actor — the Israeli, the Syrian, the American, the Saudi, the neighbor, the cousin, the prosecutor — with equal fluency, because he has learned, from his father who learned from his father, that every external actor is temporary and every alliance is transactional and the only constant is his own wits.

The country is one country in international law. The lived map is many. Lebanon is smaller than many world cities and contains, at last count, more than one thousand and one hundred municipalities. Connecticut, for comparison, has a hundred and sixty-nine municipalities on roughly forty percent more land. Lebanon is not chopped into eleven hundred local authorities because eleven hundred is the optimal number for delivering services. It is chopped that way because every dotted line on the map is a chance to draw a *them* and an *us*. Eleven hundred is what you get when the only political technology the country knows how to run is the production of small in-groups defended against larger out-groups. Fragmentation is not the symptom. Fragmentation is the policy.

## The person this produces

I want to be careful here because the easy version of this argument turns into a list of Lebanese vices. That is not what this is. The person the system produces is not a moral failure. He is, in a precise sense, an optimization. The fractal sovereignty machine has selected, across three generations, for a particular kind of human being, and it has selected with impressive precision.

He is a tactical polyglot. He can shift register, accent, posture, and emotional temperature in mid-sentence depending on who he is talking to. He speaks Arabic with the cousin, French with the Parisian contact, English with the American, Khaleeji with the Saudi, and a different Arabic with the neighbor than he speaks with his mother. Three or four social codes running simultaneously, the way Phoenician traders ran four currencies on the same trip.

He is a hyper-networker without a network. He has five hundred contacts and zero institutions. He knows someone in every ministry, every embassy, every hospital, every bank. But these connections are all bilateral. They do not form a mesh. They form a star pattern with him at the center. This is why a Lebanese man in São Paulo or Abidjan or Sydney can, within forty-eight hours of arrival, find another Lebanese who knows a Lebanese who can help him with whatever he needs. The individual node is spectacular. But put five hundred such nodes in the same country and they cancel each other out, because each is optimizing against all the others.

He is capable of a generosity so extravagant it will embarrass you, and he extends it without hesitation to anyone inside his circle. He will feed you until you cannot physically eat. He will fight you for the bill with a ferocity that approaches violence. He will mortgage his apartment for his cousin's tuition without a second thought. The generosity is real, and it is also a form of accounting. In a zero-trust society, your reputation for generosity is your credit score. You will need it when you need a hospital bed, a permit, a job for your son.

And in the same week he can do all of this, he can double-park his car on the sidewalk without a flicker of guilt. Because the sidewalk, to him, is not public space. There is no public space, in the moral sense. There is unclaimed private space, which is space available to whoever arrives first. The Lebanese person does not break rules. He operates in a space where rules are understood to be suggestions issued by people who lack the enforcement capacity to make them stick. So he invents his own.

The Arabic word for what he becomes when he plays the system is *mehtail*. The translation people reach for is "scammer," but that is wrong, or at least too crude. *Mehtail* is closer to *player*. He sees the angle and takes it. He is not motivated by malice. He is motivated by an extremely clear-eyed reading of the environment. The environment has taught him, from childhood, that nobody else is watching out for him and he had better watch out for himself. *Mehtail* is the verb form of that lesson. And the country rewards fluency in the unofficial. The mehtail is not outside the system. He is the system's most fluent speaker. He is the person to whom the system has, by its design, delegated all the actual work of survival.

This is also what makes Lebanese hospitality, Lebanese ingenuity, Lebanese commercial brilliance look so impressive at the individual level and so disastrous at the aggregate. Each Lebanese person routing around the state makes perfect sense. Six million Lebanese routing around the state produces a state that cannot be reformed, a country that cannot arrive at a negotiating table as a single actor, a banking system that vaporized everyone's savings with the bureaucratic efficiency of an institution that knew it would never be prosecuted, and a port that sat loaded with ammonium nitrate for six years because no single institution had both the authority and the incentive to remove it.

The fractal protects each unit and destroys the whole.

## The Jews of the Jews

You said something to me once that I want to come back to because I think you were already half the way to the answer. You said: the Lebanese are the Jews of the Jews. You meant, I think, that we have the same diaspora toolkit — the multilingualism, the merchant cunning, the commercial brilliance, the unkillable adaptability under hostile conditions — and that we use it against each other.

I think the comparison is sharper than even that. The Jewish diaspora developed institutions that sat between the individual and the hostile state — the synagogue, the burial society, the lending circle, the landsmanshaft, the political lobby, eventually the state itself. Those institutions created what economists would call a commons. Individual ambition could draw from the commons and feed back into it. The cunning was networked. The talent compounded.

Lebanon has the equivalent structures on paper — the church, the mosque, the political party, the family office. But they are all sectarian, which means they replicate the fragmentation rather than bridging it. Every Lebanese institution that *could* have functioned as a commons was instead built as a wall. The Maronite institution serves Maronites. The Sunni institution serves Sunnis. The Shia institution serves Shia. There is no Lebanese institution. There is only a federation of communities each running its own parallel infrastructure for its own people.

So we took the diaspora toolkit and used it against ourselves. The commercial brilliance that built Carlos Slim's empire in Mexico produces blackouts in Beirut. The networking instinct that built AIPAC fragmented here into eighteen competing patronage systems. The merchant cunning that turned Lebanese into the most successful trading diaspora in West Africa, in Brazil, in Australia, became, in the home country, the cunning of one zaeem playing another, one sect outmaneuvering another, one neighborhood walling itself off against another.

We are our own hostile host country. We are the pogrom and the refugee in the same body.

## The exit and the loop

This is the deepest damage, and I want to name it before I close, because everything else in this essay is preface to it.

The country trains its best people to treat the future as something to evacuate from. Money leaves before it can be trapped. Children are educated toward departure. Families measure success by the quality of the exit route. The Lebanese person who can leave does leave, or arranges to leave, or arranges for his children to leave. The ones who stay are the ones who could not arrange the exit, or the ones whose work is so locally embedded they cannot transplant it, or the ones, like me, who keep telling themselves they are staying because the work is here.

The diaspora then sends money back. The remittances keep households alive. The dollar goes further than the lira. The relative in Lagos pays the cousin's hospital bill in Beirut. The relative in São Paulo sends the down payment on the apartment. The system, which produced the exit, is then saved by the exit. The remittances become the oxygen the dysfunction breathes. The system survives on the very people it pushed out, which means the system has no incentive to stop pushing people out.

Tourism becomes a national prayer. The airport road gets cleaned because that is where the visitor enters. Posters come down, asphalt appears, the facade is polished. The tourist, the Gulf visitor, the expat with dollars — everyone waits for them with the desperate dignity of a country that has forgotten how to produce and learned how to receive. The currency of the country is no longer manufactured goods, or services, or anything that requires a functioning state to produce. It is the goodwill of its diaspora. The country has become a museum of itself, maintained for the visitors who arrive once a year to verify that the museum is still there.

Meanwhile, the deeper system remains untouched. New faces enter government. Better CVs appear. Technocrats speak well. But each ministry is still a prize, each appointment still passes through sectarian arithmetic, each service still carries leverage. The cake is smaller after 2019 than it was before. The knife is the same.

This is why I do not believe the story of Lebanon as mere incompetence. Incompetence cannot be this durable. A bad system that lasts this long is doing something for someone. The collapse of the banking system in 2019 was not stupidity. It was brilliance in the clinical sense, and morally empty. The people who emptied the deposit accounts knew exactly what they were doing. The political class that protected them knew exactly what they were doing. The system was not failing. It was functioning at maximum efficiency, just for a smaller and smaller set of beneficiaries.

That is what hurts. Lebanon does not waste talent by failing to produce it. It wastes talent by routing it into escape. The same people who can build businesses in Lagos, São Paulo, Dubai, Montreal, Abidjan, Sydney, and Detroit spend their lives in Lebanon solving problems that should not exist. How do I get electricity? How do I protect my savings? How do I get the permit? How do I keep my money outside the country? How do I make sure my children can leave if they need to? How do I survive the next devaluation, the next war, the next capital control, the next official lie?

At a certain point, leaving does not feel like betrayal. It feels like competent planning.

## What I keep coming back to

I want to come back to that night in Sakiet el-Janzeer, because the whole argument lives in it.

Abu Ali defeating a State Security summons is not, on its own, a story worth writing about. Generators are not, on their own, a story worth writing about. Eleven hundred municipalities are not, on their own, a story worth writing about. None of these things is the story. The story is what they share. The same shape keeps appearing at every scale of Lebanese life. The state fails. Someone arrives. The community owes him. He owes the system. The system owes him. The reform never comes.

The generator is in the basement. It is humming right now, actually. Constant, indifferent, profitable. It does not care who is president. It does not care which militia controls which port entrance. It does not care whether the ceasefire holds or whether the reform package passes or whether the international community sends another delegation to express concern. It runs on diesel and on the structural impossibility of the alternative, and in that sense it is the most honest institution in the country.

The night they tried to question Abu Ali, the state was sent a message in a dialect no diplomat could miss. *You do not enforce here. You ask permission here.* The state, true to form, asked permission, accepted the one-month extension, opened the roads, and went home. Somewhere in the Saraya, a minister was almost certainly drafting a press release explaining that the rule of law had been preserved through dialogue.

Lebanon will have solved its political problem the day the generator goes quiet. The day a Lebanese citizen, somewhere in any neighborhood in any city, opens his electricity bill and recognizes the entity at the top of the page as belonging to a country he also belongs to. The day a meter reading does not require a thousand-man protest movement. The day Abu Ali, whoever the next Abu Ali is, becomes simply a man who needs to comply with the law because there is no other game in town.

I would not hold your breath.
