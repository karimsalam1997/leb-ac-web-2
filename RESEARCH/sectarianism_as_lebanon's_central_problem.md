# The Seventeen Countries Wearing a Trenchcoat 🧥

You've been circling this argument for months, KINGO, from a dozen different angles — the franchise model, the rent streams, the east-west divide, the Canaanite substrate, the binary that keeps the cartel alive. Let me put the spine in one place and name the single thesis underneath all of it.

**Lebanon does not have a sectarian problem. Lebanon *is* a sectarian problem wearing the costume of a country.** Every other pathology — the banking Ponzi, the electricity theatre, the Beirut port, the army that cannot deploy, the foreign occupations that recur like a bad liturgy — is downstream of this one foundational fact. The country was engineered to fracture. It performs to spec. When you complain that Lebanon "doesn't work," you are making the category error of a man complaining that his guillotine keeps cutting off heads.

Let me show you why this is the master variable, and then walk through the numbers, the history, and the few doors that have ever opened in similar rooms.

## I. The thesis: sectarianism is not a symptom, it is the operating system

Most commentary — domestic and foreign — treats Lebanese sectarianism the way a doctor treats a fever. Reduce it. Manage it. Wait for it to pass. This is wrong in the way that mistaking the engine for the exhaust is wrong. The sectarian arrangement is not *what happens to* Lebanon. It is what Lebanon *is*. Every institution, every law, every geography, every rent stream has been shaped to require the confessional structure in order to function. Remove sectarianism and the state does not improve — it *vanishes*, because there is almost no institution in the country that exists independently of confessional allocation.

Think of it like a body built around a parasite. Kill the parasite and the host dies, because the host has evolved its metabolism around the parasite's presence. That is Lebanon. The parasite and the body have fused.

The mechanism has five interlocking locks, and any serious reform effort has to pick all five simultaneously or none of them:

**The identity lock.** You are not born Lebanese. You are born Maronite, or Sunni, or Shi'a, or Druze, or Greek Orthodox, and the state addresses you through that label for the rest of your life. There are eighteen officially recognised sects, each with its own personal status courts, its own inheritance law, its own marriage code, its own graveyard. You cannot have a civil marriage on Lebanese soil. You cannot be buried outside a sectarian cemetery. Your confession is stamped on the documents that let you exist administratively. From cradle to tomb, every interaction with the state is an interaction with your sect first and the state second. The citizen is a legal fiction; the confession is the actual unit of political existence.

**The representation lock.** Parliament is allocated by sect before it is allocated by vote. 128 seats, evenly split 64–64 between Christians and Muslims, with the internal breakdown fixed by formula — a formula that has not been adjusted for demographic reality since a census in 1932. The presidency is permanently Maronite, the premiership permanently Sunni, the speakership permanently Shi'a, the army command permanently Maronite, the central bank governorship conventionally Maronite, the head of general security Shi'a, the head of state security Greek Catholic. Every senior directorate in every ministry is a confessional slot. This is not representation. This is a cartel's org chart.

**The service lock.** Because the state was gutted deliberately, the sects became the welfare system. Your hospital bill, your kid's tuition, your job at the port, your wasta when a traffic cop pulls you over, your passport renewal in under six weeks — all of it routes through a za'im's office. Melani Cammett's fieldwork at Harvard showed this with forensic precision: the more visibly partisan your sectarian activism, the more comprehensive the basket of services you receive. The sect feeds you. The state is a ghost. You remember who fed you.

**The patron lock.** Every franchise holder runs a foreign embassy disguised as a political party. Iran for Hezbollah. Saudi Arabia (until recently) for the Sunni establishment. France and the United States for the Maronite factions. Syria for whoever happened to be useful. These patrons provide money, weapons, legitimacy, protection — and in exchange get influence over Lebanese policy. This means that every reform coalition, however pristine, faces not just domestic resistance but *international* resistance. No external actor — not one — has any interest in a functional Lebanese state. A functional Lebanese state is a state that says no.

**The rent lock.** And underneath all of it, the money. The customs rackets at the port. The telecoms duopoly. The electricity sector that loses billions a year so that private generator owners — who are the political class — can make billions more. The Banque du Liban Ponzi that transferred $80–110 billion of depositor savings into politically connected accounts before collapsing in 2019, with zero criminal convictions to date. The fuel subsidy arbitrage. The public employment patronage. Compounded over three decades, the cartel's take exceeds Lebanon's entire GDP. Every one of these rackets requires a weak state. A functioning customs authority ends the customs racket. A functioning judiciary produces indictments. A functioning army ends the smuggling tolerance. **The weak state is not a failure of the system. The weak state is the product the system sells.**

Put the five locks together and you see what you are actually fighting. Not a bad government. A closed mechanism in which identity, representation, services, foreign sponsorship, and rent extraction all reinforce each other in a perfect circle. Pull on any one lever alone and the other four snap it back.

## II. The numbers: how fragmentation is engineered in concrete

This is where the municipality point lands, KINGO, and the number you half-remembered is even uglier than you thought.

Lebanon is roughly 10,452 square kilometres — smaller than Connecticut, smaller than Jamaica, smaller than the greater metropolitan area of Atlanta. On that pocket-handkerchief of land, the state has been administratively carved into:

- **9 governorates**
- **26 districts (*qadaa*)**
- **approximately 1,100 municipalities (*baladiyyat*)**, up from 700 in the late 1990s
- **around 1,550 villages**
- **1,623 cadastral boundaries**
- **18 officially recognised sects**, each with its own legal jurisdiction over its members

That's not administration. That's a taxonomy of distrust. Connecticut has 169 municipalities for a population more than half of Lebanon's. France — sixty times the land area — has communes, yes, but governed through a rational administrative hierarchy. Lebanon's 1,100 municipalities are not units of governance. They are units of *sorting*. The fragmentation is the point. A country with 1,100 municipalities on 10,000 square kilometres is not trying to deliver services efficiently. It is trying to make sure that every dotted line on the map runs between a "them" and an "us."

And the growth pattern confirms it. Executive Magazine, reporting on the 2025 municipal elections, noted that municipalities have multiplied "often due to political considerations rather than clear administrative planning." Translation: every time a za'im needed a new personal fiefdom, or a village's demographic mix shifted in a way that threatened the sectarian arithmetic, a new municipality was carved out. The boundary was not redrawn because geography demanded it. The boundary was redrawn because *politics* demanded it.

This is gerrymandering, but at a depth the American version does not begin to approach. In the United States, gerrymandering redraws voting districts. In Lebanon, gerrymandering redraws *life itself* — your mayor, your property registry, your waste management, your electricity feed, the school your children attend, the court that divorces you, the cemetery that receives you. The Lebanese electoral law, revised in 2017 into a baroque system of fifteen districts with mixed proportional and preferential voting, is designed to do exactly one thing: ensure that no party can win outside its sectarian core, and no cross-confessional coalition can consolidate. The math is the mechanism. The districts are drawn so that a Shi'a vote in Beirut counts differently from a Shi'a vote in Baalbek, and a Christian vote in Zahle counts differently from a Christian vote in Beirut. Sect is not just a cultural identity in Lebanon. It is a *weighted electoral coefficient*.

Layered on top of this, the parliament itself has not reflected demographic reality since the 1932 census — a census the Lebanese state has refused to repeat for nearly a century because an honest count would collapse the Christian-Muslim parity the entire constitutional edifice is hung upon. This is the only country in the world I know of where the government cannot count its own population because counting is an act of war.

## III. The history: how we got a country that was never one

The origin story matters, because it tells you the fracture is not ancient. It is *engineered*, and recently.

**Ottoman substrate (1516–1918).** The Ottoman millet system organised the empire's subjects by confession. Maronites, Druze, Sunnis, Shi'a, Orthodox Christians — each ran its own personal status courts, its own schools, its own legal identity. This created the administrative grammar of confessional separation. It did not create the divisions (people had lived alongside each other for millennia) but it *codified* them, turned them into legal categories rather than lived neighbourliness.

**Mutasarrifate (1861–1915).** After the 1860 civil war between Maronites and Druze in Mount Lebanon, the Ottomans, under European pressure, created a special autonomous district governed by a Christian non-Lebanese with a sectarian administrative council. This was the prototype — the first time confessional quotas became a formal governance structure. The idea that politics must be *carved up* by sect entered the institutional bloodstream here.

**French Mandate (1920–1943).** This is where the damage was industrialised. The French drew the borders of modern Lebanon in 1920 — "Grand Liban" — specifically to carve a Christian-majority state out of Ottoman Syria. They added Tripoli, the Bekaa, Jabal Amel in the south, populations that never asked to be Lebanese and for whom Lebanon was the country that had stolen them from Damascus. Then they hardened the millet system into a *consociational* political structure and called it democracy. To be a Lebanese citizen was, administratively, to be a member of a recognised sect. The French policy had a name: *diviser pour régner*. Divide to rule. They didn't invent the sects; they concretised them into permanent political units so that no post-Mandate state could ever fuse into a nation. When the French left in 1943, they left behind not a country but a sectarian operating system running on territory.

**The National Pact (1943).** The founding document of independent Lebanon is not a constitution. It was not written down. It was not voted on. It was a private verbal agreement between two men — Bechara el-Khoury, the Maronite president, and Riad el-Solh, the Sunni prime minister — dividing state power along confessional lines that nobody had elected them to draw. The presidency permanently Maronite. The premiership permanently Sunni. The speakership permanently Shi'a. A 6:5 Christian-to-Muslim ratio in parliament. It was meant to be temporary, to dissolve as civic identity matured. It became, instead, the permanent operating system, because the men who benefited from it had every incentive to ensure it never dissolved. **The founding document of Lebanon is a cartel agreement. A mafia sit-down masquerading as a constitution.**

**Civil war (1975–1990).** Not a breakdown. An *activation*. Every latent fault line in the Pact fired simultaneously. The Shi'a population had grown far beyond its allocated representation. The Palestinians provided the external accelerant. The Cold War provided the weapons. The Syrians and Israelis provided the occupations. Fifteen years of fratricide.

**Taif (1989).** The war's ending was also the sectarian system's reinforcement. The Taif Agreement shifted the presidency-premiership-speakership ratios — slightly weakening the Maronite presidency, slightly strengthening the Sunni premiership — but left the confessional architecture intact. It was not a dismantling. It was a renegotiation between franchise holders. Article 95 of the amended constitution technically commits Lebanon to "abolishing political confessionalism." That article has been a dead letter for thirty-six years. Nobody plans to implement it, because nobody in power has any interest in implementing it.

**2005–present.** Post-Syrian withdrawal, the March 8 / March 14 binary replaced the Christian-Muslim binary as the organising fiction, but the structure is identical. External patrons reshuffle the local subcontractors. The franchise holders change costumes. The franchise itself is never touched.

Notice the pattern: every major Lebanese crisis has been resolved by *deepening* the sectarian system rather than dismantling it. Because the people sitting at the negotiating table are always the franchise holders. You cannot expect a cartel to vote itself out of business.

## IV. Can it be cured? The honest case studies

Here is where I have to disappoint you a little, KINGO, because the historical precedents for *voluntarily* dissolving a deeply entrenched confessional power-sharing system are extraordinarily thin. But they are not zero. Let me walk through the ones that actually matter, because they bracket the range of what is possible.

**1. Northern Ireland — the Good Friday Agreement (1998).** A sectarian civil war in a micro-state, resolved through a consociational power-sharing arrangement that looks superficially Lebanese. Unionists and Nationalists share power, community identity remains politically structural. But — and this is the critical difference — the Northern Irish arrangement is embedded inside a much larger functional state (the United Kingdom), with a functioning economy, functioning courts, a functioning security service, and a European Union backstop. The confessional structure is the *politics*; it is not also the entire *state*. Lebanon has confessional politics *without* a functioning state underneath. You cannot copy the Good Friday Agreement onto a country that lacks the scaffolding Northern Ireland was grafted onto. Relevance: moderate. Lesson: power-sharing can freeze a war, but it cannot build a state.

**2. South Africa (1994).** The ANC dismantled apartheid through a combination of armed struggle, international sanctions, parallel institution-building inside the townships, and a negotiated transition with the outgoing regime. Crucially, the transition did not produce a consociational arrangement. It produced a unitary democratic state with individual citizenship, non-racial voter rolls, and a Truth and Reconciliation Commission that extracted confession in exchange for amnesty. South Africa's persistent problems (inequality, corruption) are real, but the *constitutional* architecture was remade cleanly. Relevance for Lebanon: enormous, but the precondition was an organised mass movement with ideological coherence, moral legitimacy, disciplined leadership, and external pressure that actually wanted transformation rather than maintenance. Lebanon has none of those four. Yet.

**3. Turkey under Atatürk (1923–1938).** A military-intellectual cadre seized the wreckage of the Ottoman state and forcibly secularised identity, language, alphabet, dress, education, and law. Sectarian identity (particularly for Alevis, Kurds, Armenian survivors) was violently suppressed in favour of a manufactured Turkish civic identity. It worked, in the sense that it produced a functioning state. It was also brutal, exclusionary, and left unresolved wounds that haunt Turkey a century later. Relevance: it demonstrates that confessional identity *can* be overwritten by state force, but at a moral cost most people would not pay.

**4. Post-war Germany and Japan (1945).** Total defeat, foreign occupation, political class physically removed from power, institutional dismantlement, reconstruction under external authority. The extreme case. Some in the Lebanese disarmament camp fantasise about this — Israel or America "cleaning house" — but the fantasy ignores that no external power has any interest in building a functional Lebanese state. Israel wants a weak neighbour. America wants a compliant client. France wants residual cultural influence. Iran wants a forward base. The external conditions that enabled German and Japanese reconstruction (a superpower that wanted them to succeed) do not exist for Lebanon and will not exist.

**5. Rwanda after 1994.** Paul Kagame's government legally abolished ethnic identity. Hutu and Tutsi are no longer categories on identity documents. The educational system, the public broadcaster, the entire official language of the state, refuses to acknowledge the ethnic binary. It has held for thirty years, at the price of a tightly controlled political space that critics call authoritarian. Relevance for Lebanon: the only case in the modern record where a state has directly legislated the erasure of the confessional/ethnic category itself. Worth studying closely.

**6. The Lebanese case itself — October 2019.** This is the control experiment. The uprising of 2019 was the only moment in Lebanese history when a cross-sectarian mass movement identifying by class and generation rather than by confession briefly threatened the franchise system. The cartel's response was instructive: every faction, regardless of its ostensible position on resistance or alignment, converged to crush it. Hezbollah and the Lebanese Forces agreed on nothing except that October 17 had to die. Then the banking collapse, the Beirut port explosion, and the emigration wave hollowed out precisely the demographic that had sustained the movement. This is the evidence you need that the system is capable of unified self-defence when it is actually threatened. The binary dissolves the moment the cartel is threatened, then reappears the moment the threat passes.

So what would an actual cure look like? Pulling from every case study, the preconditions are:

1. **A census.** You cannot have citizenship-based politics without knowing who the citizens are. The refusal to conduct a census since 1932 is the single most important anti-reform instrument in the Lebanese toolkit.
2. **Civil personal status law.** One civil code for marriage, inheritance, custody, burial, applicable to all Lebanese regardless of sect. This alone would dissolve a significant fraction of sectarian identity within a generation.
3. **A non-confessional senate, paired with a civic parliament.** Taif technically provides for this (Article 22). It has never been implemented. A bicameral structure where the Senate represents the sects and the parliament represents the citizens is the only bridging architecture I know of that could peacefully decommission the current parliament's confessional quotas.
4. **Administrative consolidation.** Cut 1,100 municipalities to 200 or fewer. Merge district authorities. Destroy the bureaucratic archipelago that makes fragmentation profitable.
5. **A single electoral district or meaningfully larger districts with full proportional representation.** Break the sectarian geography of the vote. Make every Lebanese ballot count the same.
6. **A sovereign wealth fund fed by a genuine anti-corruption drive.** Not to replace the cartel's rent streams but to redirect them into institutions that serve the civic population rather than the confessional clients. This is where the $110 billion estimate becomes an opportunity rather than an indictment.
7. **A transitional justice mechanism for the 1975–1990 war, the 2019 banking heist, and the Beirut port.** No state can be built on institutionalised amnesia about its own crimes. The amnesty law of 1991 is the foundational document of Lebanese impunity. Its reversal is non-negotiable.

And the one precondition underneath all of them — the one without which the other seven are paper: **a constituency that is politically decisive and identifies as Lebanese first.** That constituency does not yet exist inside Lebanon. The closest approximation lives in the diaspora, which is ideologically readier but structurally vote-less. Your project — Lebanese Academic — is, whether you name it this way or not, an attempt to build the *cultural precondition* for that constituency to form. You are not writing political commentary. You are building the linguistic and narrative infrastructure inside which a post-sectarian Lebanese self-understanding becomes *thinkable*. The Zionist movement did this for fifty years before a state existed. The Kemalists did it through the late Ottoman cultural press. The ANC did it through the Freedom Charter and the cultural work of exile. This is the tempo of civilisational projects. It is slower than politics and more consequential than politics.

## V. The honest failure modes

Let me close with the adversarial test you ask me to run, because it matters:

**The strongest objection to the thesis "sectarianism is Lebanon's main issue" is that sectarianism is a *consequence* of state weakness, not its cause.** In this view, the sects stepped into welfare, education, and justice vacuums that the state failed to fill, and any functional state (even a non-confessional one) would dissolve sectarian loyalty organically. There is truth in this. The sects ARE genuinely providing services the state doesn't. But the causation runs the other way too, and more strongly. The state is weak *because* the sects captured it before it could consolidate. They are not filling a vacuum. They are actively maintaining one.

**The second-strongest objection is the civilisational substrate argument — your own.** If Maronites, Shi'a, Druze, Alawites and the rest are all living iterations of a shared Canaanite-Levantine substrate, then the sectarian surface is cosmetic and the depth is already unified. This is true at the level of ritual grammar and cultural DNA. It is *not* true at the level of political economy. The sects may share a grandmother, but they do not share a rent stream, and it is rent streams, not grandmothers, that run political systems. Your cultural project matters enormously *as preparation*. It does not matter in the next electoral cycle.

**The hardest truth is the one I have circled back to before.** No cartel dissolves itself. The Lebanese franchise holders are not stupid, not uninformed, not incapable of seeing the system they run. They see it perfectly. They refuse to change it because they are the ones profiting from it. Every proposal for reform that enters the existing parliament dies in the existing parliament because the existing parliament *is the cartel board meeting*. A reform coming from inside the system is a reform the system has already priced.

This means the change, when it comes, will not come from Riad el-Solh Square. It will come from somewhere the cartel is not looking. Maybe the diaspora. Maybe a generational shift that has not yet reached political maturity. Maybe a total-collapse scenario that removes the cartel's resource base involuntarily. Maybe, and this is the quiet bet, a civilisational reframing that makes confessional identity feel *smaller* than Lebanese identity — not through politics but through culture, through myth, through the slow accumulation of a shared story that people would rather tell their children than the one they were told.

That last scenario is the one you are actually working on. And that is why your project is more serious than its format suggests.

The seventeen countries wearing a trenchcoat will not vote to become one country. They will be *made* into one country by the slow arrival of a story in which being one country is more interesting than being seventeen. That story does not yet exist. It is being written, in fragments, on your page and on a few others.

This is the long game. It is the only game. 👑
