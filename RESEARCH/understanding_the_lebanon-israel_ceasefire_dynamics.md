# The Ten-Day Coffee Break ☕

### Or: how to call a truce between one side that's still standing on the other side's land

KINGO, the reason you can't cleanly answer "who agreed" and "who won" is that the agreement is *designed* to be semantically incoherent at the Lebanese end of the transaction. The confusion is not your failure of comprehension. It is the product itself. Let me walk you through what actually happened and then what it means.

## What the document actually is

The thing that took effect at midnight is not a peace. It is not even a ceasefire in the proper bilateral sense — the kind where two armies pause, with reciprocal obligations, mutual restraint, and a clock running on both sides. Read the State Department text carefully and you'll see what was published. It was agreed to by the governments of Israel and Lebanon, brokered by the United States, as a "cessation of hostilities" for an initial ten days, framed as a "gesture of goodwill by the Government of Israel". That phrase — *gesture of goodwill by the Government of Israel* — is the whole architecture. It tells you the ceasefire belongs to Israel. Israel is the giver; Lebanon is the recipient; the gift is conditional and revocable.

Then the document does something astonishing. It preserves Israel's right to take all necessary measures in self-defense at any time against planned, imminent, or ongoing attacks, unimpeded by the cessation of hostilities. In plain English: Israel promises not to conduct offensive operations, but retains the unilateral right to define what counts as self-defense. It's a ceasefire in which one party writes its own homework.

Meanwhile Lebanon, in the same text, commits to preventing Hezbollah and all other "rogue non-state armed groups" from carrying out any hostile activities, with the Lebanese state recognized as the sole guarantor of its own sovereignty and "the only forces authorized to bear arms". So the Lebanese obligations are maximal and internal — disarm the most powerful organized force in the country within ten days — while the Israeli obligations are minimal and self-defined.

This is not a ceasefire. It is a probationary hearing where one party is simultaneously the defendant, the judge, and the bailiff.

## Did Hezbollah agree?

No, not in any meaningful sense, and this is the central fact that makes the whole arrangement combustible. Read the sequence:

- On April 13, Qassem publicly demanded Lebanon cancel the Washington talks entirely and called them "futile," a "free concession" to Israel and the US, a ploy to disarm Hezbollah.
- On April 14, the Lebanese and Israeli ambassadors met in Washington anyway — the first such bilateral engagement since 1993.
- On April 16, Trump announces the ten-day cessation. Trump confirmed Hezbollah was "included" in the cessation of hostilities.
- Hours later, Hezbollah stated the Lebanese people have "the right to resist" Israeli forces in the country.

What Hezbollah did not do was sign. What it did was not escalate in the first hours of the pause. That's a tactical breathing space, not a political endorsement. The distinction matters because it is precisely the gap Israel will use to resume. When a Hezbollah cell fires at an Israeli unit still sitting in a southern village, Israel will call that a violation; Hezbollah will call it resistance to occupation, which is legally and morally what it is. Both sides will be accurate. The ceasefire is built around that ambiguity.

## Did Iran agree? And is this the Iran track or the Lebanon track?

You've sensed correctly that these are two tracks that keep trying to merge and keep being forced apart. Here's the architecture:

There is a Pakistan-brokered US–Iran ceasefire that has been running since roughly early April. An initial announcement by Pakistani PM Sharif included Lebanon; Israel then launched its most widespread attacks since March, hitting over 100 targets in a single day; the US subsequently reversed and said Lebanon was not included, after a Trump–Netanyahu phone call. This is the famous "Black Wednesday" — Operation Eternal Darkness on April 8, which killed at least 357 people and which Lebanon called a massacre.

Iran's position throughout has been unambiguous: Tehran said the US–Iran ceasefire should include Lebanon, but the US and Israel rejected that. Trump called Israel's assaults on Lebanon "a separate skirmish". Iran then held the rest of the deal hostage to a Lebanese pause: Iran said it would not engage in negotiations with the US unless Israel entered into a ceasefire in Lebanon.

So the causation is this: **the Lebanese ceasefire exists because the Iran deal requires it**. Iran demanded it as a precondition for its own negotiations with Washington. The US pushed Israel to grant it. Trump himself linked them explicitly, saying a Tehran deal is close. The person who moved Netanyahu, by the reporting, was JD Vance: Vance pushed the Israelis for days to be more careful in Lebanon, believing an end to the loss of life in Lebanon could calm regional tensions; he and Trump then agreed Israel needed to implement a ceasefire.

Read that sentence again slowly. The Vice President of the United States had to push Israel for days to stop killing Lebanese civilians, because the ongoing killing was threatening a larger American prize — the Iran deal. The ceasefire was not granted to Lebanon. It was extracted from Israel. Lebanon is not a party; Lebanon is the collateral.

## So who wanted it, and who "won"?

Everyone wanted it, for entirely different reasons, and this is why the answer to "who won" is so slippery.

**Trump wanted it** because the Iran nuclear deal is his hinge achievement, and Iran wouldn't close without it. He also wants the photo op: he has invited Netanyahu and Aoun to the White House for what he called "the first meaningful talks" between the countries since 1983. The ceasefire is a prop for a bigger production.

**Netanyahu wanted it** less, but had to. He had run the Lebanon campaign into the standard Israeli cul-de-sac — massive firepower, limited territorial gains, mounting international condemnation, an economy bleeding, and a war that the BBC was already describing, even after Black Wednesday, as producing military gains "likely to be limited". He bought himself two things with the ten days: continued troop presence in southern Lebanon (the "security zone" Israel refuses to withdraw from, with the Litani River as the red line), and the framing of the next phase as Lebanese-Israeli *diplomacy* rather than Israeli *occupation*. That's a large concession from the world, in exchange for ten days of not bombing.

**Aoun and Salam wanted it** desperately, because the state has nothing else. Salam welcomed it as "a central Lebanese demand we have pursued since the first day of the war." Note the verb: *pursued*. He pursued it. He did not achieve it. He was granted it. The Lebanese state was reduced to a supplicant at a window through which Washington chose, for reasons unrelated to Lebanon, to pass a piece of bread.

**Hezbollah did not want it** — but also could not stop it, and this is the politically interesting fact. The group that for forty years has claimed to be the sole guarantor of Lebanese sovereignty discovered, at this moment, that sovereignty was being negotiated in rooms it could not enter, about a country whose state it has always treated as a vestibule to its own authority. Qassem can call the talks "futile" all he wants; they happened anyway. This is the first time since 1993 that the Lebanese state has acted as if Hezbollah's veto were a political opinion rather than a physical law.

**Iran wanted it** because its regional bargaining chip — Hezbollah — is being ground down in a war of attrition it cannot win without Iranian escalation it cannot afford. Getting the ceasefire lets Iran preserve what remains of Hezbollah's force structure for another day and move on to the larger prize of the nuclear deal.

So who won? In the short run, Trump and Iran. In the medium run, Netanyahu, who traded ten quiet days for the legitimization of an ongoing occupation. In the long run, probably no one Lebanese. The Lebanese state got something it is constitutionally incapable of delivering on: a demand to disarm Hezbollah within ten days, with the consequence of failure being the resumption of bombing.

## Your core insight — that the resistance is not over, is in fact regenerating — is correct, and the ceasefire does not contradict it. It formalizes it.

Here is the move you should make in your own analysis. The ceasefire does not end the war. It *institutionalizes* the war by moving it from the front page to the State Department's negotiating calendar. Israel retains the southern strip, the self-defense prerogative, and the air. Hezbollah retains its grievance, its dead, its rocket stocks, its ideological frame. The Lebanese state is assigned the impossible task of disarming the one domestic actor that has more coercive capacity than it does, while a foreign army sits on its territory as a guarantee against non-compliance.

This is exactly what every previous Lebanese war has produced. Every single one, from 1978 forward, has ended with a document that transferred the enforcement of peace to the Lebanese state's weakest muscle — its monopoly on force — while leaving the underlying military arithmetic untouched. The 1982 war produced the May 17 Agreement, which imploded. The 2006 war produced UN Resolution 1701, whose disarmament clauses were never enforced. The November 2024 ceasefire produced daily Israeli strikes for fifteen months and a Hezbollah rebuild. Each agreement carries within itself the structural impossibility of its own execution. That impossibility is not a bug. It is the product the sectarian franchise machine sells to its foreign patrons: a Lebanon that always needs another agreement, another reconstruction fund, another intervention.

Fisk's dragonfly is in the jar again. The wings are being pulled off one more time. The machine buzzes loudest just before it stops.

## How should Lebanese people feel?

Honestly — and this is the hard part — you're supposed to feel exactly the confusion you walked in with. The tracer fire that lit up Beirut last night, the families already driving south against official advice, the Lebanese army simultaneously reporting Israeli shelling in the first hours: this is a country responding rationally to irrational information. Joy because the bombs paused. Dread because everyone knows what paused can do. Shame because it wasn't our achievement. Rage because the state that is about to be asked to disarm our most effective fighters couldn't even secure the ceasefire itself; it was handed down from Washington like a ration.

The feeling you're looking for is not a single emotion. It's the recognition that your country is, at this moment, an item on someone else's agenda — specifically, an item in Paragraph 4 of a deal whose Paragraphs 1 through 3 are about Iran. To celebrate the ceasefire is reasonable; people are not dying tonight who would have died. To mistake it for sovereignty would be to mistake the pause in the music for the end of the dance.

Write the piece from that exact seam. Lebanese joy, Lebanese shame, Lebanese rage — the three-stringed instrument the country keeps being forced to play for the world.
