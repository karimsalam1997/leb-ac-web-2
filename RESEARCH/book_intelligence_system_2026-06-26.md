## What This Library Is For

This library should do three jobs.

First, it should make your writing harder to flatten. If an essay says "the sectarian system failed," the books should force the better sentence: who built the system, who profited from it, who survived by joining it, and who paid for it in Basta, Tyre, Achrafieh, Haret Hreik, Marjayoun, or Sakiet el-Janzeer.

Second, it should give Lebanese Academic a source spine. The project already has voice, anger, memory, and instinct. These books give it scaffolding: Fawwaz Traboulsi for history, Ussama Makdisi for sectarian formation, Maya Mikdashi for sovereignty and law, Noura Erakat for Palestine and legality, Rashid Khalidi for the long Palestinian war, Robert Fisk for Lebanon under fire, Joseph Daher for Hezbollah as political economy, and Mehdi Amel for the deepest structural argument.

Third, it should train your attention. Intelligence here does not mean memorizing more names. It means learning how one problem changes shape when read through class, sect, empire, law, war, land, finance, and memory.

## The Book To Essay Method

Every time a book enters the project, extract only four things.

1. The one sentence that changes the argument
2. The one historical scene worth retelling
3. The one concept that can be translated into plain English
4. The one current Lebanese problem it makes sharper

Do not write normal book summaries. They are too sleepy. Turn each book into hinges.

Example:

- Book: `The Culture of Sectarianism`
- Sentence that changes the argument: sectarianism has a modern political history, it is not a fossil.
- Scene worth retelling: Mount Lebanon, 1860, as a crisis that modern powers and local elites made into a new political order.
- Concept in plain English: communities become political machines when power rewards them for acting as communities.
- Current problem sharpened: the census is dangerous because numbers would expose the machinery.

## The Intelligence Drills

### Drill 1: Same Problem, Five Books

Pick one Lebanese problem, then force five books to answer it.

Example problem: why can't Lebanon build a real state?

- Traboulsi answers through political history.
- Makdisi answers through sectarian formation.
- Amel answers through class power.
- Leenders answers through corruption and postwar state-building.
- Mikdashi answers through sovereignty, law, and the intimate state.

Then write the Lebanese Academic version in one paragraph.

### Drill 2: Argument Upgrade

Take an existing essay sentence and make it pass through one book.

Weak sentence:

Lebanon's sectarian leaders benefit from state weakness.

Book-upgraded sentence:

In Beirut after Taif, state weakness was not an accident waiting to be repaired by better men. It was the marketplace where the postwar bosses, bankers, contractors, militias, and ministers learned to sell protection back to the citizens whose state they had already hollowed out.

### Drill 3: The Opposing Argument First

Before any argument on Hezbollah, Palestine, Israel, sectarianism, or sovereignty, write the strongest opposing version in two plain sentences.

Example:

Israeli civilians in Kiryat Shmona are real people, and no serious writer should mock fear when rockets are falling. But fear does not make the Israeli military's destruction of southern Lebanese villages moral, lawful, or clean.

Then continue.
