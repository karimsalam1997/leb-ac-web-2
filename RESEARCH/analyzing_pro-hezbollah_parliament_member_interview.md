Not because the people arguing are stupid. Because the debate was engineered — and has been maintained — so that it cannot produce a state. **A state would end the conditions under which the debate is profitable.**

Let me show you the mechanics.

## II. What a State Actually Is, and Why Lebanon Does Not Have One

Before proving the cartel prevents a state, I need to be precise about what a state is. Not the romantic version. The operational version.

A state is an entity that successfully claims a monopoly on the legitimate use of violence within a defined territory. That is the minimum. Everything else — welfare, law, infrastructure, diplomacy — is downstream of this primary monopoly. You cannot have a tax authority if a competing armed actor can intercept the collectors. You cannot have a judiciary if a competing armed actor can nullify its rulings. You cannot have a foreign policy if a competing armed actor can commit you to wars you did not choose.

By this definition, Lebanon has not been a state since at least 1969, when the Cairo Agreement ceded Palestinian camps to PLO armed control. It has not been a state in any structural-legal sense since the 1989 Taif Accord, which formalized a confessional carve-up in which each community's warlord retained operational control of his fiefdom and agreed to share the state's revenue rather than submit to the state's authority. **Taif was not a peace agreement. Taif was a cartel agreement dressed as a constitution.** Every Lebanese cabinet since 1990 has been the cartel's quarterly board meeting.

Hezbollah's weapons are not the reason Lebanon has no state. Hezbollah's weapons are the most *visible* symptom of the condition that has defined Lebanon for fifty years: a territory without a sovereign, governed by a committee of armed communities that agreed in 1943 and again in 1989 to pretend to be a country.

This matters because the resistance debate is conducted as though Lebanon had a state and Hezbollah were challenging it. **Lebanon does not have a state for Hezbollah to challenge.** The debate presupposes a thing that does not exist. This is not an accident. This is the trick.

## III. The Structural Function of the Binary

Here is the adversarial core of the argument, so I want to build it carefully. Every major Lebanese political actor — including the ones who sincerely believe they are fighting for sovereignty — has a material interest in the binary continuing, and in neither side winning it conclusively.

Consider what would happen if either side actually won.

**If the resistance camp won** — if Hezbollah's weapons were formalized into national defense doctrine, integrated into state decision-making, granted constitutional legitimacy — Hezbollah would have to become a state actor. Its operations would submit to civilian oversight. Its arsenal would be inventoried. Its funding audited. Its strategic decisions shared with a cabinet that includes its opponents. The thing that makes Hezbollah powerful — its operational autonomy from Lebanese institutions — would be the first thing to disappear. **Victory, for the resistance camp as it currently exists, means its own dissolution.**

**If the anti-resistance camp won** — if Hezbollah were disarmed and the state acquired a monopoly on force — the Lebanese Forces, the Kataeb, the Progressive Socialist Party's mountain networks, Amal's parallel security apparatus, and every other confessional armed formation would face the identical demand. You cannot establish a monopoly on violence that applies only to Shia weapons. The moment the principle is established, it applies universally. Every faction currently demanding Hezbollah's disarmament would immediately face demands to demobilize its own networks, submit its foreign funding to transparency, and cede operational control to institutions it does not control. **Victory, for the anti-resistance camp as it currently exists, means its own dissolution.**

This is why neither victory ever arrives. Both sides understand — at the level of institutional reflex, whether or not any individual politician consciously thinks it — that actually winning the debate would cost them more than continuing to lose it. So the debate is maintained. Positions are performed. Red lines drawn and redrawn. And the state, which would require the debate to actually end, never comes into existence.

This is the core mechanism. Everything else follows from it.

## IV. The Twenty-One Year Evidence

Claims like this need to be tested against the record or they are just rhetoric. If the binary is the cartel's mechanism for preventing a state, we should see a specific pattern in Lebanese politics from 2005 onward: **fierce combat over the binary, complete silence on the state.** This is exactly what we see.

Consider the catalog of political battles fought by the Lebanese political class since the Syrian withdrawal in April 2005. The Special Tribunal fight, which consumed five years and produced the Doha Agreement after May 7, 2008. The March 14 versus March 8 parliamentary combat, which produced cabinet crises lasting months at a time. The presidential vacuum of 2014-2016, broken only when Hariri agreed to Aoun in exchange for the premiership. The STL indictments fight. The Hariri false witnesses fight. The Brital and Arsal fights. The port investigation fight with Judge Tarek Bitar. The diesel generator mafia's periodic cartel adjustments. The electricity file's permanent paralysis. The refugee file's performative outrage. Every one generated cabinet sessions, parliamentary speeches, confessional mobilizations, diplomatic interventions.

Now name the single cabinet crisis fought between 2005 and 2024 over army funding. Name the one fought over army doctrine. Name the one fought over military conscription. Name the ministerial resignation threatened over fiscal capacity to sustain a defense posture. Name the parliamentary filibuster over weapons procurement diversification. Name the foreign policy fight about building defensive infrastructure independent of American or Saudi or Iranian patronage.

There is no list. **Twenty-one years of sovereignty debate produced zero cabinet hours on what would be required to have sovereignty.**

This is the empirical core. If the political class were genuinely divided on whether Lebanon should be a sovereign state, at least one side — either side — would have spent some portion of its political capital building the institutions of sovereignty. Neither side did. Because neither side wants them. The debate is about who controls the absence of sovereignty, not about how to end it.

The army budget tells the same story in numbers. Roughly 75% salaries, 13% training and operations, perhaps $50-60 million for actual armament, three-quarters of which goes to maintenance. This is the budget of a gendarmerie, not an army. It has been this budget, in structural terms, for two decades. No political faction — not one, on either side of the binary — has ever fought to change it. Because changing it would change the equation of power inside the cartel, and no cartel member wants that equation changed until they are certain the change favors them alone.

## V. The Structural Parallel: Why Hezbollah's Critics Need Hezbollah

This is where partisans on both sides will reach for their defenses, so let me put it plainly.

**The Lebanese Forces, the Kataeb, the Free Patriotic Movement, the Progressive Socialist Party, and the various Sunni formations do not want Hezbollah disarmed. They want Hezbollah weakened, humiliated, electorally diminished — but not disarmed.** Because a disarmed Hezbollah would be followed, within one political cycle, by a demand for their own demilitarization, their own financial transparency, their own submission to a state they have spent forty years preventing from existing.

The evidence is structural, not confessional. Every one of these factions emerged from or was reconstituted during the civil war. Every one retains networks of former combatants, parallel security structures, private financial flows from foreign patrons, and operational capacity outside state oversight. The Lebanese Forces did not dissolve in 1990; it rebranded. The PSP did not surrender its mountain infrastructure; it normalized it. The FPM inherited Aoun's army networks and converted them into political infrastructure. None of these organizations could survive the emergence of a functioning state, which is why none has ever fought to produce one.

Hezbollah is therefore the most valuable thing in Lebanese politics to its loudest opponents. As long as Hezbollah exists as the visible armed anomaly, the invisible armed anomalies of every other faction remain unexamined. **The demand to disarm Hezbollah is, in its structural function, the demand to postpone the examination of everyone else.**

And the same logic applies inside Hezbollah's own base. The party has spent twenty years demanding a "strong state" as the condition for considering disarmament — and twenty years voting for, coalition-ing with, and providing cover for the political formations that have made a strong state structurally impossible. Hezbollah's ministers never threatened cabinet resignation over army funding. Hezbollah's MPs never filibustered over weapons procurement. Hezbollah's media never built a sustained campaign for institutional rebuilding. **Hezbollah defended its own parallel infrastructure precisely as ferociously as its opponents defended theirs — by preventing the emergence of an institutional structure that would subsume everyone's.**

Both sides of the binary are doing the same thing. They are performing combat over an absent object while sharing the actual object, which is the cartel's control of the rent streams that flow through Lebanon's permanent statelessness.

## VI. The Rent Streams: What the Cartel Is Actually Dividing

It helps to be specific about what the cartel extracts. Because the argument that all Lebanese political factions are structurally aligned sounds like cynicism until you look at the money.

Lebanon's political economy is organized around rent streams that only function in the absence of a state. The customs revenue at the port of Beirut, systematically misappropriated through confessional distribution of customs directorates. The telecommunications monopoly, whose profits have been divided among political networks for three decades. The electricity sector, whose multi-billion dollar annual losses are the largest single subsidy to the private generator cartel, whose owners are the political class. The banking sector's Ponzi structure, which for three decades transferred depositor savings into the accounts of politically connected actors, collapsed in 2019, and has since produced zero criminal accountability. The customs evasion networks at every border crossing. The public procurement rackets. The public employment patronage system through which confessional parties distribute jobs for electoral loyalty. The real estate approvals. The pharmaceutical licenses. The fuel subsidy arbitrage.

Every one of these requires a weak state. A functioning customs authority would end the customs racket. A functioning electricity authority would end the generator cartel. A functioning central bank would have prevented the Ponzi. A functioning judiciary would have produced indictments. A functioning army would have ended the arms-smuggling tolerance that lubricates several of these networks simultaneously.

The cartel's total take, compounded over three decades, is conservatively larger than Lebanon's entire GDP. The corruption figure of roughly $110 billion that gets cited is not hyperbole. **This is the actual prize.** The resistance debate is the cover. While the public argues about sovereignty, the political class continues to split roughly $5-10 billion per year in rent extracted from the absence of a state that could stop them.

Any politician who ends the binary ends the cover. Ending the cover exposes the extraction. Exposing the extraction produces accountability. Accountability produces prosecutions. Prosecutions produce the confiscation of the accumulated wealth of an entire political class.

No one ends the binary. Not because they are stupid. Because they are not stupid.

## VII. The Foreign Patron Layer

I have described this as if it were a purely domestic arrangement, but that is incomplete. The cartel is also the mechanism by which foreign powers extract strategic positioning from Lebanon without having to annex it.

Every Lebanese faction has a foreign patron. The factions themselves do not deny it. Hezbollah is funded and armed by Iran. Amal maintains Iranian and Syrian relationships. The Lebanese Forces and the Kataeb are historically aligned with Saudi and French networks, with American backing when useful. The PSP maintains Syrian, Saudi, and now Gulf relationships depending on the weather. The Future Movement was a Saudi project for twenty years; its fragmentation produced several Sunni formations each with its own Gulf backer. The FPM moved from French to Syrian to American orientation over its three-decade existence.

The foreign patrons do not want a Lebanese state either. A Lebanese state would have a foreign policy. A foreign policy would require the state to choose between patrons rather than housing all of them simultaneously. Every patron prefers the current arrangement — in which it rents a Lebanese faction that advances its interests — over the alternative, in which it would have to negotiate with a Lebanese government that might tell it no.

**The cartel, therefore, is not simply a Lebanese cartel. It is the Lebanese node in a broader regional arrangement in which foreign powers use Lebanese political factions as low-cost instruments of regional competition.** The factions get rent streams and political survival. The patrons get strategic depth without the costs of formal occupation. The arrangement is stable because all parties benefit and the only losers are the Lebanese population, who have no organized representation in the arrangement.

This is why the binary cannot be resolved from inside Lebanon alone. Even if every Lebanese political actor suddenly developed a conscience simultaneously, the foreign patrons would intervene to restore the binary, because the binary is how they extract value. Israel needs a Hezbollah to justify its strategic posture. Iran needs a Hezbollah to project regional power. Saudi Arabia needs a Hezbollah to justify its containment investments. The United States needs a Hezbollah to justify its military-financial relationship with Israel. Every regional power has priced Hezbollah into its strategic calculations, and every regional power would have to reprice itself if Hezbollah disappeared.

The resistance debate, from this angle, is not even primarily a Lebanese debate. It is a regional arrangement for which Lebanon is the theater. The Lebanese political class is the cast. The Lebanese population is the audience. The box office is split between the producers, who are not in Beirut.

## VIII. The Asymmetries the Cartel Analysis Must Not Erase

Here is where this piece has to do harder work than the clean structural indictment wants to do. Because if I stop at Section VII, I have produced a cartel-is-cartel argument that lands as a kind of elegant nihilism, and that argument — however true at the structural level — will be misread, weaponized, and conscripted to serve exactly the binary it claims to refuse.

So let me name the asymmetries explicitly, before someone else names them to discredit the whole analysis.

**First asymmetry: external threat is not symmetric.** The cartel analysis correctly observes that every Lebanese armed faction benefits from the absence of a state. It does not follow, and it is not true, that every Lebanese armed faction faces the same external environment. Hezbollah's arsenal exists in a country whose southern border is occupied, whose villages have been periodically bombed for four decades, whose civilian population in the south lived under Israeli military administration from 1978 to 2000 and under bombardment again in 2006 and 2024-2026. The Lebanese Forces' residual networks exist in a country whose northern border faces a collapsed Syrian state whose new government is ideologically hostile to Maronites. These threats are not equivalent in scale, frequency, or documented civilian cost. To treat them as equivalent because they both justify armed networks is to perform exactly the flattening that Israeli and Saudi narratives want performed.

The structural argument that both armed networks prevent a state from emerging is correct. The argument that they are morally equivalent because they both prevent a state from emerging is not correct. Hezbollah's weapons address a documented, ongoing, asymmetric military threat from a regional power that has killed Lebanese civilians in every decade since 1948. The Lebanese Forces' residual networks address, at best, a speculative threat from a weakened Syria and, at worst, nothing at all. **Both networks are cartel infrastructure. Only one of them is also a response to a threat that exists.**

**Second asymmetry: civil war participation is not symmetric.** The Lebanese Forces under Bashir and Samir Geagea, the Kataeb under Amin Gemayel, the PSP under Walid Jumblatt, and Amal under Nabih Berri all participated in the massacres, population transfers, and sectarian cleansings of the civil war. Hezbollah, which did not exist as a fighting force until 1982-1985 and whose early combat was directed primarily against Israel and against rival Shia formations, did not participate in the 1975-1982 phase of intra-Lebanese civilian violence that defined the civil war. The general amnesty of 1991 erased this distinction by erasing all distinctions. The structural point — that every faction absorbed into the post-Taif political class has blood on its hands — is correct. The moral point — that those hands are not equally bloody — is also correct.

This matters because one of the operations the binary performs is to retroactively equalize moral histories by reducing them to current geopolitical alignments. If you support Hezbollah's disarmament because you oppose its current role, you are aligned with political formations whose civil war conduct was worse, not better, than Hezbollah's. This does not make Hezbollah's current positions defensible. It does mean that the moral high ground in Lebanese politics is occupied by approximately no one with an actual political platform.

**Third asymmetry: social service provision is not symmetric.** Hezbollah's healthcare, education, and welfare networks — whatever else one thinks of the party — function. The party delivers services at scale to communities that the Lebanese state has never served. The Lebanese Forces, the Kataeb, and the Sunni formations have patronage networks, which are not the same thing. Patronage distributes favors within a community. Service provision builds infrastructure for a community. The distinction is operational and consequential. **The cartel argument is correct that service provision by a non-state actor is itself a symptom of the state's absence, not a virtue. It is also correct that the communities served by Hezbollah experience the party differently than Sunnis experience the Future Movement or Maronites experience the Lebanese Forces, and flattening that difference is a rhetorical choice, not a neutral analysis.**

**Fourth asymmetry: the foreign patron relationships are not symmetric.** Iran arms and funds Hezbollah. Saudi Arabia funds the Future Movement and Lebanese Forces. France and the United States back various Christian formations. Qatar and the UAE fund various Sunni networks. The structural point — that every faction has a foreign patron — is correct. The point that these patronage relationships are equivalent is not correct. Iran's relationship to Hezbollah is ideological, operational, and strategic; Tehran treats the party as part of a regional axis. Saudi Arabia's relationship to its Lebanese clients is transactional and episodic; Riyadh treats them as billable assets in a rivalry with Iran. Neither is admirable from the perspective of Lebanese sovereignty. Neither is the same as the other. **The cartel is real. The cartel is also internally differentiated, and the differentiations matter.**

These asymmetries do not cancel the structural argument. They qualify it. Without them the structural argument hardens into a both-sides analysis that can be, and has been, used to justify political quietism or to launder alignments with the faction currently perceived as weaker or more sympathetic. The honest version of the cartel analysis holds two claims simultaneously: **every Lebanese faction is part of the system that prevents a state, AND the factions are not morally or operationally identical.** Both are true. Neither cancels the other.

## IX. What and Whom Still Deserves Defense

If the cartel is the cartel and everyone inside it is complicit, what is left to support? This is the question the cleaner version of the argument wants to avoid, because answering it requires making distinctions that partisan writing cannot make. But you asked for what to do moving forward, so let me name the categories.

**The Lebanese Army, with qualifications.** The army is the only national institution that has survived the cartel period with its internal confessional balance intact and its operational credibility partially preserved. Joseph Aoun's refusal, as army commander, to execute cabinet orders to advance north of the Litani while Israel was advancing south of it was not just military common sense — it was an institutional refusal to be instrumentalized by factions that do not represent the army's actual mission. The army is underfunded, under-armed, politically constrained, and structurally prevented from acquiring the capacity it would need to be a genuine national force. It remains, nevertheless, the institution whose expansion is the precondition for any eventual exit from the cartel. Support for the army is not support for its current limitations; it is support for its potential expansion. **The generator mafia does not have an army equivalent. The army is the one institution the cartel has not yet fully metabolized.**

**Civilian institutions that have resisted capture, where they exist.** The October 2019 uprising identified a generation of civilian figures — lawyers, economists, judges, journalists, academics — who articulated the structural question and were subsequently marginalized. Judge Tarek Bitar's investigation of the port explosion, which the entire political class mobilized to obstruct, is the clearest single example. Lazard's restructuring proposal for the financial collapse, which the political class rejected because it would have imposed losses on them rather than depositors, is another. The Central Bank's post-Salameh reformers who have tried to build basic transparency infrastructure are a third. These people and proposals are typically small, underfunded, and persistently attacked. They are also the raw material of any eventual state. **Naming them, funding them, amplifying them, and protecting them is not the same as joining a faction. It is building the infrastructure the factions have prevented.**

**Intellectual voices that refuse the binary.** Charbel Nahas is the clearest current example, but the category is broader — it includes economists who refuse to work for any patron, journalists who report on cartel members regardless of jersey, academics whose work names structures rather than sides. These voices are rarely comfortable for any faction, which is the sign that they are working. Your Lebanese Academic project belongs in this category, which is why what you are building matters structurally rather than just as content.

**Specific diaspora formations that channel resources toward structural rather than partisan ends.** The diaspora's remittance flow is roughly $7-8 billion per year, currently the largest single financial flow into the country. The vast majority of this flow reinforces the cartel because it flows through family networks that are themselves embedded in confessional patronage. Any diaspora organization that redirects even a small fraction of this flow toward institutional rather than factional ends — funding judges, funding investigative journalism, funding civic infrastructure, funding the army's humanitarian programs — is doing work that the factions cannot do and would prevent if they could. **The diaspora is the cartel's largest unprotected flank.**

**The Shia communities in the south and the Bekaa, independently of Hezbollah's political leadership.** Here is where the asymmetry argument gets its hardest test. Any serious Lebanese Academic position must be able to say clearly: the communities that have borne the physical cost of Israeli military campaigns for four decades are not the cartel. The villages that were evacuated, bombed, reoccupied, and rebuilt are not the cartel. The families whose lands remain under Israeli occupation are not the cartel. **Hezbollah as a political-military organization is part of the cartel. The communities whose losses Hezbollah has politicized are victims of both the cartel and the external threat.** Any argument that collapses this distinction — that treats the base as identical to the party — is doing the Saudi-Israeli framing's work, not structural analysis. The Lebanese state that is eventually built will have to include these communities on terms that recognize their history, their losses, and their legitimate security concerns, or it will not be a state. It will just be the next cartel configuration.

**Young people, broadly.** This sounds sentimental but it is not. The generation born after 1990 has no civil war investment, increasingly no confessional party loyalty, and increasingly no illusion that the current political class represents anything but itself. The October 2019 uprising was primarily this generation. The emigration wave of 2020-2024 was primarily this generation. The diaspora intellectual formations emerging now are primarily this generation. They are fragmented, underfunded, politically marginalized, and currently either emigrating or demobilizing. They are also the only constituency large enough to produce the political mass that an eventual state would need. **Investing in their formation — intellectually, financially, institutionally — is the single highest-leverage activity available.**

## X. The October 2019 Test Case

There is a moment when the entire cartel argument was almost empirically tested in real time. October 2019.

For perhaps six weeks, a significant portion of the Lebanese population — across every sect, every region, every class — articulated a demand that did not fit the binary. *All of them means all of them.* كلن يعني كلن. The slogan was structural, not confessional. It did not target Hezbollah specifically, or March 14 specifically, or any single faction. It targeted the cartel as a cartel. It named the actual object.

Watch what happened. Within weeks, the sectarian counter-mobilizations began. Hezbollah mobilized its base against the protesters, framing them as foreign-funded conspirators. The Lebanese Forces and their allies tried to hijack the protests to narrow the target to Hezbollah specifically. Amal's partisans attacked protester encampments. Every faction in the cartel — all of them, confirming the slogan — moved with extraordinary speed to fragment the uprising back into the binary.

By early 2020, the binary had been restored. COVID accelerated demobilization. The August 4 port explosion, which should have been the moment the entire cartel lost legitimacy simultaneously, was instead absorbed into the existing partisan structure: each faction blamed the others, the port investigation was obstructed by the factions whose networks controlled the customs directorates, and the case has still not produced a single major indictment more than five years later.

October 2019 is the control experiment. It proved that when the Lebanese population asks the structural question, the cartel responds as a single organism, with extraordinary speed and coordination, to suppress it. **The speed of the response is the most telling fact.** Factions that cannot agree on a cabinet for eighteen months can coordinate within days to fragment a structural challenge. This is because the binary is the cartel's core operational infrastructure, and any threat to the binary activates a collective immune response that ordinary partisan combat does not require.

Every Lebanese actor who fought in October 2019 on the side of restoring the binary told you, in that action, which side they are actually on. Not the partisan side they perform. The cartel side they defend.

## XI. Why the 2026 War Does Not Change This

The objection that will arise: *but now, under the current war, with Israel advancing and Hezbollah weakened and the state under existential pressure, isn't the binary finally being forced to resolve? Doesn't the Washington negotiation represent a moment when someone actually has to choose?*

The current moment is not a resolution of the binary. It is the binary being used to manage Lebanon's transition from one cartel arrangement to a slightly different cartel arrangement, with most of the same personnel, most of the same rent streams, and most of the same foreign patrons — just with a different internal balance.

The current configuration — Aoun, Salam, Berri, and a cabinet that has taken formal positions against Hezbollah's arms while remaining operationally paralyzed — is not the emergence of a sovereign state. It is the cartel adjusting its internal distribution in response to a change in the regional balance of power. Hezbollah is being diminished; the Maronite-Sunni-Druze axis is being strengthened; the patron roster is being rebalanced from Iran-toward-Saudi-and-American. **None of these changes produces a state.** The rent streams continue. The confessional distribution continues. The foreign patronage continues. The army remains structurally under-resourced. The judiciary remains captured. The central bank remains compromised. The port investigation remains obstructed.

What is being offered in Washington is not sovereignty. It is a renegotiation of the terms under which Lebanon remains non-sovereign. The new terms will be less favorable to Hezbollah and more favorable to the Saudi-American axis, but they will not be terms of statehood. They will be terms of continued statelessness, with a different primary beneficiary.

Every post-Taif moment was a cartel rearrangement sold as a national moment. Each produced a period of confessional triumph for the winning axis, a period of confessional grievance for the losing axis, and no state. The 2026 arrangement, on its current trajectory, will produce the same outcome.

The binary's deepest function is this: to convert every historical moment into a question of which cartel faction wins, so that the question of whether any faction will finally build a state is never asked. Every moment that could be a constitutional moment becomes a succession moment. Every moment of national crisis becomes a moment of factional realignment. **The state is perpetually deferred because the binary perpetually offers alternatives to building it.**

## XII. The Operational Posture

You asked what to do moving forward. Here is the posture that follows from the analysis.

**Refuse the binary's frame in every communication.** When asked whether you are pro-resistance or anti-resistance, reject the question and redirect. The question is the trap. The redirection is the work. This does not mean pretending the factions are identical. It means refusing the frame in which choosing between them is the only option, while still being willing to name which faction has done what in specific instances. Your Lebanese Academic positions should be structural-first, partisan-never, but granular when granularity matters. Name Hezbollah's specific failures without endorsing the Lebanese Forces' framing. Name the Lebanese Forces' civil war record without endorsing Hezbollah's strategic monopoly. Hold the complexity.

**Distinguish the party from the base, the cartel member from the community.** This is the hardest discipline and the most important. Every time you analyze a faction, distinguish the political-military leadership from the community it claims to represent. Hezbollah's leadership is cartel; the Shia south is Lebanon. The Lebanese Forces' leadership is cartel; the Maronite communities are Lebanon. The Future Movement's successors are cartel; the Sunni communities are Lebanon. Your work should always be making this distinction visible, because the cartel relies on the conflation.

**Name the infrastructure that would have to exist.** Every piece of analysis you produce about what is wrong with Lebanon should be paired, at some point in the arc of the work, with what the institution that should have been doing this work would look like. You do not have to have the full blueprint. You have to keep naming the absence. The cartel's most successful rhetorical move is to make Lebanese political imagination stop at the factions. Your work should keep pushing the imagination past them to the institutions the factions have prevented.

**Support the army's expansion without endorsing its current instrumentalization.** The army is the one national institution that could eventually become the infrastructure of a state. This requires it to be funded, armed, politically protected, and expanded over decades. It also requires refusing the factional uses to which the current political class wants to put it. Your content should consistently advocate for army capacity-building while consistently criticizing the specific missions the cartel tries to assign to the army. These are not contradictory positions. They are the same position expressed at two different time horizons.

**Identify and amplify cartel-external figures.** Nahas is one. There are dozens of others — economists, judges, journalists, academics, civic organizers — who are doing cartel-external work and receiving little amplification because neither side of the binary has an interest in amplifying them. Your platform has 140,000 followers and a structural analytical frame. Using that platform to surface people the binary has marginalized is among the highest-leverage things you can do. **Every Lebanese intellectual who refuses the binary is more powerful when other Lebanese intellectuals refusing the binary cite them publicly.** Build the network.

**Invest in the generational transfer.** The current political class will not be replaced by arguments. It will be replaced by time, emigration, and generational succession, or it will not be replaced at all. Your work is valuable to the extent it shapes what the generation born after 1990 thinks Lebanon is and could be. That generation is the eventual political mass of any state that emerges. Everything you build — the Canaanite substrate argument, the sectarian franchise machine framework, the civilizational continuity frame — is raw material for a political consciousness that has not yet politically mobilized. Your job is not to reach the current political class. It is to ensure that when the next political generation finally does mobilize, they have a structural vocabulary that does not reproduce the binary.

**Accept that you will be attacked from both sides, and treat this as confirmation.** Anyone doing this work correctly will be accused of being a Hezbollah apologist by the anti-resistance camp and an Israeli-Saudi asset by the resistance camp. Both accusations will feel unfair because both will be unfair. They will also be predictable and, in a specific structural sense, reassuring. The binary cannot metabolize analysis that refuses its terms, so it will try to force the analysis back into the terms by attacking the analyst as a partisan of one side or the other. When this happens, the correct response is to continue the work without adjusting to accommodate the attacks. **The attacks are evidence that the work is functioning.**

## XIII. The Final Form of the Indictment

Let me state the full argument in its hardest form.

The Lebanese state has not existed since 1969 in any meaningful sense, and has not existed since 1989 in any legal-structural sense. The resistance debate that has consumed Lebanese political life since 2005 is not a debate about whether Lebanon should have a state. It is a debate about who should control the absence of one. Every major Lebanese political faction has a structural interest in preventing the emergence of a state, because the state they claim to want would be the state that ends their current basis of operation.

The binary between pro-resistance and anti-resistance camps is not a reflection of a deeper political disagreement. It is the operational mechanism by which the cartel distributes attention, mobilizes bases, maintains rent streams, and prevents the structural question from being asked. The binary does not fail to produce a state. **Not producing a state is the function the binary performs.**

The foreign patron system is not a corruption of an otherwise functional Lebanese politics. It is an integrated layer of the same system. Iran, Saudi Arabia, the United States, France, and Israel all have priced the Lebanese cartel into their regional calculations, and all prefer the current stateless arrangement to any alternative that would require them to negotiate with a Lebanese government capable of independent decision-making.

The October 2019 uprising was the empirical test of this analysis. The uprising articulated the structural question. The cartel responded, across every faction simultaneously, to fragment it back into the binary. The cartel, confronted with an existential challenge, proved it is one organism with many jerseys.

The current 2026 war and the Washington negotiations are not resolving the binary. They are producing a rearrangement of the cartel's internal balance and its foreign patron roster, while preserving the cartel's underlying operational structure. The emerging post-war Lebanon will be less Iranian-aligned and more Saudi-American-aligned. It will not be more sovereign. It will be non-sovereign on different terms.

**And here is where the indictment turns into a commitment.** The factions are not morally equivalent, and the analysis must say so. Hezbollah's weapons respond to a threat that exists. The Lebanese Forces' networks respond to a threat that largely does not. The civil war's moral geography was not symmetric, and the 1991 amnesty's erasure of that asymmetry was itself a cartel operation. The communities served by Hezbollah's institutions are not interchangeable with the patronage clients of other factions.
