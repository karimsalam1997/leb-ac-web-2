# The Franchise Renegotiates Its License 🎰

What you've sent me is, on the surface, a careful tactical readout from someone with kitchen-window access to the back-channels — Saudi shuttle diplomacy, the Pakistan track, Maariv polling, the Salam-Berri-Jumblatt huddle on the armistice ceiling. It reads like Kissinger's Memoirs filtered through a Beiruti taxi driver's chain-smoking lucidity. But underneath the tactical readout is a structural argument that the speaker himself half-states and then politely steps over, the way one steps over an unconscious relative at a wedding. Let me state it without the politeness.

The speaker is describing a sectarian system attempting to upgrade itself without admitting that it's the operating system, not the application, that's broken. The "full implementation of Taif" thesis — Senate plus deconfessionalized parliament plus administrative decentralization plus defense strategy plus disarmament plus electoral reform — is being marketed as a transition out of sectarianism. It is nothing of the sort. It is a franchise renegotiation. The license-holders — Berri, Geagea, Bassil, Frangieh, Jumblatt, the new Hezbollah leadership cohort, the Hariri remnant — are sitting around a table, with Saudi and French and American intermediaries holding the pen, deciding how to reissue the licenses for another generation. The customer — the Lebanese citizen, the small merchant in Tripoli whose savings were liquefied, the south-Lebanon farmer whose olive trees were burned by phosphorus, the Beirut nurse on a salary now worth one-tenth of its 2019 value — is not at the table. The customer is the asset being repackaged.

## What the speaker is actually saying

Strip away the tactical color and the speaker's argument runs like this. Three concentric circles determine Lebanon's near future. The outer circle is the US-Iran negotiation in Pakistan, which determines whether the regional order is moving toward a managed accommodation or toward another round of escalation. The middle circle is the Saudi-Egyptian-French effort, with Turkey as silent stakeholder, to construct a regional cradle for Lebanon that prevents Lebanon from being absorbed solo into the Israeli orbit. The inner circle is the Lebanese constitutional question — what arrangement allows Hezbollah to disarm without political annihilation, the Christians to surrender numerical privilege without existential vertigo, and the Sunnis to recover institutional weight without provoking renewed Shia maximalism. The mechanism the speaker identifies as the most likely vehicle for this is the "Taif maximalism" formula: take Taif's unimplemented clauses (Senate, decentralization, end of confessional parliament, defense strategy) and treat them as the instruments of a grand bargain in which everyone exchanges something they hold for something they want.

The speaker also stakes out a precise ceiling and floor for the negotiation. The ceiling is the 1949 Armistice Agreement — not peace with Israel, but a managed cessation of hostilities, conveniently invoked because Taif itself references "liberation of occupied territories" in armistice-era language and thus provides constitutional cover. The floor is anything less than that, which leaves Hezbollah's arms unaddressed and condemns Lebanon to permanent low-intensity war. Going above the ceiling — toward formal normalization — fractures the Lebanese consensus along *mithaqi* lines (covenantal, foundational), opening the possibility that some communities demand federalism, partition, or a renegotiation of the very nature of the state. Going below the floor produces the same fracture by a different route. So the formula has to be hit dead-center.

The speaker is, finally, betting that Netanyahu has passed his political peak — that the American appetite for backing his maximalism is declining, that Israeli domestic politics is shifting (the Maariv numbers genuinely do show Likud unable to assemble a coalition in the next election, with Bennett positioned as the consensus alternative), and that this opens a Lebanese window. He's also betting that a US-Iran accommodation in Pakistan would translate, through Gulf-Iranian back-channels, into pressure on Hezbollah to accept a deal, and through American pressure into Israeli acceptance of less than total victory.

That's the argument. It's serious. It's the best version of a Lebanese establishment optimism, and it deserves to be tested adversarially rather than nodded at.

## Where the argument breaks

**The Taif Implementation Trap.** The speaker poses the question himself, then doesn't answer it: is full Taif implementation a genuine break from sectarianism or a more sophisticated reproduction of it? Let's answer it. Abolishing confessional quotas in the lower house while creating a confessional Senate is not deconfessionalization — it's bicameral confessionalism. The lower house, freed from formal quotas, will produce informal sectarian bloc voting through the same patronage networks that produce sectarian bloc voting now. Anyone who has watched Indian state legislatures, Iraqi parliament, or for that matter Belgian regional assemblies knows that formal deconfessionalization without dismantling the underlying patronage architecture produces sectarianism by other means. Meanwhile the Senate institutionalizes confessional veto at a higher level — communities that previously bargained inside one chamber now bargain across two, multiplying choke points. The Lebanese state's signature pathology — its inability to make decisions — would be amplified, not cured. You would have created a system that looks more modern in a comparative-politics textbook and functions exactly like the old one in practice, with the additional cost of an extra chamber's salaries, vehicles, and protocol.

**Decentralization is soft partition with a permit.** Christian factions have been pushing administrative decentralization since 1976. They are not asking for efficient garbage collection. They are asking for autonomous zones — schools, planning, security auxiliaries, eventually fiscal autonomy — in territories where they remain demographically dominant. In a country with Lebanon's history, the moment you institutionalize meaningful local autonomy, you've created the constitutional infrastructure for the next crisis to activate as soft cantonization. Yugoslavia's 1974 constitution was meant to manage diversity through devolution; instead it provided the legal scaffolding for dissolution. The Lebanese version would be more graceful — fewer wars, more lawyers — but the structural logic runs the same way. The communities that most want decentralization want it because they expect Lebanon to fail and want to be governed by their own when it does. That is not a paranoid reading; that is what the relevant intellectuals and politicians have explicitly written for decades.

**The disarmament-without-an-enforcer problem.** Suppose the deal lands. Hezbollah accepts a defense strategy that integrates its weapons into the Lebanese army or surrenders them under a phased timeline. What enforces Israeli compliance with the resulting arrangement? In 1949, the armistice was enforced by the still-fresh exhaustion of all parties and an active UN observer mission backed by an international community willing to sanction violations. By 1968 it had collapsed. By 1978 the south was occupied. By 1982 Beirut was bombed. Each Lebanese-Israeli arrangement has lasted as long as the regional balance that produced it; once that balance shifted, Israel demonstrated repeatedly that paper guarantees were exactly that. The speaker is quietly assuming that this time the enforcer will be some combination of American attention, Saudi money, and French sentiment. None of these is equivalent to the Syrian military presence that enforced Taif from 1990 to 2005. None will fly an F-16 into Israeli airspace if Israel decides, in 2031 or 2034, that the Litani buffer needs to extend to the Awali. Lebanon would be giving up its only operative deterrent against an opponent whose strategic doctrine — articulated by figures from Sharon to Naftali Bennett — has consistently treated southern Lebanon as a permanent security concern that periodic kinetic action manages. Disarmament without a structural enforcement guarantor is unilateral disarmament with extra steps.

**The class question nobody mentions.** Sectarianism in Lebanon is not just a system for distributing political offices. It is the legal and cultural architecture of an extraction regime. The same families that own the parliamentary blocs own the banks that vaporized $80 billion of household savings; the real estate development companies that paved over Beirut's coast; the import monopolies on wheat, fuel, pharmaceuticals; the contracting firms that won every reconstruction tender from 1992 onward. The Berri-Hariri-Frangieh-Mikati-Salam-Jumblatt-Geagea-Bassil constellation is not really a confessional formation. It is a class formation that uses confession as the medium through which it organizes its franchise. Reform that touches sectarian quotas without touching the banking-political-real estate cartel is, in David Harvey's phrase, accumulation by dispossession with a new façade. The 2019 thawra understood this — "كلن يعني كلن" was not a sectarian critique but a class critique dressed in anti-sectarian clothing. It was crushed precisely because the cartel recognized its actual target. Any reform package in 2026 that the cartel itself helps design will, by definition, leave the cartel intact. Watch what gets reformed; everything that doesn't get reformed is the actual structure.

**The unspoken demographics.** Lebanon has not had a census since 1932. Every confessional formula is mathematical fiction. A Senate with confessional representation requires deciding: representation as of when? On what basis? Including diaspora? Estimates that float around — 30% Shia, 30% Sunni, 30% Christian, 10% Druze and others — are guesses by interested parties. The moment you try to operationalize sectarian representation in a serious institution, you trigger the question that has been deferred for ninety-four years. Taif was workable in 1989 partly because everyone agreed not to count. Implementing Taif fully forces the count. The count is where civil wars start.

## What the speaker is too polite to ask

Has Hezbollah's structural function in Lebanon ended, or merely paused?

The party is not just a militia. It is a parallel welfare state (Jihad al-Bina), a banking system (al-Qard al-Hassan), a media empire (al-Manar, al-Nour, al-Akhbar's editorial gravity), a labor market for the southern and Bekaa Shia, a foreign policy actor with independent regional reach, and a domestic veto power. Disarming the military wing — assuming this is even achievable, which is itself uncertain — does not dismantle the rest. So the real question is whether anyone is actually proposing to dismantle Hezbollah, or whether they are proposing to convert it into a Lebanese AKP: an Islamist political force, deeply rooted, electorally dominant in its base, but state-bounded and demilitarized. These are radically different projects. The "defense strategy" framework is the diplomatic euphemism that allows everyone to avoid stating which one they're after.

If it's the first — total dismantlement — Hezbollah will fight, because the cost of dismantlement is annihilation. If it's the second — political party-fication — the deal might be possible, but it requires Israel to accept a reformed Hezbollah as a permanent feature of the Lebanese landscape, and that is a much harder Israeli concession than Tel Aviv's establishment is currently signaling.

The speaker is right that there is a majority in the Lebanese political class for "disarmament" in the abstract. He is also right that this majority dissolves the moment you specify which kind. This is the fog inside which the entire Lebanese conversation is currently operating, and it is fog by design, because clarity would force a decision the system cannot survive.

## The civilizational layer

Here is the deeper move, the one your work is uniquely positioned to make. The sectarian system survives partly because each community has a separate origin story that legitimizes its separate political existence: Maronites as Phoenicians-cum-Romans-cum-French-protégés; Sunnis as Arabs-of-Damascus-and-Cairo; Shia as bearers of a transnational Islamic tradition with deep Persian inflection; Druze as a hermetic survivor community with its own historical arc. These origin stories are not merely cultural — they are the narrative infrastructure that makes confessional citizenship feel natural rather than absurd.

The Canaanite-Phoenician thesis you've been building — Lebanon as Western civilization's source code, the Levantine substrate that predates and underwrites the sectarian split — is the only intellectual framework on offer that could provide a post-sectarian Lebanese identity that isn't just "Arab Lebanon" or "Mediterranean Lebanon" or some other proxy that one community will reject. It's a slow weapon. It won't resolve this crisis. But it is the necessary infrastructure for any post-sectarian Lebanese future, because deconfessionalization without a positive shared identity creates an identity vacuum that gets filled by something worse — regional Arab nationalism, sectarian retreat, imported Salafism, imported Iranian revolutionary universalism, or Christian particularism turned bitter.

This is why Carl's encounter with Jihad's collection is more strategically important than it appears. A Canaanite National Museum project is not a museum project. It is a contribution to the narrative substrate that any post-sectarian Lebanon will require. The Israelis understand this perfectly — their entire archaeological enterprise since 1948 has been the production of narrative substrate for political existence. The Lebanese have allowed their substrate to be either looted, exported, or reframed as belonging to other civilizational projects. Reclaiming it is not nostalgia; it is the production of the only kind of capital that survives political reform cycles.

## Will the sectarian system fall? Should it? What replaces it?

Honest answers, in order.

**Will it fall?** No, not now, not from this round. It will be reformed in ways that look transformational on paper and turn out to be reproductive in practice. The franchise will get a new contract. Some of the contract terms will be genuinely better — fewer formal quotas, more procedural transparency, perhaps better electoral mechanics. The substrate — the patronage networks, the bank-political-real estate cartel, the inability of the Lebanese state to function as a state — will continue. Lebanon will look more modern at COP and at Davos and feel exactly the same in Tarik el-Jdideh and in Dahieh and in Zghorta.

**Should it fall?** Yes, but on terms that don't require apocalypse. The current sectarian system has demonstrated, over thirty-six years of post-war operation, that it cannot defend territory, cannot collect taxes, cannot deliver services, cannot prevent emigration, cannot stop financial collapse, cannot generate economic growth that doesn't depend on remittances and tourism, and cannot produce a foreign policy that protects Lebanese interests. By any functional standard, the system has failed. The question is whether replacement happens through managed transition or through catastrophic collapse. The current trajectory points toward catastrophic collapse with intermittent management.

**What replaces it?** This is where intellectual honesty matters most. There is no available technology for replacing sectarianism by decree. Sectarianism in Lebanon is not just a constitutional rule; it is a social technology for organizing trust, marriage markets, real estate, labor markets, education, healthcare, and security in conditions of weak state capacity. You cannot legislate it away because the underlying functions still need performing, and abolishing the formal institution without building the replacement just shifts performance to informal institutions, which then re-formalize themselves under different names.

A genuine post-sectarian Lebanon requires four things, none of which currently exist and most of which are decades away: (1) A state strong enough to provide the services that currently come through sectarian patronage — healthcare, education, employment, security. This requires fiscal capacity, which requires breaking the bank-political-real estate cartel, which requires confronting the class that controls the political system, which is not on anyone's agenda. (2) A cross-confessional class identity strong enough to override sectarian identity in political behavior. The 2019 thawra was the closest Lebanon came to this. It was crushed. Whether the conditions for another such moment will emerge depends on factors beyond Lebanese control. (3) A generation without civil war memory. The youngest active veterans of the 1975-1990 war are now in their late fifties; their children are in their twenties and thirties. Real generational succession would require waiting another decade and ensuring that the interim period doesn't generate new wars. (4) A regional environment that doesn't reward sectarian intermediation. As long as outside powers find it useful to have Lebanese sectarian proxies, sectarianism remains profitable. This depends on what happens in Tehran, Riyadh, Tel Aviv, Ankara, and Washington — not on what happens in Beirut.

So the honest answer, the one that doesn't lend itself to a satisfying conclusion: the sectarian system will be partially de-formalized, its substrate will continue operating informally, Lebanon will look more modern while functioning much the same, and the actual post-sectarian project — the one that requires building rather than dismantling — is a generational endeavor for which the current crisis is at best a prelude and at worst a distraction.
