Not the political leaders who made the decisions. Not the faction heads who wagered on external patrons. Not the za'ims who kept the state too weak to defend itself or deter this war. The people who pay are the 600,000 displaced from the south. The families in Nabaa hit by strikes for the first time in history. The children in Tyre, in the Bekaa, in Dahiyeh. The shopkeeper in Bint Jbeil whose home will be demolished on the Gaza model. The farmer in the south whose olive groves are being sprayed with defoliants. They pay. They always pay. And when the invasion ends and the soldiers leave and the cliff gets its new inscription, those people will come back to rubble and be told by every faction that the sacrifice was worth it, that the resistance won, that Lebanon endured. And they'll look at the ruins and wonder who exactly won and what exactly was endured.

This is the frame that keeps you honest. The historical pattern of failed occupations is real. It is also, in the living present, a pattern written in the bodies of civilians who had no say in the decisions that put them in the crossfire. The dragonfly metaphor is beautiful. But the dragonfly's sting doesn't kill the invader. It kills the host first and the invader second. Lebanon doesn't win these wars. Lebanon survives them. And survival is not victory. It's just the absence of extinction.

## The Structural Layer: How We Got Here

Now bring in the full framework. Because the question your audience should be asking isn't just "will the invasion fail" but "why does Lebanon keep being invaded in the first place, and why is it always defenceless when it happens?"

The answer is the system we've been dissecting all conversation. Lebanon has no army capable of deterrence because every faction in the country has independently decided that a strong army threatens their franchise. $21 billion in imports flowed through the economy last year. The government allocated nothing for military spending. Not because there's no money. Because a funded army is the one institution that could override the sectarian balance. So the army watches drones fly overhead and can do nothing, because doing nothing is what it was designed to do.

Lebanon has no economic sovereignty because the financial system was engineered as an extraction mechanism, not a productive economy. The Ponzi scheme that Hariri built and everyone else fed consumed $120 billion in deposits and left the country with no fiscal capacity to respond to a crisis. When war comes, Lebanon can't mobilise resources because the resources were stolen. When reconstruction comes, Lebanon can't fund it domestically because the domestic economy was hollowed out by design. So reconstruction becomes another donor conference, another round of external dependency, another opportunity for the same political-financial cartel to skim contracts and rebuild luxury districts while ordinary people wait.

Lebanon has no unified political will because the sectarian system produces two populations that see the same war through opposite lenses. One community sees a resistance defending them against an existential threat. The other sees a militia dragging them into a war they didn't choose. Both are responding rationally to the information architectures they were raised inside. And the system needs them to keep arguing about this, permanently, because the argument about weapons is the argument that prevents the argument about who robbed the country, who defunded the army, who let the ammonium nitrate sit in the port, who let the banks steal the deposits, who kept the state too weak to protect anyone.

The invasion didn't happen because Hezbollah launched rockets. That was the trigger, not the cause. The invasion happened because Lebanon is a country that has been systematically stripped of every form of sovereign capacity — military, economic, institutional — by its own political class over thirty years, leaving it unable to deter aggression, unable to defend its territory, unable to respond to catastrophe, and unable to rebuild afterward without begging the same international powers that enabled or ignored the destruction. Hezbollah filled the vacuum that the state's deliberate weakness created. Israel exploits the vacuum that the state's deliberate weakness created. Both are parasites on the same host. And the host was weakened on purpose by the people who were supposed to be feeding it.

## The "Peace" Question

Now address the peace campaign directly, because your audience is hearing it and needs a structural frame for evaluating it.

There is a campaign accelerating inside Lebanon pushing for immediate peace with Israel. The Abraham Accords as model. Exhaustion as justification. Prosperity as promise. You understand the exhaustion. You live in it. But you need to ask three structural questions before you accept the premise.

First: peace with whom? Not some abstract Israel. This Israel. A state in full rightward messianic drift where the ambassador quotes the Torah to justify borders, the finance minister demands annexation, and the sitting government declares the Litani to be divine territory. You would sit across from this with no army, no economy, no leverage, and no ability to say no without punishment. That is not negotiation. That is ratification of conquest.

Second: peace producing what? Egypt signed in 1979 and has received billions annually ever since. It is nobody's model of prosperity. Jordan signed. Same result. The UAE was wealthy before the Abraham Accords for reasons that have everything to do with oil and nothing to do with Israel. The economic argument for peace does not survive contact with evidence. What survives is the pattern: signing from weakness produces terms that serve the stronger party. Ibn Khaldun described it eight centuries ago. Peace through submission lasting only as long as the submission holds.

Third: peace instead of what? The sovereignty argument that pushed Syria out of Lebanon in 2005 was never "we are stronger." It was "we refuse." Bodies in the street and an argument that would not die. People were imprisoned. People were killed. And it worked. So when that sovereignty logic is applied selectively, when it was sacred against Syria but negotiable with Israel, when it meant everything when Christian political influence was eroded by Damascus but means nothing when Shia villages are flattened by the IDF, the question sits there with no comfortable answer: is this about sovereignty, or is it about whose sovereignty, and over whom?

This is the passage that will stop both audiences in their tracks because it's the one question neither camp can answer cleanly. The sovereignty camp has to explain why Syrian occupation was intolerable but Israeli occupation is a negotiating opportunity. The resistance camp has to explain why they fought Syrian hegemony (oh wait, they didn't, they allied with it) while fighting Israeli hegemony. Neither side's sovereignty rhetoric survives a consistent application. And that inconsistency is the system's fingerprint. Sovereignty in Lebanon has never meant sovereignty. It has meant "alignment with my preferred external patron presented as independence."

## The Closing: The Rock

Come back to the cliff. But don't end where your drafts end, with the triumphalist implication that Lebanon always wins. End with the structural truth that's harder and more honest than either side's narrative.

The rock at Nahr al-Kalb has space for one more carving. It has been patient for twenty-five centuries. It will wait for the IDF to finish demolishing the border villages and declaring the security zone and announcing that the situation has been fundamentally changed. And then, when the last Israeli soldier crosses back south in three years or five or fifteen, the rock will still be there. The inscription will join the others. Cracked and fading. Another monument to an army that came to Lebanon believing it would be the last. None of them were. None of them ever are.

But here's what the rock doesn't record. It doesn't record what happened to the people who lived in the shadow of the cliff while the armies came and went. It doesn't record the homes demolished. The children killed. The families displaced. The savings stolen. The futures cancelled. It doesn't record the 800,000 who left in six years because the country between the invasions was unlivable too. It doesn't record the $120 billion that vanished. The port that exploded. The journalists who were murdered. The investigations that were strangled. The state that was never built because building it would have threatened the people who profit from its absence.

The rock records the invaders. It doesn't record the Lebanese. And maybe that's the most honest thing about it. Because the Lebanese were never the authors of this story. They were the surface it was written on. The invaders came and carved and left. And the Lebanese political class watched from the mountain and calculated which invader to align with and how to profit from the reconstruction and how to ensure that the next invasion would find the country exactly as defenceless as the last one, because defencelessness is the product they sell. To the Americans: protect us, fund our army, give us donor conferences. To the Iranians: arm us, back us, give us a role in your regional project. To the Saudis: invest in us, align with us, give us leverage against the other patrons. To the Israelis, eventually, inevitably, if the exhaustion campaign works: give us peace, take what you want, just let us manage what's left.

The dragonfly bites the invader. But it stings the Lebanese first. And the people selling you the story of Lebanon's eternal resilience are the same people who made sure Lebanon would need to be resilient, over and over, forever, because resilience is cheaper than sovereignty and suffering is cheaper than a state.

That's where you end. Not with triumph. Not with despair. With the structural truth that contains both: Lebanon will probably outlast this invasion the way it has outlasted every other one. And Lebanon will probably enter the next crisis exactly as broken as it entered this one, because the system that makes invasion survivable is the same system that makes invasion inevitable. The cycle doesn't break until someone builds the thing that has never been built: a state that belongs to its citizens rather than to its factions. And every day that conversation is delayed by the argument about weapons, or the argument about resistance, or the argument about peace, is another day the machine runs, and another carving gets added to the rock. 👑
