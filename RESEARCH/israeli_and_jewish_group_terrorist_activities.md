It is because they have access to unmediated information, Palestinian voices, human rights documentation, satellite imagery, soldier testimony from Breaking the Silence, that previous generations simply did not have. The epistemological monopoly that Fisk identified, the power to define what counts as violence and who counts as a victim, required information scarcity. It required gatekeepers. Those gatekeepers are dying or being bypassed.

The implications are enormous. AIPAC's power depends on the ability to impose electoral costs on politicians who deviate. That ability depends on a voter base that punishes deviation. If the next generation of American voters views Israel the way the current generation views apartheid South Africa, which is the trajectory the polling data describes, then the structural foundation of the lobby erodes. Not tomorrow. Not in five years. But within fifteen to twenty years, the political cost-benefit calculation for an American senator will look fundamentally different. And AIPAC knows this, which is why it spent unprecedented sums in 2024 targeting progressive candidates: it is fighting a rearguard action against demographic inevitability.

## III. THE SOUTH AFRICA PARALLEL AND ITS LIMITS

The most honest historical parallel is neither the one Israelis want (a permanent democratic garrison state like Cold War West Berlin) nor the one many Arabs want (total dissolution). It is South Africa, but with critical differences that cut both directions.

The similarity is structural. South Africa was a state that maintained ethnic supremacy through legal architecture, military force, and the backing of a Western superpower (in that case, primarily the US and UK during the Cold War). It justified itself through a siege mentality that was not entirely fabricated: the ANC did conduct armed operations, there were genuine security threats. And for decades, its supporters argued that it was the only democracy in its region, that its enemies were Soviet-aligned, that its internal arrangements were nobody else's business. Every one of these arguments has its Israeli equivalent.

South Africa did not collapse through military defeat. It collapsed because three things converged: international isolation reached a tipping point (sanctions, divestment, cultural boycott); the internal cost of maintaining the system became unbearable (economic stagnation, brain drain, the constant low-grade warfare of the townships); and the white population produced a critical mass of people who recognised that the system was destroying them too. The transition was negotiated, not imposed.

The differences with Israel are significant. South Africa's white minority needed black labour; the economic interdependence created leverage that the oppressed population could wield. Israel does not need Palestinian labour in the same way, having largely replaced it with foreign workers and technology. South Africa did not have nuclear weapons. South Africa's patron, the United States, was eventually persuaded to shift by Cold War calculus: once the Soviet threat receded, the strategic rationale for backing Pretoria evaporated. Israel's lobby is far more deeply embedded in American politics than the South Africa lobby ever was.

But the direction of travel is the same. The BDS movement, whatever you think of its effectiveness today, is the structural successor to the anti-apartheid movement, and it is growing, not shrinking. International legal institutions are slowly closing the gap between what everyone knows and what is officially acknowledged: the ICJ advisory opinion, the ICC arrest warrants, the cascade of apartheid determinations. Each one individually changes nothing. Cumulatively, they shift the normative landscape.

## IV. THE RUTHLESS PREDICTION

Here is what I think happens over the next thirty years, stated as directly as I can.

**The next decade (2025-2035):** Israel continues to consolidate de facto annexation of the West Bank while maintaining the legal fiction that the occupation is temporary. Gaza remains a destroyed, besieged territory whose governance is unresolved. The US continues to provide unconditional support, but the domestic political cost rises with each election cycle. The Abraham Accords framework (Gulf normalisation) survives but does not deepen in the way Israel hoped, because Arab publics will not permit their governments to fully embrace Israel while the images from Gaza and Lebanon circulate freely. Iran remains the organising bogeyman, and Israel continues to push the US toward confrontation. Tech sector growth continues to mask the underlying political unsustainability.

**The following decade (2035-2045):** The generational shift in American politics begins to bite. Conditionality on US aid becomes a live political question for the first time. Not cutting aid entirely, but attaching conditions: no use of American weapons in the occupied territories, compliance with international law as a prerequisite. This alone would represent a seismic shift. European states, already further along this trajectory, begin to impose meaningful consequences: arms embargoes, trade restrictions, diplomatic downgrades. Israel responds by deepening its relationships with illiberal powers: India under Hindu nationalism, authoritarian Gulf states, possibly a future right-wing Russia. It becomes, in effect, what it is already becoming: a garrison state aligned with the authoritarian axis rather than the democratic one. The irony, a state founded as a refuge from persecution aligning with the world's persecutors, will not be lost on historians.

**The third decade (2045-2055):** By this point, the question of one state versus two states has been answered by facts on the ground: it is one state. The only remaining question is what kind. The struggle becomes an internal one, closer to the anti-apartheid movement's endgame than to a traditional interstate conflict. Palestinian citizens of Israel, Palestinians in the West Bank and Gaza, and the diaspora increasingly coordinate around a single demand: equal rights in a single state. This demand is unanswerable on liberal democratic terms, which is precisely why it terrifies the Israeli establishment more than any rocket from Gaza ever could. Because you can intercept a rocket. You cannot intercept a moral argument whose time has come.

## V. THE DEEPEST INSIGHT

And here is the thing I think you should build your content around, KINGO, because it is the observation that will be as true in thirty years as it is today:

**Israel's tragedy is not that it has enemies. It is that it has confused dominance for security.** Every wall it builds, every assassination it conducts, every village it bombs, every settlement it expands, every UN resolution it ignores, produces a short-term tactical gain and a long-term strategic loss. It is winning every battle and losing the war, not the military war, but the war for legitimacy, which is the only war that ultimately matters. Rome did not fall because the barbarians became stronger. It fell because the cost of maintaining the frontier exceeded the capacity of the centre. The British Empire did not collapse because India raised an army that could defeat it. It collapsed because the moral and economic cost of subjugation became unsustainable, and because a generation arose, both in the colonies and in Britain itself, that refused to accept the foundational lie.

Israel is producing that generation right now, on both sides. Every young American who watches a Palestinian child pulled from rubble on their phone is one more person for whom the narrative of Israeli exceptionalism does not hold. Every young Israeli who serves in the occupied territories and sees what occupation actually looks like, and then speaks through organisations like Breaking the Silence, is one more crack in the consensus. The information architecture that sustained the old narrative is collapsing, and no amount of AIPAC spending can rebuild it, because you cannot lobby reality back into a box once people have seen it.

The most ruthless prediction is not that Israel will be destroyed. It is that Israel will be **transformed against its will**, the way South Africa was transformed, the way every system built on the legal subordination of one people by another has eventually been transformed, because such systems contain within them the seeds of their own delegitimisation. The timeline is uncertain. The direction is not. And the bitter irony, the one that Fisk saw more clearly than anyone, is that every act of violence Israel commits to delay that transformation accelerates it. Every bomb dropped on Beirut, every child killed in Gaza, every settlement hilltop seized, is a deposit in an account of moral debt that will eventually come due. Not because the universe is just. But because information is free, memory is long, and the young do not inherit the loyalties of the old.

That is the content that will age well. Not because it is anti-Israel. But because it is the same story that has played out in every colonial project in human history. And Israel, for all its sophistication, has not found a way to exempt itself from history. Nobody ever has.
