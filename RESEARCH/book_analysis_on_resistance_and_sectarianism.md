But Nahas's answer — build the state, it's technically feasible, here are the fiscal mechanisms — still doesn't answer the harder question. Which brings us to the thing Amel's thinking actually adds to what we built.

**The question we kept circling but didn't fully land: who breaks first?**

The binary — pro-resistance versus anti-resistance — survives because both sides need it. The Lebanese Forces need Hezbollah as an existential threat to justify their own parallel existence. Hezbollah needs the Lebanese Forces to justify not disarming. The Sunni franchise networks needed the resistance-versus-sovereignty frame to avoid answering whether they ever intended to collect taxes. Every faction needs an enemy on the other side of the binary because the binary is what makes the internal conversation — "are we actually building a state?" — permanently postponable.

Amel's argument is that the forces capable of breaking the binary are, paradoxically, the same forces currently fighting from within it using its language. The Shia community that supports Hezbollah does so because the state never built them a hospital or a school or a road. The resentment is completely legitimate. But the political form through which that resentment is organized — a transnational armed movement funded by a foreign theocracy — is the political form that guarantees the state will never build them a hospital or a school or a road. Because that hospital, that school, that road would make Hezbollah's organizational rationale obsolete.

The Shia working class in Dahiyeh and Bint Jbeil is in the same structural position as the Maronite working class in Bcharre and the Sunni working class in Tripoli. They are all being mobilized vertically — toward the za'im, toward the party, toward the foreign patron — to prevent horizontal mobilization toward each other. The generator in Sakiet el-Janzeer is not a Sunni problem or a Shia problem. It is Abu Ali Itani's business model, which exists because the state was kept too weak to run the grid. Abu Ali didn't create the system. But his existence depends on the system continuing.

---

**The 2026 update:**

The war has done something to this argument that we need to sit with. Hezbollah has been militarily degraded to a degree that changes the franchise calculus. The assassination of Nasrallah, the destruction of the command structure, the withdrawal under ceasefire pressure — these are not just military defeats. They are legitimacy events. The argument "we exist because the state cannot protect you" weakens considerably when you have just led the south through a devastating war and the state you bypassed did not protect anyone either.

For the first time since 2006, the space exists — briefly, and it will close — for someone inside the Shia political world to say: what has this cost us, and what did we get? Not from the Lebanese Forces' position, which is "disarm or we'll use America to disarm you." But from inside the community. That is the conversation Amel thought was possible and necessary — forces fighting from within the binary breaking from its terms — and the war has cracked the terms open in a way that hasn't happened since 2019.

The question for Lebanese Academic, as a platform, is whether you can create the intellectual architecture for that conversation before the window closes. Not "the resistance was wrong" — that's the Lebanese Forces' content and it has its own agenda. But: "the resistance did what the state wouldn't do, and in doing it, became the reason the state couldn't be built." That's the argument nobody is making in a way that can reach the people it needs to reach. That's the gap you're positioned to fill.
