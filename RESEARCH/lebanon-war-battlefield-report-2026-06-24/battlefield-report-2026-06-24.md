## Executive Judgment

On 24 June 2026, Israel is winning the air war, the demolition war, and the assassination war. It is losing the occupation war.

That sentence needs discipline. Israel can kill Hezbollah commanders, destroy whole neighborhoods, hold strips of southern Lebanon, keep northern Lebanese villages empty, and force Beirut into negotiations under American pressure. Those are real achievements. Anyone who pretends otherwise is writing slogans, not analysis.

But in Nabatieh, Bint Jbeil, Bayada, Majdal Zoun, Naqoura, Kfar Tibnit, Ali al-Taher Ridge, and the road systems below the Litani, Israel has failed to turn violence into quiet. Hezbollah has not expelled the Israeli army, and it has paid heavily. Still, it has made the Israeli presence costly, filmed, politically contagious, and hard to normalize.

That is the hidden loss. Israel wanted distance, deterrence, and a silent security belt. It got a watched occupation, an American-Iranian diplomatic cage, an army still taking deaths after ceasefire announcements, and a Lebanese battlefield where cheap drones and local geography keep finding the expensive parts of a modern army.

## What Changed Week By Week

### Week 1, 2 to 8 March

The war restarts around 2 March 2026 after the wider US-Israel war with Iran. Hezbollah's first logic is regional retaliation and Lebanese border defense together. Israel's first logic is shock: heavy strikes in Beirut, the south, and the Bekaa, with a promise that Hezbollah's military structure can be broken by force.

Lebanon's civilian price begins immediately. By 20 June, Lebanon's Health Ministry would say Israeli attacks since 2 March had killed 4,057 people and wounded 12,121, including 135 health and emergency workers, according to AFP carried by the Daily Star.

### Week 2, 9 to 15 March

The battlefield begins to separate from the press sentence. Israel speaks in target counts and command structures. Hezbollah speaks in response, endurance, and anti-occupation language. Jad Ghosn's 9 March interview frame, saved in the transcript pack, is already about the Israeli refusal to accept a real peace structure rather than a surrender formula.

The strongest Israeli argument at this point is that Hezbollah chose to re-open fire and exposed Lebanon to ruin. That argument has weight. Hezbollah is armed outside the state, it tied Lebanon to Iran's war, and many Lebanese opponents, from Kataeb voices to parts of the governing class, say the country is being dragged into another war without consent.

The reply is that Israel's operation rapidly exceeds defense. By mid-March, the Israeli method has moved past interception and retaliation. It is territorial seizure, village clearance, infrastructure destruction, and the making of a southern zone whose inhabitants are meant to stay away.

### Week 3, 16 to 22 March

This is where the campaign becomes an occupation problem. The Israeli army moves deeper into southern Lebanon. The terrain starts doing what terrain does in Lebanon: it turns advance into exposure. Roads, wadis, ridges, and ruined towns become the real map, not the arrows on a briefing slide.

The Electronic Intifada sequence from 19 March, with Jon Elmer, treats Hezbollah's escalation as part of a wider Iranian-led front. Whether one accepts his politics or not, the military point holds: once the front is regional, Israel cannot solve Lebanon inside Lebanon alone.

### Week 4, 23 to 29 March

The drone question becomes central. By late March and early April, the local research file already identified the structural turn: a few hundred dollars of drone can threaten a multi-million dollar armored system, especially when the army must move, fuel, repair, and gather in predictable places.

The important thing is not that every Hezbollah claim is true. The important thing is that the claim type changes. Hezbollah is no longer only claiming rockets against settlements or anti-tank missiles against armor. It is claiming a battlefield made of watching, waiting, and selecting.

### Week 5, 30 March to 5 April

Israel keeps killing. Hezbollah keeps adapting. The war becomes ugly in the old Lebanese way: formal negotiations in one room, rubble in another, and a vocabulary of "infrastructure" laid over places with names.

In the local X dossier from 22 May, the Israeli public line is already visible: "forward defensive effort," "security buffer," "anti-tank line." Those phrases matter because they show the retreat of ambition. The story is no longer "Hezbollah will be defeated." It is "danger must be pushed farther away."

### Week 6, 6 to 12 April

The 8 April strikes are the rupture. After a US-Iran ceasefire framework, Israel launches major attacks on Lebanon. Local and international accounts describe one of the deadliest days of the war.

This is the first deep sign that Israel is trapped by the regional frame. If Lebanon is included in an Iran-US de-escalation structure, Israeli freedom of action shrinks. If Israel rejects that structure, it looks like the actor wrecking the deal Washington wants. Neither position is comfortable.

### Week 7, 13 to 19 April

The local Hezbollah archive starts its daily count sequence on 17 April. From 17 to 20 April, the archive records 57 declared or fallback operations in that partial week. Alma, an Israeli-facing monitor, later says the period after 17 April still contained hundreds of Hezbollah attack waves despite ceasefire language.

This matters because the war has entered the contradiction zone: public ceasefire, private continuation, formal diplomacy, daily attrition.

### Week 8, 20 to 26 April

The local archive records 22 operations for the week beginning 20 April. This looks like a dip, but the qualitative pattern is more important than the raw number. Jon Elmer's 23 April episode, already in the older transcript folder, focuses on drones hunting Israeli tanks.

The Israeli army can still operate. It can still destroy. But the enemy has found the daily organs of occupation: armor, bulldozers, command vehicles, repair crews, staging areas, and the soldiers around them.

### Week 9, 27 April to 3 May

The local archive records 49 operations. The target set broadens. Drones are battlefield tools, propaganda images, and political signals at the same time. They are used against equipment and presence.

On the Lebanese political side, Al-Mahatta and Jad Ghosn transcripts from late April show the argument shifting from "will there be talks?" to "what kind of Lebanon is being negotiated?" That is a political loss for Israel too, because southern Lebanon is no longer only a military front. It is again the central argument over sovereignty.

### Week 10, 4 to 10 May

This is a major acceleration: 132 operations in the local archive for the week beginning 4 May. The 7 May Electronic Intifada episode is titled around FPV drones stalking Israeli invaders. The language is partisan, but the phenomenon is visible across hostile sources: drones are changing the cost of staying.

Israel's strongest counterargument is that Hezbollah loses far more men and far more civilian cover than Israel loses soldiers. That is likely true in raw body counts. But wars of occupation are not won by raw exchange ratios alone. They are won by making the occupied space administratively boring. South Lebanon is not becoming boring.

### Week 11, 11 to 17 May

The local archive records 138 operations. The 14 May EI transcript is one of the clearest reads of the drone problem: not every drone needs to destroy a tank; some drones choose the weaker, more useful target nearby. That is the doctrine-level shift.

On 15 May, the saved Jad Ghosn / Gebran Bassil transcript puts the political argument in Lebanese terms: if Hezbollah's weapons are part of a national protection formula, who controls the formula? That is the question Israel cannot bomb away, because the answer is Lebanese, sectarian, institutional, and historical at once.

### Week 12, 18 to 24 May

The local archive records 139 operations. The 21 May EI transcript frames FPV drones as a swarm against bases. This is where the occupation story becomes more dangerous for Israel: the base itself becomes the battlefield.

The X dossier's best sentence still holds: Israel's public accounts show action, while the drone footage shows dependence. A base needs fuel, power, communications, roads, repair, and people. Hezbollah does not need to beat the whole army in one scene. It needs to make each requirement expensive.

### Week 13, 25 to 31 May

The local archive records 94 operations. Alma's 1 to 7 June report says Hezbollah carried out 198 attack waves that week, with 168 against IDF forces inside Lebanon and 30 toward Israeli territory. Alma also says four IDF soldiers were killed that week and that the ceasefire period had already seen 975 Hezbollah attack waves by 7 June.

Alma is not a pro-Hezbollah source. That makes it useful. Its numbers support the central point: even Israeli-aligned monitors are describing a sustained high-tempo campaign against Israeli forces in Lebanon after ceasefire language entered the story.

### Week 14, 1 to 7 June

The local archive records 153 operations, the highest weekly number in the local count. Alma's same week count is even higher at 198 attack waves. The overlap is uneven because the counting methods differ, but the direction is the same.

This is the week where Israel's loss becomes more visible. A war that was supposed to produce a safer northern front is producing a new regularity: Israeli forces inside Lebanon are the main target. The border is not quiet because the army is "forward." The army is forward, and therefore it is being hit.

### Week 15, 8 to 14 June

The local archive records 107 operations. The visible ledger for 14 June is especially important: drone swarms, Ababil attack drones, Merkava tanks at Majdal Zoun and Yahmar al-Shaqif, a Namer vehicle, engineering equipment, and repeated attempts to force Hermes drones away from Sidon and Iqlim al-Tuffah.

The point is system pressure. Hezbollah is trying to kill soldiers and make the Israeli army's eyes, armor, engineering arm, and logistics feel hunted.

### Week 16, 15 to 21 June

The local archive shows only 2 operations on 15 June because Hezbollah then stops issuing the same daily claim pattern. Alma's 15 to 21 June report fills part of the gap: it counts 36 attack waves, down from 134 the previous week, but says six IDF soldiers were killed that week, five of them on 19 and 20 June.

That is the strangest and most telling week. Claims fall. Lethality remains. Hezbollah narrows its public messaging around Kfar Tebnit and Ali al-Taher Ridge. The Guardian reports that Hezbollah killed four Israeli soldiers and Israel answered with strikes that killed at least 47 people, disrupting the opening of US-Iran implementation talks.

## Casualties And The Numbers I Would Use

Lebanese dead and wounded: the least-bad public number I found for 20 June is Lebanon Health Ministry via AFP, carried by the Daily Star: 4,057 killed and 12,121 wounded since 2 March, including 135 health and emergency workers. The Guardian's 23 June live report uses "more than 4,000" killed and at least 1.2 million displaced.

Israeli military dead: confirmed public counts are lower, but the June figures are no longer small. The Guardian reported at least 32 Israeli soldiers and three civilians killed by Hezbollah by 19 June. The Washington Post snippet in search results reported at least 34 IDF soldiers and four civilians since March. Alma says 17 IDF soldiers and one Israeli civilian killed from 17 April to 7 June, then six more IDF soldiers killed from 15 to 21 June. The Jerusalem Post reported five soldiers dead in the 19 to 20 June incidents.

My estimate for Israeli military deaths by 24 June is 40 to 65. I would not publish a higher number as fact without hospital, cemetery, or family-notice evidence. The reason I would place the real number above the most conservative public count is the combination of daily attack tempo, armor/vehicle hit claims, acknowledged June deaths, censorship patterns, and the repeated Israeli need to drip casualties by incident rather than aggregate the northern front as a political fact.

Hezbollah military dead: this is the hardest count. Israeli claims run high, Hezbollah's admissions run low, and Lebanon's Health Ministry does not separate civilians and fighters in the headline toll. A cautious working range by late June is 1,200 to 2,500 Hezbollah and allied fighters killed or permanently removed from combat. The upper end may be too high if Israeli "militant" classification is broad. The lower end may be too low given the scale of strikes, assassinations, and ground combat.

Civilian dead: I would use a broad range of 1,800 to 3,000 Lebanese civilians killed by 24 June, with the warning that this is inferred from the total Health Ministry count, strike patterns, emergency-worker deaths, and the repeated targeting or destruction of populated areas. The civilian share cannot be honestly fixed from the sources I have.

## Who Is Winning

Israel is winning if the measure is destruction. It has killed commanders, displaced huge numbers of Lebanese civilians, damaged or erased large parts of southern Lebanon, forced Lebanon's state into negotiations under pressure, and kept the Israeli public story centered on security.

Hezbollah is winning if the measure is denial of Israel's political objective. Israel has not made the north feel resolved. It has not made southern Lebanon quiet. It has not turned occupation into a stable buffer. It has not separated Lebanon from the Iran track. It has not stopped drones from making soldiers, armor, and bases visible.

Lebanon is losing in the most literal sense. The villages lose houses. Tyre receives the displaced. Nabatieh, Bint Jbeil, and the border towns pay the price of every strategic theory. The Lebanese state looks present in communiques and absent in enforcement. This is why Jad Ghosn's question keeps returning: who gets to decide war and peace in a country whose sovereignty is invoked by everyone and obeyed by almost no one?

Iran is winning more than Israel wants to admit. The war has forced Lebanon into the US-Iran file. The Guardian's 23 June reporting says Rubio and Vance discussed a ceasefire monitoring body with President Joseph Aoun after the Iran-US memorandum created the longest lull. That means Israel's Lebanon front is no longer purely Israel's theater.

## How Israel Is Losing In Ways It Will Not Admit

First, Israel is losing the economy of force. The cheap system keeps touching the expensive system. Drones, rockets, artillery, and guided weapons force Israel to spend money, movement, interceptors, armor time, reserve time, repair time, and political capital.

Second, Israel is losing the grammar of victory. If the goal is to destroy Hezbollah, Hezbollah's continued fire is failure. If the goal is a security zone, Israeli soldiers dying inside that zone is failure. If the goal is to return northern residents to normal life, an open-ended occupied belt is not normal life.

Third, Israel is losing the diplomatic frame. Once Lebanon is tied to the Iran-US memorandum, Israeli escalation risks looking like sabotage of Washington's own regional exit plan. That is why Netanyahu insists on "full freedom" in Lebanon, while Washington talks about monitoring bodies and deconfliction.

Fourth, Israel is losing the image of technological supremacy. Trophy, airpower, signals intelligence, and armored maneuver still matter. But the footage world has changed. The drone does not need to win the whole war. It needs to show the soldier that he is seen.

Fifth, Israel is losing the permanence test. A buffer has to last. The old South Lebanon occupation collapsed in 2000 because the cost of staying exceeded the benefit of staying. The 2026 version is more technologically exposed, more diplomatically constrained, and more visible to the phone screen.

## What I Predict Next

For the next two weeks, through 8 July 2026, I expect a partial lull in public claims and a continuation of localized attacks around the contact areas where Israeli forces remain exposed, especially Kfar Tebnit, Ali al-Taher Ridge, and the approaches around Nabatieh and the western sector. The danger is a single high-casualty incident that forces Israel to strike Beirut or the Bekaa again.

For July 2026, I expect Israel to avoid a full withdrawal unless Washington forces a face-saving formula. Netanyahu cannot easily sell withdrawal as victory after promising security. Hezbollah cannot easily stop attacks while Israeli troops remain inside Lebanon. That means the most likely outcome is a managed bleeding line, with American language placed over Lebanese ground.

For Israeli deaths, I would expect 10 to 25 additional military deaths in July if Israeli forces hold exposed positions and Hezbollah keeps attacks mostly inside Lebanon. If Israel tries another major push or Hezbollah resumes high-volume claims, the July number could rise sharply.

For Lebanese deaths, the range is much darker. If the lull holds, July civilian deaths may fall below the worst April and May periods. If Israel answers a lethal Hezbollah strike with another wave like 19 to 20 June, dozens can die in one day again. The civilian casualty curve is controlled more by Israeli air choices than by the ground contact line.

For Hezbollah deaths, expect continued attrition through assassinations, drone strikes, and strikes on launch or command infrastructure. Hezbollah can absorb losses better than Israel admits, but not without internal strain. The question is not whether Hezbollah is hurt. It is whether hurt changes its decision. So far, it has not.

## What To Scrape Next

The next source layer should be Hebrew, not English. Search Hebrew X, Telegram, TikTok, and local council pages for northern Israeli communities, reservist families, casualty funerals, rehabilitation fundraisers, and complaints about returning north. That is where the Israeli loss will sound less like politics and more like family life.

The second layer should be cemetery and obituary tracking. Build a spreadsheet of IDF names, unit, date, place, and burial location from official IDF releases, Jerusalem Post, Times of Israel, local council posts, and funeral notices. This is slow work, but it is stronger than rumor.

The third layer should be video geolocation. Take Hezbollah drone clips, extract stills, match terrain and road geometry, and compare claims against satellite imagery. This is how to separate real battlefield evidence from recycled triumph footage.

The fourth layer should be Lebanese hospital and civil defense logs. The Health Ministry number is the headline, but emergency worker deaths, ambulance strikes, and hospital pressure tell the civilian story better.

The fifth layer should be channel-specific podcast tracking. The current pack has Jad Ghosn, Al-Mahatta, Tayyoun/Riwaya, Spot Shot, Makdisi Street, BreakThrough News, and EI. We still need a cleaner pass on Bodcast and more Lebanese Arabic channels with transcripts.
