# Lebanon Swallowed Itself Before Israel Arrived 🐉

**The Full Take — Script for Reel 1**

---

There is a cliff at the mouth of the Dog River, just north of Beirut, where every army that has ever entered Lebanon carved its name into the rock. The Egyptians. The Assyrians. The Romans. The Crusaders. The French. Napoleon III. The British. They came for different reasons, with different gods and different flags, and they all left. The Lebanese didn't build that monument. They didn't need to. The cliff filled itself.

Robert Fisk ended his chapter on the 1978 invasion with a single image. A Russian-born Israeli soldier standing at a freshly taken checkpoint in the south, looking out at the Lebanese hills, and saying quietly: *"This sure is a beautiful country."* Fisk's last line was his entire argument: he had been bitten by the dragonfly, and he had not even felt the sting.

That was forty-eight years ago. The IDF is back. The hills are just as beautiful. The dragonfly is still flying.

But here is what nobody is saying — and it is the thing that matters most.

Israel did not walk into an ambush. It walked into a wound that Lebanon made in itself.

---

Before the first Israeli tank crossed the border in March 2026, Lebanon had already done most of the work. A state that cannot fund its own army. A government that cannot enforce a decision past the city limits of the capital. A sovereignty that exists in press releases and UN resolutions, nowhere else. When Naim Qassem fired rockets after Khamenei's assassination and the Lebanese government called it a violation of sovereignty — the government that cannot pay its soldiers, that cannot stop arms shipments, that cannot investigate a port explosion that killed its own citizens — the word sovereignty was doing extraordinary work for so little infrastructure beneath it.

This is not a critique of the Lebanese people. It is a description of a machine.

The machine works like this. Every major sect in Lebanon runs a vertical structure — a party, a militia, a welfare network, a media empire, a patronage grid. The structure serves the community from top to bottom, and in return the community serves the structure. What the machine cannot afford is a functioning Lebanese state, because a functioning Lebanese state would make the vertical structures redundant. If the army worked, who needs a militia? If the courts worked, who needs a wasta network? If the treasury worked, who needs a party to pay your hospital bill?

So the state is kept weak. Not by accident. By incentive.

Hezbollah is the most powerful expression of this machine, but it is not the only one. The Lebanese Forces runs the same franchise under a different flag. One takes its orders from Tehran, the other lobbies Paris and Riyadh, and both need the Lebanese state to remain exactly as broken as it currently is. They are not enemies. They are market competitors in the same industry: the industry of filling the vacuum the state was never allowed to become.

This is why no Lebanese faction has ever seriously funded the Lebanese Army. Not Hezbollah, not the Lebanese Forces, not Future Movement, not Amal, not the PSP. They give it speeches. They photograph themselves saluting generals. Then they vote against the budget. Because the army, if it were real, would be the end of all of them.

---

And into this vacuum, Israel marched.

The IDF crossed the border with three divisions, destroyed every bridge over the Litani River, and pushed north along multiple axes through villages that had already been emptied by air strikes. The advance looked decisive on a map. Defence Minister Katz said it was "only the beginning." Finance Minister Smotrich said the Litani was Israel's new border. This is the triumphalist phase. Every invasion of Lebanon has one. It lasts until the occupation phase begins, and then the terrain starts teaching.

Naim Qassem said something before the invasion started that deserves to be read carefully. He said: *"When the enemy threatens a ground invasion, we tell it — this is not a threat, but one of the traps you will fall into. Because every advance of a ground invasion allows the resistance fighters to achieve gains through close confrontation with the enemy."*

He was not being brave. He was reading from a script that has been performed five times on the same stage. In 1978, Israel told itself it was pushing the PLO north of the Litani. It was. And it created, in the vacuum it left, the precise conditions that produced Hezbollah. In 1982, it came back to destroy Hezbollah's predecessor and stayed for eighteen years. It left in 2000 because Israeli mothers formed the Four Mothers movement and decided the body count was no longer defensible. Not a military defeat. A social one. In 2006, it came back for thirty-four days, declared victory, and Hezbollah held a celebration rally in the rubble. Now it is back again, and it has officially stated — three weeks into the campaign — that disarming Hezbollah is *"not among the goals."* The ambition has already deflated to match reality, and the bodies haven't even finished accumulating.

The occupation manufactures the resistance it claims to eradicate. This is not a political take. It is what the Jerusalem Post wrote. It is documented history. It is the machine running its cycle.

---

So where does Lebanon stand?

Here is the honest answer, and I say it as someone who lives here, who has a stake in what this country becomes.

The Shia community of the south has paid an unimaginable price. Villages flattened. A million displaced. People who had nothing asked to give everything. That sacrifice is real and it demands acknowledgment without qualification.

And Hezbollah made choices that contributed to this. The October 8th decision to open the Lebanon front — the solidarity front, as they called it — was a political choice made by a leadership that calculated the costs would be bearable. They were not borne equally. The family in Bint Jbeil bore them. The leadership in the bunkers did not.

This is not a statement of which side to be on. It is a statement about power and who absorbs its consequences.

The Lebanese Forces and the March 14 camp will tell you this war proves they were right — that Hezbollah's weapons led here. They are right about the destination. They are wrong to ignore that the weapons filled a vacuum the Lebanese state, including their own leadership, was never willing to close.

Everyone in Lebanon's political class has a clean story about why it was someone else's fault. The machine runs on clean stories. The clean stories are what keep it running.

---

Israel will leave. It always leaves. The cliff at Dog River has no inscription that says *we stayed*. It says *we came* — and then, beneath that, someone else's name.

The question is not whether Israel will eventually withdraw from the south. The question is what Lebanon will look like when it does. And that question has nothing to do with Israel.

It has to do with whether the Lebanese, from every community, are willing to name the machine and dismantle it before it runs the cycle again. Before the next foreign army finds the same vacuum and decides it's an invitation.

The dragonfly bites the invader. But the garden has to stop welcoming everyone who comes with a flag and a reason.

Lebanon is not a footnote to someone else's war. It is a civilization five thousand years old that has been reduced to a battlefield by the people entrusted to govern it.

That is the story. Not the one about who started the war. The one about how you build a country that can end one.
