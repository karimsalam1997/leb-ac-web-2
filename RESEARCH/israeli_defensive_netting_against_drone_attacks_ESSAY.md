# The Mosquito Net and the Empire

**By Karim Salam**
**Lebanese Academic, May 2026**

Israel is now draping netting over its vehicles in southern Lebanon to defend against drone attacks. Roughly 158,000 square meters of wire netting have been distributed to forces so far, with another 188,000 in procurement. Around 346,000 square meters when fully delivered. About the area of twenty soccer fields.

Hold that number in your hand for a moment, because it tells you everything about the conceptual register the IDF is operating in. Twenty soccer fields. Against a battlespace I would put at three hundred to four hundred square kilometers, the area of southern Lebanon where the actual fighting is concentrated. The ratio is roughly one part defense to a thousand parts terrain. They are not covering southern Lebanon. They are covering parking lots.

The question is whether this works. Not in the narrow sense of whether the netting stops the drone, because the IDF's own officials are already admitting that it does not. In the larger sense. Whether the netting is going to save the war.

## The shame of the chain-link fence

Picture, for a moment, the soldier inside the Merkava. He has been told his entire life that he sits inside the most sophisticated tank on earth. Seventy years of military doctrine that fused German Panzer engineering with American industrial scale and Israeli paranoia into a single object whose entire purpose was to make the Jewish body, finally and forever, unkillable inside it. That is what the Merkava was for. Not just a tank. The promise of the Israeli state to the Israeli soldier, in armored form.

And now his commanders are draping fishing net over it. Fishing nets. Camouflage nets. Even, in some reports, soccer nets. Along with drills involving small-arms fire at incoming drones, which is to say, men firing rifles at flying objects the size of a loaf of bread. A senior IDF official tells the Israeli press that the formal solution is "highly problematic" because the lower portions of the vehicle remain exposed and the adversary will simply detonate the drone at a distance from the net, dispersing shrapnel onto the men inside.

This is what defeat looks like before anyone is ready to call it defeat. Not collapse. Not retreat. The slow, embarrassed installation of chicken wire over a thirty-million-dollar vehicle, by men who twenty years ago believed history had ended in their favor.

The IDF is telling on itself in its own reporting. The current solution is described as an experimental concept rather than a deployed doctrine. Detection is the actual problem, not interception. Soldiers in the field have repeatedly reported spotting drones only seconds before impact. The new "detection kits" being rushed to the front, the IDF privately admits, do not guarantee a 100% success rate, perhaps not even 90%, but are expected to give troops the precious seconds needed to take cover.

Read that sentence again, slowly. The strategic doctrine of the Middle East's most advanced military, the army that was supposed to win the Iran war through clairvoyant precision and end the Hezbollah file in a single campaign, has been reduced to giving the boys a second or two to duck.

## The dragonfly was right

This is where the dragonfly thesis I have been carrying around since Fisk stops being literary decoration and becomes military description.

Fisk's dragonfly was not Israel. It was Lebanon. A beautiful country that welcomes the invader in, distracts him with the scenery, and lets him discover the bite only after the swelling begins. That was the point of the image. Lebanon's revenge is delayed. The first days feel manageable. The roads open. The tanks move. The officers give briefings. Then the country begins to work on the army from the inside. The checkpoint becomes a market. The patrol becomes a target. The local ally becomes a liability. The security zone becomes a reason to stay, and then the reason staying becomes impossible.

That is why the drone matters. Hezbollah has ramped up FPV attacks the deeper Israel has pushed into the south. The deeper they push, the more they are stung. This is not a coincidence. This is the geometric law of the southern Lebanese terrain meeting the asymmetric weapon, and the result is what it has always been: the occupier discovers that the cost of staying exceeds the cost of leaving, and the calendar begins to tick toward another humiliated withdrawal.

The drone is just the latest expression of a much older equation. Substitute the RPG-7 of 1982, the roadside IED of 2000, the Kornet missile of 2006, the fiber-optic FPV of 2026. The technology changes. The arithmetic does not. A guerrilla force embedded in mountainous terrain among a population that has chosen, for reasons that predate Hezbollah by centuries, not to surrender, will always find the tool that imposes intolerable per-unit cost on the invader. The dragonfly cannot perch.

## Why the casualty numbers are lying, and how we know

The Israeli casualty numbers should be read with suspicion. This is not because every Hezbollah claim is automatically true. It is because the structure of the battlefield gives Israel enormous room to hide what it can and minimize what it cannot.

A few weeks ago, three Israeli civilians were wounded, two seriously, in a Hezbollah drone attack in the Rosh Hanikra area. The reporting noted, almost in passing, that no sirens sounded in the area. Meaning the drone was not detected by the military before it arrived.

Hold that sentence in your mouth. The army didn't see it coming. If they didn't see it coming, they didn't film it. If they didn't film it, they control the narrative about what happened inside the vehicle, the post, the gathering. The only Hezbollah strikes Israel cannot conceal are the ones Hezbollah itself films from the drone's perspective, the moment of impact published on Telegram before Israeli censors can move. Everything else, every Humvee disabled but not destroyed, every Merkava penetrated but not killed, every soldier wounded but not photogenically so, lives in the dark space the IDF Spokesperson's Unit has cultivated for sixty years.

This is the structural asymmetry the Israeli information environment cannot solve. In Gaza, Israel controls the battlefield, the territory, the bodies, the press access, the satellite imagery, the morgues. In southern Lebanon, Hezbollah controls much of the camera. The drone is not just a weapon. It is a documentation system. Every successful filmed strike produces its own evidence file, uploaded before the IDF has time to draft a denial.

That is why Hebrew Twitter can describe the drones as a nuisance, a minor irritant. The minimization is not a tactical assessment. It is a psychological necessity. Israeli society cannot metabolize the truth that the technological gap has closed at the front lines where it actually matters, because the founding covenant of the state to its citizens, since 1948, was that the Jewish body inside the IDF uniform would be protected by an asymmetry of capability so vast that no Arab adversary could ever close it.

The drone closed it. For a few thousand dollars apiece, a network of fiber-optic FPVs has done what the entire conventional Arab military apparatus failed to do across four wars: it has made the Israeli soldier ordinary. Killable. Filmable in the moment of his killing. And the Israeli state's response, netting, denial, the Hebrew-language minimization, is the response of a society that has not yet figured out how to tell its citizens what just happened.

## What the netting reveals

There are a few layers that make the netting more revealing than it first appears.

**The factory is the tell.** Israel has announced that it is building a new factory to produce thousands of these weapons itself. The IDF is currently establishing a plant for FPV suicide drones intended for use across all theaters of war. Read this with a cold eye. The state that prides itself on being the world's drone pioneer, the country that exported the Heron and the Hermes to half the world's air forces, is now hurriedly building a factory to produce Hezbollah's weapon. Not their weapon. Hezbollah's. This is what doctrinal capitulation looks like in the procurement record. They are not catching up. They are admitting that the conceptual category they invented, the high-altitude, long-endurance, multi-million-dollar ISR platform, has been beaten at the tactical edge by a fiber-spooled commodity that costs less than a used Toyota.

**Fiber optics changes the entire electromagnetic war.** This is the part most coverage glosses over, so let me lay it out. The standard Israeli counter-drone playbook for two decades has been electronic warfare: jam the radio link between the drone and its operator, and the drone drops out of the sky. The fiber-optic FPV severs this entire branch of the doctrine. There is no radio link to jam. There is a literal glass thread, thinner than a human hair, paying out behind the drone as it flies, and the operator sees through its eye from a bunker three kilometers back. Hezbollah is not just using a new weapon. They are using a weapon that renders Israel's most expensive countermeasure investments, the Skylock systems, the Drone Dome, the integrated EW suites the IDF has spent a billion dollars on, into very expensive irrelevance. The netting is the public concession that the electronic moat has dried up.

**The Litani question is the real strategic frame.** The Israeli desire for a buffer goes back to Ben-Gurion's 1954 diaries, where the founding prime minister of the state sketched the Litani as the natural northern boundary of the Zionist project. Sharon picked up the file in 1982. Olmert picked it up in 2006. Netanyahu, through whichever cabinet faction is currently in ascendance, picked it up again in 2024. Every Israeli attempt to fix the northern border has been an attempt to push the line from the political frontier, the Blue Line, to the geographic frontier, the Litani river. Every attempt has failed at the same hinge: the terrain north of the Blue Line is the wrong terrain for an armored invader. It is precisely the terrain a light infantry guerrilla force is built for. The mountains stop the tank. The valleys hide the launcher. The villages absorb the soldier. The drone, now, finishes the syllogism.

**The older border underneath the modern one.** The frontier between the Galilean lowlands and the Lebanese mountain mass is one of the oldest hard ecological and political boundaries in the Levant.

The Bronze Age city-states of the coastal plain, Tyre, Sidon, Byblos, looked one way, north and west to the sea. The inland kingdoms looked another. The Hula basin to the east was a marshland frontier. The Litani was an ecological hinge. The mountains were a refuge. Modern military geography has not erased this. The Israeli army is, without knowing it, banging its head against the same wall the Egyptian armies of Ramses II banged their heads against, the same wall the Assyrians failed to fully close, the same wall the Crusaders abandoned. The Lebanese mountain is not a difficult problem of military occupation. It is *the* problem. It has been the problem for four thousand years.

## The thing that should haunt the Israeli planners

It is this. They cannot occupy. They cannot withdraw without conceding the second Lebanese defeat in twenty years. They cannot escalate to total war without American permission they are unlikely to receive from a Trump administration whose interest in Iran has been resolved by other means. They cannot defeat the drone with netting. They cannot match Hezbollah's drone with their own without first admitting they need to. And they cannot tell their own public any of this, because the founding myth of the state forbids it.

So they buy twenty soccer fields' worth of chicken wire, and they call the drones a nuisance, and they wait for something to change. Nothing will change. The dragonfly will lift off. It always does.
