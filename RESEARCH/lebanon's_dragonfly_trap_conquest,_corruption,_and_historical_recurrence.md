## The Dog River Remembers What the Knesset Forgot

North of Beirut, where the Mediterranean pushes its long grey tongue into a narrow gorge, there is a muddy trickle called Nahr el Kalb. The water barely qualifies as a river. The rock above it, however, qualifies as a courtroom, and it is the longest running courtroom in recorded history. For twenty-five centuries, every army that passed through here has paused to carve its name into the limestone above the stream. Nebuchadnezzar of Babylon did it. The Third Gallic Legion of Caracalla did it. The Assyrians, the Egyptians, the Greeks, the Arab armies, the French expeditionary force of 1860, General Gouraud's mandate troops, the British army of 1941. Every one of them arrived in the conviction that they were decisive, that their presence altered something fundamental about this sliver of coast. Every one of them is now a tourist attraction.

Robert Fisk, who knew this stretch of Lebanon the way a coroner knows a cadaver, described the stelae in Chapter 4 of Pity the Nation as memorials to pride and power, already being eaten by undergrowth and neglect when he wrote about them in the early 1980s. The most pompous inscription of all, the Pharaonic one, had been deliberately defaced in antiquity, probably by the next conqueror to arrive. The Third Gallic Legion of the Roman army lost its name to the chisel after it was disgraced in some forgotten internal feud. The final inscription, carved in 1946 by Lebanese president Beshara al-Khoury, records what the man genuinely believed to be the exit of the last foreign army from Lebanese soil. He was writing that sentence roughly thirty years before the PLO took over the south, thirty-two years before the first Israeli invasion, thirty-nine years before the Syrian occupation matured into a permanent colonial presence, and eighty years before a former comedian in Tel Aviv and a seventy-six-year-old prime minister in Jerusalem would declare yet another final solution to the Lebanese problem.

The rock keeps better minutes than any foreign ministry.

This is the first thing to understand about Lebanon, and it is the reason Fisk began his reporting here and never really left. It is the only nation on earth whose geology functions as a historian. The armies that enter this country are convinced they are the verb in the sentence. The country, patient as sediment, understands itself to be the paragraph.

## The Dragonfly Nobody Sees

Fisk's best image for Lebanon, buried in the middle of his Bekaa reporting, is the dragonfly. A dragonfly, he observed, can bite a man without the man ever feeling the nip. The wings are too beautiful. The creature too improbable. The stranger walks on, charmed by his luck at having encountered so fine a thing. Hours later, his skin itches. He scratches absently, tries to remember when and where he picked up the mark. Much later, the flesh swells. Much later still, the wound proves fatal.

That is the Bekaa. That is the hashish fields ripening in the sun of 1977 while Syrian T-54 tanks guarded the harvest. That is the Damascene officer class that came to Lebanon as disciplinarians and left as drug dealers. That is the Israeli army in 1982, twenty-five thousand soldiers pouring across the border to root out what Begin called the evil weed of the PLO, a contemptuous botanical metaphor that would be vindicated only in the sense that weeds tend to outlive the gardeners who curse them. Within weeks, Merkava tank crews were siphoning their own diesel and selling it to Lebanese taxi drivers for American dollars. Within months, they were trading in hashish and running protection rackets of their own. Fisk's line, which deserves to be stitched onto the pillow of every Israeli general who will ever contemplate crossing the Litani, is that Lebanon's revenge on its invaders is to welcome them in and kiss them to death.

The kiss is the trick. The welcome is the anaesthetic. The country does not even have to fire the first shot. It simply opens itself, provides the hashish, the black market, the obliging cousin with a checkpoint story, the sectarian faction hungry to become a client, the gold-toothed grandmother who offers coffee and accidentally points out where the roadblock is thinnest. Within eighteen months, the occupying army has mutated. It is no longer a military. It has become a local militia with better equipment and foreign passports. Within thirty-six months, half its officers are on Israeli television explaining why the operation has been a success, while half are in interrogation rooms explaining why they smuggled artefacts out of Tyre.

The Israelis are doing it again in 2026. They have levelled, by sober estimate, fifty-five villages in the south. They have seized roughly 250 square kilometres of Lebanese land. They have declared a security buffer from which Lebanese citizens are now barred. They are calling this, with the dreary euphemism of occupiers through all of recorded time, a temporary operational necessity. Beshara al-Khoury could have told them how that story finishes. The stones at Nahr el Kalb already know.

## The Seven-Stage Arc

There is a pattern to Israeli operations in Lebanon, and it has the dead regularity of a liturgy. It has repeated six times since 1978. It is repeating now. Fisk could not name it cleanly because he was living inside it; we can name it because we have forty-eight years of footage.

Phase one is the provocation. A guerrilla attack, a rocket, a massacre, a hostage. The cause is always real. The response is always disproportionate. In 1978 it was the Coastal Road attack. In 1982 it was the attempted assassination of the Israeli ambassador Shlomo Argov in London, an operation so obviously not the work of the PLO that even Begin's cabinet understood this. In 2006 it was the Hezbollah raid that captured two Israeli soldiers. In 2023 and after, it was Hamas on October 7 and the northern front Hezbollah opened in solidarity. The pattern of the trigger matters less than the pattern of the reply.

Phase two is the air campaign. The villages across the Litani are bombed into pumice. The Knesset is shown footage of surgical strikes. Western editorial pages explain why Israel has no choice. This phase is the easiest. Air power is forgiving. Pilots go home to their wives.

Phase three is the ground invasion. This is where the army discovers, with slow and dawning horror, that the map has been lying. In 1982 they reached Beirut in a week and stayed for eighteen years. In 2006 they stopped at the Litani and still took casualties so heavy their doctrine had to be rewritten. In 2026, they are still counting.

Phase four is where they are now. This is the phase of occupation without a plan. The generals have won the war they said they wanted to win. The cabinet has declared victory on three separate occasions already. The villages are rubble. The border is, by the narrow technical measure of Tel Aviv, quieter. But the Israeli soldier who walks into Aita al-Shaab tomorrow morning will be walking into a topography that has been preparing for him since 1978. Every tunnel is older than he is. Every rooftop has a drone operator on it. Every orchard conceals a wire. The local population, such as remains, has lost its houses, its relatives, its olive press, its grandfather's grave. It has nothing left to bargain with and nothing to lose. This is the moment when a uniform becomes a target for a rifle that does not need a general's permission to fire.

Phase five is the long grind. This is what is beginning now. FPV drones in the late afternoon light, priced at a few hundred dollars apiece, finding the gap between an armoured vehicle and the bridge it has just crossed. Static turrets, dug in, paid for in Tehran or improvised in a machine shop in Baalbek, operated by a nineteen-year-old in a basement three villages away. Night raids by small units whose entire operational horizon is to kill three Israelis and vanish before sunrise. The point is never to win a battle. The point is to make the occupation expensive. Hezbollah was designed, by Qassem Soleimani and his local apprentices, precisely for this phase. It is the reason the organisation exists. It was born in 1982 out of exactly these conditions, and it has spent four decades refining the craft. To ask whether Hezbollah will stop attacking Israeli positions while Israel holds fifty-five Lebanese villages is to ask whether water will stop flowing downhill.

Phase six is political attrition inside Israel itself. Reservists start missing their call-ups. An investigative journalist at Haaretz publishes the casualty figures the army has been suppressing. A small rally in Tel Aviv becomes a large rally. A minister resigns. This takes years. It always takes years. But it always comes.

Phase seven is the withdrawal, conducted under the face-saving euphemism of unilateral redeployment, which is the same phrase that was used in 2000. The Israeli army leaves. Another stele is inscribed onto the rock at Nahr el Kalb, though these days it is carved in pixels and uploaded to Wikipedia. Within six weeks, the chisel finds it.

## The Christian Card

Here Tel Aviv has one remaining card to play, and you identified it correctly. The card is to return to the 1975-82 playbook and prop up a Maronite faction as a proxy. Bashir Gemayel is long dead, vaporised in September 1982 by an SSNP operative with a suitcase bomb, but the Lebanese Forces still exist. The Kataeb are a political party again. The Christian heartland is anxious, demographically shrinking, increasingly estranged from a Shia-dominated state, and susceptible to any foreign power that can promise to restore its former weight. Samir Geagea has spent years positioning himself as the one Christian leader willing to say aloud what Tel Aviv wants said.

It is an available strategy. It would not be the first time Israel has reached for it. It is also, for reasons that Fisk documented at length and that the four decades since have only deepened, a strategy that accelerates the poisoning rather than relieves it. Every sectarian intervention in Lebanon has intensified the internal contradictions of the faction that was propped up. Bashir's elevation destroyed the Maronite project as a coherent political idea. The massacre at Sabra and Chatila, conducted by Phalangist militiamen inside an Israeli-ringed perimeter, permanently stained an entire community in the eyes of its neighbours. The Maronite political class that emerged from 1982 was fractured, subordinated, foreign-clientelised, and is still paying the bill in 2026. Any Maronite faction that accepts an Israeli embrace now will suffer the same metabolic fate. The embrace is the dragonfly's kiss in a different tuxedo.

The deeper reason this card fails is structural. Israel requires a Lebanese partner who can deliver stability. Lebanon, since its constitution, has been a state whose equilibrium depends on the strategic impotence of every individual sect. No single Lebanese faction has ever been strong enough to deliver on a promise made to a foreign power. That is the entire point of the sectarian franchise. Every client Israel has ever recruited in Lebanon has been, by the structural logic of the place, a broken lever.

## What the Stones Know

So where does this leave us. It leaves us, I think, with the prophecy Fisk filed to The Times on 11 June 1982, watching the first Israeli columns push into Beirut. He wrote that every army that has ever entered Lebanon has found itself bogged down in a political and military quagmire, humiliated before its enemies, and that there was no historical reason Israel should be spared this fate. He wrote it on the day Ariel Sharon was enjoying his triumph. He wrote it because the stones at Dog River had told him.

Forty-four years later, the stones are still telling.

The dragonfly is not a moral actor. The dragonfly does not hate Israel. It did not hate Syria, or the PLO, or the Assyrians, or the Third Gallic Legion. It does not even know it is biting. It is simply what this country does to visitors who overstay their welcome. To put it in the register of your Canaanite reclamation project, the land itself is older and more cunning than any politics imposed upon it. The mountains are too close to the sea. The valleys fold wrong. The sects are too many and too intermarried and too convinced of their respective historical truths. Any foreign army that enters is squeezed into a shape for which it was not engineered. Within two years, its exhaust pipe smells of hashish and its junior officers can recite the family tree of every man in the village. Within five, its own parliament is asking why their sons are still dying in a country they cannot locate on a map.

The Israelis in 2026 believe they have absorbed the lessons of 1982 and 2006. They believe the buffer zone is the answer Sharon did not have and that Halutz botched. They believe superior drones and superior intelligence and superior air supremacy have altered the fundamental arithmetic of the place. They are wrong in the particular way that is unique to imperial confidence: they have mistaken a change in their own technology for a change in the nature of the country they have entered. The dragonfly does not care what rifle you are carrying. The dragonfly is interested only in your willingness to remain in the garden.

They will remain. And then, as the stelae testify, they will leave. And somewhere on that stretch of limestone north of Beirut, an inscription will record the date, and within a single human lifetime it will begin to be defaced.

Gibran, writing in 1933, asked us to pity the nation that greets bullies as heroes and mistakes the glittering conqueror for a benefactor. The line has become so famous that we have stopped reading it properly. He did not mean Lebanon. He meant the conquerors. He meant that the pity is owed to the one who rides in with drums and trumpets and confidence, the one whose name will be chiselled onto rock by a grateful nineteen-year-old, the one whose descendants will one day ask, in genuine puzzlement, what their great-grandfather had been doing in that narrow gorge, beside that muddy trickle, on that particular afternoon in history.

The answer is on the stone. It has always been on the stone.

# 🪨 Fifty-Five Villages and the Fiction at the Negotiating Table

KINGO 👑,

Let me put the whole thing in one frame, because the trend you are watching is not chaos. It is a machine running exactly on schedule, in three different rooms at the same time. Each room contains a different kind of Lebanese, speaking a different kind of language, arriving at a different kind of conclusion. The rooms cannot hear each other. This is the design, not the accident.

## The Ground

Start with the map, because the map is the only honest document in this war. Fifty-five villages in the south, held by the Israeli army behind a cordon they have begun calling the "yellow line," copying verbatim the euphemism they used for the depopulated strip they gouged out of Gaza. Roughly 250 square kilometres of Lebanon is off-limits to Lebanese citizens. About twenty of those villages have been reduced to concrete mulch. Forty thousand homes across the south no longer have roofs. Between one hundred and two hundred thousand Lebanese, most of them Shia, most of them from families who have farmed these valleys since the Fatimids were a fresh idea, are now homeless inside their own country with no legal right to walk home. The total displaced at the peak exceeded one million, about one in five Lebanese. The Lebanese health ministry counts more than twelve hundred dead, which is almost certainly an undercount. The Israelis are flattening villages without entering most of them, because they remember 2006, and they know that entering a Lebanese village on foot is how Israeli boys stop being Israeli boys and start being case studies in a Haaretz op-ed.

On the slopes above these villages, invisible to the drones and unkillable by the ordnance, Hezbollah cells are settling in for the phase they have always told us was coming. They have spent forty-four years rehearsing for it. The FPV drones are being assembled in workshops three valleys away. The Kornet teams are cycling through firing positions. The ambushes are being plotted with the patience of a chess problem. Israeli armour is being lost at rates Israel has not seen since the Yom Kippur war, on numbers the IDF refuses to publish, because publishing them would force political consequences the cabinet is not prepared to absorb.

This is the state of play. An Israeli occupation the cabinet calls temporary and the ground calls permanent. A Lebanese resistance the cabinet calls defeated and the ground calls fully operational. Two narratives, one geography, no reconciliation.

## The Lie at the Centre

Before we go anywhere else, let us kill the central lie, because until the lie is dead, nothing else makes sense.

The Israeli justification, repeated by Netanyahu, by Katz, by every Western journalist who phones it in from Jerusalem, is that the buffer zone exists to protect northern Israeli communities from rocket fire. This sentence fails the first physical test you can apply to it.

A buffer zone is a bet on the geometry of ground warfare. It assumes the enemy comes at you across a line, that depth equals safety, that adding kilometres between your population and your adversary subtracts from the adversary's capacity to hurt you. This was a sound principle in 1918. It was dubious in 1967. It is incoherent in 2026. Hezbollah's arsenal includes rockets and missiles with ranges of 250 and 300 kilometres. Beirut to Tel Aviv is 200. Every Israeli population centre that could be hit before the buffer zone exists can be hit after it exists, from the Bekaa, from Baalbek, from the Dahiyeh, from north of the Awali, from any valley the Israeli army cannot reach without simultaneously declaring war on Syria.

You cannot buffer-zone a missile. Missiles do not respect topography. Missiles fly over. The buffer zone does not protect northern Israel. It protects one very specific thing, which is the political viability of the war. It gives the Israeli public a visible token for the suffering the army has absorbed. It is a trophy, engineered to look like a strategy.

The proof sits in the timeline. The night before the April 16 ceasefire was announced, Hezbollah fired projectiles at Israeli positions from inside the yellow line itself. Not around it. Not over it. From inside the zone that was supposed to prevent this. The zone failed on the first day of its existence. It will fail on the thousandth. It was never going to work. Everyone in the room knows this. The gap between what is being said and what is known is what you are watching when you watch Netanyahu speak.

So any Lebanese political position that accepts the Israeli security narrative at face value is accepting a lie. There is no honest argument inside Lebanon that begins with the phrase "they are doing this for their security." They are doing this to hold Lebanese land and to convert the holding into leverage for a political arrangement they have wanted since May 17, 1983, and have failed to obtain six times since. Everything else is decoration.

## The Two Lebanons

Now the second room, which is the room you are really asking me to diagnose.

There are two Lebanons in this war, and they are not metaphorical. They are two entirely separate polities, each with its own command structure, its own foreign patron, its own theory of victory, its own timeline.

The first Lebanon is the one holding the guns. It is composed of Hezbollah's military wing, a fraction of the Amal base, a measurable portion of the Sunni and Druze rural south, and a substantial slice of the Shia diaspora that sends money home. Its foreign patron is Tehran. Its theory of victory is the theory that has worked six times since 1978, which is to absorb the invasion, disperse into the terrain, bleed the occupier for years, and hand the world a withdrawal ceremony to put on television. Its timeline is measured in decades. It does not need to win. It needs only to exist at a cost the other side cannot pay forever.

The second Lebanon is the one holding the pens. It is Nawaf Salam's government, President Joseph Aoun, Ambassador Nada Hamadeh Moawad at the Washington table, the Lebanese Forces and their chairman Samir Geagea calling publicly for Hezbollah's disarmament, and the flank of the Maronite business class that has concluded peace with Israel is the only path to protect what remains of Lebanese capital. At the most compromised edge of this second Lebanon sits Antoun Sehnaoui, chairman of SGBL, romantic partner of the former US envoy Morgan Ortagus, who stood beside him at the Holocaust Memorial Museum last week while she described him publicly as the product of "generations of Lebanese Christian Zionists." His father Nabil was, in the 1970s and 1980s, a primary funder of the Lebanese Forces during their alliance with Israel; the continuity is familial, ideological, and financial. The second Lebanon's foreign patrons are Washington and, by proxy, Tel Aviv. Its theory of victory is that Lebanon can trade sovereignty over the south for solvency over the rest. Its timeline is immediate. It wants the war done by the May elections.

Here is the fatal problem, the one almost nobody in Beirut is willing to say aloud. The second Lebanon does not have the authority to deliver what it is promising. Its representatives sat at a table in Washington on April 14 and agreed to a document in which the Lebanese state becomes the sole guarantor of Lebanese sovereignty. That document was signed by a government that does not control the southern border, does not command the loyalty of its own army south of the Litani, and does not possess the political capacity to order Hezbollah to stop firing from Lebanese soil. Two days later, Trump announced a ten-day cessation the State Department called "a gesture of goodwill by the Government of Israel," a phrase that quietly tells you who is the giver and who is the supplicant. Hours after the announcement, Hezbollah stated that the Lebanese people have the right to resist Israeli forces on Lebanese territory, which in plain language means: we did not agree to your document. The Lebanese state had signed a promise it is structurally incapable of keeping. Everyone at the table knew this. The Americans knew it. The Israelis knew it. The Lebanese delegation knew it. Only the television audience was supposed to believe it.

## Why the State Cannot Deliver

This is where I have to make you uncomfortable, KINGO, because the structural answer is the answer we have built across this entire project, and most Lebanese, even very intelligent ones, will work extraordinarily hard to avoid accepting it.

The Lebanese state is a cartel agreement signed between a handful of sectarian families in 1943, dressed in the vocabulary of a republic. The National Pact is a franchise arrangement in which each sect is licensed to extract rent from a specific portion of the economy; the state is deliberately kept weak enough that no sect can dominate the others; and every structural crisis, the civil war, the Syrian occupation, the 2019 collapse, the 2020 port explosion, this war, is treated as a threat to be absorbed into the franchise rather than an opportunity to dissolve it.

Inside this model, the Lebanese Army is a jointly held asset of the franchise. Its officer corps is slotted by sect. Its budget is kept small enough that it cannot exercise monopoly force. Its leadership is selected for its willingness to refrain from using the force it does possess. The army is an atrophied muscle by design. Every faction, including the Lebanese Forces, agrees to this because a functional army would make every private militia, every za'im, every sectarian patronage network structurally redundant. Hezbollah's weapons are the most dramatic expression of this logic. They are also the least exceptional. Every Lebanese faction has arms, or the capacity to arm, or the sectarian population-base to generate arms on short notice. Hezbollah is the system's most successful product, and the franchise's most useful hostage.

This is why Geagea can call for Hezbollah's disarmament in good faith and know, in the same breath, that he does not actually want a Lebanese state strong enough to enforce disarmament, because a state strong enough to disarm Hezbollah would be a state strong enough to dismantle the Lebanese Forces the week after. The two parties are load-bearing walls of the same sectarian building. Each side performs enmity toward the other because the performance is the membership fee. Both sides understand that the building must remain standing.

Nawaf Salam's government is performing sovereignty because performing sovereignty is all a Lebanese government has ever actually done. Its writ does not extend to the south. It never has. Aoun's photograph above an army barracks in Nabatieh is a courtesy the local Hezbollah commander allows because the photograph costs him nothing. The moment the photograph costs him something, the photograph comes down. This is not a secret. This is how the machine has run since the French left.

## Why Opposing Hezbollah Within This War Is Futile

So. You are a Lebanese citizen. You despise Hezbollah. You hold the party responsible for dragging you into a regional proxy conflict you did not volunteer for. You want it to stop. Fine. These are defensible positions and many serious Lebanese hold them. Now apply a structural test to what follows.

The only domestic instrument capable of disarming Hezbollah is the Lebanese Army. The army is jointly owned by the franchise and cannot be strengthened without Hezbollah's consent, because Hezbollah holds a veto through its allied Shia officer corps, its parliamentary bloc, and its capacity to trigger sectarian escalation. Disarming Hezbollah by force would require a civil war fought on terrain Hezbollah has fortified for four decades, against a force with roughly 100,000 armed men, while simultaneously being bombed by Israel from the south. The Lebanese Forces, at the outside, could mobilise five or six thousand fighters. The arithmetic is simple.

The only external force capable of disarming Hezbollah is Israel. Israel has been attempting this for twenty months. The current score is roughly 2,100 Lebanese dead, 1.2 million displaced, 55 villages held, 40,000 homes destroyed, and Hezbollah launching projectiles from inside the yellow line the night before the ceasefire. Whatever Israel is doing, it is not disarming Hezbollah. As in 1978, 1982, 1996, 2006, and 2024, it is cementing Hezbollah's position as the one Lebanese institution whose purpose the war has confirmed. Israel produces Hezbollah in the same way that heat produces condensation. The relationship is physical.

The conclusion that follows is a hard conclusion for anti-Hezbollah Lebanese to swallow. Opposition to this specific war cannot be conducted by cheering for Israel. Israel is not disarming the threat. Israel is claiming your land. Sehnaoui's position, whatever its personal motives, is functionally a position that trades Lebanese territory for Lebanese commercial interests. It does not produce a Lebanon without Hezbollah. It produces a Lebanon smaller by 250 square kilometres, in which Hezbollah is stronger and more legitimate than before, having been confirmed in its core claim that the state cannot defend the south and the resistance therefore must. You are paying cash for a product that does not exist.

The honest anti-Hezbollah position is to accept that Hezbollah cannot be removed by this war, and therefore that the land being taken in the name of removing Hezbollah is a separate and additional loss any patriotic Lebanese must refuse. You do not have to love the resistance. You do have to refuse the territorial theft. These are not the same judgement, and the ability to hold both in the same head at once is the test of whether a Lebanese person is thinking structurally or thinking tribally.

## What Happens Next

Here is the machine, then, running in all three rooms simultaneously.

In the south, Hezbollah conducts attrition. Drones on armour. Kornets on bulldozers. Snipers on engineers. Mines on supply routes. The casualty rate is low by the standards of conventional war and politically lethal by the standards of Israeli democracy. Every week, two or three Israeli families receive a call. The cabinet explains that sacrifice is noble. The reservists, now being called up for cycles of nine weeks instead of six, begin refusing. Haaretz publishes the real numbers. Sinwar, dead, is replaced by grief, which is alive. This phase lasts as long as it needs to. The Israeli side will keep count of the dead. The Lebanese side will not need to.

In Washington and Beirut, the second Lebanon and its American partners stage a theatre. The ten-day ceasefire is extended, or it is not. Another document is drafted. Another ambassador calls it constructive. Sehnaoui is photographed somewhere else. Ortagus is quietly removed from the Lebanon file when the conflict of interest becomes impossible to hide. The Salam government issues a press release reiterating its commitment to sovereignty it does not possess. None of this affects the slope above Bint Jbeil.

In the villages, displaced families live in relatives' apartments in Sidon and Tyre and the Bekaa, watch Al-Manar and LBC with equal skepticism, and wait. The state will not rebuild their homes, because the state has no money, no will, and no reason to. Hezbollah will rebuild what it can, because every reconstructed house is a political asset that will pay dividends in the May elections and for a generation afterwards. What Hezbollah cannot afford, Iran and Qatar and the diaspora will. The villages that nobody rebuilds remain what the Israelis made them: visible arguments, sitting on the landscape, for whatever Lebanese political movement is willing to carry the grievance.
