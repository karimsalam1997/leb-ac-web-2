# The Gods Your Ancestors Forgot to Write Down 🔥

## What We Have, What We Lost, and Why

Start with the brutal inventory. The Phoenicians were prolific writers. They gave the Greeks, and through them the Romans, and through them the entire Western world, the alphabet you are reading right now. They had libraries. Carthage alone reportedly held a vast collection that Rome, in its infinite cultural generosity, burned to the ground in 146 BCE (some texts were supposedly handed to Numidian kings, then vanished). But here is the structural problem: Phoenicians wrote on papyrus. Papyrus rots in the Lebanese climate. The Mesopotamians wrote on clay tablets, which survive millennia in desert conditions. The Egyptians had bone-dry tombs. Your ancestors had humidity and war. The result is that we possess almost zero direct Phoenician literary texts. The civilization that made literacy portable left almost no surviving literature of its own.

What we do have comes from three imperfect windows. First and most important: the Ugaritic texts, discovered at Ras Shamra in Syria in 1929, a find that rewrote our understanding of the ancient Near East overnight. Ugarit was a Canaanite city destroyed around 1200 BCE, and its scribes wrote on clay tablets in a cuneiform alphabet. These give us the Baal Cycle, the Legend of Keret, the Tale of Aqhat, extensive ritual texts, and a full pantheon. Ugarit was not technically "Phoenician" (that label applies to the Iron Age coastal cities, Byblos, Sidon, Tyre), but it was Canaanite, and the religion is clearly the same family. Second: Philo of Byblos, a Greco-Phoenician writer from the 1st-2nd century CE who claimed to be translating the ancient work of one Sanchuniathon, a Phoenician priest. His text survives only in fragments quoted by the Christian apologist Eusebius, who preserved them precisely in order to mock them. Third: the Hebrew Bible itself, which is, among many other things, a sustained polemic against Canaanite religion, and therefore an involuntary archive of what it was trying to destroy. Add scattered inscriptions, Egyptian and Assyrian references, and archaeological evidence, and that is essentially the entire corpus. For a civilization that dominated Mediterranean trade for over a millennium, it is heartbreakingly thin.

## The Myths Themselves

The Canaanite pantheon is headed by **El**, the father god, called "the Bull," the wise, aged, somewhat distant patriarch who presides over the divine council. His consort is **Athirat** (later Asherah), the mother of the gods. Below them rages the real drama. **Baal Hadad**, the storm god, is the protagonist of the central mythic cycle. Baal is not the supreme god but the active king, the one who fights for cosmic order. His enemies are **Yam**, the god of the sea and primal chaos, and **Mot**, the god of death. **Anat** is the ferocious warrior goddess, Baal's sister (and possibly consort), described in the texts smashing enemies with a violence that makes Homer look restrained. **Astarte** (Ashtart) governs love and war, and her cult was enormous across the Mediterranean. **Melqart**, the patron of Tyre, became so important that the Greeks simply identified him as Heracles. **Eshmun**, Sidon's healing god, they mapped onto Asclepius. And **Adonis**, centered at Byblos, whose very name is the Semitic word *Adon*, "Lord," became one of the most celebrated figures in Greek religion.

The Baal Cycle is the masterwork. In the first movement, Baal battles Yam for supremacy. Yam represents the sea, chaos, the formless destructive power that threatens ordered civilization (if you want the resonance: this is the same archetype as Tiamat in Mesopotamian myth and *tehom*, "the deep," in Genesis 1:2). Baal defeats Yam with divine weapons forged by the craftsman god Kothar-wa-Khasis, and claims kingship. In the second movement, Baal confronts Mot, death itself. And here is where it gets structurally fascinating: Baal descends to the underworld. He dies. The earth withers. Anat rages, searches for him, finds Mot, and literally hacks death apart with a blade, winnows him like grain, and scatters him across the fields. Baal returns to life. The rains come back. The cycle of fertility and death is restored. This is not a minor mythic motif. This is the dying-and-rising god, one of the most consequential religious archetypes in human history, and it originates here, in Canaanite religion, on your soil.

## Phoenician, Greek, Roman: The Line of Descent

Now to your central question: does it go Phoenician to Greek to Roman? The answer is yes, substantially, but it is not a clean photocopy. It is more like a river system with tributaries. The Greeks themselves acknowledged this freely. Herodotus states that Cadmus came from Phoenicia and brought the alphabet to Greece, and the mythological figure of Europa, whom Zeus abducts, is explicitly a Phoenician princess from Tyre or Sidon. The "Orientalizing period" of Greek culture (roughly 8th-7th centuries BCE) was an era of massive cultural absorption from the Levant and Mesopotamia, mediated substantially through Phoenician trade networks.

The specific transmissions are striking. Aphrodite is almost certainly a Greek transformation of Astarte; her major cult centers (Cyprus, Cythera) are Phoenician trade stations. Adonis passes from Byblos to Greece with barely a name change. The divine succession myth, where a younger generation of gods overthrows an older one, runs from the Hurrian-Hittite Kumarbi myth (Kumarbi castrates Anu, the sky god) through Canaanite tradition and into Hesiod's Theogony (Kronos castrates Ouranos, Zeus overthrows Kronos). The parallels are not vague thematic echoes; they are structural blueprints with specific narrative beats preserved. The cosmogony Philo of Byblos attributes to Sanchuniathon, beginning with dark wind and primordial chaos, reads like a draft of what Hesiod polished. Rome then absorbed Greek religion wholesale, remapping the names (Zeus to Jupiter, Aphrodite to Venus) but changing little of the underlying architecture. So yes: the load-bearing walls of classical Western mythology were framed in Canaan.

## The God of Israel Walked Out of This Same House

Here is where it gets genuinely uncomfortable for people invested in treating the Abrahamic religions as wholly separate revelations. Early Israelite religion did not arrive from outer space. It emerged from within Canaanite culture and defined itself, painfully and over centuries, in opposition to it. The evidence is not speculative. **El is literally the name of the Israelite god.** Elohim, El Shaddai, Beth-El, Isra-El: the supreme Canaanite deity is embedded in the DNA of Hebrew theology. The scholarly consensus is that Yahweh likely originated as a deity from the southern desert regions (Midian, Edom, the Sinai), who was brought into the Israelite tradition and gradually merged with or replaced El. The oldest Hebrew poetry (the Song of Deborah, the Song of the Sea) shows a world where Yahweh is not yet the only god; he is the most powerful god in a crowded field.

The Hebrew Bible's obsessive fury against Baal worship is itself evidence of how deeply entangled the two traditions were. You do not spend centuries writing prophetic denunciations of something your people are not actually doing. Elijah's confrontation with the prophets of Baal on Mount Carmel is not a culture clash between foreigners; it is a civil war within a shared religious landscape. The archaeological find at Kuntillet Ajrud (8th century BCE) includes an inscription referencing "Yahweh and his Asherah," suggesting that even the consort of El was worshipped alongside Yahweh in popular Israelite religion. The Psalms contain passages that are structurally and sometimes verbally parallel to Ugaritic hymns to Baal. The creation narrative in Genesis, with its formless void and waters of chaos, is a monotheistic reworking of the same cosmogonic material that the Baal Cycle dramatizes as combat myth.

Christianity then adds another layer. The dying-and-rising god, Baal who descends to death and returns bringing life to the earth, is an archetype that resonates obviously with the central Christian narrative. I am not making the reductive claim that Jesus "is" Baal; that collapses important theological distinctions. But the symbolic grammar, a divine figure who dies, descends, and returns so that life may continue, was already ancient in the Levant two thousand years before the Common Era. The land itself was saturated with this pattern. The Adonis River (the Nahr Ibrahim near Byblos) ran red every spring, and the locals said it was the blood of the dying god. Christianity did not invent the dying-and-rising archetype. It inherited it, transformed it, and made it historical rather than cyclical.

## What This Means for You

So is the view that these traditions are related "too far-fetched"? It is the opposite of far-fetched. It is the scholarly mainstream. The line is not a clean descent like a family tree; it is more like a watershed, where the same high-altitude springs feed rivers that flow in different directions but carry the same mineral content. Canaanite religion is the deep aquifer beneath Greek mythology, Israelite monotheism, and by inheritance, Christianity and Islam. The archetypes, the dying god, the storm god who defeats chaos, the divine council, the mother goddess, the combat between order and the sea, these are not coincidences or borrowings in one direction. The tragedy, and this connects directly to your concept of memorycide, is that the people who built this infrastructure had their records systematically destroyed: by climate, by Rome, by the very monotheisms that emerged from their tradition and then declared it paganism. # She Was There Before All of Them 🌙

KINGO, the divine feminine is not a modern feminist invention projected backwards onto ancient religion. It is the oldest continuously documented religious impulse in human history, and the Levant is one of its deepest wells. Let me build this from the ground up.

The earliest objects that are unambiguously religious in any human culture are female figurines. The so-called "Venus figurines" of the Upper Paleolithic (35,000 to 10,000 BCE), found from France to Siberia, are small carved women with exaggerated breasts, bellies, and hips. They predate every named god, every temple, every written myth by tens of thousands of years. Whatever humans worshipped first, before they worshipped anything else they could articulate, they worshipped *this*: the female body's capacity to produce life. This is not a cultural accident confined to one region. It is as close to a human universal as religion gets. The biological logic is not subtle. The Greeks did not develop a love goddess independently and then discover she happened to resemble the Phoenician one. They met Astarte through trade and colonization, recognized her power and appeal, pulled her into their own mythological system, gave her a name that sounded Greek enough, and generated origin stories that naturalized her as one of their own. This is not cultural exchange between equals. This is absorption with the serial numbers filed off.

The Romans then performed the same operation on the Greek version. Venus was originally a minor Italian deity associated with gardens and vegetation, with almost none of the erotic or cosmic significance she later acquired. It was only through contact with Greek culture, particularly during the Hellenization of Roman religion in the 3rd and 2nd centuries BCE, that Venus absorbed Aphrodite's mythology, imagery, and theological weight wholesale. The circuit is complete: Levant to Cyprus to Greece to Rome to Levant. She left as Astarte and returned as Venus, and the people who welcomed her back almost certainly knew, at some level of cultural memory, that she had been theirs all along.
