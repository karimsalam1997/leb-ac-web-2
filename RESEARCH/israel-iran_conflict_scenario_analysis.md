The Third Temple and the Fall of Al-Aqsa: Strategic Scenario (2035–2040)
Part I: Prelude to Catastrophe (2035–2037)
Israel in 2035 – A New Status Quo on the Brink
By 2035, Israel had been transformed by a convergence of ideological, legal, and geopolitical trends. A decade of ascendant religious nationalism had pushed the nation’s politics sharply rightward ￼ ￼. Ultra-nationalist and messianic politicians held key ministries, openly advocating Jewish sovereignty over all biblical lands and even hinting at fulfilling eschatological visions on Jerusalem’s Temple Mount ￼ ￼. Crucially, a controversial series of judicial overhauls in the late 2020s had eroded the once-formidable checks on government power. By stripping the Supreme Court of meaningful review authority and tightening political control over judge appointments ￼ ￼, the ruling coalition removed the last “slim and inconsistent” constraints on policies impacting Palestinian rights ￼. In effect, the government – bolstered by a loyal parliamentary majority – was free to reshape long-standing arrangements at will, unencumbered by judicial oversight or dissenting voices.
Demographic currents reinforced these political shifts. Jerusalem, Israel’s symbolic heart, had a population nearing one million with a solid Jewish majority that was growing more devout each year ￼. High birth rates among ultra-Orthodox Jews (Haredim) were rapidly enlarging their share of the populace – on track to comprise about a third of Israel’s citizens by 2040 ￼ ￼. This “demographic boom” of religious Jews was mirrored by a passionate grassroots movement that viewed Israel’s recent military struggles and victories in messianic terms ￼. The Temple Mount movement, once fringe, had become a significant force. Temple activism migrated “from the margins to the mainstream” of religious Zionism, with lobby groups in the Knesset and tens of thousands of Jews visiting the Mount each year ￼. Many among the religious nationalist base believed the time was nearing to reclaim the Temple Mount (Haram al-Sharif) and even rebuild the ancient Temple, replacing the Islamic sanctuaries. They dismissed the old Orthodox caution of waiting passively for a messianic era; instead, they embraced Rabbi Kook’s philosophy that one could “take physical acts to manifest the messianic timeline” ￼ – in other words, proactive steps to fulfill prophecy.
Regionally, Israel in 2035 enjoyed unprecedented strategic depth but also heightened isolation from the Palestinian issue. The Abraham Accords of the previous decade had blossomed into robust partnerships with several Gulf states, yielding lucrative trade, joint infrastructure projects, and military technology deals ￼ ￼. The UAE and Bahrain, in particular, deepened ties with Israel’s high-tech and defense sectors ￼ ￼, and even Saudi Arabia had edged toward tacit cooperation. Yet this “peace and prosperity” was built atop a glaring omission: the Palestinians. ￼ ￼ The normalization agreements pointedly lacked any roadmap for Palestinian statehood, a “shaky ground” that drew bitter ire from Iran, Turkey, and Islamist movements ￼. Indeed, since 2020 the Palestinians had been increasingly sidelined; their living conditions deteriorated as Israeli settlements expanded and West Bank lands were quietly annexed in all but name ￼. In May 2024, Israel had even transferred parts of the occupied territories’ administration from military to civilian control – a bureaucratic sleight-of-hand widely seen as paving the way for open annexation ￼. Amid these shifts, hardline ministers like Itamar Ben Gvir (then National Security Minister) engaged in open religious provocations, such as visiting and praying at the Temple Mount in defiance of protocol ￼. These acts further inflamed Palestinian fears that Al-Aqsa was under threat.
Meanwhile, the United States – Israel’s traditional patron – had markedly pulled back its direct engagement in Middle Eastern conflicts. After the turbulent 2020s, Washington was laser-focused on China and Indo-Pacific security, no longer viewing the Mideast as the strategic center of gravity ￼ ￼. A pivot in U.S. priorities had reduced its military footprint in the region and sapped American appetite for refereeing intractable disputes ￼ ￼. In effect, Israel faced a future where it would “take greater responsibility for its security” without expecting cavalry from Washington ￼. This relative superpower disengagement emboldened Israel’s leaders to act unilaterally, while also signaling that if a regional firestorm ignited, the U.S. might limit itself to statements and UN vetoes rather than boots on the ground.
In summary, by 2035 Israel was a nation both empowered and imperiled by its trajectory. Domestically, an emboldened religious-nationalist government – backed by favorable demography and unrestrained by courts – was poised to realize long-held dreams at almost any cost. Diplomatically, Israel was more integrated into the Middle East economy than ever, yet the Palestinian question festered like a powder keg beneath this new order ￼ ￼. And globally, a distracted great power patron meant that the usual circuit breakers on extreme action were weak. All of the ingredients for a historic conflagration were present. It was only a matter of when and how the spark would come.
The Catalyst – “The Night of the Shaking Ground”
That spark arrived on March 11, 2037, in the form of a disaster at once natural and politically explosive. Just before dawn on a cool spring morning, a 6.3-magnitude earthquake struck the Dead Sea Rift Valley. The seismic shockwave rippled northward, and in Jerusalem the ground shuddered violently for half a minute – “the longest seconds of our lives,” as survivors would say. When the dust settled, the Temple Mount/al-Haram al-Sharif compound had suffered its worst structural damage since the 1927 British Mandate quake. The southern end of the compound – location of the Al-Aqsa Mosque (al-Qibli) – bore the brunt. Stones from the mosque’s parapets and its slender minaret rained down onto the plaza. Inside the 1,300-year-old mosque, several ornate wooden columns splintered and a section of the wooden ceiling near the mosque’s southeastern corner collapsed onto the carpeted floor. Scores of worshippers who had gathered for the Fajr (dawn) prayer were caught in the collapse. Amid the early morning darkness and choking dust, panic ensued. Eleven Muslim worshippers were killed by falling debris and over a hundred injured, turning a sacred sanctuary into a scene of tragedy.
In the immediate aftermath, both the Israeli and Jordanian authorities rushed to respond – and to shape the narrative. The quake had impacted the whole city (several old East Jerusalem buildings in Silwan and the Muslim Quarter had crumbled, and even the Western Wall had shed some stones), but all eyes were on Al-Aqsa. Initial photographs circulating on social media – broken slabs of the mosque’s dome on the ground, a gaping crack running down one of its exterior walls – sent shockwaves through the Muslim world. At first light, the Jerusalem Islamic Waqf (the Jordanian-backed trust administering the holy site) moved to close the mosque and assess damage. Jordan’s King Abdullah II made a televised statement by mid-morning, expressing heartbreak at the damage to “the sacred Al-Aqsa” and vowing that “we will restore the mosque, every stone and pillar”. Israeli officials, for their part, extended public condolences for the loss of life – but they also seized an opening. Citing Israel’s sovereignty over “unified Jerusalem” and its responsibility for public safety, Prime Minister Yoav Sarig (a fictional successor to Netanyahu, leading a hawkish coalition) announced that Israeli engineers and structural specialists would be “joining the inspection to ensure the stability of the Mount.”
Under the longstanding status quo arrangement, Jordan’s Waqf had day-to-day custodianship of the Temple Mount, while Israel maintained overall security control ￼. But now Israeli authorities moved unilaterally. Within hours of the quake, units of the Israel Police and Border Police encircled the compound, officially to keep the public safe from aftershocks and debris. In practice, they established a cordon that also kept out Waqf officials unless accompanied by Israeli engineers. Citing emergency powers, Jerusalem’s police chief announced that the Temple Mount complex was closed to all civilians – Muslim or Jewish – until further notice. The Chief Sephardic Rabbi of Israel urged Jews not to ascend the Mount due to the danger, while the Jordanian Waqf called for calm and cooperation. Publicly, initial coordination between Israel and Jordan appeared civil. But behind closed doors, a storm was brewing.
Israeli structural engineers – including some from the Antiquities Authority – conducted a rapid survey of the damage. By that evening, they leaked a dire preliminary finding to the Israeli press: Al-Aqsa Mosque’s southern wall and foundation had been “severely compromised.” According to the Israeli team, there was a risk the entire mosque could collapse if another tremor hit. Whether intentionally or not, these leaks fed a narrative that the ancient mosque was now a public hazard. They argued that immediate reinforcements or partial demolition might be required for safety. Jordanian engineers vehemently disagreed, insisting that with proper shoring the structure was salvageable. This technical dispute quickly took on a political dimension: who had ultimate authority over repairs? Israel’s government asserted that since the mosque posed a safety risk to all of Jerusalem, Israeli authorities must direct the reconstruction effort. Jordan protested that as custodian, it was their role – and that Israel was using the crisis to encroach on Muslim administration of the holy site.
Tellingly, elements of Israel’s ruling coalition saw the quake not only as a crisis but as “a sign from God.” Within days, prominent voices on the religious right were framing the event in prophetic terms. A viral quote from a Temple Mount activist rabbi proclaimed: “The Almighty has cracked open the door of heaven on His holy mountain.” In settler yeshivas and WhatsApp groups, rumors spread that Al-Aqsa’s destruction was an act of divine providence clearing the path for a Third Temple. Though the government officially dismissed such talk, these ideas resonated with ministers who privately shared messianic beliefs. National Security Minister Avraham “Avi” Ben-Gvir (the spiritual heir to the earlier far-right figure Itamar Ben Gvir) was quoted in a leaked cabinet meeting as saying “there are opportunities in every crisis – even holy opportunities.”
On March 15, 2037 – just four days after the quake – the Israeli Cabinet invoked the Emergency Antiquities Protection Order. This measure, never before applied to a Muslim religious site, declared the Temple Mount a zone of “extraordinary historical and structural sensitivity” requiring direct state supervision. The Cabinet’s statement insisted this was a temporary step to ensure the stability of structures and protect lives, “not a change to the status quo”. But for the first time since 1967, Israel had effectively asserted administrative control over the heart of the compound. Jordanian and Palestinian officials decried the move as a de facto “takeover under the guise of safety.” In Amman, crowds protested outside the Israeli embassy, and Jordan’s parliament convened an emergency session threatening to suspend the 1994 Israel-Jordan peace treaty. The Arab League met in Cairo and issued a statement warning Israel “not to exploit the Al-Aqsa earthquake for political gain.” Despite this, international reaction was relatively muted – a few sharp condemnations, but also calls for “both sides to coordinate on repairs.” Washington urged “respect for holy sites” but notably did not directly demand Israel stand down. U.S. officials privately calculated that openly clashing with Jerusalem might imperil quietly brokered Saudi-Israeli normalization talks that were underway. Thus, a critical moment passed: global actors voiced concern yet failed to impose any real constraint. The Israeli government’s calculus – that the U.S. and others would ultimately look away – seemed vindicated.
Dismantling the Status Quo, Step by Step

Citing Israel’s sovereign jurisdiction over Jerusalem and the “imminent public danger,” the court ruled that government oversight was lawful ￼. It was a stark departure from earlier decades when the court might have admonished the state to respect the delicate status quo. Now, with critics warning that even the courts had become rubber stamps ￼, the legal floodgates opened. Israeli authorities wasted no time drafting new regulations for the site’s “rehabilitation.”
•	May 2037: Engineering Assessments and Controlled Demolitions: An Israeli-appointed panel of engineers (notably, no Jordanian or Palestinian engineers were included) released a report on the compound’s safety. It declared the Al-Aqsa Mosque structure beyond safe repair – a conclusion hotly disputed by Muslim authorities. The report recommended that the damaged sections of Al-Aqsa be carefully dismantled “to prevent an uncontrolled collapse.” Within weeks, under heavy police guard, Israeli Public Works crews erected scaffolding around the mosque’s cracked southern wall. Despite global calls for independent inspections, Israel pressed on, citing this as purely a technical decision. In June, crews removed the Al-Aqsa minaret that had partially toppled, and began taking down the most fractured portions of the mosque’s outer structure. Each stone removed was documented and sent “for restoration” – a gesture to imply the mosque would eventually be rebuilt. But on the ground, Palestinians and Muslims worldwide saw images of Al-Aqsa’s dome and walls being deconstructed, stone by stone. The symbolism was devastating. Protest strikes erupted in East Jerusalem, the West Bank, and Amman. The Israeli government insisted the measures were temporary and reversible, yet the optics told a different story: the physical icon of Muslim heritage in Jerusalem was being peeled away.

They argued that with Al-Aqsa closed for repairs, why shouldn’t Jews be allowed to pray openly on the Mount’s open spaces? After all, Muslims couldn’t use the mosque now – daily prayers had been relocated to an alternate area just outside the compound – so the old prohibition on Jewish prayer, they claimed, no longer applied. National Security Minister Ben-Gvir openly endorsed this view, breaking with even the Prime Minister’s careful line. He instructed police to “use their discretion” regarding quiet Jewish prayers on the Mount. In practice, Israeli police on-site began tolerating, and at times facilitating, small groups of Jewish worshippers who ascended and prayed on the plaza north of the mosque’s ruins. This was a de facto breach of the status quo – the decades-old arrangement forbidding Jewish worship at the site ￼ ￼. Security chiefs in the past had warned that even symbolic Jewish prayer on the Mount could trigger unrest ￼ ￼. For Jewish Temple activists, this was a triumphant step toward “normalizing” their presence. For Palestinians, it was an intolerable provocation, confirming their fears that Israel intended to Judaize the holy site fully.
•	August–September 2037: Escalating Confrontations: Tensions boiled through that summer. With the compound partially open (for limited Muslim prayers at a designated outdoor area near the Dome of the Rock, and parallel Jewish visits under heavy guard), confrontations multiplied. On Tisha B’Av (the Jewish day of mourning the Temples’ destruction) in August, thousands of Jewish pilgrims – far more than police had ever permitted – ascended the Mount. They carried Israeli flags and recited prayers aloud. Although Prime Minister Sarig had quietly urged restraint, ministers from the Religious Zionist bloc joined the march, smiling for cameras in front of the golden Dome. The Jordanian Waqf officials, marginalized and insulted, walked out in protest. Scuffles broke out when young Palestinian men, enraged by the sight of what they viewed as religious desecration, threw stones at the Jewish worshippers. In response, Israeli police unleashed tear gas and rubber bullets within the holy compound itself – another first. Dozens were injured on both sides. The images of smoke wafting around the Dome of the Rock and blood on the stone pavement electrified social media across the Muslim world. Riotous protests erupted that night in Amman, Cairo, and Istanbul. The situation teetered on the edge of a larger conflagration, yet Israeli authorities doubled down. The government spokesman accused “Islamist extremists” of instigating violence and maintained Israel’s right to ensure freedom of worship for all – casting the Jewish prayer as mere “religious equality.”

These sober warnings reflected a rare moment of unity among Israel’s security old-guard: this path could lead to war.
Yet, the political leadership was riding a different wave. Buoyed by their base and convinced that regional powers would ultimately grumble but not act, the Cabinet gave tacit backing to Halevi’s bill. Prime Minister Sarig, while more cautious, feared that blocking it would fracture his coalition. In a telling episode in October 2037, the IDF Chief of Staff – a decorated general haunted by the specter of a multi-front war – privately told the Prime Minister that he would resign if the government continued undermining stability on the Mount. Defense Minister Bezalel Smotrich (a hardliner) brusquely rebuffed him, reportedly saying “we’ve faced intifadas and wars before; we will handle whatever comes.” The general’s warning went unheeded, and he quietly “retired” soon after, replaced by a more amenable officer. This epitomized the broader trend: pragmatic voices in the establishment were being sidelined, while ideologues gained the upper hand.
Warnings from Within – Security vs. Prophecy
Even as events barreled forward, an intense battle was waged in the shadows of Israel’s security apparatus. The world will protest but quickly move on.” Some genuinely believed that rebuilding the Temple would usher in an era of peace (at least for Israel), citing fringe interpretations that “once the Third Temple stands, it will be a Temple of Peace open to all peoples” – a talking point even Temple movement PR had used ￼ ￼. They sketched nightmare scenarios in intelligence briefings: a third Intifada spiraling out of the West Bank refugee camps; Hezbollah raining thousands of precision missiles down from Lebanon; a furious Iran mobilizing militias in Syria and Iraq to march on Jerusalem; even the collapse of friendly regimes like the Hashemites in Jordan under popular outrage. For Israel’s security chiefs, the Temple Mount was the ultimate tripwire. They recalled that the Second Intifada in 2000 had been triggered by a mere visit of a politician (Ariel Sharon) to the Mount ￼. Now, Israel was doing far worse – it was altering the very fabric of the site. Shin Bet Director Eli Naveh (fictitious) circulated a memo in November 2037 bluntly titled “Assessment of Blowback: Worst-Case Projections.” It enumerated potential consequences: mass civil unrest among Israel’s own Arab citizens, the Palestinian Authority’s collapse, regional war involving multiple states, and a wave of global terror attacks targeting Israelis and Jewish diaspora communities. “The Temple Mount/Al-Aqsa is the most potent symbol at the heart of the conflict, capable of morally justifying and sacralizing violence,” one section reminded, quoting an academic study ￼. Naveh urged the political leadership to step back before it was too late.
These warnings, however, ran up against a wall of ideology and political calculation. Within the National Security Council and cabinet, a new narrative took hold: that the threats were manageable. Pro-government strategists argued that the Arab world was too divided and war-weary to unite against Israel now. They pointed to the Abraham Accord states – UAE, Bahrain, even Saudi Arabia – whose monarchs had grown economically entwined with Israel and reliant on Western support. “Riyadh won’t throw away billions in deals over the old city of Jerusalem,” one Israeli diplomat confidently asserted in a meeting, noting how Gulf leaders had muted their reactions so far. As for military threats, Israel’s leadership was perhaps overconfident in the nation’s technological edge. By 2037, Israel had deployed new laser air-defense systems and AI-enhanced surveillance networks. The Defense Minister touted that “Fortress Israel” could withstand rocket bombardments better than in the past, and that preemptive strikes could decapitate enemy capabilities if needed. In essence, the ideologues convinced themselves and many others that any blowback would be contained, not existential.
Amid these internal rifts, one notable voice of caution came from an unexpected quarter: the Chief of the Israeli Shin Bet’s Jewish Extremism Division. This officer had spent his career monitoring Jewish ultra-nationalist groups. Now he warned that the government’s actions were emboldening exactly the radicals they used to keep in check. Indeed, Jewish extremist cells (fringe elements even to the government) began plotting unsanctioned actions – for instance, a scheme to smuggle explosives onto the Mount to “finish the job” by blowing up what remained of Al-Aqsa’s foundation. Throughout late 2037, Israeli society itself grew more polarized and anxious. Secular and center-left Israelis, a minority in this scenario, demonstrated in Tel Aviv with slogans like “Save Al-Aqsa, Save Israel”, fearing the nation was courting disaster. The trauma of recent conflicts with Hamas and Hezbollah (notably the brutal 2023 Hamas terror attack and subsequent Gaza war) had hardened public opinion. It formally abolished the Waqf’s administrative role and brought the site under Israeli Ministry of Religious Services management. International reactions were furious in rhetoric – the UN Security Council convened (the U.S. vetoed any punitive resolution), and Muslim-majority nations blasted Israel – but there was no collective action. In Israeli intelligence circles there was a grim joke that, “We’ve jumped off the cliff and found we can fly – so far.” But as the spring of 2038 arrived, events were hurtling toward a point of no return.
Part II: The Temple Rises – Collapse of the Old Order (2038–2040)
The Demolition of Al-Aqsa – Point of No Return
On May 14, 2038, a date already resonant as Israel’s 90th Independence Day, the status of Jerusalem was about to reach a catastrophic inflection. Despite mounting tensions, Israeli authorities had continued with “rehabilitation” work on the Temple Mount. The remaining shell of the Al-Aqsa Mosque – stripped of minaret, many walls propped by scaffolds – still loomed over the southern end of the compound, a poignant reminder of what once was. That morning, as Israeli VIPs attended Independence Day ceremonies across town, an earth-shattering explosion ripped through the Temple Mount. A fireball and thick black smoke rose from the southeastern corner of the compound – precisely where the last remnants of Al-Aqsa stood. Within seconds, the historic mosque’s weakened shell collapsed in a heap of rubble. The ground tremor was felt across the Old City. Within an hour, as videos began flooding media channels, the horrifying truth emerged: Al-Aqsa Mosque was gone – not carefully deconstructed stone by stone, but obliterated in one terrible moment.
Both sides – Israelis and Palestinians – immediately traded accusations over what had happened. The Israeli government claimed that “extremist terrorists” had detonated a bomb at the mosque. Indeed, they swiftly pointed to the arrest weeks prior of Jewish extremists with explosives, insinuating perhaps Palestinian militants had similarly stashed bombs to cast blame on Israel or to martyr Al-Aqsa. Palestinian officials, in turn, accused Israel of a deliberate act of sabotage. In their narrative, Israel had grown impatient and decided to erase the mosque under the cover of an “accident.” Conspiracy theories abounded: some Arab media claimed Israel’s agents planted explosives under the mosque during engineering works; others suggested an errant Israeli missile or drone strike. No independent investigation would ever fully settle the matter. What mattered is that perception was cemented rapidly in the minds of hundreds of millions: Israel had destroyed Al-Aqsa.
The immediate aftermath in Jerusalem was chaos. Thousands of Palestinian Jerusalemites poured into the streets, wailing in grief and fury at the sight of their beloved mosque reduced to smoking debris. Clashes erupted citywide – from the Old City alleys to Shuafat refugee camp. Israeli police and Border Police, already on high alert, responded with live ammunition when overwhelmed by armed Palestinian gunmen who emerged amid the protests. By nightfall on May 14, Jerusalem resembled a war zone: gunfire echoed, helicopters circled overhead, and fires burned in several Arab neighborhoods. The Israeli government, asserting that it had evidence of a “terrorist bomb plot,” imposed a military curfew on East Jerusalem and mobilized reserve Border Police battalions.
King Abdullah of Jordan, appearing visibly shaken on state TV, denounced the “blatant crime by Israeli occupation forces” and declared that “peace has died today.” He announced Jordan’s immediate withdrawal from the peace treaty with Israel – a dramatic step he had long hesitated to take. Across the Middle East, from Rabat to Islamabad, massive crowds swarmed the streets after Friday prayers, chanting for the liberation of Jerusalem. Even in the Gulf states that had normalized with Israel, governments had to allow public protests to vent anger. In Cairo, the Israeli embassy was evacuated just ahead of a mob that torched the compound. The entire region was ignited by an unprecedented wave of popular rage. Al-Aqsa – the third holiest site in Islam – was seen as having been martyred, and a cause for holy war was now on every tongue.
Inside Israel’s war rooms, the scenario long feared (and foretold in those Shin Bet warnings) was now materializing at speed. The IDF braced for what they code-named “Operation Solomon’s Wall” – a defensive contingency for multi-front conflict. Within 48 hours of Al-Aqsa’s fall, the following flashpoints erupted:
•	The West Bank: In cities like Nablus, Hebron, Jenin, and Ramallah, tens of thousands of Palestinians rose up, many armed with guns smuggled in over recent years. The Palestinian Authority, already weakened and delegitimized, effectively disintegrated. Palestinian security forces either melted away or joined the unrest. An improvised coalition of militant factions (from Fatah’s Al-Aqsa Martyrs to Hamas and Islamic Jihad cells) declared a Third Intifada. This uprising was far more militarized than the stone-throwing protests of decades past. Militants seized control of several PA police armories. Running gun battles with IDF troops broke out on multiple fronts. Israeli military convoys rushing to reinforce checkpoints were ambushed. Within a week, the IDF found itself re-engaging in parts of the West Bank it had not entered in years, effectively fighting to re-occupy cities amidst fierce resistance. The tactics were brutal urban warfare – artillery and drone strikes in refugee camps like Balata and Jenin, met by sniper fire and IEDs from Palestinian fighters. Casualties mounted into the thousands. The West Bank had become a full-fledged battlefield.
•	Gaza and Israel’s South: Though Gaza had been devastated in the 2023 war, Hamas and Islamic Jihad remnants seized the Al-Aqsa crisis as a divine mandate to resume jihad. Rocket fire on southern Israel resumed at a scale not seen since the early 2020s. Within 72 hours, over 2,000 rockets and mortar shells were launched from Gaza toward Israeli towns and cities, including a few longer-range missiles that reached the outskirts of Tel Aviv. Israel’s multi-tiered missile defenses (Iron Dome, David’s Sling, and the new Iron Beam laser) intercepted many ￼, but not all – some overwhelmed batteries caused casualties in Ashkelon and Be’er Sheva. The IDF responded with massive airstrikes across Gaza. But unlike previous rounds, this time Egypt – usually a mediator – kept its border sealed and lambasted Israel’s actions, even as the humanitarian crisis in Gaza deepened. A small but symbolically shocking development: a brigade of volunteer fighters from Egypt’s Sinai (bedouin and other Islamists, apparently unchecked by Cairo in the chaos) tried to breach the Gaza border to join the battle. Though largely repelled by the IDF, their mere appearance signaled how the conflict was spilling beyond its old bounds.
•	Lebanon and Syria – The Northern Front: On May 18, Hezbollah’s leader Hassan Nasrallah gave a fiery speech declaring “Jerusalem’s Al-Aqsa calls out to us. We will answer.” Hours later, Hezbollah unleashed its arsenal. A barrage of over 500 precision-guided missiles and drones rained down on northern and central Israel in the first day of fighting. Key infrastructure was targeted: the oil refineries in Haifa were hit, starting a massive fire, and several IDF bases and radar stations were struck with surprising accuracy. Israeli Arrow-3 and Patriot batteries managed to shoot down a handful of the heavier missiles, but the sheer volume meant some got through. Simultaneously, along the northern border, Hezbollah and allied Shiite militias from Iraq (brought into Syria by Iran) attempted cross-border raids.
