**The argument is always offered as a choice.**

Are you with the resistance, or are you with the state? Are you with Hezbollah, or with sovereignty? Are you with defending the south, or are you with disarming the only force that has actually fought Israel?

The question sounds serious because the stakes are serious. Land is being occupied. Villages are being destroyed. People are being displaced. A country that cannot defend itself is not a country for very long.

But the choice, as presented, is not a choice. It is an apparatus. And understanding what the apparatus does requires understanding what it is protecting — not Lebanon, but the structure that has replaced Lebanon for the past eighty years.

**Every major Lebanese political leader commands a vertical slice of society.**

Not a class. Not an interest. A column. Rich and poor, banker and depositor, landlord and tenant, contractor and worker, employer and unemployed — all sealed beneath one confessional roof. The za'im does not represent his community's interests in the way a trade union or a political party might. He represents its existence as a unit. His power derives not from delivering outcomes but from maintaining the column's coherence.

This is the machine's central problem. And also its central solution.

The moment any leader talks about taxation, they split their own column. The banker and the depositor want opposite things. The landlord and the tenant want opposite things. The contractor and the worker want opposite things. Real policy — the kind that distributes costs and benefits, that tells you who pays and who gains — tears the vertical coalition apart. It forces people to vote their class instead of their confession. It builds horizontal solidarity with strangers who share their economic position across every sect. It makes the zu'ama obsolete.

So every sectarian leader is structurally prohibited from doing politics. Not by personal cowardice. By the architecture of survival.

What holds the column together, then, if not policy? The enemy outside it. Talk about Israel and the column coheres. Talk about Iran and the column coheres. Talk about American interference, Saudi money, Western pressure — the column coheres. Every sectarian leader is fluent in this language because it is the only language that does not split his base. The arms question is not the central question of Lebanese politics because it is the most important question. It is the central question because it is the only question that can be asked without fracturing anyone's coalition.

**This is why they need each other.**

Not out of cynicism. Not by conspiracy. By structural necessity.

The Lebanese Forces need Hezbollah's weapons to remain the permanent proof of their own politics. Without Hezbollah as the armed sectarian threat, Geagea has to answer a different set of questions: what is your economic program? What happens to the depositors who lost their savings? What is your position on labor rights, electricity, the port reconstruction, the banks? These questions fracture the Christian vertical coalition between those who benefit from the current order and those who are buried by it. Hezbollah as enemy is the roof that keeps that contradiction from cracking open.

Hezbollah needs the Lebanese Forces, and the wider anti-resistance camp, to remain the foreign-aligned force aimed at disarming the Shia community on Israel's behalf. Without that threat, Hezbollah faces its own community's version of the same questions: you have spent twenty years inside the Lebanese government. Why is the electricity still broken? Why did you protect the same banking order that destroyed Shia depositors alongside every other depositor? Why did you leave the state hollow while claiming to need a strong state before disarmament? The Israeli threat is the roof that keeps those questions from being asked.

Each faction's enemy is the other's alibi. The binary is not a failure of politics. It is the form politics takes in a system where real politics would be suicidal.

**The anti-resistance argument begins with one truth.**

No normal republic can have a private army deciding when the whole country goes to war. That is correct. Hezbollah opened the Lebanese front without a national decision. It fused a real defensive need with an Iranian regional strategy. It built a state within the state, then used the weakness of the state as proof that its own parallel authority must remain.

But then the anti-resistance camp asks us to make a leap it has not earned. Give the weapons to the state. Which state?

The state that has not built electricity. The state that cannot protect a judge investigating the port explosion. The state that let the banks swallow people's savings and shielded those responsible. The state that kept the army underfunded, the courts exposed, the ministries divided, the ports captured, and every appointment trapped in sectarian arithmetic.

You cannot demand that a community surrender its defensive capacity to an abstraction. You cannot tell people in the south, after decades of occupation, bombardment, displacement, and abandonment, that the same state which never arrived for them will now protect them because a minister used the word sovereignty at a press conference. That is not a plan. It is theatre — and it is theatre that serves a function, which is to keep the anti-resistance column unified around an enemy while avoiding every question about what an actual state would require from the people currently asking for it.

**The resistance argument begins with its own truth.**

Israel is not imaginary. Its invasions, occupation, and habit of converting security language into territorial control are not imaginary. The south did not invent its fear. Hezbollah did not appear because Lebanese Shia woke up one morning with a taste for militias. It appeared in the vacuum left by a state that did not defend them, did not serve them, did not count them.

But Hezbollah has turned that historical fact into a permanent license. Because the state failed, Hezbollah is necessary. Because Hezbollah is necessary, the state can never fully form. Because the state never forms, Hezbollah remains necessary. The circle is not an accident. It is the arrangement. What began as a response to abandonment has become one of the forces that profits from the conditions of abandonment — not by design, but by the same structural logic that governs every other faction. Hezbollah is not an exception to the cartel. It is the cartel's most heavily armed member.

**The deeper machine is not the weapons. It is what the weapons protect.**

A real state would not only absorb Hezbollah's military role. It would audit everyone. It would ask where the bank money went, who controls the ports, who profits from generators, fuel, customs, quarries, contracts, schools, hospitals, charities, ministries, and municipalities. It would force parties to become political parties instead of sectarian protection rackets. It would force the vertical slice open — and when the vertical slice opens, the banker and the depositor find themselves on opposite sides, the contractor and the worker find themselves on opposite sides, and suddenly Lebanese people are voting their interests instead of their identities.

That is what they all fear. The state is not the missing thing. The state is the threat.

**October 2019 was the proof.**

When "all of them means all of them" briefly escaped the binary — when the argument became structural, became about the cartel rather than its camps — every faction responded simultaneously. Foreign conspiracy. Anti-Hezbollah plot. Anti-Shia plot. Anti-Christian plot. Anti-Sunni plot. Anything except the obvious thing: the cartel had been named, and the cartel's immune system activated across every column at once. The uprising was not defeated by one side. It was smothered by all of them together, because for a moment it had asked the wrong question — the real question — and the system has only one response to that.

The port explosion should have forced the reckoning again. It did not. The dead were mourned. The judges were blocked. The files were buried. Lebanon did not lack grief. It lacked consequences — because consequences, in a system where every investigative thread leads eventually to someone's column, are what the vertical coalition exists to prevent.

**So where does that leave us?**

Not neutral. Neutrality here would be obscene. Israel is the external aggressor. The south has a right to be defended. The people living under bombardment are not props in an argument about Hezbollah — they are Lebanese citizens whose fear is real, whose dead are real, whose land is real.

But defending the land does not mean accepting Hezbollah's permanent exception. And rejecting that exception does not mean accepting the fantasy that a hollow state, funded and pressured from abroad, can become sovereign by demanding disarmament it has never prepared itself to receive.

The honest position is harder than either slogan. Israel is the immediate external threat. Hezbollah is both a response to state abandonment and a participant in the system that profits from it. The anti-resistance camp is correct that no republic survives a private army — and fraudulent when it offers no republic capable of replacing one. The cartel is the deeper machine. The vertical coalition is the mechanism. The binary is the product they sell to keep both columns standing.

The question is not whether Lebanon should defend itself. Of course it should.

The question is whether Lebanon will ever build a state strong enough to make defense national instead of sectarian, and politics something more serious than two columns pointing at each other's gun while the people inside each column lose everything except their identity.

Until then, every faction will keep pointing at the other side's weapons, patron, corruption, and betrayal.

And all of them will keep hoping that nobody asks the question that would end them:

*If your enemy disappeared tomorrow, what would you have to say to your own people?*
