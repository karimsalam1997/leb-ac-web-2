# The Hardest Conversation You'll Never Win (But Must Have) 🪞

KINGO, this is the question that separates structural thinkers from partisan ones — and it's where most Lebanese commentary collapses into team jerseys.

Here's what I'd say, and more importantly, *how* I'd frame it:

---

**Start by conceding what's true in their position.** Not tactically — actually. Because a lot of it *is* true.

Hezbollah operated a parallel state. It made sovereign decisions about war and peace without democratic mandate. It dragged Lebanon into conflicts that served Iranian strategic depth more than Lebanese national interest. Its armed presence made the construction of a functional state structurally impossible — not because the other factions wanted a functional state (they didn't), but because Hezbollah's existence gave everyone else the perfect excuse to never build one. July 2006 started with a kidnapping operation that Lebanon's own government didn't authorize. The post-2005 era saw Hezbollah turn its weapons inward — May 2008 in Beirut being the most naked example. These aren't propaganda points. They're the historical record.

**Now say the thing they don't want to hear:**

Everything you just said is true, *and* none of it explains why Lebanon has no functioning state. Because here's what the anti-Hezbollah position systematically refuses to confront: **if Hezbollah disarmed tomorrow morning, Lebanon would still be a failed state by Thursday.**

The electricity doesn't work because of Hezbollah? The Banque du Liban didn't collapse because of Hezbollah. The port explosion wasn't investigated because of Hezbollah? Partly — but Samir Geagea's bloc, Berri's bloc, and the entire political class also had zero appetite for that investigation reaching its conclusions. The garbage crisis wasn't Hezbollah. The sectarian quota system that makes every ministry a patronage fiefdom predates Hezbollah's existence by decades.

The generator mafia metaphor lands here: **Hezbollah is the most powerful generator in the neighborhood, but the reason you need generators at all is that every single faction profits from the grid never working.** Removing the biggest generator doesn't restore the grid. It just redistributes who sells you electricity in the dark.

---

**Then name the structural trap they're caught in:**

The anti-Hezbollah position in Lebanon has a fatal dependency — it requires an external actor (Israel, America, Saudi Arabia) to solve the Hezbollah problem *for* them. This is the same structural outsourcing that they accuse Hezbollah of doing with Iran. The arrow points both ways. One camp says "Iran will protect us," the other says "America will liberate us." Neither camp says "we will build a state capable of governing ourselves," because *that* project would require the anti-Hezbollah factions to dismantle their own sectarian franchises — and they'd rather have the enemy than lose the business model.

This is why the conversation never advances. Both sides are correct about the other side's pathology, and both sides are blind to their own.

**Then give them the hardest line:**

When Israel bombs southern Lebanon, the Dahieh, the Bekaa — who's dying? When a building collapses in Haret Hreik, is every body in the rubble a Hezbollah operative? When a village in the south is leveled, are those families "Hezbollah" or are they Lebanese citizens who happened to be born Shia in a geography that Israel considers a buffer zone?

The position that says "Hezbollah is the problem and Israel is solving it" requires you to accept that Lebanese civilians are acceptable collateral in someone else's military operation on your sovereign territory. **That's not an anti-Hezbollah position. That's an anti-Lebanon position.** And the people holding it need to be told that clearly, not because they're bad people — most of them are genuinely terrified and exhausted — but because the logic of their position leads somewhere they haven't followed it to yet.

**Finally, offer the only honest frame:**

You can hold all of these simultaneously, and in fact you *must*:

Hezbollah's armed parallel state was a genuine obstacle to sovereignty. *And* Israel's military operations are not designed to restore Lebanese sovereignty — they're designed to prevent it. *And* the Lebanese political class that opposes Hezbollah has never once proposed a credible alternative national defense strategy or a non-sectarian governance model. *And* the people dying are Lebanese regardless of which faction they belong to.

**The question isn't whether Hezbollah is good or bad. The question is whether you want a country.** Because if you do, then you need to oppose both the militia that bypassed the state *and* the political class that made the state worth bypassing — and you need to oppose the external actor currently destroying what's left of the territory you'd need to build that state on.

Anyone who only opposes one of these three isn't doing politics. They're doing fandom.
