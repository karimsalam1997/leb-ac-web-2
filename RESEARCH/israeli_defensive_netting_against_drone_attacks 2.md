## The shame of the chain-link fence

Picture, for a moment, the soldier inside the Merkava. He has been told his entire life that he sits inside the most sophisticated tank on earth, a rolling cathedral of armor and optics, the inheritor of seven decades of military doctrine that fused German Panzer engineering with American industrial scale and Israeli paranoia into a single object that was supposed to make Jewish bodies, finally and forever, unkillable. And now his commanders are draping fishing net over it. Fishing nets, camouflage nets, even soccer nets, along with drills involving small-arms fire at drones. A senior IDF official tells the press the formal solution is "highly problematic" because it leaves the lower portions exposed and the adversary will simply detonate the drone at a distance from the net, causing shrapnel to disperse toward the forces.

This is what defeat looks like before anyone is ready to call it defeat. Not collapse. Not retreat. The slow, embarrassed installation of chicken wire over a thirty-million-dollar vehicle, by men who twenty years ago believed history had ended in their favor.

Your instinct on efficacy is right, and the IDF itself is telling on itself in the reporting. The current solution is an experimental concept rather than something that is already operational in the field. Detection is the actual problem, not interception: soldiers in the field have repeatedly reported spotting drones only seconds before impact. The new "detection kits" being rushed to the front, the IDF privately admits, do not guarantee a 100% success rate, perhaps not even 90%, but are expected to give troops the precious seconds needed to take cover. Read that sentence again. The strategic doctrine of the Middle East's most advanced military, the army that was supposed to win the Iran war through clairvoyant precision, has been reduced to *giving the boys a second or two to duck*.

The Lebanese mountain is not a difficult problem of military occupation. It is *the* problem. It has been the problem for four thousand years.
