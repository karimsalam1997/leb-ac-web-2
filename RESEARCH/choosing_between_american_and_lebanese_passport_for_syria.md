**KINGO, the answer is: enter on your Lebanese passport.** Here's the full reasoning, because the logic runs against the reflex and you should understand *why*, not just *that*.

**The structural asymmetry.** Post-Assad Syria (the Sharaa transitional government) rewrote the visa regime in January 2025. After the fall of the Assad regime, the Syrian transitional government announced a new visa policy, and Lebanese and Iranian citizens received different treatment from the rest of the world — all other nationalities must either obtain a prior e-visa or pay a visa-on-arrival fee based on a "principle of reciprocity." Lebanese passports are not in the "restricted" bucket that triggers weeks of security clearance, and they are not subject to the American/European visa fee schedule. In practice, Lebanese nationals crossing at Masnaa–Jdeideh pass through the smoothest, cheapest channel that exists — this is the one corridor the new Damascus regime deliberately kept porous, because the economies on both sides of the Anti-Lebanon range are now stitched together by Syrian return migration, trade, and labour flows.

**The American passport is the expensive, suspicious option.** On an American document, you are a US citizen entering a country where the U.S. Embassy in Damascus is currently suspended, the U.S. government cannot provide emergency or routine services to U.S. citizens in Syria, and U.S. citizens in Syria seeking emergency help must contact the Czech Embassy in Damascus, which serves as the protecting power. You pay a cash USD visa fee, you get flagged for extra review — as a safe planning rule, if your passport requires extra review, allow up to 15 working days (for example, United States of America and Indonesia can sometimes take longer than average) — and if anything goes sideways, Washington has no one on the ground to call on your behalf. You also inherit, gratuitously, the entire political baggage of being an American in a country still negotiating its relationship to US sanctions and Caesar Act residue. None of that touches a Lebanese passport holder crossing at Masnaa.

**The hard rule both passports share.** If your passport shows a visit to Israel, or you hold Israeli nationality, you will not be allowed entry into Syria under any circumstances. Check both books before you go. Stamps from the Egyptian or Jordanian border crossings adjacent to Israel count as evidence of travel to Israel and will also get you refused.

**The procedural rule that catches people.** You use the same passport entering and leaving. If you hold dual nationality, the passport you use to enter Lebanon or Jordan must be the same passport you use to enter Syria, and attempting to switch passports at the land border may result in revoked entry. For you, entering from Lebanon, this is moot on the Lebanese side — you are a Lebanese citizen on Lebanese soil, so you use your Lebanese ID or passport to exit Lebanon anyway, which aligns cleanly with using the Lebanese passport to enter Syria. Do not hand one passport to the Lebanese officer at Masnaa and a different one to the Syrian officer fifty metres later. That is the precise manoeuvre that gets flagged.

**The practical kit for the crossing.**
- Bring USD in small bills — ATMs do not work in Syria and credit cards are not accepted; exchange to USD before arriving, especially coming from Lebanon
- Masnaa–Jdeideh is open 06:00 AM to 11:00 PM — don't cut it close to closing
- Passport valid for at least three months, one blank page minimum
- If you are returning to Lebanon by land, budget the ~$12 Lebanese re-entry stamp fee

**One thing worth saying, because it's your project and not just your trip.** If you're going in for the usual reasons — Aleppo, Palmyra, Ugarit, Mari, the coast, the churches of the Qalamoun — this advice holds. If you're going in anywhere near sensitive ground (border zones with Israeli-occupied territory in the southwest, the remaining contested pockets in the northeast, anywhere security services take an interest in your cameras or your notebook), the calculus changes. The Lebanese passport that eases your entry also strips you of the thin diplomatic scaffolding an American passport notionally provides. In a country where U.S. citizens have been targets of abduction and unjust detention by non-state armed groups and by the previous Assad regime, neither passport is a shield — but the Lebanese one is at least a native costume.

Enter as a Lebanese. Leave as a Lebanese. Keep the American one in a zipped inner pocket as an emergency document you hope never to need.
