# Flagship Essay

## The Cartel in the Costume of a Country

**Final title:** The Cartel in the Costume of a Country  
**Dek:** Lebanon is not a failed state. It is a successful system for preventing a state from being born.  
**Byline:** Karim Salam  
**Publication date:** April 30, 2026  
**Estimated reading time:** 11 minutes  
**Pull quote:** "The binary does not fail to produce a state. Not producing a state is the function the binary performs."

**Suggested footnotes/citations:**

1. Max Weber, "Politics as a Vocation," for the state as a monopoly on legitimate force.
2. Lebanese Constitution, Article 95, on the staged abolition of political confessionalism: [Constitutional Council](https://cc.gov.lb/en/constitution-and-laws/constitution/final-and-temporary-provisions/).
3. The Taif Agreement, English text: [UN Lebanon PDF](https://www.un.int/lebanon/sites/www.un.int/files/Lebanon/the_taif_agreement_english_version_.pdf).
4. World Bank, *Lebanon Economic Monitor: The Great Denial*, on elite capture and deliberate depression: [World Bank](https://www.worldbank.org/en/news/press-release/2022/01/24/lebanon-s-crisis-great-denial-in-the-deliberate-depression).
5. Human Rights Watch and Amnesty International on obstruction of the Beirut port investigation: [HRW](https://www.hrw.org/news/2025/08/04/lebanon-five-years-without-justice-port-explosion-victims).
6. Melani Cammett, *Compassionate Communalism*, on sectarian welfare and political loyalty.
7. UN Security Council Resolution 425, on Israeli withdrawal and UNIFIL: [UNSCR](https://unscr.com/en/resolutions/425/).
8. Le Monde on Israel's current "yellow line" buffer zone in southern Lebanon: [Le Monde](https://www.lemonde.fr/en/international/article/2026/04/23/in-lebanon-israel-establishes-an-uninhabited-buffer-zone-behind-a-yellow-line_6752747_4.html).

**Full body:**

The phrase "failed state" is too generous to Lebanon. It assumes there was a state trying to perform its functions and failing at them. It imagines a machine built for public purpose, now broken by incompetence, corruption, war, poverty, bad luck. But Lebanon is more exact than that. Its weakness is not an accident. Its weakness is administered.

A state, in the plain political sense, is an authority that can tax, judge, defend, decide, and enforce. It collects revenue without begging its citizens through sectarian intermediaries. It commands one army. It decides war and peace. It punishes theft even when the thief belongs to a powerful family. Lebanon performs these gestures without possessing the thing behind them. It has ministries, flags, uniforms, delegations, communiques, and embassies. It does not have sovereignty in the operational sense. It has a franchise system wearing a republic's clothes.

The founding wound was not that Lebanon was divided. Many countries are divided. The wound was that division was made into the operating system. The National Pact of 1943 was not a civic social contract. It was an unwritten allocation of offices among communal elites. The presidency, premiership, speakership, command posts, directorates, and eventually almost every public appointment became sectarian property before they became national responsibility. Taif, in 1989, did not abolish this logic. It reorganized it after the war. The warlords were not defeated by the state. They entered it.

This is why the argument over "resistance" and "sovereignty" has become the perfect Lebanese argument. It is morally charged enough to feel ultimate and structurally narrow enough to prevent the real question from appearing. Are you with the resistance or against it? Are you with the state or against the militia? Are you with Iran or the West? Each question sounds decisive. Each one keeps the citizen inside the room the cartel built.

The missing question is simpler: did anyone ever intend to build a state?

The resistance camp has a real historical wound behind it. Southern Lebanon was abandoned for decades. Israel invaded, occupied, bombed, and returned. Villages learned that the Lebanese state arrived late, if it arrived at all. Hezbollah did not emerge from fantasy. It emerged from a vacuum, and vacuums are never innocent. A community that has been repeatedly left undefended will not be persuaded by lectures from those who never paid the same price.

But Hezbollah has also been inside the state for two decades. It had ministers, MPs, veto power, alliances, budgets, and bargaining weight. It demanded a strong state as the condition for disarmament, while helping preserve the political order that made such a state impossible. It did not spend its political capital building an army able to replace its own role. It did not make tax capacity, judicial independence, or national defense doctrine the center of its statecraft. It protected a parallel authority because that authority was the basis of its power.

The anti-resistance camp has its own real wound. It is not madness to object to a non-state army deciding war and peace. It is not colonialism to want one authority, one defense policy, one law. No functioning republic can tolerate a military actor that can commit the whole country to consequences the whole country did not choose.

But this camp also spent twenty years asking Hezbollah to hand its weapons to a state it never built. It demanded disarmament without constructing the institutional capacity that would make disarmament anything other than demilitarization. It made "sovereignty" its television language while relying on embassies, donors, Gulf money, American guarantees, and foreign pressure. It wanted the word state, not the cost of one.

This is the core fraud. Both camps need the state as rhetoric and fear it as reality. A real state would force Hezbollah to submit its military decision to public authority. It would also force the Lebanese Forces, Amal, the PSP, the Kataeb, the FPM, and the Sunni successor formations to submit their finances, patronage networks, judicial protections, municipal fiefdoms, and foreign relationships to scrutiny. A state strong enough to absorb Hezbollah is a state strong enough to audit everyone.

More deeply, a state would force Lebanese parties to have politics. Not identity. Not grievance. Politics. Tax policy, labor law, electricity reform, school curricula, banking accountability, housing, public transport, agriculture, military procurement. These are the questions real parties answer. Lebanese sectarian parties cannot answer them because each one is a vertical slice through its sect. Rich and poor, employer and worker, landlord and tenant, depositor and banker, all are kept in one confessional column. Any serious economic position would split the column. The resistance question keeps it together.

The binary does not fail to produce a state. Not producing a state is the function the binary performs.

The generator explains the country better than most constitutions. The public grid fails. A private operator fills the failure. The operator becomes indispensable. The politician protects him. The citizen pays twice, once for the absent state and once for the private substitute. Reform becomes dangerous because it would remove the substitute through which loyalty and money flow. Scale this outward and you have Lebanon: private schools replacing education, private hospitals replacing health, party charities replacing welfare, militias replacing defense, banks replacing economic policy, sects replacing citizenship.

The weak state is not the thing the cartel failed to fix. It is the market the cartel controls.

That market is not only domestic. Every Lebanese faction is also an embassy in local costume. Iran, Syria, Saudi Arabia, France, the United States, Israel, Turkey, Qatar, the UAE, each has rented Lebanese weakness in one form or another. A sovereign Lebanese state would have to say no. A cartel Lebanon never has to say no because no one voice speaks for it. Every patron can find a faction. Every faction can find a patron. The country becomes a floor plan for other people's strategies.

October 2019 was the test. For a brief moment, Lebanese anger escaped the binary. "All of them means all of them" was dangerous because it named the cartel as a cartel. It did not say one sect had stolen the state from another. It said the state had been stolen through all of them. The response came quickly. Every faction worked to return the uprising to familiar lines. Hezbollah called it foreign. Others tried to narrow it to Hezbollah. Amal attacked camps. Old parties tried to harvest new anger. The structure recognized the threat before many protesters did.

The port explosion was the second test. Two hundred lives lost, a capital wounded, a paper trail of warnings, negligence, and impunity. If any event could have forced a state to appear, it was that. Instead the investigation itself became evidence of the thing it was investigating. Judges were threatened, sued, obstructed, removed, paralyzed. The dead were mourned publicly and abandoned procedurally. The system did not deny grief. It absorbed grief, televised grief, commemorated grief, and refused accountability.

This is why the current war and the current negotiations cannot be understood through the language being offered. A Lebanese government may sit at a table. That does not mean Lebanon, as a sovereign actor, is sitting there. A cabinet may issue a position. That does not mean the position binds the ground. A foreign envoy may speak of disarmament, stabilization, withdrawal, reconstruction. These words assume the existence of an authority capable of delivering them. Lebanon has a performance of authority, distributed among people whose private survival depends on keeping authority incomplete.

None of this means neutrality. Israel is an external threat. Its history in Lebanon is not imaginary; it is written in occupations, invasions, bombardments, and now in renewed buffer-zone fantasies. Southern communities have paid in land, bodies, displacement, and memory. Any Lebanese politics that treats their exposure as acceptable collateral is not sovereign politics. It is sectarian resentment with a passport.

Nor does the reality of Israeli aggression absolve Hezbollah. The party turned a legitimate defense need into a permanent parallel authority. It fused community protection with regional strategy. It made the south defensible in one register and politically captive in another. It is possible, and necessary, to say both things without letting either side recruit the sentence.

The position worth holding is not a middle position. It is a harder position. Israel is the primary external aggressor. The cartel is the enduring internal arrangement. Hezbollah is both a response to abandonment and a participant in the system that profits from abandonment. The anti-resistance camp is right that no republic can survive a private army, and fraudulent when it offers no republic capable of replacing it. The state is the name of the missing thing, not the property of either camp.

What follows from this is not despair. It is discipline. Refuse the binary's frame. Distinguish communities from the parties that claim them. Defend Lebanese territory without sanctifying every armed actor that fights on it. Demand army capacity without turning the army into a factional instrument. Defend judges, journalists, auditors, archivists, teachers, and civic institutions because these are the tissues from which a state is eventually made. Direct diaspora money away from sectarian channels where possible. Build a language in which young Lebanese can imagine themselves as citizens before they are clients.

The cartel's greatest victory has been making the Lebanese forget that a state is not a speech. It is not a flag, a presidential portrait, a ministerial convoy, or a donor conference. It is a structure that can outlive the men who occupy it. Lebanon has had men. It has had many. It has had heroes, martyrs, billionaires, generals, warlords, clerics, bankers, and mourners. What it has not had is a state strong enough to make these men less important.

That is the task. Not to choose the better costume. To stop mistaking costume for country.

## The Mehtail Republic

**Final title:** The Mehtail Republic
**Dek:** The Lebanese person is not corrupt. He is the most rational actor in the most irrational system on earth.
**Byline:** Karim Salam
**Publication date:** May 1, 2026
**Estimated reading time:** 9 min read
**Topic tags:** Political Economy, Society, State Failure
**Excerpt:** The Lebanese person is not corrupt. He is the most rational actor in the most irrational system on earth — a man who learned, across generations of evidence, that the only governance unit that ever worked was himself.
**Pull quote:** "The mehtail did not create the dysfunction. He adapted to it. The adaptation became culture. The culture now reproduces the dysfunction."

**Full body:**

The Lebanese person is not corrupt. He is the most rational actor in the most irrational system on earth — a man who learned, across generations of evidence, that the only governance unit that ever worked was himself.

This is the argument that Lebanese people themselves resist most violently, because it implicates them in a structure they also hate. But hatred of a system and participation in it are not contradictions. They are the definition of a trap.

Start with the driving. Every visitor to Beirut notices it within ten minutes and frames it as evidence of some essential Lebanese character defect — anarchic, individualistic, unable to follow rules. This is the wrong diagnosis. The Lebanese driver is not ignoring traffic law. He is correctly reading an environment where traffic law is a suggestion issued by an institution that cannot enforce it, and where every other driver has made the same calculation simultaneously. The double-parked Mercedes blocking a lane is a man who has understood, at a cellular level, that public space is simply unclaimed private space. He is wrong morally. He is correct analytically. The enforcement apparatus does not exist. He is playing the game as it is actually configured, not as it is formally described.

Scale that logic across every domain of Lebanese life and you have the country.

The generator. The private school. The wasta call to the ministry official. The building permit obtained through a cousin rather than an application. Each of these is an individually rational response to an institutionally broken system. The Lebanese person did not create the dysfunction. He adapted to it, as any organism adapts to its environment. The adaptation became culture. The culture now reproduces the dysfunction. This is the trap, and it has a name: the mehtail cycle.

The Arabic word mehtail resists clean translation. It is not simply a fraudster or criminal — it carries the connotation of strategic cunning, the player rather than the thief. A mehtail works within the rules by finding their gaps. The distinction matters: Lebanese social organization rewards the mehtail precisely because gap-finding is the primary survival skill in a system where formal rules are unenforced and informal networks are the actual infrastructure.

The mehtail emerges wherever the following conditions hold simultaneously: formal institutions exist on paper but deliver nothing in practice; informal networks deliver everything but only to those inside them; there is no credible enforcement of rules against those with sufficient connections; and the time horizon for any individual actor is short because the system has repeatedly demonstrated its capacity to collapse without warning. Under these conditions, the only rational move is to extract what you can, protect what you have, and extend trust only to those whose loyalty you can verify through kinship or long mutual dependence. Lebanon has run this experiment for eighty years. The results are in.

What makes Lebanon's version distinctive is the civilizational talent the system is wasting. Lebanese emigrant communities are among the most economically successful on earth. Carlos Slim built the largest private fortune in Latin American history from a Lebanese immigrant family. Lebanese merchants ran West African commerce for a century. In Australia, Brazil, Canada, the Gulf, wherever Lebanese communities settled with functioning state institutions around them, the same raw material — the cunning, the multilingualism, the commercial instinct, the adaptive intelligence — produced extraordinary outcomes.

Plugged into Lebanon, the same node produces blackouts.

The difference is not character. It is infrastructure. The Jewish diaspora survival model, which the Lebanese diaspora most resembles in its raw components, was built on a paradox: fierce individual ambition in service of collective persistence. The synagogue, the burial society, the lending circle, the political lobby — these were institutions that created a commons individual talent could draw from and feed back into. Lebanon has the processor. It ripped out the motherboard.

The Lebanese solidarity structure is not absent. It is precisely targeted. Watch a Lebanese family mobilize for a wedding, a funeral, a cousin's hospital bill. The coordination is immediate, total, and extraordinarily efficient. The same people who will not yield one centimeter in a traffic jam will mortgage their homes to cover a relative's debt. This is not a contradiction. It is two different systems running simultaneously on different logics.

The family and sect run on honor, shame, obligation, and reciprocity. It works. The state and public sphere run on extraction, cunning, and impunity. That also works — for those running it. What does not exist is the bridging layer: the institution that translates family-level solidarity into civic-level coordination. Every attempt to build that layer has been captured by the franchise holders and converted into another patronage node.

The 2019 uprising briefly demonstrated what Lebanese civic solidarity looks like when it exists: cross-sectarian, cross-class, organized around shared grievance rather than shared sect. It was crushed — by the combined force of every franchise holder, regardless of their position on Hezbollah or sovereignty or the Americans — because it was the only thing that has ever genuinely threatened the arrangement. The solidarity could improvise a tent city on Martyrs' Square. It could not outlast a banking collapse, a port explosion, and a pandemic. The bridging institution was not there.

The mehtail republic needs not moral reformation but the thing it has never had: an institutional environment in which the individually rational move and the collectively rational move are the same move. The Lebanese person is rational. The system is the problem. These are not consoling conclusions — they redirect blame from the citizen to the structure, and the structure has very effective defenders.

## The Census That Cannot Be Taken

**Final title:** The Census That Cannot Be Taken
**Dek:** Lebanon has not counted its population since 1932. It will not count it now.
**Byline:** Karim Salam
**Publication date:** May 1, 2026
**Estimated reading time:** 9 min read
**Topic tags:** Demographics, Sectarianism, Constitutional Reform
**Excerpt:** Lebanon has not counted its population since 1932. It will not count it now. A census is the one act the Lebanese political class unanimously agrees cannot happen — because the result would end the arrangement they have all spent their careers protecting.
**Pull quote:** "The state is the only entity on earth that cannot know how many citizens it has, because knowing would require it to become something other than what it is."

**Full body:**

Lebanon has not counted its population since 1932. It will not count it now. A census is the one act the Lebanese political class unanimously agrees cannot happen — because the result would end the arrangement they have all spent their careers protecting.

That is not hyperbole. It is the operating logic of the Lebanese state, stated plainly by its own architects. The 1943 National Pact was built on the French census of 1932, which recorded Christians as 54 percent of the population. The 6:5 Christian-to-Muslim parliamentary ratio flowed from that count. The Maronite presidency, the Sunni premiership, the Shia speakership — all of it hangs from a single demographic snapshot taken ninety-four years ago by a colonial power with a specific interest in the result it produced.

The French wanted a Christian-majority Lebanon. They got one, on paper, because they designed the census to get one. The 1932 census counted Lebanese emigrants, who were disproportionately Christian, as resident Lebanese citizens. It excluded or undercounted nomadic and rural Shia populations in the south and Bekaa. The French government's motivation was transparent — they had carved Grand Liban in 1920 specifically to create a Christian-majority polity. The census ratified their political project. Lebanon has been governed by that ratification ever since.

The French are gone. The demographics are not what the census recorded. Everyone in Lebanon knows this, discusses it in private, and refuses to address it in public. What the actual distribution looks like is itself a contested political act: every community claims the numbers that maximize its leverage. Shia political leaders quote figures suggesting their community comprises 35 to 40 percent of the resident population. Christian leaders maintain parity claims that require a demographic reality most demographers consider implausible. There is no agreed number for anything because agreement on numbers is agreement on power.

The parliament sits at 128 seats, split 64-64 between Christians and Muslims. This ratio was adjusted by Taif in 1989 from 6:5 to 1:1, not because the demographics supported it but because the civil war's power balance demanded it. It was a negotiated outcome between armed factions, not a democratic reflection of a citizen body. The internal allocation — Maronites get 34 seats, Sunnis 27, Shia 27 — was fixed by formula in 1989 and has not moved since. Every election since has been conducted inside this cage.

The army command is permanently Maronite. The central bank governor is conventionally Maronite. The head of general security is Shia. Every senior directorate in every ministry is a confessional slot filled according to a sectarian org chart so institutionally entrenched it operates automatically, without anyone needing to enforce it. This is not representation. It is a frozen photograph of a country that no longer exists, used to allocate power in a country that does.

The demographic reality that the census cannot name is roughly this: the Shia community is almost certainly the largest single confessional group in resident Lebanon today. Civil war emigration hit Christian communities hardest. Post-war emigration from the 1990s through the 2019 banking collapse accelerated in communities with the strongest diaspora networks — again, disproportionately Christian and Sunni. The Shia community has the lowest emigration rate and highest birth rate among Lebanon's major sects. By most credible estimates, they comprise somewhere between 30 and 38 percent of the resident population.

The political system allocates them 27 parliamentary seats out of 128. Their actual population entitlement under proportional representation would be substantially higher. This gap — between demographic weight and political representation — is one structural reason Hezbollah's arms are politically sustainable within the Shia community. The weapons compensate for the representation deficit. They are the army of a community that the state's arithmetic has systematically underweighted since a colonial census was designed to underweight it.

Consider what a census would actually require politically. It would require agreement on who counts as Lebanese — which immediately surfaces the Palestinian refugee question (approximately 200,000 registered residents, mostly Sunni Muslim, whose political status has been deliberately frozen for seventy-five years because their naturalization would shift the sectarian balance), the Syrian refugee question, and the diaspora question. None of these questions have answers that any major Lebanese political actor accepts, because each answer reshapes the power distribution.

The census cannot be conducted because its preconditions cannot be agreed — and its preconditions cannot be agreed because agreeing them would already constitute the redistribution of power the census is supposed to produce. This is the closed loop. The census would require political agreement. The political agreement requires the census first. The system freezes. Lebanon has been governing itself by the arithmetic of 1932. The count that would close it cannot be taken, so the state that cannot be counted continues to decay inside the frozen arithmetic of a mandate-era French bureaucrat's clipboard.

## Sovereignty Theatre

**Final title:** Sovereignty Theatre
**Dek:** The Lebanese state does not exercise sovereignty. It performs it, on stage, for the Gulf patrons who pay for the performance.
**Byline:** Karim Salam
**Publication date:** May 1, 2026
**Estimated reading time:** 9 min read
**Topic tags:** Sovereignty, State Failure, Gulf Relations
**Excerpt:** The Lebanese state does not exercise sovereignty. It performs it — on stage, for the Gulf patrons who will pay for the performance, and who have no interest whatsoever in the real thing.
**Pull quote:** "The state did not lose a confrontation with a generator owner. It demonstrated, again, that the Lebanese state cannot extend its writ even to a meter reading in Sakiet el-Janzeer."

**Full body:**

The Lebanese state does not exercise sovereignty. It performs it — on stage, for the Gulf patrons who will pay for the performance, and who have no interest whatsoever in the real thing.

The tell is always in the target. When the Lebanese state wants to demonstrate that it functions, it does not go after the actors who have genuinely hollowed it out — the port managers who let 2,750 tons of ammonium nitrate sit for seven years, the central bankers who engineered an $80 billion transfer of depositor savings to politically connected accounts, the party militias that maintain parallel security architectures across half the country's territory. Those actors have real defenders. Going after them produces real consequences. Instead, the state selects targets that are large enough to photograph, small enough to survive without, and positioned in communities that cannot fight back effectively in that moment.

In April 2026, the Lebanese state tried to summon a generator owner named Abu Ali Itani for questioning over electricity fraud in Sakiet el-Janzeer, a Sunni neighborhood in West Beirut. Within ninety minutes of the State Security summons, motorcycles had blocked the roads surrounding his neighborhood, a Future Movement coordinator was on live television declaring the arrest an attack on the Sunni community, and garbage was being burned on the streets below. By nightfall, the state had quietly walked away. Abu Ali Itani was still in his apartment. The meters were still spinning.

The Lebanese state lost a confrontation with a generator owner. This is the condition of sovereignty in Lebanon in 2026.

The summons was never really about Abu Ali Itani. It was about optics. The Salam government, formed under international pressure in early 2026 as a precondition for Gulf reconstruction funding, needed deliverables. Not the deliverables that would actually restructure Lebanon's political economy — those would require confronting actors who can end governments — but the kind that photograph well in a ministerial press release. A crackdown on the generator sector. Evidence that the Lebanese state can extend its administrative writ into domains where it previously did not. Something to show Riyadh and Washington that the money is going into a state that functions.

The generator sector is perfect for this purpose. It is genuinely predatory — an estimated $2 billion annual extraction from Lebanese households through inflated rates, manipulated meters, and monopoly over the essential service the actual state catastrophically fails to deliver. It is widely hated. It has no sophisticated international defenders. What the state did not correctly calculate was that Abu Ali Itani was not a soft target. He was a Future Movement neighborhood operator in a Sunni community that had been politically orphaned since Saad Hariri's withdrawal in 2022, sitting on a reservoir of accumulated humiliation and unspent political energy. The summons became an occasion.

The lawyer for Itani stated publicly on television that they had secured a one-month administrative extension from the financial prosecutor to "prepare documents and settle violations." An admission, broadcast live, that the normal procedure for generator fraud is negotiation and settlement, not prosecution. The lawyer was saying this as a defense. He did not realize it was a confession. This is the Lebanese legal system describing itself in real time.

The Gulf states do not actually want a fully sovereign Lebanon any more than anyone else who has historically been involved in Lebanese affairs. A fully sovereign Lebanon — with a functioning army that secures all its territory, a judiciary that prosecutes all political actors regardless of sect, a tax authority that collects from import monopolies and generator cartels — would be a Lebanon that says no. It would say no to Iranian weapons transits. It would say no to Saudi political money. It would say no to American pressure on its banking system. None of these actors want that Lebanon. They want a Lebanon that performs sovereignty well enough to remain a viable client state, but not so well that it becomes an inconvenient one.

The performance serves everyone. The Lebanese state gets its reform credentials without the dangerous work of actual reform. The franchise holders retain their rent streams. Abu Ali Itani goes home. The meters keep spinning. And somewhere in the Saraya, a minister drafts a press release explaining that the rule of law has been preserved through dialogue.

The deepest problem with sovereignty theatre is not that it fails to produce sovereignty. It is that it actively crowds out sovereignty. Every successful performance of a fake crackdown reduces pressure for a real one. Every press release announcing a generator sector reform reduces the urgency of actually reforming the generator sector. Lebanon has been performing sovereignty for thirty years — for the IMF, for Saudi Arabia, for France, for the United States — and the performances have allowed every patron to maintain the fiction of engagement while the actual state has hollowed itself out below the theatrical surface.

## The Brilliant Nodes

**Final title:** The Brilliant Nodes
**Dek:** Lebanese diaspora produced some of the wealthiest people on earth. Lebanon produced the greatest banking heist in modern history. The difference is not talent.
**Byline:** Karim Salam
**Publication date:** May 1, 2026
**Estimated reading time:** 9 min read
**Topic tags:** Diaspora, Banking Crisis, Political Economy
**Excerpt:** Lebanese diaspora produced some of the wealthiest individuals on earth. Lebanon produced the greatest banking heist in modern history. The difference is not talent — it is the total absence of solidarity infrastructure.
**Pull quote:** "Lebanon has the processor. It ripped out the motherboard."

**Full body:**

Lebanese diaspora produced some of the wealthiest individuals on earth. Lebanon produced the greatest banking heist in modern history. The difference is not talent — it is the total absence of solidarity infrastructure.

Carlos Slim built the largest private fortune in Latin American history from a Lebanese immigrant family in Mexico City. The Safra banking dynasty — originally from Beirut — controlled financial institutions across three continents by the mid-twentieth century. Lebanese merchants ran the commercial arteries of West Africa for a hundred years. In São Paulo, in Sydney, in Dearborn, wherever Lebanese communities settled with functioning state institutions around them, the same raw material — the cunning, the multilingualism, the commercial instinct, the adaptive intelligence — produced extraordinary outcomes.

Meanwhile, in Beirut, the banking sector transferred an estimated $80 to $110 billion of depositor savings into politically connected accounts between 1993 and 2019, then collapsed, freezing what remained. Four million Lebanese lost access to their savings. The lira lost 98 percent of its value. The architects of this system — the politicians, the central bankers, the financial institution heads — have not been prosecuted. Their accounts, conveniently moved to foreign banks before the collapse, remain intact.

The raw material for Lebanese success is identical wherever you find it: extraordinary commercial instinct, tactical multilingualism, the adaptive intelligence of a people who have survived Phoenicians, Assyrians, Persians, Greeks, Romans, Arabs, Crusaders, Ottomans, and French without being permanently erased by any of them. In a competitive market economy with functioning institutions, these qualities produce exactly what they produce in diaspora: wealth, institutions, influence. In Lebanon they produce generator mafias, banking Ponzi schemes, and import monopolies. The same intelligence, aimed differently.

The Jewish diaspora survival model, which the Lebanese diaspora most closely resembles in its raw components, was built on a paradox: fierce individual ambition in service of collective persistence. The synagogue, the burial society, the lending circle, the landsmanshaft, the political lobby, the university pipeline — these were institutions that sat between individual ambition and collective survival, creating enforceable mutual obligations. The Hebrew concept captures it: all of Israel is responsible for one another. Not a platitude. A binding social contract. Lebanon has the ambition without the contract. Five hundred brilliant nodes in the same country cancel each other out, because each is optimizing against all the others with no institution to enforce cooperation.

The banking collapse is the brilliant node thesis written in fire. The Lebanese banking sector was built by Lebanese talent — genuinely sophisticated financiers who understood international capital markets, who built institutions that attracted diaspora remittances and Gulf petrodollars, who created the dollar-denominated deposit infrastructure that briefly made Beirut a regional financial hub. This was real. The talent was real. And then the same talent was applied to extracting the system's value rather than producing it. The financial engineering that drained depositors of $80 billion was sophisticated. It required intelligence, connections, and a precise understanding of how to route money through regulatory gaps. It was brilliant, in the clinical sense, applied to theft.

The specific tragedy of the Lebanese middle class is this: the professional stratum that stayed, believed in something being possible, deposited its savings, paid its taxes, sent its children to private schools, and accumulated whatever modest capital years of work produced. These were the people who behaved as citizens in a country that was not designed for citizens. They were the nearest thing to the solidarity infrastructure that Lebanon lacked. And they are the ones with frozen accounts and depreciated liras. The diaspora billionaires had moved their money out. The political class had moved their money out. The doctors and engineers and accountants who stayed — they are the collateral damage of a system that converts every talent into a weapon pointed at the people who can least afford to be weaponized against.

## What Taif Actually Said

**Final title:** What Taif Actually Said
**Dek:** The Taif Agreement did not end the Lebanese civil war. It converted the war's logic into administrative procedure.
**Byline:** Karim Salam
**Publication date:** May 1, 2026
**Estimated reading time:** 9 min read
**Topic tags:** Taif Agreement, Civil War, Constitutional Reform
**Excerpt:** The Taif Agreement did not end the Lebanese civil war. It converted it into administrative procedure — gave the militias suits, gave the checkpoints license numbers, and called the result a constitutional settlement.
**Pull quote:** "Taif redistributed the confessional parliamentary seats. Christians and Muslims now each hold 64. This is presented as reform. It was a renegotiation of the cartel's internal pricing mechanism."

**Full body:**

The Taif Agreement did not end the Lebanese civil war. It converted the war's logic into administrative procedure — gave the militias suits, gave the checkpoints license numbers, and called the result a constitutional settlement.

In Ta'if, Saudi Arabia, in October 1989, the Lebanese parliamentarians who had been elected in 1972 — seventeen years earlier, in a country that had since destroyed itself — sat down and wrote a document that would govern Lebanon for the next four decades. The men in the room had spent fifteen years either fighting each other or profiting from the fighting. The document they produced adjusted the sectarian power formula, transferred some authority from the Maronite president to the cabinet, mandated the "abolition of political confessionalism" as a future goal, and ended the legal basis of the civil war without resolving a single one of its causes.

Article 95 of the amended constitution: "Abolishment of political confessionalism is a fundamental national objective." That article was written in 1989. It has been dead since 1989. No government has moved to implement it. Every political actor in Lebanon quotes it when convenient and ignores it always. It is not a commitment. It is a placeholder — the diplomatic language that allows everyone to say the confessional system is "transitional" while ensuring it transitions nowhere.

Nabih Berri, whose Amal movement had conducted the War of the Camps against Palestinian refugees in the 1980s, became parliamentary speaker in 1992 and has remained so continuously since — thirty-four years, the longest tenure of any parliamentary speaker in the world's functioning democracies. Walid Jumblatt, whose Progressive Socialist Party conducted the Mountain War against the Lebanese Forces in 1983, became the indispensable Druze political broker of the post-war order. Samir Geagea was imprisoned — but this was not justice, it was his political rivals using the post-war state to settle a factional score, as he was the only warlord prosecuted while his counterparts were amnestied.

The amnesty law of 1991 erased the legal record of the civil war crimes. No transitional justice. No tribunal. No accountability process. The men who ordered massacres became the men who designed reconstruction. They brought to civilian governance the same management philosophy they had applied to armed conflict: extract maximum rent from your position, protect your community's share, maintain the ambiguity that allows threat without formal threat.

Now the same deal is being discussed again, in different clothes. The current proposal — circulating in Saudi diplomatic channels since late 2025 — is a "Taif renovation": maintain the framework's language, implement the senate provision of Article 22, liberate the lower chamber from confessional quotas, integrate Hezbollah into a Lebanese national defense strategy in exchange for disarmament. This proposal has structural merits. A bicameral system with confessional representation in an upper chamber and proportional representation in a lower chamber would, in principle, break the sectarian arithmetic that has prevented any genuine policy from emerging from the Lebanese parliament since 1943.

It will fail for the same reason every Lebanese reform proposal fails: the people who would implement it are the franchise holders. No cartel votes itself out of business from the inside. The senate provision was in Taif in 1989. The people who wrote it in 1989 are still in power in 2026, or their children are, or their political heirs are. None of them implemented Article 95 in thirty-six years. The current deal asks the same people to implement a different article of the same document they have systematically ignored since they signed it.

What Taif actually said, beneath the constitutional language, was this: the franchise holders have agreed to divide Lebanon's resources in a new formula. The formula will be presented as a peace settlement. The peace settlement will require an ongoing Lebanese state, and the franchise holders will provide one — weak enough to be managed, present enough to absorb international criticism, constitutionally legitimate enough to justify external funding. Every subsequent "Lebanese settlement" has said exactly the same thing. What Taif actually said was: we will do this again.

Between birth and death, your access to the state is mediated through your sectarian identity at every significant administrative moment.

The service state trap is reinforced by its own success. The longer the sect delivers and the state doesn't, the more dependent citizens become on the sect, the more loyalty the sect commands, and the more political capital the sect's leaders can deploy to ensure the state continues to fail. The cycle is self-sealing. The sect is not filling a vacuum. It is maintaining one.

Education does not change the service calculus while the service calculus operates at the material level. The most educated Lebanese citizen, fully aware that the sect's hospital was built with politically captured state funds that should have funded a public hospital, will still take their child to the sect's hospital rather than the dysfunctional public one, because their child needs care now and the structural analysis is for later. A cartel agreement is between producers who have agreed to divide a market among themselves. It specifies territory — who sells to whom and where. This origin story matters for understanding why Lebanese reform is systematically impossible from within the system. Reformers who enter the Lebanese parliament expecting to change it are operating in an institution whose fundamental design purpose is to prevent exactly what they intend. The parliament is not a deliberative body for the Lebanese people. It is the cartel board meeting, where the franchise holders negotiate their shares. A reform MP elected to the parliament is present at a meeting she has not been invited to and whose actual business she is not party to.

The cartel's internal logic explains what would otherwise be baffling: why Lebanese political actors who publicly despise each other, who accuse each other of treason and corruption, reliably unite when the fundamental arrangement is threatened. Hezbollah and the Lebanese Forces — organizations whose political and military histories are a story of sustained mutual antagonism — agreed on exactly one thing during the 2019 uprising: it had to stop. The critique of the National Pact is not that power-sharing was chosen. A genuine inter-communal accommodation would have been written down, ratified democratically, subject to regular review, and designed with mechanisms for evolving toward civic citizenship as trust developed. Eighty years later, Lebanon is governed by the verbal agreement of two men in 1943. The franchise holders have changed faces and sometimes families. The deal has not changed. It cannot change from inside itself. The institution designed to prevent the renegotiation of the deal cannot renegotiate the deal. Constitutional problems require constituent power — the power of a people to reconstitute their political arrangements from outside the existing framework. Lebanon has not yet generated that power. Lebanon is often described as a country with a sectarian problem. This is polite and wrong. Lebanon is a sectarian arrangement with a country problem. The sects are not debris scattered across the state. They are the units from which the state was assembled.

From birth, the Lebanese citizen encounters the republic through a confession. Marriage, inheritance, divorce, burial, political representation, public appointment, school networks, welfare access, even certain forms of fear: all pass through the communal gate. The individual citizen exists in speeches. The sect exists in law.

This is why reform so often sounds like translation from a language the system does not speak. "Abolish sectarianism" is a noble slogan, but it asks the machine to remove its engine and continue driving. The ministries are allocated. The jobs are allocated. The courts are allocated. The security posts are allocated. The municipalities are carved into local sovereignties. The hospital bed, the school scholarship, the passport renewal, the traffic violation, the condolence call, the envelope after the funeral: each has a route, and the route is rarely civic.

The Lebanese state was never allowed to become the main provider of life. That role was preserved for the sect, the family, the party, the za'im. This is not only corruption. It is pedagogy. Every citizen learns where help comes from. The state is the place that fails you. The sect is the place that answers the phone.

The pattern repeats at every scale. The country fractures into sects. Sects fracture into parties. Parties fracture into families. Families fracture into buildings. Buildings buy generators, water tanks, guards, cameras, filters, subscriptions, private arrangements. When all else fails, sovereignty descends into the individual body: your passport, your cash, your contact, your cousin, your escape plan.

This is why the Lebanese individual can be brilliant and the Lebanese collective catastrophic. The system trains people to survive without trusting the public. It produces tactical genius and civic paralysis. A Lebanese person can arrange a hospital bed at midnight through three phone calls, negotiate in four languages, build a company abroad, feed thirty guests without warning, and still throw trash into a public ravine because the public has never been experienced as his own.

War makes this visible. When people are displaced, solidarity appears instantly and beautifully. Volunteers open kitchens. Families host strangers. A Christian employer shelters a Shia worker. A Tripoli household receives a Dahieh family with tenderness. These acts are real. They matter. They prove the soul has not been fully captured.

But the solidarity has no house. It lives in impulse, conscience, exhaustion, crisis. The sectarian architecture has offices, budgets, flags, television stations, schools, charities, security men, graves, songs, and voting lists. The volunteer goes home. The party remains.

This is Lebanon's tragedy in miniature: the good is often personal, the bad is institutional. Personal generosity cannot defeat organized fragmentation unless it becomes something durable. The system does not need Lebanese people to hate each other all the time. It needs their love for each other to remain administratively useless.

The fracture was the blueprint. The Green Line was not invented in 1975; it became visible then. The civil war activated an architecture already present in law, geography, memory, and fear. Taif did not dismantle it. Taif changed the load-bearing walls and invited the same contractors back inside.

The question, then, is not whether Lebanese people can feel national solidarity. They can, and they do, sometimes with unbearable grace. The question is whether that solidarity can find form before the next crisis returns everyone to the institution that feeds them.

A country is not made by sentiment. It is made when sentiment becomes law, budget, school, court, army, tax office, public square, and shared consequence. Lebanon has the sentiment in flashes. It lacks the form. Until the form exists, every war will do what wars here always do: reveal the fracture, deepen the dependency, and send the citizen back to the sect that was waiting for him.

## Stones That Outlived Their Gods

**Byline:** Karim Salam  
**Publication date:** April 30, 2026  
**Estimated reading time:** 6 minutes  
**Topic tags:** Antiquity, Monuments  
**Excerpt:** Rome did not bring gods to the Lebanon range. It paid for the renovation of gods it found there.

**Suggested footnotes/citations:** UNESCO on Baalbek: [UNESCO](https://whc.unesco.org/en/list/294). Faqra inscription to Baelgalasos: [Livius](https://www.livius.org/pictures/lebanon/faqra/faqra-tower-of-claudius/faqra-tower-of-claudius-inscription-above-the-door/). **Full body:**

The phrase "Roman temple" does violence to Baalbek by telling the truth too narrowly. Yes, Rome built what still overwhelms the eye: the columns, podiums, courts, staircases, and stones large enough to make modern machinery feel provincial. But Rome did not invent the holiness of the place. It monumentalized it.

Baalbek was sacred before Rome arrived. The local god, the lord of the valley, was dressed in imperial language and called Jupiter Heliopolitanus. Astarte could become Venus. Adon could be absorbed into Bacchus or Mercury's orbit. This was not theological replacement in any simple sense. It was translation backed by marble. Rome knew better than to insult a god whose people had been worshipping him longer than Rome had existed. It put him in a toga and called the arrangement civilization.

The same pattern appears more quietly at Faqra, high on Mount Sannine. Tourists drive past ski chalets and find "Roman ruins." But the inscription on the Tower of Claudius names the ancestral god Baelgalasos. Ancestral is the word that matters. The god was already there. The emperor's name was added to a mountain address older than the empire.

Why so high? Because the old religion of the coast was not only maritime. It was vertical. The mountain gathered storm, snow, spring, thunder, fog, pasture, and fear. A storm god belongs where weather is born. A sanctuary at altitude is not inconvenient if the point is to ascend. Pilgrimage is the theology of distance made physical.

At Faqra, the tower was not only a monument. It was an altar platform. Animals were brought upward. Blood, smoke, crowd, priest, stone, weather. The ritual belonged to a world in which the divine was not abstracted into doctrine. It lived in place. It had an address, a season, a smell.

Christianity and Islam later reworked this landscape, but they did not erase its instincts. The high church, the hilltop shrine, the monastery above a valley, the saint on a ridge, the spring below a chapel, the fire on a feast night: these are not decorative survivals. They are the old grammar continuing under new names.

This does not require fantasy. It requires attention. The Roman layer is visible because Rome built in durable stone and left an archive of names. The older layer is harder to see because it was absorbed, smashed, renamed, reused, baptized, quarried, forgotten. But the pattern is legible. The sanctuary stays. The god changes clothes. The people keep climbing.

Baalbek is not proof that Lebanon was Roman in any simple civilizational sense. It is proof that Rome, at its most powerful, still had to negotiate with the sacred geography of the Levant. The empire could finance scale. It could not erase origin. The largest stones are not only a statement of Roman confidence. They are also a confession that local holiness required imperial tribute.

The empire is gone. The stones remain. The names changed. The mountain still gathers weather. The people still look upward.

## The Land That Mourns in One Language

**Dek:** Good Friday, Ashura, Adonis, and Tammuz are not the same religion. They are the same ritual grammar wearing different names.  
**Byline:** Karim Salam  
**Publication date:** April 30, 2026  
**Estimated reading time:** 7 minutes  
**Topic tags:** Ritual, Memory  
**Excerpt:** The Nahr Ibrahim runs red every spring. It does not ask which sect is watching.

**Suggested footnotes/citations:** Nature on Canaanite genetic continuity: [Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-017-01972-1). Ugaritic Baal Cycle and Ras Shamra context: Mark S. Smith and Wayne Pitard, *The Ugaritic Baal Cycle*.

**Full body:**

Every spring, the Nahr Ibrahim runs red. The old explanation was that Adonis had been wounded in the mountain and that his blood entered the river. The modern explanation is iron-rich soil washed down by rain. Both explanations are true in the way myths and minerals can be true at once. One explains the water. The other explains why people stood beside it and wept.

The Levant has been mourning the beautiful, righteous, murdered figure for thousands of years. Tammuz dies. Adonis dies. Christ dies. Hussein dies. The names change, the theology changes, the calendar changes, the argument around the death changes. The bodily grammar remains: women grieving, chests struck, bodies gathered, a community rehearsing loss until loss becomes identity.

This is not a claim that Christianity is "really" paganism or that Ashura is "really" Adonis. That would be crude. Religions are not disguises over a single secret. They are living systems with their own doctrines, histories, disciplines, and truths. But religions also grow from landscapes, and landscapes have habits. The eastern Mediterranean littoral has a habit of turning innocent suffering into communal memory.

Agriculture taught the first version. The seed is buried and returns. The field browns and greens. The god disappears and rises. The people learn that life can require a period underground. Empire taught the second version. On this coast, power often arrives from elsewhere. The righteous figure is killed by the stronger one. The community survives by remembering the death better than the killer remembers the killing.

This is why the ritual keeps returning. It answers a political condition as much as a theological one. For people repeatedly ruled, invaded, taxed, displaced, and renamed, mourning becomes a form of sovereignty. You may control the palace, the road, the army, the port. You do not control what we remember.

Lebanese politics understood this long before Lebanese political theory did. The dead leader becomes more powerful than the living one because the dead cannot compromise. Bashir Gemayel, Moussa Sadr, Kamal Jumblatt, Rafik Hariri, Hassan Nasrallah: each was processed through a sectarian grief machine that made criticism feel like desecration. Posters, songs, anniversaries, tombs, portraits, vows. The ancient grammar of sacred death moved from god to martyr to za'im.

This is both beautiful and dangerous. It binds communities through loyalty to the wounded. It also traps them inside the authority of the dead. A living leader can be questioned. A martyr becomes a wall.

The deeper possibility is that the shared grammar might be made visible. The Maronite grandmother on Good Friday and the Shia grandmother on Ashura are not performing identical beliefs. But their bodies understand a related truth. The woman at the tomb, the woman at Karbala, the woman beside the red river, the woman at the funeral of a son killed in the south: each knows that grief must be carried publicly or it rots inside the house.

Lebanon's sectarian system survives by making kin look like strangers. It tells every community that its mourning is unique, its dead uniquely pure, its wounds uniquely misunderstood. The older landscape says something else. It says the wound has many dialects.

If there is a usable civilizational memory here, it cannot be built by flattening differences. It has to make differences audible inside a shared register. Same coast. Same mountains. Same springs. Same instinct to sanctify the dead and insist that disappearance is not the end.

The Nahr Ibrahim runs red every spring. It does not check IDs. It does not ask whether the mourner is Christian, Shia, Sunni, Druze, Alawite, or nothing at all. The river remembers a grammar older than the categories. The question is whether the living are brave enough to hear it.

## The Looted Coast

**Dek:** Lebanon's antiquities problem is not only that objects were stolen. It is that memory itself was left without a state.  
**Byline:** Karim Salam  
**Publication date:** April 30, 2026  
**Estimated reading time:** 6 minutes  
**Topic tags:** Archives, Heritage  
**Excerpt:** A private collection in Lebanon is often not a romance. It is an indictment.

**Full body:**

The Lebanese coast is an archive with holes where its shelves should be. Some holes were made by empire, some by war, some by developers, some by collectors, some by poverty, some by the indifference of a state that should have guarded the ground and instead sold the ground twice: once to politics, once to real estate.

The Phoenician record is already fragile. The climate did not preserve libraries as Egypt's sand did. Papyrus rotted. Parchment disappeared. Cities were built over themselves for millennia. Tyre, Sidon, Byblos, Beirut, Arwad, each lived too continuously to become a neat ruin. Continuity is a gift to civilization and a curse to archaeology.

Then came removal. Sarcophagi to Istanbul. Inscriptions to Paris. Ivories scattered through imperial collections. Coins, stelae, figurines, glass, bowls, seals, fragments, each traveling under the polite language of acquisition. The great objects of the Phoenician homeland often became famous elsewhere, detached from the soil that made them intelligible.

Then came the civil war. Sites were opened without records. Necropolises were disturbed. Dealers thrived in the spaces where law had collapsed. The National Museum survived because people like Maurice Chehab encased objects in concrete. That sentence should be read slowly. A civilization's memory had to be hidden inside concrete in its own capital so that its own war would not eat it.

The post-war period brought a different violence. Construction became excavation without scholarship. Bulldozers moved faster than archaeologists. The old city became a development problem. Memory was surveyed, documented, relocated, and monetized. The market learned the value of antiquity while the republic failed to learn the value of archives.

This is why private collections in Lebanon cannot be treated sentimentally. Some may contain genuine, important objects that would otherwise be unknown. Some may contain fakes. Some may contain looted material. Often they contain the whole moral mess at once. A collector's living room can become the only place where an object survived, and also the reason the object no longer has a context.

That contradiction has to be held honestly. Provenance skepticism is necessary. Provenance nihilism is lazy. To dismiss every undocumented object is to abandon a large part of what war and collapse left undocumented. To celebrate every undocumented object is to reward the very violence that stripped it from the earth.

The proper path is slower and less glamorous than social media wants. Authentication. Petrography. Tool-mark analysis. Epigraphy. Publication. Engagement with the Directorate General of Antiquities. Digital catalogues. Scholarly access. Repatriation claims where possible. Public storytelling based first on documented material, with private material entering only through credible study.

Lebanese civilizational work must be able to survive hostile examination. It cannot depend on a thrilling object with no provenance and no tests. The documented record is already extraordinary: Ahiram's sarcophagus, Eshmun's sanctuary, the Byblos temples, the Sidonian royal necropolis, Tyrian stelae, glass, coins, ivories, inscriptions, the alphabet itself. The tragedy is not that Lebanon lacks evidence. The tragedy is that Lebanon has not taught its people how to see the evidence it already has.

A private collection may yet matter. It may contain pieces that shift scholarship. But the larger indictment remains. A country that cannot protect its banks cannot protect its dead. A country that cannot investigate an explosion cannot investigate a temple. A country that treats its coast as a real estate balance sheet should not be surprised when its ancestors end up behind foreign glass.

The looted coast is not only a heritage crisis. It is the oldest version of the state crisis. Who owns memory when the institution meant to guard it has been absent for fifty years? Who speaks for the dead when the living have no republic?

## Downtown Without a City

**Dek:** Post-war Beirut was rebuilt as a symbol of recovery. It also became the most polished monument to exclusion in the country.  
**Byline:** Karim Salam  
**Publication date:** April 30, 2026  
**Estimated reading time:** 6 minutes  
**Topic tags:** Beirut, Urbanism  
**Excerpt:** Solidere did not simply rebuild downtown. It taught Beirut that memory could be privatized.

**Suggested footnotes/citations:** AUB scholarship on old Beirut souks and Solidere: [AUB ScholarWorks](https://scholarworks.aub.edu.lb/handle/10938/10243?show=full). Recent critical review of Solidere's legacy: [MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/2413-8851/10/4/184).

**Full body:**

Rafik Hariri is the most difficult Lebanese leader to judge because he built something. In a political culture of speeches, he moved concrete. Post-war Beirut was shattered, and downtown did return: stone cleaned, facades restored, roads laid, cafes opened, investors reassured. The ruin acquired polish.

That is precisely why the damage is hard to name. Failure is easy to condemn when it looks like rubble. More difficult when it arrives with limestone paving, arcades, lighting, security, and table service.

Solidere did not simply reconstruct the city center. It changed the ownership structure of memory. The old souks had been dense, noisy, irritating, commercial, intimate, mixed. They were not perfect. No real city is. But they were Beirut's texture: argument, bargaining, smell, proximity, class friction, accidental citizenship. The post-war city replaced much of that with an administered downtown where the public could walk, but rarely belong.

The old disorder had contained a civic promise. A souk is a democratic technology in ways a luxury district is not. Rich and poor may not meet as equals, but they collide. They hear each other. They occupy the same air. A city needs these collisions because citizenship is not only a legal category. It is a habit of encountering people who are not arranged for your comfort.

Post-war downtown reduced encounter and increased image. The city became a postcard addressed to investors. It looked like recovery from far away. Up close, it often felt socially dead. Too clean, too managed, too expensive, too vacant after dark, too detached from the people whose capital it claimed to be.

Hariri's defenders will say the alternative was worse: paralysis, rubble, militia claims, no reconstruction at all. This cannot be dismissed. A destroyed city needed capital. The state was broke. The war had left property claims impossible. Something had to be done.

But necessity does not absolve design. The reconstruction chose a model. It could have preserved more ownership, more irregularity, more affordable use, more civic mixture, more public intimacy. Instead it produced a central district that embodied the post-war bargain: peace through debt, stability through exclusion, memory through corporate management.

This is the same logic that later devoured the banking system. Lebanon would appear solvent while becoming structurally hollow. It would look restored while losing the public basis of restoration. It would buy time instead of building institutions.

A city is not healed by being made photogenic. It is healed when its people can inhabit it without auditioning for the role of consumer. Beirut still lacks that right in many of its most symbolic spaces. The poor are visible as workers, guards, cleaners, delivery riders, waiters, but not as citizens at leisure. The middle class visits as memory tourists. The rich treat the center as curated atmosphere. The dead old city sits beneath them all.

This is why a bench can be political. A park can be political. A public square where no one must purchase the right to remain can become more radical than a manifesto. In a country where every service has been privatized and every identity franchised, the right to sit without paying is not small. It is the seed of public life.

Beirut does not need nostalgia. The old souks cannot be resurrected as theatre. What it needs is a future that does not require amnesia as admission price. It needs spaces dense enough for disagreement, cheap enough for ordinary use, layered enough to remember what stood there before, and open enough to let people stay.

Solidere taught Beirut that memory could be privatized. The next urban project worth defending must teach the opposite: that memory belongs to those who live with its consequences.

## Cousins Across a River That Shouldn't Exist

**Dek:** The Syrian coast is not Lebanon's neighbor in civilizational terms. It is the northern wing of the same house.  
**Byline:** Karim Salam  
**Publication date:** April 30, 2026  
**Estimated reading time:** 6 minutes  
**Topic tags:** Coast, Identity  
**Excerpt:** The Nahr al-Kabir is a border on paper. The land on both sides is older than the paper.

**Full body:**

Cross the Nahr al-Kabir and the map says you have left Lebanon. The mountain disagrees. The limestone continues. The terraces continue. The olives, tobacco, citrus, coffee, grandmother, balcony, shrine, and evening argument continue. The coast does not break because a French pencil broke it.

The Syrian coast, Latakia and Tartus, is often treated in Lebanese imagination as elsewhere: Assad country, Alawite country, Syrian country. Politically, this is true enough. Civilizationally, it is a mutilation. Ugarit sits above Latakia. Arwad faces Tartus. Amrit preserves one of the great Phoenician sacred complexes of the coast. This is not a neighboring appendix to the Canaanite-Phoenician world. It is part of the trunk.

Ugarit matters because it gave the old gods back their voices. Baal, El, Anat, Mot, Astarte, Dagan: not as polemic in someone else's scripture, but in their own mythic system. The Baal Cycle is not a footnote to biblical studies. It is one of the master texts of the eastern Mediterranean imagination. Any Lebanese civilizational project that stops at the modern border amputates itself from the archive that explains it.

The people of that coast complicate the story further. Alawites, Greek Orthodox, Melkites, Armenians, Sunnis, Ismailis, mountain villagers and port families: a mosaic that resembles Lebanon without Lebanon's formal confessional machinery. The Alawites in particular carry one of the region's strangest inheritances, an esoteric tradition shaped by Shiism, Christianity, Neoplatonism, and older Syrian religious currents. The mountain did for them what Mount Lebanon did for Maronites and Druze. It protected heterodoxy by making it hard to reach.

This is a Levantine law: the plain belongs to empire, the mountain to memory. Official religion enters through cities, courts, armies, schools. The mountain keeps the older thing alive in guarded forms. A feast, a saint, a hidden doctrine, a shrine, a spring, a reincarnation belief, a holy meal, a woman who knows which candle to light where. The surface changes. The ritual reflex remains.

Lebanese villages modernized differently. Silk, missions, schools, remittances, emigration, AUB, USJ, Sao Paulo, Detroit, West Africa, the Gulf. A village in Koura or Metn became a global node. Syrian coastal villages remained poorer, more rural, more directly tied to land and army. Assad's capture of the Syrian state did not produce the same local civic modernization that Lebanese diaspora money brought to Mount Lebanon. It produced protection, fear, dependence, and now, after the collapse of the old order, a terrifying uncertainty.

The Syrian coast should therefore be approached with care. A Lebanese telling this story can sound annexationist without intending it. The point is not that the Syrian coast is secretly Lebanese. It is that both Lebanon and that coast are modern fragments of an older littoral whose religious and cultural grammar predates both states. The family resemblance is not a claim of ownership. It is an invitation to stop thinking like the borders were born before the land.

The Nahr al-Kabir is real. Borders become real when armies, schools, passports, prisons, and wars enforce them. But the sacred geography is older. From Ugarit to Byblos, from Arwad to Tyre, from Amrit to Afqa, from Mount Saphon to Mount Lebanon, the same coast has been teaching different peoples the same habits for four thousand years: climb to pray, gather at springs, mourn the righteous dead, hide the inner teaching, survive the empire, return to the table.

A serious Lebanese Academic cannot be only Lebanese in the narrow state sense. It has to be littoral. It has to know that the coast continues beyond the passport office. It has to speak of kinship without conquest, continuity without ownership, memory without fantasy.

The river should not exist as a civilizational wall. It exists as a political fact. The work is to remember what the fact interrupted.

## Every Letter on This Screen

**Dek:** The alphabet is Lebanon's cleanest civilizational claim, and almost no Lebanese child is taught to feel its magnitude.  
**Byline:** Karim Salam  
**Publication date:** April 30, 2026  
**Estimated reading time:** 5 minutes  
**Topic tags:** Phoenicia, Archives  
**Excerpt:** Ahiram's sarcophagus is not an antiquity. It is the ancestor of the sentence.

**Suggested footnotes/citations:** Nature on Canaanite continuity: [Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-017-01972-1). For Ahiram and the Phoenician alphabet, see standard treatments in Joseph Naveh, *Early History of the Alphabet*.

**Full body:**

The most important Lebanese object may be a coffin.

The sarcophagus of Ahiram of Byblos does not look, to the untrained eye, like the center of world history. It is stone, royal, severe, guarded by carved lions and procession. But on its lid is an inscription that belongs in every classroom from Beirut to Boston: one of the earliest extended examples of the developed Phoenician alphabet, the script from which Greek, Latin, Hebrew, Aramaic, Arabic, Cyrillic, and many other writing systems descend.

Every letter on this screen is a late descendant of that revolution.

The alphabet was not simply a technical invention. It was a democratization of writing. Cuneiform and hieroglyphic systems required long apprenticeship, scribal castes, heavy institutions. Alphabetic writing compressed speech into portable signs. It made trade easier, record keeping faster, transmission wider. It turned sound into something a merchant, sailor, priest, administrator, or artisan could carry.

This is the Lebanese civilizational claim that needs no inflation. It does not require genetic purity, romantic Phoenicianism, or fantasies of superiority. It is enough to say the truth: the cities of this coast were central to one of the most consequential technologies human beings ever made.

Yet the claim is strangely absent from Lebanese public consciousness. The alphabet appears as trivia, not inheritance. Children learn the Phoenicians were traders, invented purple dye, sailed the sea, gave the alphabet to the Greeks. The sentence lands with the deadness of a textbook fact. It does not enter the nervous system.

It should. A country whose modern state has failed so badly needs a usable relationship to forms of achievement older than the state. Not to escape the present, but to enlarge it. The point is not to say "we were great" and then return to corruption with better posture. The point is to ask what kind of public culture would treat Ahiram's inscription as an obligation.

If the alphabet is part of the Lebanese inheritance, then language matters. Archives matter. Schools matter. Translation matters. Museums matter. Typography, publishing, journalism, scholarship, inscriptions, laws, receipts, court records, poems: all of these become part of the same lineage. A people descended from alphabet makers cannot remain indifferent to the destruction of its documents.

That is the bitter irony. Lebanon gave the world a technology of record and then became a country where records disappear. Port files vanish into procedure. Bank deposits vanish into accounting fog. Civil war archives remain buried under amnesty. Antiquities lose provenance. State memory is fragmented across sects, foreign repositories, family albums, and dead journalists.

The answer is not pride alone. Pride is too cheap. The answer is archival seriousness. Digitize. Translate. publish. footnote. teach. photograph. catalogue. protect. Build a culture that treats documentation as a civic virtue, because without documentation every cartel wins.

Ahiram's sarcophagus is a tomb. It is also a commandment: write it down so power cannot pretend it never happened.

The emotional temptation is clear. If every crime is external, then the resistance remains clean. Lebanon remains a victim of outsiders only. Your side does not have to look at its own hands.

But no serious politics can be built on innocence fantasies. Israel is an enemy. Hezbollah can also have killed Lebanese. Syria can have ordered Lebanese killed. Lebanese factions can have benefited from the silence. These truths do not cancel each other. They coexist because the world is more degrading than propaganda allows.

Do not give Israel the dignity of being the only villain. Lebanon has produced enough of its own.

Your friend,  
Karim

## Letter to the Young Lebanese Abroad

**Location:** Beirut  
**Date:** April 30, 2026  
**Estimated reading time:** 3 minutes  
**Excerpt:** Exile is not only loss. It can become the place where the cartel cannot reach the sentence before it is written.

**Full body:**

Dear you,

You left because the country made staying feel like a punishment for loyalty. You left for salaries, medicine, electricity, papers that move, streets that function, courts that answer, futures that do not require a cousin in an office.

Then you felt guilty for breathing easier.

Do not romanticize exile. It is a wound with good infrastructure. You will miss stupid things: the way someone says your name, the bread, the sea appearing between buildings, the shamelessness of home. You will also become freer in ways you should not waste.

Distance can make people shallow. It can turn Lebanon into nostalgia, cuisine, rage, and WhatsApp politics. But distance can also make you harder to recruit. The local cartel has fewer tools over you. It cannot threaten your job as easily. It cannot deny your permit. It cannot send your aunt to shame you at lunch. It cannot make you dependent on its charity to fix your roof.

Use that freedom.

Fund institutions, not factions. Read history, not only headlines. Refuse the binary when it arrives in the family chat wearing urgency as perfume. Build archives. Support journalists. Translate serious work. Teach your children a Lebanon larger than the sect that claims them and more honest than the tourism poster.

You are not outside the story. You are one of the places where the next version of it can be drafted.

With expectation,  
Karim

## Letter to the Nahr Ibrahim

**Location:** Byblos  
**Date:** April 2026  
**Estimated reading time:** 2 minutes  
**Excerpt:** You run red and people keep changing the name of the wound.

**Full body:**

Dear river,

They have explained you many times.

Iron in the soil. Spring rain. Runoff from the mountain. A seasonal event, perfectly natural, nothing to see here except geology moving through water.

They are right, of course. But explanation is not the same as meaning.

For centuries, people stood beside you and said Adonis was bleeding. They were not chemists. They were not fools. They understood something about the land that a laboratory cannot replace. A mountain can bleed if a people needs the mountain to carry what they cannot carry alone.

Now the names have changed. Christ, Hussein, a leader, a son, a village, a vanished imam, a prime minister, a fighter, a child. The wound keeps receiving new language. You keep running.

If the Lebanese ever learn to see themselves clearly, perhaps it will begin beside water like you: one river, many mourners, no sectarian checkpoint at the bank.

Keep your old secret.

Karim

## The Generator

**Date:** April 30, 2026  
**Short excerpt:** The generator is not a workaround. It is a constitution in diesel form.

**Full body:**

The state fails to provide power. A private man fills the darkness. The private man becomes indispensable. The politician protects him. The citizen pays the bill and thanks the person who made reform impossible.

This is Lebanon in miniature.

Every private substitute for a public good becomes a political relationship. The generator is electricity, but also school, hospital, court, militia, passport, water, security. The failure is not empty. Someone lives inside it.

## A Bench Is a Political Object

**Date:** April 30, 2026  
**Short excerpt:** In Beirut, the right to sit without buying anything is nearly revolutionary.

**Full body:**

A bench looks harmless until you ask who may use it.

If a city has no free place to remain, the citizen becomes either customer, worker, trespasser, or threat. A public bench says: you may stay without explaining your economic purpose.

After war, blast, collapse, and exile, this is not urban amenity. It is civic repair.

## The Building as Republic

**Date:** April 30, 2026  
**Short excerpt:** In Lebanon, the apartment building is often the smallest functioning state.

**Full body:**

The building has a generator, water tank, WhatsApp group, doorman, feud, treasury, emergency plan, and unwritten constitution. It negotiates fuel, fixes pipes, settles noise complaints, monitors strangers.

The nation failed downward until sovereignty lodged in the stairwell.

Sometimes the building works better than the republic because shame still operates there. You cannot disappear into abstraction when your neighbor hears the leak.

## The Horns at Faqra

**Date:** April 30, 2026  
**Short excerpt:** The mountain did not become sacred because Rome arrived. Rome arrived because the mountain was sacred.

**Full body:**

At Faqra, the inscription names an ancestral god. That word is everything.

The god kept the address. Rome paid for renovations.

## Ahiram's Keyboard

**Date:** April 30, 2026  
**Short excerpt:** Every typed sentence is a late echo of a coffin in Byblos.

**Full body:**

The alphabet should not be taught as a fact. It should be taught as inheritance with consequences.

If the letters on our screens descend from signs carved on the coast, then writing is not decorative to Lebanese identity. It is central. Archive, publish, document, translate, preserve: these become civic duties.

A people descended from alphabet makers should be harder to rob on paper.
