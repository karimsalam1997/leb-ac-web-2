# The City That Could Not Repair Itself

**Working title:** The City That Could Not Repair Itself
**Alternate titles:** The Country Under the Parking Lot; The City That Was Taught to Forget; Beirut, Bulldozed Twice; Memorycide in the Heart of Beirut
**Dek:** Downtown Beirut was not simply rebuilt badly after the war. It was rebuilt in a way that made later crises harder to absorb: vacancy became rational, repair became optional, ownership became distant, and the symbolic center of the capital lost the ordinary citizens who make cities heal.
**Byline:** Karim Salam
**Publication status:** Draft for Lebanese Academic
**Estimated reading time:** 18-22 min read
**Topic tags:** Beirut, Downtown, Solidere, Memorycide, Archaeology, Political Economy, Urbanism, Heritage

**Full body:**

Walk through Downtown Beirut now and the old critique no longer quite fits. It is not simply glossy. That would be easier to condemn. Gloss has a clean surface. Downtown today is stranger: restored stone beside blast-damaged facades, expensive apartments above dark windows, reopened restaurants near empty streets, international brands returning to souks that still feel more like a commercial experiment than a city center, sidewalks that can look manicured in one stretch and broken in the next.

This is not only the failure of a 1990s reconstruction project. It is the failure of a district to recover from everything that came after: the 2006 sit-in, the long security closure around Parliament, the 2019 uprising, the financial collapse, Covid, the port explosion, the years of stalled repair, the 2024 war, and the fragile attempts at revival in 2025. Downtown was built to signal that Lebanon had returned. But when Lebanon kept breaking, the center did not know how to repair itself.

That is the deeper riddle. Downtown did not fail because nothing was built. It failed because the wrong thing was built with astonishing confidence, and because that wrong thing had no ordinary social metabolism. Beirut's old center was not only a piece of land. It was the city's shared nervous system: souks, cinemas, offices, homes, bus routes, banks, churches, mosques, hotels, bookstores, coffee shops, shortcuts, arguments, rituals of shopping and passing and recognizing. It was not innocent. No city center is. It carried class hierarchy, sectarian tension, uneven ownership, bad infrastructure, and all the ordinary cruelties of commerce. But it performed the work a center must perform. It forced difference into use.

After the war, Downtown was treated less as a wounded civic organ than as an underperforming asset. The reconstruction promised to return Beirut to itself. Instead it asked Beirut to recognize itself in an image.

The common story is familiar: the war destroyed Downtown, Rafic Hariri rebuilt it, Solidere privatized it, and the result became beautiful but empty. That story is not wrong. It is only too thin. It misses the deeper violence, which was not only architectural or economic. Downtown Beirut was the place where Lebanon might have confronted three layers of memory at once: the Ottoman and Mandate city of everyday coexistence, the civil war city of rupture and accountability, and the ancient city buried beneath the asphalt, nearly five millennia of continuous habitation under the streets of the modern capital.

Instead, the reconstruction selected the memories it could monetize, displayed the ruins it could domesticate, erased the claims it could not absorb, and called the whole operation progress.

This is why the word memorycide matters. Albert Naccache used it to describe the destruction of Beirut's archaeological record in the 1990s. It is a severe word, and it should be. It names something more precise than neglect. It names the killing of the conditions through which a people can remember itself.

Downtown Beirut was not only rebuilt. It was taught to forget.

But forgetting was only the first failure. The second was more practical, and in some ways more damaging: Downtown was rebuilt in a way that made repair weak.

### The Second Destruction

The first destruction of Downtown came through war. The second came through reconstruction. The third has been quieter: the slow destruction of non-use.

Non-use does not look dramatic at first. A shuttered shop is not a massacre. A dark apartment is not a bulldozer. A sidewalk left broken after a blast does not appear in history books the way a battle does. But non-use changes the city at the cellular level. It means fewer residents pressing for repairs, fewer shopkeepers sweeping the same pavement every morning, fewer children making the street answer to daily life, fewer neighbors noticing damage before it becomes abandonment.

A city occupied by residents complains. A city held as an asset can wait.

That difference became brutally visible after the Beirut port explosion. In lived neighborhoods such as Gemmayzeh, Mar Mikhael, Rmeil, and parts of Achrafieh, residents and volunteers moved immediately because the place was inhabited. People knew which staircases were unsafe, which old woman lived alone, which shopkeeper needed plywood, which architect could assess a wall, which NGO was working on which block. The response was exhausted, uneven, underfunded, and unfair, but it had social intelligence.

Downtown had something else: distance. Too much of it had been built for owners, investors, visitors, office tenants, high-end consumers, and political-security arrangements that do not behave like a resident community. When such a district is damaged, there is less neighborhood pressure to repair quickly. A storefront can wait for the right tenant. A luxury apartment can remain dark until the right buyer appears. A damaged facade can sit beside a restored one because the social cost of vacancy is not paid by the same people who hold the asset.

This does not mean nothing is happening. In 2024 and 2025, the souks began to reopen in pieces, restaurants and brands returned, Saifi's food-and-beverage scene revived, and Solidere announced the removal of concrete barriers, flower boxes, and other obstacles from major streets to improve circulation. This matters. A dead center should not be romanticized as proof of purity. Life returning is good.

But revival is not the same as recovery. A district can reopen without becoming public. It can attract brands without creating residents. It can fill restaurants without solving ownership. It can raise occupancy claims without restoring civic life. The question is not whether a few streets are busier than they were. The question is whether Downtown has regained the social machinery that lets a city heal itself after damage.

So far, the answer is not convincing.

### The Old Center Was Not a Symbol

Lebanese nostalgia often lies by making the past too lovely. The old Beirut souks can easily become a soft-focus postcard: tiled arcades, elegant shopkeepers, mixed crowds, a vanished cosmopolitanism that asks nothing difficult of the present. That version is comforting because it turns the old center into mood.

But the real importance of the old Downtown was not that it was charming. It was that it was useful.

Al-balad worked because it gathered necessities. People came because they had to come: for cloth, shoes, gold, documents, banking, transport, repairs, books, restaurants, offices, cinema, government, gossip, work. Christians, Muslims, Armenians, Palestinians, Syrians, rural migrants, merchants, port workers, students, bankers, tourists, taxi drivers, clerks, servants, rich families, poor families, the respectable and the marginal, all moved through a center that belonged to no single community because it was too necessary to be possessed by one.

That kind of coexistence is not sentimental. It is infrastructural. A city does not become shared because everyone loves each other. It becomes shared when people must use the same streets.

The civil war shattered that use. The center became a front line, a scar, a battlefield, a buffer, a place avoided by the living and occupied by militias, snipers, weeds, and memory. When the fighting ended, the question was not simply how to rebuild buildings. The question was whether the center could again become a mechanism of shared life.

That question required patience. It required property settlement, public transport, archaeology, housing, low-rent commerce, memorialization, democratic planning, and some honest account of what had happened there. It required the state to behave like a custodian of collective memory.

Lebanon did not have that state. What it had was a postwar elite settlement, a ruined capital, a prime minister with immense ambition and financial reach, a planning imagination trained by Gulf modernity, and a political class eager to turn reconstruction into the architecture of the new order.

In a broken country, doing something can become an argument against asking what should be done.

Saree Makdisi saw this almost immediately. In his 1994 "Letter from Beirut," he described a city covered in the imagery of an imaginary future, posters and construction sites presenting reconstruction as destiny. The public was offered a false choice: accept the present ruins or accept the future being sold to you. The logic was brutally simple. Something must be done. This is something. Therefore this must be done.

That sentence could be the motto of postwar Lebanon.

### The Machine That Replaced the City

Solidere was not only a developer. It was a legal invention.

The Beirut Central District had an enormous property problem. Lots were fragmented. Ownership claims were layered across generations. Tenants, shopkeepers, landlords, heirs, waqf properties, damaged buildings, missing records, contested rights, and ruined infrastructure made ordinary reconstruction nearly impossible. Makdisi describes the scale as extraordinary, with hundreds of thousands of property-related claims tied to the old center. Tamam Mango's thesis traces the way this crisis became the justification for a private real estate holding company with powers no ordinary developer should possess.

Law 117 of 1991 created the framework. Solidere was established in 1994. Former property rights were converted into Class A shares; fresh capital entered through Class B shares. The company was given the authority to acquire, assemble, develop, sell, lease, and exploit the central district. In Solidere's own annual-report language, its mission included acquiring real estate, financing infrastructure, preparing and reconstructing the Beirut Central District, restoring and erecting buildings, developing the landfill, and selling or leasing lots. That sounds administrative. In practice, it meant that the symbolic center of the republic was transferred into a corporate structure whose obligations were not the same as the obligations of a city.

The distinction matters. A city center must answer to memory, access, citizenship, use, and time. A real estate company must answer to capital, land value, shareholders, debt, market timing, and saleable product. Sometimes those interests overlap. In Downtown Beirut, the overlap was never strong enough to carry the moral weight of the place.

Mango's work gives the abstract machinery a human scale. In one case, a property owner whose home fell inside the new district found that title had shifted into the company's framework and that retaining ownership required a payment he could not simply produce. The language around such cases was always clean: compensation, shares, redevelopment, public interest. But the lived experience was closer to dispossession translated into paperwork.

The old owners did not simply lose buildings. They lost position inside a social map. A merchant in the old souk did not own only square meters. He owned a route, a customer network, a reputation, a place in the memory of others. A tenant did not simply rent space. She participated in the dense web through which the city knew where things were and who belonged near them. When those rights were transformed into shares, the legal system could pretend value had been preserved. It had not. A balance sheet can price land. It cannot price being known.

This is the first central mistake of the Downtown project: it confused settlement with repair.

Settlement is what happens when claims are processed. Repair is what happens when a social world is given conditions to live again. Solidere settled many claims by absorbing them into a new corporate order. But the city that had produced those claims, the actual tissue of shops, residents, trades, paths, habits, and mixed use, was not restored. It was replaced by a cleaner and more expensive idea of itself.

Hariri's defenders have always had one serious argument: without a powerful vehicle, Downtown might have remained rubble for decades. That argument should not be dismissed too quickly. Lebanon's public institutions were weak, broke, captured, and exhausted. The postwar state could barely discipline warlords it had just reabsorbed into government. The bureaucratic route might have produced paralysis, theft, delay, and decay.

But this defense proves only that Lebanon needed capacity. It does not prove that capacity had to be organized as a private land bank with extraordinary public powers. It does not prove that the center had to be rebuilt primarily for high-end consumption, foreign capital, and land-value appreciation. It does not prove that archaeology had to be sacrificed, ordinary commerce priced out, and public memory thinned into ornament.

The tragedy is not that Hariri built. It is that he built the postwar order in stone.

### The Future As Blackmail

Downtown was sold to the Lebanese through the language of return: the heart will beat again, the souks will come back, memory will be preserved, the ancient city will meet the modern future. The promotional vocabulary was almost tender. Beirut would be ancient and futuristic, wounded and healed, familiar and new.

This language mattered because it disguised a change in ownership as an act of restoration.

Makdisi was especially sharp on this point. Solidere's reconstruction did not abandon memory. It used memory constantly. It promised old names, old facades, old routes, old souks, old charm. But the memory was increasingly visual and theatrical. A new shopping district could be called the Beirut Souks because it gestured toward the old souks in location and design, even if the social order that made a souk a souk had disappeared. The sign survived the referent.

A souk is not just a retail form. It is a memory machine. It remembers through repetition and informality: the tailor who knows a family across generations, the vendor who extends credit, the clerk who knows which alley avoids the heat, the argument over quality, the old religious institution at the edge of the commercial lane, the cheap meal, the bus nearby, the mixed crowds produced by need rather than ideology.

Replace that with a high-end shopping complex and you may preserve the name, but you have changed the verb. The old souk gathered. The new souk filters.

One early text about rebuilding the souks promised that the old memory would be carried into the future. It described a vast new structure on the original location, shops, offices, housing, leisure, cafes, esplanades, a large underground parking lot, archaeological integration, and green public spaces. The promise was not crude erasure. It was something subtler: curated continuity.

Curated continuity can be more dangerous than destruction because it lets the destroyer speak in the voice of the custodian.

The souks became the perfect object lesson. Downtown's reconstruction did not simply replace old with new. It replaced lived complexity with controlled reference. Beirut received heritage as atmosphere, not inheritance. It received memory as branding, not obligation.

The result was visible years later. Michael Karam's 2014 account of walking through Downtown described a district that once seemed to carry postwar pride but had become a ghost town, fenced, securitized, and economically stalled. He had once believed in the project, or at least in the feeling it produced. By 2014, the patient had gone into arrest.

That ghostliness was not a mysterious failure of Lebanese vibes. It followed from the business model. Downtown had been made to depend on forms of demand that were never stable enough to animate a national center: Gulf tourists, high-end retail, luxury real estate, foreign investors, wealthy expatriates, regional calm, and the idea that land values would keep climbing if the surfaces stayed polished. When assassinations, war, political paralysis, regional crisis, and economic collapse came, the model had little ordinary life underneath it. A center built as a premium destination could not fall back on being a city.

This is the second central mistake of the Downtown project: it treated public life as an aesthetic effect of development, not as the development's foundation.

### The Political Economy of Emptiness

Downtown Beirut was never only an urban project. It was a political economy.

The postwar Hariri vision imagined Lebanon as a finance, service, tourism, and real estate hub, a Mediterranean-Gulf connector able to attract regional capital and restore Beirut's prewar role as a commercial capital. In that worldview, Downtown was not simply the old center. It was a signal. If investors could see cranes, polished facades, restored streets, and luxury addresses in the heart of the capital, they could believe Lebanon had returned.

The problem is that a signal can become detached from the system it claims to represent.

Lebanon's reconstruction relied heavily on borrowing, capital inflows, real estate appreciation, and the ability of the state and banking system to keep confidence alive. A large body of work on postwar Beirut has shown how real estate became a storage device for external money: diaspora savings, Gulf capital, speculative investment, and elite wealth seeking safety in land. Bruno Marot calls this "pegged urbanization," a useful phrase because it captures the way Beirut's property market was tied to the larger Lebanese financial model. The city did not merely grow. It absorbed money into concrete.

Downtown was the flagship of that order. It turned land into shares, memory into real estate, and the symbolic center into a balance-sheet instrument. It was supposed to prove that Lebanon could make the future bankable.

But ordinary Beirutis did not have bankable lives at the scale Downtown required. The luxury apartments were too large, too expensive, and too disconnected from local purchasing power. Research on the postwar housing market shows a persistent mismatch: high-end units built for foreigners, expatriates, and the upper classes, while actual demand in Beirut was smaller, poorer, and more local. The foreign buyer was always more fantasy than foundation. Even before the full financial collapse, Beirut was full of apartments designed for a market that appeared in presentations more reliably than in transactions.

This is why Downtown's emptiness has a political meaning. It is not just that restaurants closed or tourists stopped coming. It is that the center was built around people who did not need it enough to save it.

A real city center survives crisis because ordinary life has no choice but to keep using it. People still need the bus, the clinic, the cheap shop, the school, the ministry counter, the repairman, the cafe where one coffee can last an hour, the public bench, the crossing point, the square where politics can happen. Downtown Beirut, as reconstructed, reduced too many of these necessities. It became important symbolically and weak functionally.

There is a cruel lesson here. The old Downtown was valuable because people who disagreed about everything still needed it. The new Downtown became fragile because too many of its intended users could leave.

Luxury demand is a weak basis for national memory.

### Vacancy Is A System

This is where the draft has to become harder, because "empty" is not just a mood. It is a structure.

Public numbers on Downtown vacancy are difficult to trust because occupancy can mean different things: leased, owned, used, inhabited, open to the public, or simply not available for sale. A company can report high occupancy and a passerby can still experience emptiness. Both may be describing different realities. A luxury apartment can be owned and uninhabited. A shop can be leased and rarely active. A street can technically be open and socially avoided.

The larger Beirut data makes the pattern harder to dismiss. World Bank project documents after the port explosion describe a speculative housing market with a severe mismatch between local needs and high-end supply. They note that vacancy in higher-end multi-storied apartment buildings in Beirut Municipality exceeded 50 percent, while the vacancy rate for plots of land was about one third. They also describe a housing system that incentivized homeownership and speculation over secure rental housing, with land prices rising far faster than incomes in the decade before the collapse.

Downtown is the most concentrated expression of that system. It is what happens when the city center becomes the most prestigious place to store value, but not necessarily the most necessary place to live.

Vacancy in such a system is not an accident. It can be rational. If owners expect future appreciation, they wait. If selling at the real market price would force everyone to admit values have fallen, they hold. If rents must drop enough to attract ordinary users, owners resist because a lower rent becomes a public signal of lower value. If the buyer pool is made of expatriates, Gulf capital, wealthy locals, and investors with fresh dollars, then the building is priced for people who may not be there. The asset survives by not becoming too honest.

This is why Downtown can feel frozen. Price discovery would be politically and financially dangerous. If Downtown is the ceiling of Beirut real estate, then admitting that Downtown prices are inflated would put pressure on the rest of the market. The empty apartment is not only an unused home. It is a defense against repricing.

The same logic applies to shops and offices. A living city lowers the threshold for use because use itself creates value: foot traffic, habit, safety, familiarity, reputation, neighborhood. A financialized district often does the reverse. It protects notional value even when that protection kills use. The rent stays too high, the shop stays empty, the street loses foot traffic, the remaining shops suffer, and the owner can still say the space is worth what it was worth in the model.

That is not a market. It is a pause button.

The consultancy version of the Downtown problem begins here: the central question is not "how do we beautify Downtown?" It is "how do we force inactive value to become active use?" Without that shift, more lighting, more events, more luxury tenants, and more marketing will remain surface treatments. The district does not need only activation. It needs an anti-vacancy regime: incentives for occupancy, penalties for long-term emptiness, public reporting of use, rent structures that reward local businesses, repair obligations tied to ownership, and a development model that treats inhabited space as more valuable than speculative silence.

### The City Beneath The City

The political economy of Downtown would be damaging enough if the only losses were social and financial. But Beirut's center carried another archive, older than the war, older than the Ottomans, older than Islam and Christianity, older than the Roman city whose columns became the acceptable face of heritage.

Under Downtown lay ancient Beirut.

Naccache's writing on this is devastating because it removes the comfort of abstraction. He argued that the Beirut archaeological site was among the oldest continuously inhabited urban sites of any world capital, with nearly five millennia of history concentrated beneath the old center. The postwar reconstruction was therefore not only an urban planning challenge. It was a once-in-a-civilization archaeological opportunity.

Lebanon could have dug its capital and found a common story under the sectarian one.

The timing should have made this obvious. The civil war had ended with no real national truth process, no common history book, no honest reckoning with responsibility, no shared account strong enough to stand above sectarian memory. Every community carried its own wounds and its own heroic lies. The ancient city under Downtown could not have solved that. Archaeology cannot redeem politics by itself. But it could have offered Lebanon a rare thing: material evidence of continuity before the categories that now divide the country.

Not purity. Not Phoenician fantasy. Not sectarian archaeology. Continuity.

Layers of Bronze Age, Hellenistic, Roman, Byzantine, Islamic, Crusader, Mamluk, Ottoman, Mandate, and modern Beirut under the same ground. A city repeatedly destroyed, reused, rebuilt, renamed, and inhabited. A place where each layer complicated the slogans of the present. A country that cannot agree on whether it is Phoenician, Arab, Mediterranean, Islamic, Christian, Eastern, Western, or all of these at once could have stood over its own center and seen that the answer was not a slogan but a stratigraphy.

Instead, much of that archive was bulldozed.

Naccache reported that, in the areas where infrastructure and construction work had moved ahead, the overwhelming majority of archaeological deposits were destroyed without adequate study. In "The Price of Progress," he described ancient material hauled away in dump trucks and emptied into the Mediterranean. He estimated more than seven million cubic feet of ancient Beirut lost. In "Beirut's Memorycide," he insisted that the destruction was needless, avoidable, and carried out despite the existence of knowledge, time, and money.

The numbers matter because they puncture the usual excuse. This was not a poor country forced to choose between feeding children and preserving potsherds. The Downtown reconstruction was a multibillion-dollar project planned over many years. Naccache argued that a small fraction of the total budget, spread over time, could have allowed serious archaeological work. He noted that the company had the unusual advantage of controlling the whole site and could have integrated excavation into long-term development. The problem was not impossibility. The problem was priority.

Here the moral scale becomes almost unbearable. A private company was given extraordinary control over a national archaeological site precisely when that control could have made preservation easier. Fragmented ownership was one of the stated reasons for creating Solidere. But once the land was unified, the same unification did not produce a unified archaeology policy worthy of the site. The corporate power that could assemble the city for development did not assemble the city for memory.

This is the third central mistake of the Downtown project: it treated archaeology as an obstacle or amenity, not as the ground of national repair.

### Why Archaeology Was Dangerous

Ancient ruins are usually safer than recent memory. Everyone can admire a Roman column. A Roman column will not name the militia that shelled a street, the party that stole a property, the minister who signed a decree, the developer who benefited, the banker who financed, or the family that was evicted. Antiquity can become a neutral zone where Lebanon performs unity without accountability.

So why was the archaeological record under Downtown treated so poorly?

Because serious archaeology is not the same as decorative antiquity.

Decorative antiquity gives a development prestige. Serious archaeology interrupts the schedule. Decorative antiquity produces a plaza, a glass floor, a brochure, a heritage trail. Serious archaeology asks who owns the past, who gets to interpret it, how long excavation should take, what should remain visible, what must be changed in the master plan, and whether profit should bend before the claims of the dead.

Serious archaeology also makes identity harder to simplify. It does not give the Phoenicianist, the Arab nationalist, the sectarian communalist, or the cosmopolitan developer exactly what he wants. It shows mixture, rupture, conquest, adaptation, reuse, continuity, and loss. It shows that Beirut did not belong cleanly to any one story. That is precisely why Lebanon needed it.

Naccache understood the political stakes. He connected the loss of the site to Lebanon's failure to produce a consensual national history. He wrote from within a country where identity documents, sectarian labels, and historical narratives had life-and-death consequences. The old argument between Phoenician and Arab identity, between sectarian origin myths and shared civic belonging, was not academic vanity. It shaped how Lebanese imagined one another.

A serious excavation of Downtown could have helped teach a different civic instinct: the land is older than the sects, and every community is late.

That lesson would not erase politics. It would discipline it. It would make it harder for any faction to claim exclusive metaphysical ownership over Lebanon. It would make the center of Beirut a school of complexity. Children could have walked through the city and learned that identity is not a weapon dug from one layer, but a responsibility toward all the layers.

Instead, the ancient city was often rescued only in fragments, while the development moved on.

### The Museum That Never Became The City

To be fair, not everything was destroyed. This story should not become a lazy accusation that every archaeologist failed or every planner was indifferent. There were excavations, rescue efforts, publications, preserved elements, and later attempts to make archaeology visible through heritage trails and site integrations. Beirut's buried past did not disappear entirely.

That partial survival is part of the tragedy.

Fragments can indict as powerfully as absences. A column in a plaza, a wall behind glass, a planned garden around ruins, a buried mosaic removed into storage, a route on a heritage trail: each says the city knew there was something there. The question is why the knowledge did not govern the project.

The Garden of Forgiveness is the perfect symbol. Conceived as a place where Beirut's past, present, and future could meet, it was designed to integrate archaeological remains with a landscape of reflection near churches and mosques. Its very name carried the moral ambition postwar Lebanon avoided everywhere else. It should have been impossible to miss the significance: in the center of a city that had not forgiven because it had not confessed, a garden would ask citizens to stand among the layers and think about loss.

And yet the garden sat delayed, suspended, "on hold," like so much of Lebanon's better self.

The same can be said of other archaeological episodes in and around Downtown: the Roman hippodrome debates, the site identified as a Phoenician harbor, the modernist Egg, the traces of the war, the cemeteries and ruins that resisted smooth incorporation. Giorgio Tarraf's thesis, with its title "Beirut: The Story of a City Destroyed by Peace," captures the perversity. War destroyed the city violently. Peace continued the destruction legally, administratively, and aesthetically.

Tarraf's work is useful because it refuses to treat heritage as only old houses. Heritage is the Egg, the port, modernist buildings, social fabric, archaeological layers, war memory, green space, and the remaining physical evidence that Beirut did not begin with the latest investor deck. Save Beirut Heritage's broader definition matters for this reason. A city is not preserved by saving a few pretty facades while erasing the systems of meaning around them.

Downtown's reconstruction often behaved as though memory could be edited into a development after the financial logic had already been fixed. That is backwards. In a city like Beirut, memory is not an accessory. It is infrastructure.

### The War Layer

There is another absence in Downtown, and it may be the most politically revealing one: the civil war itself.

Postwar Lebanon did not want to remember the war publicly because public memory would have endangered the men who survived it politically. The warlords entered the state. Militias became parties. Commanders became ministers. Amnesties became foundations. Families grieved privately while the public order insisted on moving forward. The phrase "no victor, no vanquished" became less a principle of reconciliation than a method of organized forgetting.

Downtown was the ideal place to challenge that forgetting. It had been the center, the front line, the wound, the gap between East and West Beirut, the place where the city split open. A serious reconstruction could have made the war legible without turning it into sectarian revenge. It could have marked destroyed streets, named civilian loss, preserved selected ruins, built a museum of the war, recorded testimonies, created public rituals of mourning, and taught young Lebanese that the center was lost once because politics failed.

Instead, the war became strangely absent from the polished center. Ancient layers could appear because they were politically safer. The recent dead were harder. A Roman wall does not accuse a living party. A war memorial might.

This created a grotesque hierarchy of memory. Beirut could display antiquity, imitate the souks, invoke heritage, and promote a future, while avoiding the historical event that had made reconstruction necessary.

The city was asked to remember everything except what had just happened.

That omission helps explain why Downtown feels haunted. It is not haunted because Lebanese are nostalgic. It is haunted because the place contains unprocessed claims. The old owners who were transformed into shareholders. The shopkeepers whose social world vanished. The archaeological layers hauled away. The war dead unnamed in the very district where the war's geography was most visible. The citizens who are told the center belongs to them but experience it as conditional access.

Ghosts are not always supernatural. Sometimes they are unresolved rights.

### The Blast Exposed The Repair Problem

The port explosion exposed something the Solidere debate had already suggested: Beirut has buildings, owners, tenants, developers, NGOs, ministries, municipalities, religious institutions, foreign donors, and security agencies, but it often lacks a public framework strong enough to make repair happen justly.

This matters for Downtown because repair is not only technical. It is legal, social, and financial. Who owns the building? Who lives there? Who has the right to authorize work? Who pays? Who benefits if the building is repaired? Who benefits if it remains damaged and the tenant leaves? Who can afford construction material in fresh dollars? Who is waiting for a chance to sell?

The post-blast evidence is stark. L'Orient Today's 2021 reporting found that, a year after the explosion, no one could give a clear assessment of how much blast-damaged Beirut had been rebuilt. The state had largely left reconstruction to NGOs and private initiatives. The Beirut Urban Lab's work on self-recovery argued that tenure status shaped households' ability to control the repair process, and that the recovery framework worsened pre-existing housing vulnerabilities. World Bank project documents describe affected historic residential units that still needed complex interventions to become habitable. In a UN-Habitat sample, more than 41 percent of affected buildings were already vacant before the explosion, and more than 32 percent had some kind of legal issue.

This is the part that makes today's Downtown different from the old critique. The old critique said the center was too polished and exclusive. The current critique must say something more difficult: the center is polished in fragments and damaged in fragments because it is not governed as a living district. It can have repaired stone and unrepaired trauma on the same street. It can have a luxury lobby beside a broken pavement. It can have a reopened cafe across from a dark apartment stack. It can have a blast scar waiting years because the people with the legal power to repair are not the people living with the daily consequences of non-repair.

That is what absentee ownership does to urban time. Residents experience damage as urgency. Distant owners can experience damage as accounting.

Lebanon's post-blast heritage crisis made the danger explicit. World Bank documents warn that speculation threatens the cultural identity of central Beirut, including owners' plans to repurpose or upgrade property, refusal to rehabilitate damaged property, and pressure on owners without repair funds to sell to developers or investors. This is the same historical fear in a new form: not a single Solidere-scale takeover, but a scattered aftershock of ownership decisions that can remove residents, weaken heritage, and turn repair into a route toward displacement.

Downtown should be the place where Lebanon proves it has learned from that history. Instead, it remains the place where the old lesson keeps repeating: when the city is treated as property first, people become temporary.

### From Custodian To Liquidator

One distinction clarifies the whole project: the difference between a custodian and a liquidator.

A custodian holds an asset across generations. It asks how land can keep producing use, memory, identity, rent, and civic value without exhausting itself. A custodian can still be financially disciplined. It can lease, restore, develop, and profit. But it understands that some places become more valuable when they are not sold too quickly, not homogenized, not priced beyond ordinary life, not emptied of the people who make them meaningful.

A liquidator converts assets into saleable value. It asks how to unlock land, assemble parcels, raise capital, clear obstacles, and produce returns. Liquidation can look dynamic. It creates movement. It satisfies the hunger for visible action. It can even rebuild beautifully. But it struggles with the slow obligations of a city center because those obligations do not always maximize short-term financial value.

Solidere often behaved less like the custodian of Lebanon's symbolic center than like the liquidator of a wounded city.

This does not mean every decision was malicious. That is too easy and probably false. The deeper problem is structural. Once Downtown was placed inside a corporate model organized around land value, share value, sale, lease, development rights, and premium positioning, many later choices became predictable. Archaeology became delay. Old tenants became complications. Low-margin commerce became inefficient. Public disorder became brand risk. Political assembly became security threat. Ordinary Beirutis became visitors. The center's success was measured by criteria that could rise while civic life fell.

This is why the critique cannot stop at Rafic Hariri as a person. Hariri matters, of course. His ambition, charisma, money, Saudi connections, planning imagination, and postwar dominance shaped the project. But Downtown is larger than one man. It is the spatial form of a whole Lebanese settlement: public weakness, private substitution, elite capture, financialization, amnesty, selective memory, and a future sold as proof that the past no longer needs to be argued over.

Downtown is Lebanon's postwar order with sidewalks.

### The Center As Connector

What would it mean to think of Downtown differently?

Start with a simple premise: a capital's center is not primarily a trophy. It is a connector.

It should connect East and West Beirut, coast and interior, citizen and institution, old memory and new use, archaeology and education, commerce and public life, mourning and assembly, transport and walking, everyday needs and symbolic space. It should be the place where the Lebanese encounter one another without needing a sectarian broker, a private membership, a luxury budget, or a political invitation.

By that standard, Downtown's reconstruction was too weak at connection and too strong at display.

A different model would have made the center harder to monetize quickly and easier to inhabit deeply. Long leases instead of aggressive land sales. Public-interest covenants tied to affordability, cultural use, and local business. A real archaeology authority with budget and power. A war memory institution in the center, not hidden at the edge of polite society. Public transport through the district. Universities, libraries, archives, artist studios, repair trades, small shops, clinics, municipal services, mixed-income housing, and public spaces where sitting is not treated as loitering. Incentives tied not only to square meters developed, but to occupancy, diversity of use, local employment, and public access.

Some of this sounds obvious because it is. But Lebanon has a genius for making obvious public goods seem naive once a private substitute has already occupied the space.

The point is not to resurrect the old souks exactly as they were. That would be another kind of nostalgia. Cities cannot be embalmed. The old Downtown had problems that should not be romanticized. The point is to recover the function the old center performed: shared use under conditions of difference.

The center does not need to look old. It needs to behave publicly.

That means measuring different things. Not only land sales, rental income, and square meters delivered, but active ground-floor frontage, actual residential nights, affordable commercial leases, cultural programming that does not require luxury consumption, public seating, pedestrian continuity, repaired blast damage, local business survival, and the number of routes by which an ordinary person can cross the district without feeling managed.

The most important word is use.

Use is what turns architecture into city. Use is what makes a sidewalk politically meaningful. Use is what forces maintenance. Use is what makes security less paranoid because presence itself creates safety. Use is what makes memory repeatable rather than ceremonial. Use is what makes a shopkeeper care about the pavement outside his door and a resident care about the lamp below her window.

Downtown Beirut does not need another claim that it is coming back. It needs a public audit of what "back" means.

### The Question Now

The legal and corporate horizon of Solidere has always been part of the political problem. The company was created in 1994 under the postwar legal framework, with an original duration described in its annual reports as twenty-five years and later amendments and extensions that complicated the timeline. The details matter, but the larger question is simpler: how long can the center of Beirut remain governed by a model whose original promise has already been tested by history?

The regional-tourism dream did not produce a stable civic center. The luxury real estate model did not create ordinary life. The shareholder logic did not protect memory. The security logic did not produce belonging. The curated heritage did not replace the archive that was destroyed. The polished streets did not heal the war. The old owners did not return as a social world. The souks did not become souks in the old urban sense. The Garden of Forgiveness did not become the country's ritual of repair. The archaeological site did not become the national classroom it could have been.

At some point, the defense that "at least something was done" expires.

Downtown Beirut now asks a different generation a different question. Not whether the ruins should have remained. Not whether Hariri was visionary or villain. Not whether Solidere built nice streets. The real question is whether Lebanon can take its center back conceptually before it takes it back administratively. Can the Lebanese imagine Downtown not as a luxury district, not as a nostalgia object, not as a battlefield between old ideological camps, but as a memory site that still has future work to do?

That future work is not abstract. It means archives, museums, schools, public transport, rent structures, small commerce, archaeology, housing, programming, public benches, accessible waterfronts, war memory, preserved modernism, and planning authority that answers to citizens rather than only to capital. It means treating the center as a public trust even where private ownership remains. It means making memory useful, not decorative.

Above all, it means refusing the clean slate.

The clean slate is the most dangerous fantasy in Lebanon. Every faction wants one. Every developer wants one. Every foreign patron wants one. Every postwar settlement promises one. Forget the war. Forget the theft. Forget the old owners. Forget the sectarian bargain. Forget the bones under the street. Forget the money trail. Forget who signed. Forget who benefited. Forget who was priced out. Forget who cannot enter. Forget the archive at the bottom of the excavation pit. Look at the rendering. Look at the future. Look at the lights.

But countries do not heal by forgetting. They heal by learning what to do with memory that cannot be made convenient.

Downtown Beirut is still the right place to begin because it contains all of Lebanon's problems in one square geography: sectarian coexistence and rupture, public weakness and private power, real estate and debt, archaeology and identity, war amnesia and curated heritage, Gulf ambition and local exclusion, beauty and emptiness, the dream of return and the mechanics of dispossession.

The center failed as a national room because it was rebuilt as a premium address. It can only matter again if it becomes a place where the country is allowed to meet itself without a cover charge.

### The Country Under The Parking Lot

There is something almost unbearable about the parking lots beneath Downtown. Not because parking is unimportant. Cities need infrastructure. But in Beirut, the image is too perfect: layers of ancient city under asphalt and concrete, old souks replaced by controlled retail, a national memory site reorganized around cars, capital, and access.

The country was not literally buried under one parking lot. But something like the country was buried under the logic that made the parking lot feel more urgent than the archive.

Naccache's warning remains the deepest one. The loss was not only scholarly. It was civic. Lebanon lost evidence that might have helped it narrate itself beyond sectarian fragments. It lost the chance to make the capital's ground into a shared text. It lost the opportunity to teach its children that their history is not a competition between purified origins, but a crowded inheritance under their feet.

That is what memorycide means in Beirut. It is not only the destruction of ruins. It is the destruction of a people's ability to recognize itself across time.

And yet Downtown is not finished. Empty places can still be reinterpreted. Failed models can still become evidence. The ruins that survived, the documents that remain, the photographs of demolished souks, the testimonies of property owners, the archaeological reports, the ghost streets, the unfinished gardens, the Egg, the old street names, the reels people still make while walking through the polished absence, all of it can still be gathered into a sharper public memory.

Maybe that is the work now: not to mourn Downtown as though it died once, but to understand how it was killed in stages, by shelling, by law, by bulldozer, by capital, by silence, by beauty without use, by memory without obligation.

The old city is not coming back. That is not the task.

The task is to build a center that no longer needs to lie about what happened there.

Beirut does not need another rendering of a future that asks for amnesia as the price of entry. It needs a downtown capable of holding the past without freezing inside it, developing without erasing, remembering without becoming a museum, and welcoming the ordinary citizen without making him feel like he has entered someone else's investment.

The city was once bulldozed by war. Then it was bulldozed by peace.

If it is ever rebuilt again, it should be rebuilt against forgetting.
